Scientific notation is a powerful method for expressing very large or very small numbers using powers of 10. On the ACT, you'll need to convert between standard form and scientific notation, perform operations with scientific notation, and interpret exponent rules in this context. Scientific notation appears in 2-4 questions per ACT—that's about 3-7% of all math questions! This lesson covers everything from basic conversions to complex calculations involving multiplication, division, and order of magnitude comparisons.

1. Understanding Scientific Notation

Scientific notation expresses numbers as a product of a coefficient (between 1 and 10) and a power of 10. The general form is a × 10n, where 1 ≤ a < 10 and n is an integer. This format makes it easy to work with extremely large numbers (like distances in space) or extremely small numbers (like atomic measurements).

The Structure of Scientific Notation

Format: a × 10n

Coefficient (a): A number between 1 and 10 (can equal 1, but must be less than 10)

Base: Always 10

Exponent (n): An integer (positive, negative, or zero) that tells how many places to move the decimal point

Examples of proper scientific notation:

  • 3.5 × 106 ✓ (coefficient is between 1 and 10)
  • 1.0 × 10-3 ✓
  • 9.99 × 1012 ✓
  • 7 × 100 ✓ (equals 7)

Examples of improper scientific notation:

  • 35 × 105 ✗ (coefficient must be less than 10)
  • 0.5 × 108 ✗ (coefficient must be at least 1)
  • 10 × 103 ✗ (coefficient cannot equal 10)

Powers of 10 and Place Value

Understanding how the exponent relates to decimal place movement is crucial:

Positive exponents (large numbers):
100 = 1
101 = 10
102 = 100
103 = 1,000
106 = 1,000,000

Negative exponents (small numbers):
10-1 = 0.1
10-2 = 0.01
10-3 = 0.001
10-6 = 0.000001

Converting from Standard Form to Scientific Notation

For large numbers (greater than or equal to 10):

Example: Convert 45,000 to scientific notation

Step 1: Place the decimal after the first non-zero digit: 4.5

Step 2: Count how many places the decimal moved: 4 places to the left

Step 3: Write as: 4.5 × 104

Rule: Moving the decimal left gives a positive exponent

For small numbers (between 0 and 1):

Example: Convert 0.00072 to scientific notation

Step 1: Place the decimal after the first non-zero digit: 7.2

Step 2: Count how many places the decimal moved: 4 places to the right

Step 3: Write as: 7.2 × 10-4

Rule: Moving the decimal right gives a negative exponent

Converting from Scientific Notation to Standard Form

Positive exponent example: 3.7 × 105

Move decimal 5 places to the right: 370,000

Negative exponent example: 2.4 × 10-3

Move decimal 3 places to the left: 0.0024

Quick rule: Positive exponent = larger number; Negative exponent = smaller number (fraction)

ACT Example 1: Converting to Scientific Notation

The distance from Earth to the Sun is approximately 93,000,000 miles. Which of the following expresses this distance in scientific notation?

A) 9.3 × 106

B) 9.3 × 107

C) 9.3 × 108

D) 93 × 106

E) 0.93 × 108

Solution:

Step 1: Start with 93,000,000 and place decimal after first digit: 9.3

Step 2: Count decimal places moved: 93,000,000 → 9.3 is 7 places left

Step 3: Result is 9.3 × 107

Answer: B — Count carefully! Choice D has wrong coefficient (93 ≥ 10), choice E has coefficient too small.

2. Operations with Scientific Notation

Performing arithmetic operations with scientific notation requires understanding exponent rules. Multiplication and division are straightforward, while addition and subtraction require numbers to have the same power of 10. These operations appear frequently on ACT problems involving scaling, scientific measurements, and order of magnitude calculations.

Multiplication in Scientific Notation

Rule: Multiply the coefficients and add the exponents

(a × 10m) × (b × 10n) = (a × b) × 10m+n

Example: (3 × 104) × (2 × 105)

Step 1: Multiply coefficients: 3 × 2 = 6

Step 2: Add exponents: 4 + 5 = 9

Result: 6 × 109

Check: Coefficient is between 1 and 10 ✓

When the product exceeds 10:

Example: (4 × 103) × (5 × 102)

Step 1: Multiply coefficients: 4 × 5 = 20

Step 2: Add exponents: 3 + 2 = 5

Initial result: 20 × 105 (not proper scientific notation!)

Step 3: Convert 20 to 2.0 × 101

Step 4: Combine: 2.0 × 101 × 105 = 2.0 × 106

Rule: If coefficient ≥ 10, divide it by 10 and add 1 to the exponent

Division in Scientific Notation

Rule: Divide the coefficients and subtract the exponents

(a × 10m) ÷ (b × 10n) = (a ÷ b) × 10m-n

Example: (8 × 107) ÷ (4 × 103)

Step 1: Divide coefficients: 8 ÷ 4 = 2

Step 2: Subtract exponents: 7 - 3 = 4

Result: 2 × 104

When the quotient is less than 1:

Example: (3 × 105) ÷ (6 × 102)

Step 1: Divide coefficients: 3 ÷ 6 = 0.5

Step 2: Subtract exponents: 5 - 2 = 3

Initial result: 0.5 × 103 (not proper scientific notation!)

Step 3: Convert 0.5 to 5.0 × 10-1

Step 4: Combine: 5.0 × 10-1 × 103 = 5.0 × 102

Rule: If coefficient < 1, multiply it by 10 and subtract 1 from the exponent

Addition and Subtraction in Scientific Notation

Rule: Numbers must have the same power of 10 before adding or subtracting coefficients

Example with same exponent: (5 × 103) + (3 × 103)

Step 1: Exponents match, so add coefficients: 5 + 3 = 8

Result: 8 × 103

Example with different exponents: (4 × 105) + (6 × 104)

Step 1: Rewrite to match exponents. Convert 6 × 104 to 0.6 × 105

Step 2: Now add: (4 × 105) + (0.6 × 105) = 4.6 × 105

Alternative: Convert both to standard form, add, then convert back to scientific notation
400,000 + 60,000 = 460,000 = 4.6 × 105

Powers and Roots in Scientific Notation

Squaring: (3 × 104)2 = 32 × (104)2 = 9 × 108

Cubing: (2 × 103)3 = 23 × (103)3 = 8 × 109

Square root: √(4 × 106) = √4 × √(106) = 2 × 103

Rule: Apply the power to both the coefficient and the exponent separately

ACT Example 2: Multiplication with Scientific Notation

What is the product of (3.5 × 108) and (4.0 × 10-3)?

F) 1.4 × 105

G) 1.4 × 106

H) 14 × 105

J) 7.5 × 1011

K) 7.5 × 10-11

Solution:

Step 1: Multiply coefficients: 3.5 × 4.0 = 14.0

Step 2: Add exponents: 8 + (-3) = 5

Step 3: Initial result: 14.0 × 105 (coefficient too large!)

Step 4: Convert: 14.0 = 1.4 × 101, so 1.4 × 101 × 105 = 1.4 × 106

Answer: G — Always adjust the coefficient to be between 1 and 10!

3. Comparing and Ordering Numbers in Scientific Notation

Comparing numbers in scientific notation requires understanding the relationship between exponents and coefficients. The order of magnitude—determined by the exponent—is typically more important than the coefficient when comparing very different scales. This skill helps you quickly identify the largest or smallest value and estimate approximate relationships.

Comparing Numbers with Different Exponents

Rule: When exponents differ, the number with the larger exponent is larger (for positive exponents) or smaller (for negative exponents)

Example 1: Compare 3.8 × 107 and 9.2 × 105

Analysis: 107 > 105, so 3.8 × 107 is larger

Even though 9.2 > 3.8, the exponent difference dominates

3.8 × 107 = 38,000,000
9.2 × 105 = 920,000

Example 2: Compare 4.5 × 10-3 and 2.1 × 10-5

Analysis: For negative exponents, less negative = larger

-3 > -5, so 10-3 > 10-5

Therefore, 4.5 × 10-3 is larger

4.5 × 10-3 = 0.0045
2.1 × 10-5 = 0.000021

Comparing Numbers with the Same Exponent

Rule: When exponents are equal, compare the coefficients directly

Example: Compare 7.3 × 104 and 5.9 × 104

Analysis: Exponents are equal (both 4)

Compare coefficients: 7.3 > 5.9

Therefore, 7.3 × 104 is larger

Ordering Multiple Numbers

Example: Order from least to greatest:
A = 6.7 × 103
B = 4.2 × 105
C = 9.1 × 102
D = 3.5 × 105

Step 1: Group by exponent
102: C
103: A
105: B, D

Step 2: Order groups: 102 < 103 < 105

Step 3: Within 105 group, compare coefficients: 3.5 < 4.2

Final order: C < A < D < B

Order of Magnitude

The order of magnitude tells you approximately how large a number is by its power of 10.

How many times larger is 4.2 × 108 than 3.5 × 105?

Quick estimate: 108 ÷ 105 = 103 = 1,000

So it's roughly 1,000 times larger (order of magnitude difference of 3)

Exact calculation:
(4.2 × 108) ÷ (3.5 × 105) = (4.2 ÷ 3.5) × 103 = 1.2 × 103 = 1,200

Working with Mixed Forms

Sometimes you'll need to compare scientific notation with standard form.

Example: Which is larger: 7.8 × 104 or 82,000?

Method 1: Convert 82,000 to scientific notation
82,000 = 8.2 × 104
Compare: 8.2 × 104 > 7.8 × 104
82,000 is larger

Method 2: Convert 7.8 × 104 to standard form
7.8 × 104 = 78,000
82,000 > 78,000

ACT Example 3: Ordering Numbers

Which list shows the following numbers in order from least to greatest?

P = 5.4 × 10-2
Q = 3.7 × 103
R = 8.1 × 10-3
S = 2.9 × 102

A) P, R, S, Q

B) R, P, S, Q

C) R, P, Q, S

D) S, Q, P, R

E) Q, S, P, R

Solution:

Step 1: Identify negative vs positive exponents. Negative exponents are fractions (less than 1)

Step 2: Compare negative exponents: -3 > -2, so 10-3 > 10-2. Thus R > P

Step 3: Compare positive exponents: 102 < 103, so S < Q

Step 4: All positive exponent values are larger than negative exponent values

Final order: R (0.0081), P (0.054), S (290), Q (3,700)

Answer: B — Remember: for negative exponents, less negative means larger!

4. Applications and Problem-Solving Strategies

Real-world applications of scientific notation appear in contexts involving astronomy, microbiology, population statistics, and financial data. ACT problems often combine scientific notation with other mathematical concepts like unit conversion, percent change, and proportional reasoning.

Scientific and Astronomical Applications

Example: Light travels at 3.0 × 108 meters per second. How far does light travel in one year (approximately 3.15 × 107 seconds)?

Step 1: Multiply distance per second by total seconds
(3.0 × 108) × (3.15 × 107)

Step 2: Multiply coefficients: 3.0 × 3.15 = 9.45

Step 3: Add exponents: 8 + 7 = 15

Result: 9.45 × 1015 meters

This distance is called a light-year!

Microscopic Scale Applications

Example: A cell has a diameter of 2.5 × 10-5 meters. How many cells would fit end-to-end across a 1-meter line?

Step 1: Divide total distance by cell diameter
1 ÷ (2.5 × 10-5)

Step 2: Rewrite as: (1 × 100) ÷ (2.5 × 10-5)

Step 3: Divide coefficients: 1 ÷ 2.5 = 0.4

Step 4: Subtract exponents: 0 - (-5) = 5

Step 5: Result: 0.4 × 105 = 4.0 × 104 = 40,000 cells

Population and Financial Data

Example: A country has a population of 1.5 × 108 people. If the national debt is $6.0 × 1012, what is the debt per person?

Step 1: Divide total debt by population
(6.0 × 1012) ÷ (1.5 × 108)

Step 2: Divide coefficients: 6.0 ÷ 1.5 = 4.0

Step 3: Subtract exponents: 12 - 8 = 4

Result: 4.0 × 104 = $40,000 per person

Combining Scientific Notation with Percent Change

Example: A population grows from 4.2 × 105 to 5.04 × 105. What is the percent increase?

Step 1: Find the change: 5.04 × 105 - 4.2 × 105 = 0.84 × 105

Step 2: Divide change by original: (0.84 × 105) ÷ (4.2 × 105) = 0.84/4.2 = 0.2

Step 3: Convert to percent: 0.2 = 20%

Problem-Solving Strategies

Strategy 1: Convert to scientific notation first

If given standard form numbers, convert to scientific notation before computing. This makes calculations cleaner and reduces errors with zeros.

Strategy 2: Estimate before calculating

Round coefficients to simple numbers to estimate. For example, 4.8 × 107 ≈ 5 × 107. This helps you eliminate obviously wrong answer choices.

Strategy 3: Check answer reasonableness

Ask: Should my answer be larger or smaller than the original? Multiplying by a number greater than 1 increases value; multiplying by a fraction decreases it.

Strategy 4: Work with exponents separately

Handle coefficients and exponents as separate calculations, then combine. This reduces mental load and prevents arithmetic errors.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Adding exponents when multiplying coefficients

Wrong: (2 × 103) × (3 × 104) = 6 × 1012 ✗
Right: (2 × 103) × (3 × 104) = 6 × 107 ✓

Remember: Add exponents, multiply coefficients!

Mistake 2: Forgetting to adjust improper scientific notation

If your coefficient is 15.2 × 106, you must convert to 1.52 × 107. The ACT often includes partially-correct answers as distractors!

Mistake 3: Sign errors with negative exponents

When subtracting a negative exponent, remember: a - (-b) = a + b

Example: 103 ÷ 10-2 = 103-(-2) = 105, not 101

ACT Example 4: Complex Application

The mass of Earth is approximately 6.0 × 1024 kilograms. The mass of Mars is approximately 6.4 × 1023 kilograms. About how many times more massive is Earth than Mars?

F) 0.9

G) 1.1

H) 9.4

J) 94

K) 940

Solution:

Step 1: Divide Earth's mass by Mars's mass: (6.0 × 1024) ÷ (6.4 × 1023)

Step 2: Divide coefficients: 6.0 ÷ 6.4 = 0.9375 ≈ 0.94

Step 3: Subtract exponents: 24 - 23 = 1

Step 4: Combine: 0.94 × 101 = 9.4

Answer: H — Earth is about 9.4 times more massive than Mars.

  • ✓Positive exponents represent large numbers; negative exponents represent small fractions

  • ✓For multiplication: multiply coefficients, add exponents
  • ✓For division: divide coefficients, subtract exponents
  • ✓For addition/subtraction: exponents must match before operating on coefficients
  • ✓Always adjust results so coefficient is between 1 and 10
  • ✓When comparing numbers, focus on exponents first, then coefficients
  • ✓For negative exponents, less negative means larger (10-2 > 10-5)
