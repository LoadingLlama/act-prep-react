Grammar review questions synthesize all major concepts tested on the ACT English Test—you must quickly identify error types and apply the appropriate fix. This comprehensive lesson provides a systematic framework for approaching any grammar question with confidence and speed.

1. The Complete Grammar Error Checklist

  • When you encounter an underlined portion, systematically check for the most common error types
    • This mental checklist helps identify problems quickly without wasting time
    • The ACT doesn't announce which grammar rule each question tests

The "Big 7" Grammar Categories

  • These seven categories account for approximately 70-80% of all grammar questions on the ACT:
    • 1. Punctuation (15-18 questions): Commas, semicolons, colons, dashes, apostrophes
    • 2. Sentence Structure (15-18 questions): Fragments, run-ons, comma splices, subordination
    • 3. Verbs (8-10 questions): Agreement, tense, form, voice
    • 4. Pronouns (5-7 questions): Agreement, case, clarity, reference
    • 5. Modifiers (4-6 questions): Misplaced, dangling, squinting modifiers
    • 6. Parallel Structure (3-5 questions): Lists, conjunctions, comparisons
    • 7. Word Choice & Idioms (3-5 questions): Commonly confused words, prepositional idioms

Quick Diagnostic Questions

  • Ask yourself these questions in order when you see an underlined portion:
    • → Is there punctuation? Check comma rules, semicolon/colon usage
    • → Is there a verb? Check subject-verb agreement, tense, form
    • → Is there a pronoun? Check agreement, case, clear antecedent
    • → Does the sentence begin with a phrase? Check for dangling modifiers
    • → Is there a list or conjunction? Check parallel structure
    • → Can the sentence stand alone? Check for fragments/run-ons

2. Systematic Error Recognition Strategies

  • Develop a consistent approach to every grammar question
    • This systematic method prevents you from missing errors
    • Helps you work efficiently under time pressure

Step 1: Read the Sentence Completely

  • Always read from the beginning of the sentence through the end
    • Context matters—you can't identify errors without understanding the full sentence
    • Many errors depend on elements outside the underlined portion
    • Subject-verb agreement requires finding the subject (often not underlined)

Step 2: Trust Your Ear, But Verify with Rules

  • If something sounds wrong, it probably is
    • However, some errors sound correct (like "everyone...their")
    • Verify with the rules you've learned
    • Ear + Rules = Accuracy

Step 3: Eliminate Obviously Wrong Answers

  • Use process of elimination before identifying the specific error
    • Creates a fragment or run-on
    • Uses wrong verb tense for the context
    • Creates pronoun agreement error
    • Breaks parallel structure in a list

3. Time Management and Pacing

  • The ACT English section gives you 45 minutes for 75 questions across 5 passages
    • That's 36 seconds per question or 9 minutes per passage
    • Effective time management is crucial for finishing strong

The 9-Minute Per Passage Strategy

  • Aim to complete each passage in approximately 9 minutes
    • Minutes 0-2: Read the passage naturally, answering inline grammar questions
    • Minutes 2-7: Work through remaining grammar and style questions systematically
    • Minutes 7-9: Handle rhetorical skills questions (organization, purpose, style)

Pacing Checkpoints

  • Check your progress at these intervals:
    • 15 minutes: Should be finishing passage 2 (question 30)
    • 30 minutes: Should be finishing passage 4 (question 60)
    • 40 minutes: Should be on passage 5 with 5 minutes to finish

4. Test Day Strategies

  • Beyond knowing grammar rules, test day success requires strategic approaches
    • These strategies help you navigate the test efficiently
    • Avoid common pitfalls that cost points

When "NO CHANGE" Is Correct

  • About 20-25% of questions have "NO CHANGE" as the correct answer
    • Don't avoid this choice if you can't find an error
    • Choose it when you've checked all grammar rules and found no violations
    • Choose it when other answer choices create new errors

Shorter Is Usually Better

  • When choosing between grammatically correct options, the ACT typically favors conciseness
    • If two answers are both correct, choose the shorter one
    • However: Never sacrifice clarity or correctness for brevity
    • Example: "because" instead of "due to the fact that"

Final Mental Checklist

  • Before moving to the next question, quickly verify:
    • ✓ Your answer creates no new errors
    • ✓ The sentence is complete and logical
    • ✓ All subjects have verbs and all verbs have subjects
    • ✓ Pronouns have clear antecedents
    • ✓ Modifiers are placed next to what they describe
