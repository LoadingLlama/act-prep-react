Redundancy, wordiness, and irrelevance questions appear frequently on the ACT English Test. For these questions, the shortest and simplest answer choice that expresses the same idea is almost always correct—if anything is redundant, wordy, or irrelevant, eliminate it.

1. Redundancy

  • Redundancy occurs when information is unnecessarily repeated
    • Read the sentence itself AND the sentence(s) before to check for repeated information
    • If two words or phrases mean the same thing, one must be removed
  • Common redundant phrases to watch for
    • ❌ "Returned back" → ✓ "Returned" (returning already means coming back)
    • ❌ "In the Swedish forests" when Sweden was already mentioned → ✓ "In the forests"
    • ❌ "Past history" → ✓ "History" (history is always past)
    • ❌ "Free gift" → ✓ "Gift" (gifts are free by definition)

Example: Context-Based Redundancy

  • ❌ Incorrect: "In Sweden, there are about 300,000 to 400,000 moose, many of which stand over seven feet tall, roaming freely in the Swedish forests."
  • ✓ Correct: "In Sweden, there are about 300,000 to 400,000 moose, many of which stand over 7 feet tall, roaming freely in the forests."
  • Why: The sentence already specifies the moose are in Sweden, so "Swedish" is redundant

2. Wordiness

  • Sentences can be unnecessarily wordy without being redundant
    • Pick the answer choice that uses the fewest words to express the same idea
    • Eliminate flowery or unnecessarily complex language
  • Common wordy phrases and their concise alternatives
    • ❌ "Due to the fact that" → ✓ "Because"
    • ❌ "At this point in time" → ✓ "Now"
    • ❌ "In the event that" → ✓ "If"
    • ❌ "A cultural institution supporting intellectual endeavors related to renaissance art" → ✓ "A museum devoted to renaissance art"

Example: Simplifying Wordy Phrases

  • ❌ Incorrect: "The ancient Italian sculptures were donated to a cultural institution supporting intellectual endeavors related to renaissance art."
  • ✓ Correct: "The ancient Italian sculptures were donated to a museum devoted to renaissance art."
  • Why: The simpler version conveys the exact same meaning with far fewer words

3. Irrelevance

  • Irrelevant information doesn't contribute to the passage's main idea or current paragraph
    • Irrelevant details most often appear as long, wordy answer choices
    • These answer choices are always incorrect
    • Ask: Does this information help tell the story or explain the concept?

Example: Removing Irrelevant Details

  • ❌ Incorrect: "To win the game, Ricky needed to make both free throws. Players are not allowed to step in the lane during a free throw attempt. He made the first one. The entire gym was quiet as he stepped to the line for the second."
  • ✓ Correct: "To win the game, Ricky needed to make both free throws. He made the first one. The entire gym was quiet as he stepped to the line for the second."
  • Why: The rule about stepping in the lane is irrelevant to Ricky's specific free throw situation

4. How to Spot These Questions

  • The ACT directly tells you when testing redundancy, wordiness, or irrelevance
    • Look for questions asking: "Which choice is the least redundant in context?"
    • Or: "Which choice best avoids wordiness and redundancy in context?"
    • When you see these questions, the shortest answer is almost always correct
  • Important warning: ONLY use "shortest is correct" for these specific questions
    • On grammar questions (not asking about redundancy), the shortest answer may be wrong
    • Example: An ambiguous pronoun like "it" or "them" might be shortest but incorrect
    • Always apply grammar rules first unless the question specifically asks about redundancy

Recognizing Ambiguous Pronouns

  • Even though pronouns are short, they're incorrect if ambiguous
    • Watch for: they, them, it, this, that, these, those, some, none
    • If you can't tell exactly what the pronoun refers to, it's incorrect
    • Example: "Freddie ordered some" is ambiguous—some what? Coral? Aquariums?
    • Correct: "Freddie ordered some coral colonies" clearly specifies what he ordered

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Redundancy occurs when information is unnecessarily repeated—check the current sentence and previous sentences for duplicate information. Common examples: "returned back" (just "returned"), "Swedish forests" when Sweden was already mentioned (just "the forests").
  • ✓Wordiness means using more words than necessary to express an idea. Pick the answer choice with the fewest words that conveys the same meaning: "because" instead of "due to the fact that," "museum" instead of "cultural institution supporting intellectual endeavors."
  • ✓Irrelevant information doesn't contribute to the passage's main idea or current paragraph—these details often appear as long, wordy answer choices and are always incorrect. Ask yourself: does this information help tell the story or explain the concept?
  • ✓The ACT directly asks "Which choice is the least redundant in context?" or "Which choice best avoids wordiness?" For THESE questions only, the shortest answer is almost always correct. However, on grammar questions, don't always pick the shortest—ambiguous pronouns (it, them, some) may be short but incorrect.
