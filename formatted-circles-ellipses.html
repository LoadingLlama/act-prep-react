Circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas are conic sections that appear regularly on the ACT. You can almost guarantee at least one circle question, and ellipses are commonly tested in questions 35-45. While hyperbolas appear less frequently, they have been showing up more in recent years. Understanding these equations and how to graph them will give you a significant advantage.

1. Circle Equations

Circles are one of the most commonly tested conic sections. To answer circle questions efficiently, you need to know the equation for a circle and how to identify its center and radius.

Circle with Center at Origin

x² + y² = r²
where r = radius, center at (0, 0)
  • Example: x² + y² = 16
    • Center at origin (0, 0)
    • Radius is 4 (not 16!) since r² = 16
    • Common mistake: forgetting that the number represents r²

Standard Form of a Circle

(x − h)² + (y − k)² = r²
where center is at (h, k) and radius is r

This is the most important circle equation to memorize. Note that the signs in the equation are opposite to the actual coordinates.

  • Example: (x − 3)² + (y + 5)² = 49
    • Center at (3, −5), NOT (−3, 5)
    • The expression (x − 3)² means x-coordinate is 3
    • The expression (y + 5)² means y-coordinate is −5
    • Radius is 7 since r² = 49

Example 1: Writing a Circle Equation

A circle in the (x, y) coordinate plane has a center (−9, 4) and a radius of 6. Which of the following is the equation of the circle?

A. (x − 9)² + (y + 4)² = 36
B. (x + 9)² + (y − 4)² = 36
C. (x + 9)² + (y + 4)² = 36
D. (x − 9)² + (y + 4)² = 6

Solution:

  • The radius is 6, so r² = 36 (eliminates choice D)
  • Center is at (−9, 4), so we need opposite signs: +9 and −4
  • The expression should be (x + 9)² + (y − 4)²

Answer: B

2. Advanced Circle Principles

For more difficult circle questions, the ACT tests deeper principles. These may seem simple but recognizing them is critical for solving advanced problems.

Principle 1: Diameter Midpoint

  • The two endpoints of any diameter have their midpoint at the center of the circle
    • If you know two endpoints of a diameter, use midpoint formula to find center
    • If you know the center and one endpoint, use midpoint formula to find the other endpoint

Principle 2: Radius as Distance

  • The distance between the center and any endpoint of a diameter equals the radius
    • Use the distance formula to find the radius
    • Remember: the equation uses r², not r

Example 2: Finding Circle Equation from Diameter

In the xy-plane, points (4, 2) and (6, 10) are the endpoints of a diameter on a circle. Which of the following is the equation of the circle?

A. (x − 1)² + (y − 4)² = 68
B. (x − 1)² + (y − 4)² = 17
C. (x − 5)² + (y − 6)² = 68
D. (x − 5)² + (y − 6)² = 17

Solution:

  • Find center using midpoint: ((4+6)/2, (2+10)/2) = (5, 6)
  • This eliminates choices A and B
  • Find radius using distance formula from center (5, 6) to endpoint (4, 2):
  • r = √[(5−4)² + (6−2)²] = √[1 + 16] = √17
  • Since r² = 17, the answer is D

Answer: D

3. Ellipses

Ellipses are commonly tested in questions 35-45. You need to know the equation of an ellipse and how to identify its key features from the equation.

Standard Equation at Origin

x²/a² + y²/b² = 1
where a = x-radius, b = y-radius, center at (0, 0)
  • Key features:
    • Always has a + sign in the middle
    • Always equals 1
    • a shows how far left/right the ellipse extends
    • b shows how far up/down the ellipse extends
    • The four vertices are at (±a, 0) and (0, ±b)
  • Example: x²/16 + y²/4 = 1
    • a = 4, b = 2 (take square roots!)
    • Vertices at (−4, 0), (4, 0), (0, −2), (0, 2)
    • Major axis length = 8, minor axis length = 4

Standard Form with Center (h, k)

(x − h)²/a² + (y − k)²/b² = 1
where center is at (h, k)

Just like circles, the signs in the equation are opposite to the actual coordinates of the center.

Example 3: Finding Ellipse Vertices

An ellipse in the standard (x, y) coordinate plane is described by the equation (x+4)²/4 + (y+3)²/16 = 1. What are the coordinates of the vertices on the major axis?

A. (−6, −3) and (−4, −7)
B. (−2, −3) and (−4, 1)
C. (−4, 1) and (−4, −7)
D. (−6, −3) and (−2, −3)

Solution:

  • Center is at (−4, −3) (opposite signs)
  • Since a² = 4, a = 2 (x-radius)
  • Since b² = 16, b = 4 (y-radius)
  • Major axis is vertical since b > a
  • Vertices are 4 units up and down: (−4, 1) and (−4, −7)

Answer: C

4. Hyperbolas

Hyperbolas appear less frequently but have been showing up more in recent years. You need to distinguish between horizontal and vertical hyperbolas and identify their vertices.

Standard Forms at Origin

Horizontal: x²/a² − y²/b² = 1
Vertical: y²/a² − x²/b² = 1
The positive term determines the direction
  • Key features:
    • Always has a − sign in the middle
    • Always equals 1
    • The positive term (x² or y²) tells you if it's horizontal or vertical
    • a shows the distance from center to vertices
    • For horizontal: vertices at (±a, 0)
    • For vertical: vertices at (0, ±a)

Standard Form with Center (h, k)

Horizontal: (x − h)²/a² − (y − k)²/b² = 1
Vertical: (y − k)²/a² − (x − h)²/b² = 1
where center is at (h, k)

Example 4: Finding Hyperbola Equation

A hyperbola has vertices (2, 4) and (2, 10) in the standard (x, y) coordinate plane. Which of the following could be the equation of the hyperbola?

A. (y − 4)²/9 − (x − 2)²/16 = 1
B. (y − 7)²/9 − (x − 2)²/16 = 1
C. (y − 7)²/16 − (x − 2)²/9 = 1
D. (x − 2)²/9 − (y − 7)²/16 = 1

Solution:

  • Vertices have same x-coordinate, so this is a vertical hyperbola (eliminates D)
  • Center is midpoint: ((2+2)/2, (4+10)/2) = (2, 7) (eliminates A)
  • Vertices are 3 units from center, so a = 3 and a² = 9
  • For vertical hyperbola, y-term is positive and has a² in denominator

Answer: B

5. ACT Strategy Tips

Tip 1: Watch the Signs

  • For circles and ellipses:
    • The signs in (x − h) and (y − k) are opposite to the actual center coordinates
    • (x + 3) means x = −3, not x = 3
    • This is the most common mistake students make

Tip 2: Remember It's r², Not r

  • Circle equations always have r² on the right side
    • If you see = 25, the radius is 5, not 25
    • For ellipses, remember to take square roots of denominators to find a and b

Tip 3: Use the Middle Sign

  • The sign between x² and y² tells you the conic section:
    • Plus sign (+) → circle or ellipse
    • Minus sign (−) → hyperbola
    • For hyperbolas, the positive term tells you the direction (horizontal or vertical)

Tip 4: Draw It Out

  • For complex problems:
    • Sketch a quick coordinate plane
    • Plot the center and any given points
    • Draw the rough shape
    • Visual representation helps eliminate wrong answers

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Circle equation: (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r² with center (h, k) and radius r
  • ✓Signs in equations are opposite to actual coordinates (x + 3 means x = −3)
  • ✓Ellipse equation: (x − h)²/a² + (y − k)²/b² = 1 with center (h, k)
  • ✓For ellipses, a is x-radius and b is y-radius; vertices are at center ± a or ± b
  • ✓Hyperbolas: plus sign between squared terms → circle/ellipse; minus sign → hyperbola
  • ✓For hyperbolas, the positive term determines if it's horizontal (x²) or vertical (y²)
