The Science Test consists of 40 questions that you will have 35 minutes to answer across 6 passages, each followed by 5 to 7 questions. Despite its name, this section requires very little actual science knowledge—a more accurate name would be "The Analyzing Charts and Graphs on Random Topics Quickly Test," as the focus is on quickly analyzing charts and graphs.

1. What You Really Need to Know About Science Class

  • To do well on the Science Test, you do not need to be good at science class
    • If you love science class, it can certainly help you, but it is far from a requirement to get a good score
    • For those of you who do not love science class, do not worry—you can still get a great score with practice
    • The test is about data interpretation skills, not memorizing scientific facts

2. The Two Types of Passages

Passage Type Count Description
Charts and Graphs 5 passages Test your ability to read charts and graphs and interpret information given in tables, diagrams, and figures. For certain passages, you'll also be asked questions about the experimental design. This is the most common type of passage on the ACT.
Conflicting Viewpoints 1 passage Asks you to understand, analyze, and compare different hypotheses, opinions, or conclusions. This passage has much more text and sometimes has no charts or graphs at all. The headings in the passage say "Student 1, Student 2" or "Scientist 1, Scientist 2," so conflicting viewpoint passages are easy to spot.

3. The Six Types of Questions

  • Detail Questions: Ask you to locate a specific piece of data
    • To find the data point, look directly at the graphs, charts, tables, or diagrams
    • On more difficult questions, you may need to use multiple charts, graphs, tables, or figures at once to find the correct answer
    • Be sure to pay attention to the axes to make sure you're finding the right detail
  • Pattern Questions: Ask you to spot a trend on a graph, table, or chart
    • Look for increases, decreases, or other patterns in the data
    • Use the pattern to identify where a data point would be
  • Inference Questions: Ask you to make a conclusion based on the data provided
    • Most commonly, you'll need to use the data provided to draw a conclusion about a data point or situation that is not directly shown in the charts and graphs
  • Method Questions: Test your understanding of experimental setup and scientific methods
    • Ask you to "think like a scientist"
    • You may need to think about how to gather data, how to set up a control, or how experiments can be improved
  • Scientific Knowledge Questions: Require you to use some outside scientific knowledge
    • The outside science knowledge most commonly comes from biology, chemistry, or physics
    • Usually general knowledge students who have completed those classes are familiar with
    • These are the least common type of question on the test and do not appear on every ACT
  • Compare/Contrast Questions: Only appear in the conflicting viewpoints passage
    • Ask you to consider various aspects of opposing or alternative arguments

4. The Learning Curve and How to Succeed

  • The most difficult part of the Science Test is that you've never worked on this in school—there's a learning curve
    • You've taken math courses, learned grammar rules, and done reading comprehension
    • But you've probably never met anyone who has taken a "reading charts and graphs" class
    • The Science Test feels very uncomfortable to many students at first

The Best Way to Conquer the ACT Science Test

  • Three key steps to success:
    • Understand the common types of questions
    • Learn the proper approaches and strategies
    • Work through a bunch of practice ACTs
    • With practice, you will begin to feel much more comfortable and confident on this section

Hidden Separator
