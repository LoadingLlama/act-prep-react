Understanding Sentence Structure

To conquer the ACT English Test, you must be able to identify the different parts of a sentence and know the rules for how they can and cannot be combined. Understanding clauses, phrases, and sentence structure rules is fundamental to mastering ACT English grammar questions.

1. Clauses and Phrases

Clause: A group of words that contains both a subject and a verb. There are two types of clauses: independent clauses and dependent clauses.

Independent Clauses

An independent clause can stand as a sentence by itself. It always has a subject and a verb. The best way to identify an independent clause is to read the sentence and see if you can stop talking at the end. If you can stop, it is a complete sentence and an independent clause.

  • The dog chased its tail.
  • Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast.
  • He picked it up.
  • The excited child opened up his present.

Dependent Clauses

A dependent clause cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. Most often, clauses become dependent by adding a subordinating conjunction to the front of the clause.

Common subordinating conjunctions:

  • Time-related:
    • after, before, when, whenever, while, until, once, since
  • Cause/condition:
    • because, if, unless, in order to
  • Contrast:
    • although, even though, though, whether, while
  • Other:
    • as, that, where, whatever

Notice how these words turn independent clauses into dependent clauses:

  • While the dog chased its tail...
  • Although Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast...
  • Since he picked it up...
  • When the excited child opened up his present...

Phrases

A phrase lacks a subject or verb or both. A phrase does not express a complete thought and can never stand alone as a sentence. As a result, phrases provide additional descriptive information in a sentence.

  • Chasing its tail... (missing subject - who is chasing?)
  • Making some homemade peach scones for breakfast... (missing subject)
  • Picking it up... (missing subject)
  • Excited to open up his present... (missing verb and subject)

TIP: Independent Clauses vs. Dependent Clauses vs. Phrases

To tell the difference, read the sentence out loud (or "out loud" in your head):

  • If you can stop talking at the end → it is an independent clause
  • If you feel like you need to keep talking → it is a dependent clause or phrase

To tell the difference between a dependent clause and a phrase:

  • Cover the first word and read the rest
  • If you have a complete sentence → dependent clause
  • If you do NOT have a complete sentence → phrase

Example comparison:

  • Independent Clause: My brother Adam eats cookies every day.
  • Dependent Clause: When my brother Adam eats cookies every day...
  • Phrase: Eating cookies every day...

2. Sentence Fragments

A complete sentence must contain a subject and a verb. Sentence fragments are missing a subject or a verb or both and do not express a complete thought. In other words, a sentence fragment cannot stand by itself.

To spot sentence fragments, read the entire sentence. The sentence will usually just sound plain wrong, and you will realize it is missing a subject or a verb.

  • Incorrect: The student running to get to class on time.
    • Missing a verb - what did the student do?
  • Correct: The student running to get to class on time dropped her water bottle.
  • Incorrect: Excited to go to the beach and surf.
    • Missing a subject - who is excited to go to the beach?
  • Correct: My little brother Shaun is excited to go to the beach and surf.

3. Five Types of Compound Sentences

Often on the ACT, you will face more complex sentences that have multiple clauses (more than one subject and verb). These are called compound sentences. On the ACT (and in English in general), multiple clauses must always appear as one of the 5 types below.

TIP: FANBOYS - Memorize These 7 Coordinating Conjunctions!

  • For
  • And
  • Nor
  • But
  • Or
  • Yet
  • So

Type 1: Two Separate Independent Clauses with Periods

Two complete sentences separated by a period.

  • Example: Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats.
    • Independent clause + period + Independent clause

Type 2: Comma + FANBOYS Joining 2 Independent Clauses

Use a comma followed by a FANBOYS word (coordinating conjunction) to join two independent clauses.

  • Example: Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats.
    • Independent clause + comma + FANBOYS + Independent clause

Type 3: Semicolon Between 2 Independent Clauses

A semicolon functions the same as a period on the ACT and joins two independent clauses.

  • Example: Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats.
    • Independent clause + semicolon + Independent clause
    • Note: A semicolon is the same as a period on the ACT

Type 4: Dependent Clause Followed by Independent Clause (Comma)

When a dependent clause comes BEFORE an independent clause, use a comma to separate them.

  • Example: While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats.
    • Dependent clause + comma + Independent clause

Type 5: Independent Clause Straight Into Dependent Clause (No Comma)

When an independent clause comes BEFORE a dependent clause, do NOT use a comma.

  • Example: Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats.
    • Independent clause + no comma + Dependent clause

⚠️ CRITICAL: You MUST memorize these 5 rules!

The ACT will challenge you by putting answer choices that "look" or "sound" good on the exam but actually break one of these rules. The most common mistake is the comma splice.

4. Comma Splices

A comma by itself CANNOT be used to join two independent clauses. Joining two independent sentences with only a comma is called a comma splice and is always incorrect.

Examples of comma splices (INCORRECT):

  • It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.
  • I cannot believe you did not get the flowers, I left them at your front door.

How to fix comma splices using the 5 compound sentence rules:

  • Rule #1 (Period): It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red. They are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #2 (Comma + FANBOYS): It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, but they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #3 (Semicolon): It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red; they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #4 (Dependent → Independent): While it is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #5 (Independent → Dependent): It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red though they are actually colorblind.

5. How to Spot Sentence Structure Questions

Sentence structure questions usually have at least one of the following characteristics:

  • Punctuation in answer choices: Periods, semicolons, commas, and FANBOYS appear in the answer choices
    • If you see answer choices with any of these, you are most likely looking at a sentence structure question
  • Subjects or verbs vary: Some answer choices have a subject or verb and others do not
    • For example, one answer choice may say "drove" while another says "driving"
    • For these questions, be on the lookout for comma splices

Three-step approach to sentence structure questions:

  1. Find where the sentence is being "split" (the punctuation point)
  2. Look left and right of the "split" point and identify the clauses or phrases
  3. Apply the sentence structure rules to determine which answer is correct

Example Analysis:

The roller coaster is opening next week, the wait time is expected to be over 4 hours long.

Step 1: Find the split → the comma after "week"

Step 2: Identify clauses:

  • Left: "The roller coaster is opening next week" (independent clause)
  • Right: "the wait time is expected to be over 4 hours long" (independent clause)

Step 3: Apply rules → This is a comma splice! Need period, semicolon, or comma + FANBOYS

Correct answer: "week. The" or "week; with the" or "week and the"

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Independent clauses can stand alone as complete sentences, while dependent clauses (starting with subordinating conjunctions) and phrases cannot. Test by reading aloud—if you can stop talking at the end, it's an independent clause.
  • ✓ There are exactly 5 ways to combine clauses correctly: (1) period between independent clauses, (2) comma + FANBOYS between independent clauses, (3) semicolon between independent clauses, (4) dependent clause + comma + independent clause, (5) independent clause + no comma + dependent clause.
  • ✓ A comma by itself CANNOT join two independent clauses—this creates a comma splice, which is always incorrect. Fix comma splices by using one of the 5 compound sentence rules.
  • ✓ To solve sentence structure questions: (1) find the split point, (2) identify whether each side is independent, dependent, or a phrase, (3) apply the appropriate rule. Look for punctuation variations in answer choices as a clue that the question tests sentence structure.
