Adding or deleting information questions ask whether specific information should be included in a passage or not. For these questions, you must read for context—we recommend reading the entire paragraph where the question appears to determine if the information fits.

1. How to Approach These Questions

  • Read the paragraph twice: once with the information included and once with it deleted
    • This makes it clear whether the information should be included
    • Consider: Does the information fit the paragraph's focus?
    • Depending on the question, you may need to read more than one paragraph
  • Context is critical
    • You must understand what the paragraph is discussing
    • The information should support or relate to the main idea

2. Adding Information Questions

  • These questions give you a sentence or phrase and ask if it should be added
    • Answer choices have two parts: (1) Yes or No, (2) Justification why
    • First decide yes or no, then pick the correct justification

Critical Strategy: Only Consider What the Sentence Says

  • When evaluating justifications, only consider the sentence being added
    • Many students pick justifications that go far beyond what the sentence says
    • Don't include details that have nothing to do with the sentence itself
    • The justification must accurately describe what that specific sentence provides

Example Structure

  • Question: "Should the writer add this sentence about Dr. Anderson's education?"
    • A. Yes, because it establishes Dr. Paul's mentor's qualifications
    • B. Yes, because it indicates Dr. Anderson had a top-notch education
    • C. No, because it does not indicate where Dr. Paul studied
    • D. No, because it is not relevant to the passage's discussion of Dr. Paul's career
  • If the passage is about Dr. Paul's career, not his mentor's, the answer is D

3. Deleting Information Questions

  • These ask if existing information should be removed
    • Same format: Yes/No + Justification
    • Consider whether the information is relevant and necessary

When to Delete Information

  • Delete when the information:
    • Is irrelevant to the paragraph's main idea
    • Repeats information already stated
    • Distracts from the paragraph's focus
    • Doesn't add anything meaningful
  • Keep when the information:
    • Supports the main idea
    • Provides necessary detail or clarification
    • Connects ideas within the paragraph

4. Common Answer Choice Patterns

  • Good justifications are specific and accurate
    • They describe exactly what the sentence provides
    • They relate to the paragraph's focus
    • Example: "provides specific detail about the location" (if the sentence actually does that)
  • Bad justifications are vague or go too far
    • They claim the sentence says more than it actually does
    • They focus on irrelevant details
    • Example: "proves the scientist was the best in his field" (when sentence only mentions one achievement)

Common Wrong Answer Traps

  • Watch out for justifications that:
    • Reference information NOT in the sentence being added/deleted
    • Make logical leaps beyond what the sentence states
    • Focus on the wrong person, topic, or idea
    • Use absolute language ("proves," "always," "never") when the sentence is less definitive

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Read the paragraph twice—once with the information included and once with it deleted. This approach makes it clear whether the information fits the paragraph's focus and supports the main idea. Context is critical, so understand what the paragraph is discussing before deciding.
  • ✓Answer choices have two parts: (1) Yes or No, (2) Justification. First decide yes or no, then pick the correct justification. The justification must accurately describe what the specific sentence provides—don't pick options that go beyond what the sentence actually says.
  • ✓Delete information when it's irrelevant to the main idea, repeats what's already stated, or distracts from the paragraph's focus. Keep information when it supports the main idea, provides necessary detail, or connects ideas within the paragraph.
  • ✓Avoid justifications that reference information NOT in the sentence, make logical leaps, focus on the wrong topic, or use absolute language when the sentence is less definitive. Good justifications are specific, accurate, and describe exactly what the sentence provides.
