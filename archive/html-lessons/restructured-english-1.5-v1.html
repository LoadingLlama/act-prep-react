Pronoun questions make up about 9% of the ACT English Test—roughly 7 questions per test! You'll need to know how to select the proper pronoun based on case ("he" vs. "him" or "who" vs. "whom"), make sure that a pronoun agrees with the word it's replacing, and spot ambiguous pronouns.

1. Pronoun Case: Subject vs. Object

  • Pronouns are words that stand in for a person, place, or thing
    • Pronouns have two cases: subject and object
    • The pronoun's role in the sentence (subject or object) dictates which type is correct

Subject Pronouns vs. Object Pronouns

  • Subject pronouns (I, we, you, he, she, it, they, who) are used when the pronoun is doing the action
    • Example: "They went to the street festival on Saturday afternoon."
  • Object pronouns (me, us, you, him, her, it, them, whom) are used when the pronoun is receiving the action
    • Example: "The man handed us a stack of flyers."
  • For simple pronoun case questions, trust your ear

Pronouns in a List

  • Pronouns in lists are tricky because we often say them incorrectly in conversation
    • Ignore the other items in the list and just read the pronoun by itself to tell which is correct
    • Example: "The policeman asked to speak to my friends and me."
    • Test: "The policeman asked to speak to me." ✓ (not "speak to I")
    • Example: "His girlfriend and he went to the hockey game."
    • Test: "He went to the hockey game." ✓ (not "him went")

Pronouns in Prepositional Phrases

  • Pronouns in prepositional phrases are ALWAYS in the object case
    • This rule can be difficult to hear by ear alone, so remember it
    • Example: "The bet between Joe and me was for just five dollars." (not "between Joe and I")
    • Example: "To whom was the award given?" (not "to who")
    • Example: "Jerry claims that a great rivalry exists among Tim, Scott, and him." (not "among...and he")

Who vs. Whom

  • The difference between "who" and "whom" is tested on almost every ACT
    • Technically, "who" is the subject and "whom" is the object
    • But here's the trick you need to memorize:
  • The he/him replacement trick
    • If you can replace the word with "he," "she," or "they" → use "who"
    • If you can replace the word with "him," "her," or "them" → use "whom"
    • You may need to rearrange the sentence, but this trick works for any who vs. whom question
  • Examples
    • "Who stole the apple off the teacher's desk?" → Test: "He stole the apple" ✓ → Use "who"
    • "To whom should I address this letter?" → Test: "I should address this letter to him" ✓ → Use "whom"
    • "Who do you want to win the Bachelor?" → Test: "You want him to win" ✓ → Actually "whom" is correct here!

Who vs. Whom in Descriptive Phrases

  • On difficult questions, who/whom can introduce a phrase describing a person
    • The normal "he/him" trick doesn't work if you look at the entire sentence
    • Instead, look only at the phrase itself
  • Example: "The child whom I invited to the party was very noisy."
    • Look only at the phrase: "whom I invited to the party"
    • Test: "I invited him to the party" ✓ → Use "whom"
  • Example: "The child who was running down the street was very noisy."
    • Look only at the phrase: "who was running down the street"
    • Test: "He was running down the street" ✓ → Use "who"
  • Quick trick: Look at the word right after "who" or "whom"
    • If the word right after is a verb → use "who"
    • If the word right after is a noun → use "whom"

Reflexive Pronouns (myself, yourself, himself...)

  • Reflexive pronouns use the "-self" ending and can only be used when the pronoun refers back to a person or thing elsewhere in the sentence
    • ✓ Correct: "I grabbed three donuts and put them in a box for myself." (refers back to "I")
    • ❌ Incorrect: "I asked my coworker to put three donuts in a box for myself." ("myself" doesn't work because the subject is "my coworker," not "I")
    • ✓ Correct: "I asked my coworker to put three donuts in a box for me."

2. Possessive Pronouns

  • Possessive pronouns show ownership
    • These pronouns must match with the word(s) they're replacing
    • They come in two forms: possessive nouns and possessive adjectives

Possessive Nouns vs. Possessive Adjectives

  • Possessive nouns stand alone: mine, ours, yours, his, hers, its, theirs
    • Example: "That book is mine." (stands alone, not followed by a noun)
  • Possessive adjectives come before a noun: my, our, your, his, her, its, their
    • Example: "That is my book." (comes before the noun "book")

Matching Possessive Pronouns to the Antecedent

  • Possessive pronouns must match the word they're replacing in number (singular vs. plural)
    • ❌ Incorrect: "The entire team went on stage to pick up their trophy."
    • The pronoun refers back to "team," which is singular (remember: group nouns are singular!)
    • ✓ Correct: "The entire team went on stage to pick up its trophy."
  • More examples
    • "Emmanuel and James came back home after forgetting their phones." (plural subjects → "their")
    • "Thousands of tourists travel to Egypt each year to see its ancient pyramids." (Egypt is singular → "its")

3. Pronoun Agreement

  • Pronouns must agree with the antecedent, which is the noun the pronoun is replacing
    • Whenever you see a pronoun on the ACT, find exactly who or what it's referring to
    • The antecedent can be in the same sentence or in a preceding sentence
    • The antecedent can appear anywhere (unnecessary information, prepositional phrases, etc.)

Matching in Plurality

  • A singular antecedent must have a singular pronoun; a plural antecedent must have a plural pronoun
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Each attendee at the party raised their drink for a toast."
    • "Each attendee" is singular → pronoun must be singular
    • ✓ Correct: "Each attendee at the party raised his or her drink for a toast."
    • ✓ Also correct: "The guests at the party raised their drinks for a toast." (plural → plural)

Matching in Gender

  • A male antecedent must have a male pronoun; a female antecedent must have a female pronoun
    • Example: "Jane and Sarah, after a long conversation with another group of hikers on the trail, said they were too tired to go any farther today."
    • "Jane and Sarah" (female, plural) → "they" (plural, works for any gender)

Common Tricky Cases

  • Watch out for singular words that seem plural
    • "Each member of the women's track team improved her time." ("each member" is singular → "her")
    • "A person who is committed to his or her dreams can achieve them." ("a person" is singular → "his or her")
  • Consistency with pronoun style
    • "If you plan to give a presentation on the importance of accurate data, you should check your facts first."
    • Keep the same pronoun style throughout: "you...you...your" (not "you...one...one's")

4. Ambiguous Pronouns

  • For a pronoun to be correct, we must know exactly who or what it's referring to
    • If we don't know exactly who or what a pronoun is referring to, the pronoun is ambiguous and is always incorrect

Examples of Ambiguous Pronouns

  • Ambiguous "he"
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Stephen smiled at his father as he threw the ball."
    • We don't know if "he" refers to Stephen or his father, so "he" is ambiguous
    • ✓ Correct: "Stephen smiled at his father as his father threw the ball."
    • Even though it seems redundant, replacing "he" with "his father" fixes the error
  • Ambiguous "some"
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Even though the order had not yet shown up, the librarian insisted that she had ordered some."
    • We have no idea what "some" refers to
    • ✓ Correct: "Even though the order had not yet shown up, the librarian insisted that she had ordered four copies of the new book."

Spotting Ambiguous Pronouns Questions

  • Anytime you see a pronoun in the answer choices, check if it's ambiguous
    • Pronouns to watch for: he, him, his, she, her, hers, they, them, their, it, its, this, that, these, those, some, none
  • Look for answer choices that replace the pronoun with a specific noun
    • The correct answer often specifies exactly what or who is being referred to
    • Example: Instead of "he is the most famous," the answer might be "Biggie is the most famous"
    • Example: Instead of "asked for one," the answer might be "asked for a slider"

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Use subject pronouns (I, we, he, she, they, who) when the pronoun does the action, and object pronouns (me, us, him, her, them, whom) when it receives the action. For pronouns in lists, ignore the other items and read the pronoun alone. Pronouns in prepositional phrases are always object case. For "who" vs. "whom," replace with "he/him"—if "he" works, use "who"; if "him" works, use "whom."
  • ✓Possessive pronouns must match the word they're replacing in number. Remember that group nouns (team, committee, class) are singular and take singular possessive pronouns like "its," not "their."
  • ✓Pronouns must agree with their antecedent (the noun they're replacing) in both plurality and gender. A singular antecedent needs a singular pronoun; a plural antecedent needs a plural pronoun. Watch out for singular words like "each," "every," and "a person" that require singular pronouns.
  • ✓Ambiguous pronouns are always incorrect. If you can't tell exactly who or what a pronoun refers to, it's ambiguous. The correct answer will replace the ambiguous pronoun with a specific noun. Watch for pronouns like he, she, they, it, this, that, some, and one in answer choices—they're often ambiguous and need to be replaced with specific nouns.
