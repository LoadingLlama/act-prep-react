Verb questions make up about 11% of the ACT English Test—roughly 8 questions per test! These questions test you on two major topics: subject-verb agreement and verb tense.

1. Subject-Verb Agreement Basics

  • The subject is the person, place, or thing doing the action
    • Usually at the beginning of the sentence
    • Example: "Cats love to sit in empty boxes." (subject = "Cats")
  • The subject must agree with the verb in number
    • If the subject is singular, the verb must be singular
    • If the subject is plural, the verb must be plural
    • Example: "My dog barks at the mailman every day." (singular subject "dog" → singular verb "barks")

Three Ways the ACT Tricks You

  • Trick #1: Unnecessary information phrases between subject and verb
    • Unnecessary information phrases NEVER contain the subject
    • Cross out unnecessary information to find the subject
    • Example: "Tortoises, known to live to over 150 years old, are sold at the local pet shop."
    • Cross out "known to live to over 150 years old" → Subject is "Tortoises" (plural) → verb is "are" (plural)
  • Trick #2: Prepositional phrases between subject and verb (MOST COMMON)
    • Prepositional phrases NEVER contain the subject or verb
    • Cross out prepositional phrases (like "of students," "in the park," "on the table") to find the subject
    • Example: "The entire group of students is organizing a bake sale to raise money."
    • Cross out "of students" → Subject is "group" (singular) → verb is "is" (singular)
    • Example: "The pod of dolphins hunts as a team."
    • Cross out "of dolphins" → Subject is "pod" (singular) → verb is "hunts" (singular)
  • Trick #3: Group nouns that sound plural but are singular
    • Group nouns (class, committee, team, herd, jury, etc.) are SINGULAR even though the group consists of more than one person or thing
    • Example: "The sheep herd waits to be sheared." (herd = singular)
    • Example: "The jury agrees that the defendant deserves to be put in jail." (jury = singular)

Helpful Tricks

  • Read the subject and verb side-by-side
    • Once you find the subject and verb, read them next to each other and ignore the information in between
    • Example: "A large percentage of the voters, many of whom are under the age of 30, prefers the new housing proposal."
    • Cross out unnecessary phrases → "A percentage prefers the new housing proposal."
    • Now it's clear: "percentage" (singular) → "prefers" (singular)
  • Plug in "he/she/it" for singular subjects and "they" for plural subjects
    • Sometimes it's difficult to "hear" which verb matches the subject
    • For singular words, plug in "he," "she," or "it"
    • For plural words, plug in "they"
    • Example: "The exact time for the meeting with the softball coach and location for the showcase have not been finalized."
    • Cross out prepositional phrases → Subjects are "time" and "location" (plural)
    • Replace with "they" → "They (has/have) not been finalized."
    • Now it's obvious: "have" is correct

2. Verb Tense

  • For verb tense questions, trust your ear
    • As a native English speaker, you'll know what sounds right or wrong
    • Read for context by looking at the previous sentence(s) and finding other verbs
    • Match the tense in the rest of the paragraph
  • Do NOT pick a complicated tense when a simple tense works
    • Avoid unnecessary complicated forms (has run, had ran, had been running, would have run) when simple tenses work
    • Example: "Last year, Margot designed a new line of summer dresses." (simple past tense, not "was designing")
    • Example: "Before he votes tomorrow, Dale will research each candidate's positions on the major issues." (simple future tense)

Perfect Tense (has/have/had + verb)

  • Use perfect tense when there are multiple timelines in a sentence
    • Perfect tense = "has," "have," or "had" + verb
    • Example: "Many surfers enjoy paddling out, but few have ridden waves over ten feet tall."
    • Example: "While the group of entrepreneurs claimed to all be successful, only a few have started their own businesses."
    • Example: "The bison had been eating when the lions began their attack." (shows the bison were already eating before the attack started)
    • Example: "By the beginning of next year, the doctors will have finished their preliminary research on lung cancer."

Conditional Tense (would/could)

  • Conditional tense describes what would or could happen
    • The verb conjugation depends on how likely the outcome is (probable, improbable, impossible)
    • Probable: "If I make the free throw, we will win the game."
    • Improbable: "If I made the free throw, we would win the game."
    • Impossible (past): "If I had made the free throw, we would have won the game."
    • Trust your ear and go with what sounds right

Irregular Verbs

  • Irregular verbs don't follow normal patterns
    • Trust your ear and go with what sounds correct
    • Example: "Before the show began, the lights were dimmed." (not "begun")
    • Example: "The deliveryman had rung the doorbell before dropping off the package." (not "rang")
  • The "a" vs. "u" rule for irregular verbs
    • Many irregular verbs have an "a" version (swam, began, drank) and a "u" version (swum, begun, drunk)
    • With "has," "have," or "had" (perfect tense) → Use the "u" version
    • Example: "Before you arrived, I had swum some laps." (perfect tense → "swum")
    • With no "has," "have," or "had" (past tense) → Use the "a" version
    • Example: "I swam some laps this morning." (past tense → "swam")

3. Common Irregular Verbs

  • The most commonly tested irregular verbs on the ACT
    • begin → began (past tense) → has/have/had begun (perfect tense)
    • drink → drank (past tense) → has/have/had drunk (perfect tense)
    • sink → sank (past tense) → has/have/had sunk (perfect tense)
    • swim → swam (past tense) → has/have/had swum (perfect tense)
    • run → ran (past tense) → has/have/had run (perfect tense)
    • sing → sang (past tense) → has/have/had sung (perfect tense)
  • Remember the pattern
    • Past tense (no helping verb) = "a" version
    • Perfect tense (with has/have/had) = "u" version
    • This pattern applies to begin, drink, sink, swim, and sing
    • Note: "run" is irregular—both versions are "run" (I ran, I have run)

4. How to Spot Subject-Verb Agreement vs. Verb Tense Questions

  • Both question types show different forms of the same verb in answer choices
    • You need to know which type you're dealing with to answer correctly
    • The answer choices tell you what's being tested

Subject-Verb Agreement Questions

  • Look for singular and plural versions of verbs in the answer choices
    • Look for "is" vs. "are"
    • Look for "has" vs. "have"
    • Look for "was" vs. "were"
    • Look for any verb with singular (like "runs") vs. plural (like "run")
    • Anytime you see singular and plural versions, the question is testing subject-verb agreement
  • Example
    • "The addition of solar panels to the roof of a house in Nevada makes the house on average 20% more energy efficient."
    • Answer choices: make (NO CHANGE), makes, have made, were making
    • Subject = "addition" (singular) → verb must be "makes" (singular)
    • Even though there are different tenses, we only need subject-verb agreement to find the answer

Verb Tense Questions

  • Look for different tenses in the answer choices
    • For example: "requires" (present), "required" (past), "will require" (future)
  • Check that answer choices do NOT have singular and plural versions
    • If there are no options for singular vs. plural, subject-verb agreement is not being tested
    • The question is testing verb tense only
  • Example
    • "The girl scouts are hosting a bake sale tomorrow to raise money. To get ready, Adya needs to bake two trays of blondies and a box of donut holes."
    • Answer choices: would need (NO CHANGE), was needing, needed, needs
    • Previous sentence uses "are" (present tense), so this sentence should be present tense too
    • Answer: "needs" (present tense)

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓For subject-verb agreement, find the subject by crossing out unnecessary information phrases and prepositional phrases (which never contain the subject). The subject must match the verb in number—singular subjects take singular verbs, plural subjects take plural verbs. Watch out for group nouns (team, committee, herd) which are always singular.
  • ✓Use the "plug-in" trick for subject-verb agreement: replace singular subjects with "he/she/it" and plural subjects with "they" to easily hear which verb sounds correct. Read the subject and verb side-by-side, ignoring everything in between.
  • ✓For verb tense questions, trust your ear and read for context by looking at other verbs in the paragraph. Use perfect tense (has/have/had + verb) for multiple timelines, conditional tense (would/could) for hypothetical situations, and remember the "a" vs. "u" rule for irregular verbs: use "a" versions (swam, began, drank) for past tense without helping verbs, and "u" versions (swum, begun, drunk) with has/have/had.
  • ✓Identify question type by looking at answer choices: if you see singular vs. plural versions ("is" vs. "are," "has" vs. "have," "runs" vs. "run"), it's testing subject-verb agreement. If you see different tenses without singular/plural options, it's testing verb tense. This tells you which strategy to use.
