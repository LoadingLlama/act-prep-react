Punctuation questions account for about 13% of the ACT English Test—roughly 10 questions per test! To master these questions, you need to understand the specific rules for semicolons, colons, dashes, apostrophes, and quotation marks.

1. Semicolons

  • The most important rule: A semicolon can replace a period
    • Both sides of the semicolon must be independent clauses (complete sentences)
    • If you can replace the semicolon with a period and both halves make sense as separate sentences, the semicolon is correct
    • Example: "My boss called me; he asked that I pick up coffee for him."
  • Common semicolon errors to avoid
    • ❌ Incorrect: "My boss called me; asking that I pick up coffee for him." (second half is not an independent clause)
    • ❌ Incorrect: "After my boss called me; he asked that I pick up coffee for him." (first half is not an independent clause)
    • ❌ Incorrect: "My boss called me, he asked that I pick up coffee for him." (this is a comma splice—cannot connect two independent clauses with just a comma)
  • The test: Replace the semicolon with a period
    • If both halves stand alone as complete sentences, the semicolon is correct
    • If either half cannot stand alone, the semicolon is wrong

2. Colons

  • A colon introduces a list, an example, an explanation, a clarification, or a definition
    • List of multiple items: "I went to the grocery store to pick up some items for dinner: chicken, cheese, and onions."
    • List of one item: "I went to the market to get lunch: a turkey sandwich."
    • Example or explanation: "He got what he deserved: a one-week suspension without pay."
    • Clarification: "Chocolate milk is a great recovery drink after a hard workout: it has the carbohydrates and proteins that muscles need to recover."

Three Critical Colon Rules

  • Rule 1: The part before the colon must be an independent clause
    • The part before the colon must be a complete sentence by itself
    • ❌ Incorrect: "The slide showing the most popular car colors in 2018: white, gray, and black."
    • ✓ Correct: "The slide shows the most popular car colors in 2018: white, gray, and black."
    • ❌ Incorrect: "The hairstylist excelled in: cuts, coloring, and highlights."
    • ✓ Correct: "The hairstylist excelled in cuts, coloring, and highlights." (no colon needed)
  • Rule 2: Everything after the colon must be only the list, example, explanation, clarification, or definition
    • The sentence cannot continue to other topics after the colon
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Will brought his lunch to the beach: a roast beef sandwich, and he brought his friend Joey a turkey club."
    • ✓ Correct: "Will brought his lunch to the beach: a roast beef sandwich. He brought his friend Joey a turkey club."
  • Rule 3: NEVER use colons with "including," "such as," or "for example"
    • If you see a colon before OR after these phrases, it is always incorrect
    • ❌ Incorrect: "I went to the grocery store to pick up some items for dinner, including: chicken, cheese, and onions."
    • ❌ Incorrect: "I went to the grocery store to pick up some items for dinner: including chicken, cheese, and onions."
    • ✓ Correct: "I went to the grocery store to pick up some items for dinner, including chicken, cheese, and onions." (use a comma instead)
    • ❌ Incorrect: "The study relied on techniques such as: direct observation and surveying."
    • ✓ Correct: "The study relied on techniques such as direct observation and surveying." (no comma or colon needed)

Advanced: Colons Joining Two Independent Clauses

  • A colon can join two independent clauses if the second clause acts as a definition, example, explanation, or clarification of the first
    • Example: "Buddy got what he worked for: he really deserved that promotion."
    • Example: "My father gave me one rule to live by: honesty is always the best policy."
    • This is an advanced grammar rule that rarely appears on the ACT

3. Dashes

  • Dashes can function like unnecessary information commas, parentheses, or colons
    • Most commonly, a pair of dashes sets apart unnecessary information in the middle of a sentence
    • Think of dashes as a stronger, more dramatic version of commas or parentheses

Pair of Dashes for Unnecessary Information

  • Two dashes function exactly like unnecessary information commas
    • Example: "Residents of Washington D.C. - the capital of the United States - are still trying to get representation in Congress."
    • Example: "Glazed donuts - even if they are unhealthy - are my favorite."
  • CRITICAL: You cannot mix punctuation!
    • Must be a pair of commas, a pair of dashes, OR a pair of parentheses
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Deep-dish pizza - a famous meal in Chicago, does not exist in Italy." (mixing dash and comma)
    • ✓ Correct: "Deep-dish pizza, a famous meal in Chicago, does not exist in Italy." (pair of commas)
    • ✓ Correct: "Deep-dish pizza - a famous meal in Chicago - does not exist in Italy." (pair of dashes)
    • ✓ Correct: "Deep-dish pizza (a famous meal in Chicago) does not exist in Italy." (pair of parentheses)

Single Dash Acting Like a Colon

  • A single dash can introduce a list, example, explanation, definition, or clarification
    • When acting as a colon, the dash must follow the same 3 colon rules
    • Example: "You will need the following ingredients - milk, butter, flour, and eggs."
    • Example: "In order to establish his dominance, the male lion relied on one thing - his thunderous roar."
    • Example: "The study declared that the results were conclusive - mice will complete the maze faster if they are given soda instead of water."

Advanced: Single Dash Like Parentheses

  • A single dash in the middle of a sentence can separate information at the end just like parentheses
    • Example: "After three weeks, the patient was fed up with his doctor's plan (or lack of a plan)."
    • Example: "After three weeks, the patient was fed up with his doctor's plan - or lack of a plan."
    • This rule has been very rarely tested on the ACT English Test

4. Apostrophes

  • Apostrophes on the ACT have two functions: possession and contractions
    • Most apostrophe questions test whether you can identify the correct possessive form
    • Other questions test whether you can distinguish between possessive pronouns and contractions

Possession Rules

  • For singular nouns: Add apostrophe + "s"
    • Example: "Terrence's bike is much faster than my brother's roller blades."
    • For singular words ending in "s" (like "bus"): You can add apostrophe + "s" OR just an apostrophe (both are correct)
    • Example: "The bus's driver" OR "The bus' driver"
  • For plural nouns ending in "s": Add apostrophe after the "s"
    • Example: "The boys' jerseys were all covered in mud after the game."
  • For plural nouns NOT ending in "s": Add apostrophe + "s"
    • Example: "Our women's basketball team won the championship last year."

Possessive vs. Plural

  • The ACT tests whether you can distinguish between singular possessive, plural possessive, and non-possessive plural
    • Singular possessive: "I accidentally stepped on the dog's bone." (one dog possessing the bone)
    • Plural possessive: "The dogs' collars are in the basket by the door." (multiple dogs possessing collars)
    • Non-possessive plural: "I watched as the dogs played in the yard." (multiple dogs, but no possession)
  • The trick: Check if the next word is being possessed
    • If the noun owns or possesses something, use an apostrophe
    • If there's no possession, don't use an apostrophe

Possessive Pronouns vs. Contractions

  • Possessive pronouns (its, hers, yours, ours, theirs) end in "s" but NEVER use an apostrophe
    • ✓ Correct: its, hers, yours, ours, theirs
    • ❌ Incorrect: it's (contraction for "it is"), her's, your's, our's, their's
  • Contractions use an apostrophe to show where letters are removed
    • "it's" = "it is" (contraction)
    • "you're" = "you are" (contraction)
    • "they're" = "they are" (contraction)
    • "who's" = "who is" (contraction)
  • The plug-in test: Expand the contraction and see if it makes sense
    • Example: "The cat loves playing with (its/it's) new toy."
    • Test: "The cat loves playing with it is new toy." ❌ This doesn't work!
    • Answer: "The cat loves playing with its new toy." ✓ Correct (possessive pronoun)

5. Quotation Marks

  • Quotation marks are most commonly used for direct quotes
    • They can also be used to show a word is being used as a technical term, slang, or in an unusual way

Direct Quotes That Are Spoken

  • Direct quotes that are spoken are offset by a comma
    • Anytime the quote is being spoken (said, yelled, whispered, exclaimed, etc.), use a comma before or after the quotation
    • Example: "Abigail said, 'I will not pay until the painting is completed.'"
    • Example: "'The thunderstorm last night woke me up,' Paul whispered."

Direct Quotes That Are NOT Spoken

  • Direct quotes that are not spoken have NO commas
    • If the quote is just a phrase or part of the sentence (not being actively spoken), there is no punctuation before the quotation marks
    • ❌ Incorrect: "My grandfather described the shells as, 'beautiful souvenirs from a past life.'"
    • ✓ Correct: "My grandfather described the shells as 'beautiful souvenirs from a past life.'"
    • The quotation marks show these are the grandfather's words, but he's not actively speaking them in the scene

Technical Terms, Slang, or Unusual Usage

  • Quotation marks can show a word is being used as a technical term, in an unusual way, or as slang
    • Technical term: "Having defined the term 'contact variance,' Dr. Chen continued his lecture."
    • Slang or sarcasm: "Sven refers to himself as a 'professional' influencer."
    • Unusual usage: "This 'revolutionary' program has left thousands of children without a basic education."
    • Irony: "Allie could smell the 'fresh' fish from across the room."

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Semicolons can replace periods—both sides must be independent clauses (complete sentences). Colons introduce lists, examples, explanations, or definitions, but the part before the colon must always be an independent clause, and you can never use colons with "including," "such as," or "for example."
  • ✓Dashes can function like unnecessary information commas or parentheses when used in pairs, and like colons when used as a single dash. Never mix punctuation—use a pair of commas, a pair of dashes, or a pair of parentheses, but never combine them.
  • ✓Apostrophes show possession (add apostrophe + "s" for singular nouns, apostrophe after "s" for plural nouns ending in "s") or contractions. Possessive pronouns like "its," "yours," and "theirs" never take apostrophes, while contractions like "it's" (it is), "you're" (you are), and "they're" (they are) always do.
  • ✓Quotation marks are used for direct quotes (with a comma if spoken, no comma if not spoken) and to show technical terms, slang, or unusual usage. When the quote is actively being said, yelled, or whispered, use a comma to offset it from the speaker tag.
