Pronoun questions appear frequently on the ACT English Test, testing three key concepts: pronoun-antecedent agreement, pronoun case, and pronoun clarity. The ACT deliberately creates ambiguity by using pronouns with unclear antecedents, testing case in tricky prepositional phrases, and checking whether you recognize singular vs. plural agreement. Mastering pronouns means knowing when to use subjective, objective, and possessive forms, ensuring every pronoun clearly refers to a specific noun, and maintaining proper agreement in number and gender.

1. Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

A pronoun must agree with its antecedent (the noun it replaces) in number, gender, and person. The ACT tests whether you can identify the correct antecedent and match the pronoun to it, even when distracting words appear between them.

Number Agreement (Singular vs. Plural)

Singular antecedents take singular pronouns (he, she, it, his, her, its). Plural antecedents take plural pronouns (they, them, their).

✓ Correct: Each student must bring his or her textbook.

→ "Each" is singular, so pronoun must be singular

✗ Wrong: Each student must bring their textbook.

→ "Each" (singular) doesn't match "their" (plural); note: while "they" as singular is becoming acceptable in modern usage, the ACT still requires traditional agreement

Indefinite Pronouns as Antecedents

Singular indefinite pronouns (each, either, neither, one, everyone, everybody, anyone, anybody, someone, somebody, no one, nobody) take singular pronouns.

✓ Correct: Everyone should submit his or her assignment by Friday.

✓ Correct: Neither of the girls remembered her permission slip.

→ "Neither" is singular despite "of the girls"

Key Tip: Words like "each," "every," "one," and "body" signal singular. When you see these, look for singular pronouns.

Compound Antecedents

Antecedents joined by "and" take plural pronouns. Antecedents joined by "or" or "nor" take pronouns that match the nearest antecedent.

✓ "And" (plural): John and Maria submitted their reports early.

✓ "Or" (nearest): Either the teacher or the students will present their findings.

✓ "Or" (nearest): Either the students or the teacher will present her findings.

Collective Nouns as Antecedents

Collective nouns (team, group, family, committee, class) are usually singular when referring to the group as a unit.

✓ Correct: The committee announced its decision yesterday.

✓ Correct: The team celebrated its victory.

2. Pronoun Case

Pronoun case determines which form to use based on the pronoun's function in the sentence. There are three cases: subjective (subject of sentence), objective (object of verb or preposition), and possessive (showing ownership).

The Three Cases

Subjective (subject): I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who

Objective (object): me, you, him, her, it, us, them, whom

Possessive (ownership): my/mine, your/yours, his, her/hers, its, our/ours, their/theirs, whose

Subjective Case: Subjects and Subject Complements

Use subjective pronouns when the pronoun is the subject of a verb or follows a linking verb (subject complement).

✓ Subject: She and I went to the store.

✓ Subject complement: It was she who called.

→ After linking verb "was," use subjective case

Objective Case: Objects of Verbs and Prepositions

Use objective pronouns when the pronoun receives the action of a verb or is the object of a preposition. This is the most commonly tested pronoun rule on the ACT.

✓ Direct object: The teacher called him and me to the office.

✓ Object of preposition: Between you and me, this is confidential.

✓ Object of preposition: The gift is for them and us.

✗ Wrong: Between you and I, this is confidential.

→ "Between" is a preposition; object must be "me," not "I"

Test Trick: When a pronoun is paired with a noun or another pronoun, remove the other person to check case. "The teacher called him and me" → "The teacher called me" ✓ (not "called I")

Who vs. Whom

"Who" is subjective (subject of verb). "Whom" is objective (object of verb or preposition). This distinction appears frequently on the ACT.

✓ Who (subject): Who called you last night?

→ Test: "He called" (not "Him called") → use "who"

✓ Whom (object): To whom should I address this letter?

→ Test: "to him" (not "to he") → use "whom"

Memory Trick: "Whom" and "him" both end in "m." If you can substitute "him," use "whom." If you can substitute "he," use "who."

Possessive Case and Its vs. It's

Possessive pronouns never use apostrophes. The most common error tested is confusing "its" (possessive) with "it's" (contraction for "it is" or "it has").

✓ Possessive: The dog wagged its tail. (belonging to it)

✓ Contraction: It's raining outside. (it is)

Other possessives (no apostrophes): yours, hers, ours, theirs, whose

✓ Correct: That book is hers. (not "her's")

3. Pronoun Clarity and Reference

Every pronoun must have a clear, specific antecedent. Ambiguous pronoun references create confusion and are frequently tested on the ACT. If a pronoun could refer to more than one noun, or if its antecedent is missing entirely, the sentence needs revision.

Ambiguous Reference

When a pronoun could refer to two or more nouns, the reference is ambiguous and must be clarified.

✗ Ambiguous: When Sarah told Maria about the opportunity, she was very excited.

→ Who was excited, Sarah or Maria? Unclear

✓ Clear: When Sarah told Maria about the opportunity, Maria was very excited.

✓ Clear: Sarah was very excited when she told Maria about the opportunity.

Vague Reference with "This," "That," "Which," or "It"

Pronouns like "this," "that," "which," and "it" should refer to specific nouns, not vague ideas or entire clauses.

✗ Vague: I studied all night and got an A on the test. This made me happy.

→ "This" refers to the entire situation, not a specific noun

✓ Clear: I studied all night and got an A on the test. This achievement made me happy.

✓ Clear: I studied all night and got an A on the test, which made me happy.

→ In context, "which" clearly refers to getting an A

Missing Antecedent

Sometimes a pronoun has no antecedent at all. The noun the pronoun refers to must actually appear in the sentence.

✗ Missing: In the novel, they describe life in the 1800s.

→ Who is "they"? The word "novel" is not a person

✓ Clear: In the novel, the author describes life in the 1800s.

✓ Clear: The novel describes life in the 1800s.

Implied Antecedents

A pronoun cannot refer to a possessive noun or an implied idea. The antecedent must be explicitly stated.

✗ Implied: In Shakespeare's plays, he explores human nature.

→ "Shakespeare's" is possessive; "he" needs a nominative antecedent

✓ Clear: In his plays, Shakespeare explores human nature.

4. Special Pronoun Issues

Beyond basic agreement, case, and clarity, the ACT tests several special pronoun situations that require careful attention. These include reflexive pronouns, relative pronouns, and pronoun consistency.

Reflexive Pronouns (Myself, Herself, Themselves)

Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves) must refer back to the subject. Never use them as subjects or when a simple pronoun works.

✓ Intensive: I myself completed the project. (emphasis)

✓ Reflexive: She hurt herself during practice. (refers to "she")

✗ Wrong: John and myself went to the store.

→ Can't use "myself" as subject; use "John and I"

✗ Wrong: Please give the report to Maria or myself.

→ Use "me" (object of preposition), not "myself"

Relative Pronouns: That vs. Which vs. Who

Use "who" for people, "which" for things (nonessential clauses), and "that" for things (essential clauses). This distinction matters on the ACT.

✓ Who (people): The student who studied the most earned an A.

✓ That (essential): The book that I borrowed is overdue.

✓ Which (nonessential): The book, which I finished yesterday, was excellent.

Key Rule: Use "which" with commas (nonessential), "that" without commas (essential). "Who" works for both people situations.

Pronoun Consistency

Maintain consistent point of view (first person, second person, or third person) throughout a passage. Don't shift between "you," "one," "we," and "they" unnecessarily.

✗ Inconsistent: When you study hard, one can achieve great results.

→ Shifts from "you" to "one"

✓ Consistent: When you study hard, you can achieve great results.

✓ Consistent: When one studies hard, one can achieve great results.

Demonstrative Pronouns: This, That, These, Those

Match demonstrative pronouns with their antecedents in number. "This" and "that" are singular; "these" and "those" are plural.

✓ Singular: This book is interesting.

✓ Plural: These books are interesting.

✗ Wrong: These kind of problems are difficult.

→ "Kind" is singular; use "this kind" or "these kinds"

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Pronoun-antecedent agreement: Pronouns must match antecedents in number, gender, and person; singular indefinite pronouns (each, everyone, neither) take singular pronouns; antecedents joined by "and" take plural pronouns
  • ✓Pronoun case: Use subjective (I, he, she, who) as subjects; objective (me, him, her, whom) as objects of verbs/prepositions; possessive (its, yours, theirs) to show ownership without apostrophes
  • ✓Who vs. whom: "Who" is subjective (subject of verb); "whom" is objective (object of verb or preposition); test by substituting "he/him"—if "him" works, use "whom"
  • ✓Pronoun clarity: Every pronoun needs a clear, specific antecedent; avoid ambiguous references where a pronoun could refer to multiple nouns; pronouns like "this," "that," and "it" should refer to specific nouns, not vague ideas
  • ✓Special issues: Don't use reflexive pronouns (myself, themselves) as subjects or simple objects; use "who" for people, "that" for essential clauses, "which" for nonessential clauses; maintain consistent point of view throughout passages
