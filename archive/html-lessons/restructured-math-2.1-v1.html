Angles questions appear frequently on the ACT Math test, testing your understanding of vertical angles, parallel lines, and polygon properties. The key to solving these problems is labeling all known angles and using angle relationships to find unknown values.

1. Intersecting Lines and Vertical Angles

  • Vertical angles are angles that are opposite each other when two lines intersect
    • Vertical angles are always equal to each other
    • If you know one angle, you automatically know its vertical angle
  • Adjacent angles are angles that are next to each other and share a side
    • Adjacent angles are supplementary (they add up to 180°)
    • Example: If one angle is 60°, the adjacent angle is 120° (since 60° + 120° = 180°)
  • When two lines intersect, they create 4 angles
    • Opposite angles (vertical angles) are equal
    • Side-by-side angles (adjacent angles) are supplementary

2. Parallel Lines and Angle Relationships

  • When two parallel lines are crossed by another line (called a transversal), special angle relationships are created
  • The most important rule to remember:
    • There are two sets of identical angles
    • Any angle from the first set is supplementary to any angle from the second set
    • Example: If one angle is 60°, four angles will be 60° and four angles will be 120°
  • Specific angle relationships you should know:
    • Vertical angles are equal
    • Alternate interior angles are equal (angles on opposite sides of the transversal, between the parallel lines)
    • Corresponding angles are equal (angles in the same position at each intersection)
    • Opposite interior angles are supplementary (they add to 180°)
  • TIP: Extend parallel lines with your pencil
    • Sometimes parallel lines in diagrams don't extend all the way across
    • Draw them longer yourself to make angle relationships easier to see
    • Example: In a parallelogram, extend the sides to create full parallel lines

3. Interior Angles in Polygons

  • Every polygon has a specific sum of interior angles based on the number of sides
    • Triangle (3 sides): 180°
    • Quadrilateral (4 sides): 360°
    • Pentagon (5 sides): 540°
    • Hexagon (6 sides): 720°
  • The formula for any polygon:
    • Sum of Interior Angles = 180°(n − 2)
    • Where n is the number of sides
    • This formula works for any polygon, regardless of shape
  • How to use this formula on the ACT:
    • Count the number of sides of the polygon
    • Plug n into the formula: 180°(n − 2)
    • Set the sum equal to the given angles and solve
    • The shape doesn't matter—only the number of sides matters
  • Common polygon applications:
    • Finding a missing angle when other angles are given
    • Finding the value of x when angles are expressed as algebraic expressions
    • Verifying if given angle measurements are possible for a polygon

4. Test-Taking Tips for Angle Questions

  • Always label angles as you find them
    • Write angle measurements directly on the diagram
    • The more angles you label, the easier it becomes to find the angle you need
    • Use vertical angle and supplementary angle relationships to fill in values
  • Look for triangles within the figure
    • All angles in a triangle add to 180°
    • If you know two angles in a triangle, you can find the third
    • Sometimes you need to find angles in one triangle to solve another triangle
  • Watch for angle bisectors
    • An angle bisector cuts an angle exactly in half
    • If an angle is 72° and it's bisected, each half is 36°
    • Use this to find additional angles in the figure
  • Use what you know about parallel lines
    • If you see two parallel lines, look for the transversal
    • Identify which angles are equal and which are supplementary
    • Remember: there are two groups of identical angles
