Understanding circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas is essential for ACT Math success. These conic sections appear in 4-6 questions per test—about 10% of all math questions! This lesson will teach you how to identify each shape, work with their equations, and solve problems involving distance, tangent lines, and geometric properties quickly and accurately.

1. Circle Fundamentals and Equations

A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a center point. The ACT tests your ability to work with circle equations, calculate circumference, area, and understand geometric properties like chords, tangent lines, and secants.

Standard Form of Circle Equation

The standard equation of a circle with center (h, k) and radius r is (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r². For example, (x - 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25 represents a circle centered at (3, -2) with radius 5. If the equation is (x - h)² + (y - k)² = 16, the radius is 4, not 16—remember to take the square root!

Circumference and Area

The circumference (perimeter) of a circle is C = 2πr or equivalently C = πd where d is diameter. The area is A = πr². Always check whether the problem gives you radius or diameter—a common mistake is using diameter in the area formula, which gives you an answer 4 times too large!

Chords, Tangents, and Secants

A chord is a line segment connecting two points on a circle. A diameter is the longest chord, passing through the center. A tangent line touches the circle at exactly one point and is perpendicular to the radius at that point. A secant is a line that intersects the circle at two points.

Circle in General Form

Sometimes the ACT gives you a circle equation in general form: x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0. To find the center and radius, you must complete the square for both x and y terms. For example, x² + y² - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0 becomes (x - 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25 after completing the square, revealing center (3, -2) and radius 5.

2. Ellipses: Stretched Circles

An ellipse is an oval shape—essentially a stretched circle. It has two axes: the longer major axis and the shorter minor axis. The ACT tests your ability to identify ellipses, understand their equations, and calculate distances.

Standard Form of Ellipse Equation

The standard equation of an ellipse centered at (h, k) is (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1 where a and b are the semi-major and semi-minor axes. If a > b, the ellipse is stretched horizontally; if b > a, it's stretched vertically. For example, (x - 2)²/25 + (y + 1)²/9 = 1 has center (2, -1), horizontal stretch of 5, and vertical stretch of 3.

Foci and Major/Minor Axes

An ellipse has two special points called foci (singular: focus). The sum of distances from any point on the ellipse to both foci is constant. The major axis is the longest diameter, with length 2a. The minor axis is the shortest diameter, with length 2b. The distance from center to focus is c, where c² = a² - b² (for a > b).

Identifying Ellipses vs Circles

A circle is a special case of an ellipse where a = b. To identify whether an equation represents a circle or ellipse, look at the denominators: if they're equal, it's a circle; if different, it's an ellipse. The equation (x - 1)²/16 + (y + 2)²/16 = 1 is a circle with radius 4, while (x - 1)²/16 + (y + 2)²/9 = 1 is an ellipse.

Practical Applications

Ellipses appear in physics (planetary orbits), architecture (elliptical arches), and acoustics (whispering galleries). On the ACT, you might need to find the maximum or minimum distance from the center, determine if a point lies on the ellipse, or calculate the area (A = πab). The area formula uses both semi-axes multiplied by π.

3. Hyperbolas: Open Curves

A hyperbola consists of two separate curves (called branches) that open away from each other. Unlike circles and ellipses which are closed curves, hyperbolas extend infinitely. The ACT occasionally tests basic hyperbola recognition and properties.

Standard Form of Hyperbola Equation

The standard equation of a hyperbola centered at (h, k) is (x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1 (opens horizontally) or (y - k)²/a² - (x - h)²/b² = 1 (opens vertically). The key difference from an ellipse is the minus sign between terms. The positive term determines which direction the hyperbola opens.

Asymptotes

Hyperbolas have asymptotes—imaginary lines that the branches approach but never touch. For a hyperbola centered at (h, k) opening horizontally, the asymptotes are y - k = ±(b/a)(x - h). These lines form an "X" shape through the center, and the hyperbola curves get closer and closer to these lines as you move away from the center.

Vertices and Foci

The vertices are the points where the hyperbola is closest to the center, located at distance a from the center. The foci are located at distance c from the center, where c² = a² + b² (note this is plus, not minus like for ellipses). For any point on the hyperbola, the absolute difference of distances to the two foci is constant.

Recognizing Hyperbolas

The easiest way to identify a hyperbola equation is the minus sign between the squared terms. If you see (x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1 or (y - k)²/a² - (x - h)²/b² = 1, it's definitely a hyperbola. The equation xy = k (where k is a constant) is also a hyperbola, though in a different form—this represents a rectangular hyperbola with asymptotes along the x and y axes.

4. Comparing and Solving Conic Section Problems

The ACT often tests your ability to quickly distinguish between conic sections—circles, ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas. Understanding their common features, differences, and how to manipulate their equations will help you solve problems efficiently.

Quick Identification Guide

Circle: x² + y² terms with same coefficient, plus sign. Ellipse: x² + y² terms with different coefficients, plus sign. Hyperbola: x² and y² terms with opposite signs (one plus, one minus). Parabola: only one variable is squared. For example, x² + 4y² = 16 is an ellipse (different coefficients, both positive), x² - 4y² = 16 is a hyperbola (opposite signs), and y = x² + 3 is a parabola (only x is squared).

Converting Between Forms

To convert from general form to standard form, complete the square for both variables. For example, 4x² + 9y² - 16x + 18y - 11 = 0 becomes 4(x² - 4x) + 9(y² + 2y) = 11, then 4(x - 2)² + 9(y + 1)² = 36, and finally (x - 2)²/9 + (y + 1)²/4 = 1 in standard form. This reveals an ellipse centered at (2, -1).

Distance and Point Problems

Common ACT problems ask: "Does point (a, b) lie on this curve?" Simply substitute the coordinates into the equation and check if it equals the right side. For distance problems, use the fact that for circles, every point is distance r from center; for ellipses, use the sum-of-distances property; for hyperbolas, use the difference-of-distances property.

Graphing Strategy

When graphing conic sections, always start by identifying the center point (h, k). Then plot key features: for circles, mark radius; for ellipses, mark semi-major and semi-minor axes; for hyperbolas, draw asymptotes first, then sketch the curves approaching them. A quick sketch can help you eliminate wrong answer choices or verify your algebraic work.
