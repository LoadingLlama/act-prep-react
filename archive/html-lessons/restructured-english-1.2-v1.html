Commas are the most common punctuation on the ACT English Test, appearing in 15-20 questions per test. To master comma questions, you need to understand the 4 types of commas and when each one should be used.

1. The Four Types of Commas

  • The ACT tests exactly 4 comma rules—master these and you'll handle every comma question
    • Each type has a specific purpose and can't be substituted with another
    • Understanding when to use each type is critical for avoiding comma errors

Type 1: Comma + FANBOYS

  • Use a comma and a FANBOYS word to join two independent clauses
    • FANBOYS = For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So
    • Example: "My alarm clock did not go off this morning, so I arrived late at school"
    • Both sides must be independent clauses (complete sentences)

Type 2: Dependent Clause Followed by Independent Clause

  • When a dependent clause comes before an independent clause, use a comma to separate them
    • Example: "While electronic music has become very popular, many people still prefer classic rock"
    • The dependent clause starts with subordinating conjunctions like while, although, because, if
    • This is from Chapter 1's Rule #4 for compound sentences

Type 3: Unnecessary Information

  • Commas separate unnecessary information from the rest of the sentence
    • Information is unnecessary if you can remove it without fundamentally changing the sentence's meaning
    • Unnecessary information can be as short as one word or as long as a lengthy phrase
    • Example: "Mrs. Ellison, who is known for giving pop quizzes, is my least favorite teacher"
    • Example: "The basketball team, though, did not mount a comeback this week"

Type 4: Listing

  • Use commas when listing more than two items or with multiple adjectives modifying the same noun
    • Example with list: "The group ordered coconut shrimp, hot wings, and onion rings"
    • Example with adjectives: "The old, limping dog still managed to complete the 3-mile hike"
    • Use commas between all items in a series, including before the final "and" or "or"

2. Unnecessary Information Commas

  • The most common comma type on the ACT separates unnecessary information
    • Information is unnecessary if removing it doesn't fundamentally change the sentence's meaning
    • Unnecessary information can appear before a comma, after a comma, or between two commas
    • After removing unnecessary information, what remains must still be a complete sentence

The "Crossing-Out" Trick

  • To test if information is unnecessary, cross it out and read what remains
    • If the sentence is still complete and makes sense, the information is unnecessary
    • If the sentence becomes incomplete or loses critical meaning, the information is necessary (no commas)
    • Example: "The wooden beam, set at an angle, created an optical illusion"
    • Cross out "set at an angle" → "The wooden beam created an optical illusion" (still complete!)

Necessary vs. Unnecessary Information

  • Determining if information is truly unnecessary requires careful reading
    • Correct (necessary): "The dresses that need to be hemmed are hanging in the closet"
    • Without "that need to be hemmed," we don't know which specific dresses
    • Correct (unnecessary): "Judith, who went to fashion school, designs dresses for a living"
    • We already know who designs dresses (Judith), so the fashion school detail is extra

The Names Rule

  • The ACT loves testing comma usage with names—you'll see at least one question on this
    • If the identifier is NOT specific to one person (friend, teacher, physicist), the name is necessary—no commas
    • Example: "My friend Kelly recently moved to London" (I have multiple friends, so Kelly is necessary)
    • If the identifier IS specific to one person (best friend, youngest sister, CEO), the name is unnecessary—use commas
    • Example: "My best friend, Andrew, is a certified scuba diver" (I have only one best friend)

"That" vs. "Which" Phrases

  • Phrases starting with that never get commas—phrases starting with which always get commas
    • "That" phrases are always necessary information
    • Example: "The dogs that live down the street are always barking"
    • "Which" phrases are always unnecessary information
    • Example: "My neighbor's dogs, which are always barking, love to play fetch"
    • Exception: Prepositional phrases like "on which," "in which," or "of which" don't use commas before them

"ing" and "ed" Phrases

  • Participle phrases starting with "ing" or "ed" may or may not need commas
    • On the ACT, "ing" and "ed" phrases most commonly appear with commas as unnecessary information
    • Example: "Excited for the car ride, Jane's bulldog started to jump up and down"
    • Example: "Marcus applied for the internship, hoping that he would get the position"
    • If the phrase helps specify which noun, it's necessary (no commas)
    • Example: "The politician waving to the crowd is predicted to win the election"

Prepositional Phrases

  • Prepositional phrases consist of a preposition and its object
    • Common prepositions: in, of, to, for, with, on, at, from, by, about, as, into, like, through, after, over, between, during, before, among, around
    • Prepositional phrases at the FRONT of a sentence are always followed by a comma
    • Example: "On my way to work, I listened to my new favorite podcast"
    • Prepositional phrases in the MIDDLE or END almost never have commas (99% of the time)
    • Example: "The clothing on the bed is for vacation"
    • They contain essential information specifying which noun you're talking about

3. Transitional Words Like "However"

  • Transitional words (also called conjunctive adverbs) require specific punctuation
    • Common transitional words: however, though, therefore, instead, likewise, nevertheless, regardless, moreover, subsequently, furthermore, in addition, as a result, of course, otherwise, for example
    • These are NOT FANBOYS—they require different punctuation

Three Ways to Punctuate Transitional Words

  • Position 1: At the front of the sentence followed by a comma
    • Example: "However, the truth would not be revealed until next week"
    • Example: "As a result, the student was sent to the principal's office"
    • This treats the transitional word as unnecessary information you can cross out
  • Position 2: Separated by commas in the middle of a sentence
    • Example: "The truth, however, would not be revealed until next week"
    • Example: "The student, as a result, was sent to the principal's office"
    • Again, treat as unnecessary information between two commas
  • Position 3: Separated by a semicolon and comma when between two independent clauses
    • Example: "I expected to pay over $100 for my new hiking boots; however, I found a sale and got them for half price"
    • Structure: Independent clause + semicolon + transitional word + comma + Independent clause
    • CRITICAL: You need BOTH the semicolon (before) AND the comma (after) the transitional word

4. Spotting Unnecessary Information Comma Questions

  • Unnecessary information comma questions have two defining characteristics
    • The words in all 4 answer choices are exactly the same
    • The only difference is where commas are located
    • When you see this pattern, immediately use the "crossing-out" trick

How to Approach These Questions

  • Read the entire sentence carefully
    • Pay attention to any commas NOT in the underlined portion
    • These fixed commas are often important clues for finding the correct answer
    • Look out for sentence structure rules from Chapter 1 that may also apply
  • Use the crossing-out trick systematically
    • Test each answer choice by crossing out what the comma(s) would separate
    • The correct answer leaves a complete, grammatical sentence after crossing out

The Pauses and Commas Backup Trick

  • Read the sentence out loud (or "out loud" in your head on test day)
    • If you need to take a short pause for a breath, you likely need a comma
    • This is a backup method—always verify with the crossing-out trick first
    • Natural pauses often correspond to where unnecessary information appears

Key Takeaways

  • ✓The ACT tests exactly 4 comma types: (1) comma + FANBOYS joining independent clauses, (2) dependent clause before independent clause, (3) unnecessary information, and (4) listing items or adjectives
  • ✓Use the "crossing-out" trick for unnecessary information commas—if you can remove the information and still have a complete sentence, it's unnecessary and needs commas to separate it
  • ✓"That" phrases never get commas while "which" phrases always get commas—for names, use commas only when the identifier is specific to one person (best friend, CEO) but not for general identifiers (friend, teacher)
  • ✓Transitional words like "however" require specific punctuation: at the front with a comma after, in the middle between two commas, or between independent clauses with a semicolon before and comma after
