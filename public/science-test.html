Anole Behavior Study

Scientists studied the behavior of green anoles (Anolis carolinensis) in controlled laboratory environments to understand their territorial and social behaviors. The study examined how factors such as temperature, habitat structure, and population density affect anole behavior patterns.

Experiment 1: Researchers placed individual male anoles in terrariums with different temperature settings (20°C, 25°C, 30°C, and 35°C) and recorded their activity levels over 24-hour periods. Activity was measured by the number of movements per hour.

Results: Activity levels increased with temperature up to 30°C, then decreased at 35°C. Peak activity occurred at 30°C with an average of 42 movements per hour.

Experiment 2: Male anoles were placed in terrariums with varying numbers of perches and hiding spots. Territorial displays (head bobbing and dewlap extension) were recorded for each setup.

Results: Territorial displays increased with the number of perches available, reaching a maximum when 4-6 perches were present per terrarium.

Experiment 3: Population density effects were studied by placing different numbers of anoles (1, 2, 4, or 8) in identical terrariums and measuring stress indicators such as color changes and cortisol levels.

Results: Stress indicators increased significantly when more than 4 anoles were housed together in the same terrarium size.

Enzyme Activity and pH

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required. The activity of enzymes is highly dependent on environmental conditions, particularly pH and temperature. Students conducted experiments to determine the optimal pH for different enzymes.

Experiment 1: Students tested the activity of catalase enzyme at different pH levels (4, 6, 7, 8, 10, 12) by measuring the rate of oxygen gas production when hydrogen peroxide was broken down. The reaction was carried out at 25°C for 5 minutes.

Table 1: Catalase Activity
pH 4: 2 mL O₂/min
pH 6: 8 mL O₂/min
pH 7: 15 mL O₂/min
pH 8: 12 mL O₂/min
pH 10: 5 mL O₂/min
pH 12: 1 mL O₂/min

Experiment 2: Students tested pepsin enzyme activity by measuring protein digestion at various pH levels (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8). Protein breakdown was measured as the decrease in protein concentration over 30 minutes at 37°C.

Table 2: Pepsin Activity
pH 1: 85% protein digestion
pH 2: 92% protein digestion
pH 3: 78% protein digestion
pH 4: 45% protein digestion
pH 6: 12% protein digestion
pH 8: 3% protein digestion

Plant Photosynthesis Study

Researchers investigated how different environmental factors affect the rate of photosynthesis in aquatic plants. They used Elodea plants and measured oxygen bubble production as an indicator of photosynthetic activity.

Experiment 1: Plants were exposed to different light intensities (0, 25, 50, 75, 100% of maximum artificial light) while maintaining constant temperature (22°C) and CO₂ concentration.

Figure 1: Light Intensity vs. Oxygen Production
0% light: 0 bubbles/min
25% light: 8 bubbles/min
50% light: 15 bubbles/min
75% light: 22 bubbles/min
100% light: 25 bubbles/min

Experiment 2: Plants were placed at different distances from a light source (10cm, 20cm, 40cm, 80cm, 160cm) to test the inverse square law's effect on photosynthesis.

Table 3: Distance vs. Light Intensity and Oxygen Production
10cm: 100% intensity, 25 bubbles/min
20cm: 25% intensity, 12 bubbles/min
40cm: 6.25% intensity, 5 bubbles/min
80cm: 1.56% intensity, 2 bubbles/min
160cm: 0.39% intensity, 0 bubbles/min

Experiment 3: Temperature effects were studied by placing plants in water baths at different temperatures (5°C, 15°C, 25°C, 35°C, 45°C) under constant light conditions.

PASSAGE IV
Genetic Inheritance Patterns

Scientists studied inheritance patterns in fruit flies (Drosophila melanogaster) to understand how traits are passed from parents to offspring. They focused on wing shape and eye color traits.

Study 1: Researchers crossed normal-winged flies with vestigial-winged flies. All F₁ offspring had normal wings. When F₁ flies were crossed with each other, the F₂ generation showed a 3:1 ratio of normal to vestigial wings.

Study 2: Red-eyed flies were crossed with white-eyed flies. All F₁ females had red eyes, while all F₁ males had white eyes. The F₂ generation showed different patterns in males and females.

Table 4: F₂ Generation Results
Females: 50% red eyes, 50% white eyes
Males: 50% red eyes, 50% white eyes
Total F₂: 75% red eyes, 25% white eyes in initial count

Study 3: Double crosses were performed using flies that differed in both wing shape and eye color to study independent assortment of traits.

Chemical Reaction Rates

Students investigated factors affecting the rate of chemical reactions using the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide in the presence of different catalysts and under varying conditions.

Experiment 1: The effect of catalyst type was tested using manganese dioxide (MnO₂), iron(III) chloride (FeCl₃), and potassium iodide (KI). The rate was measured by collecting oxygen gas over time.

Table 5: Catalyst Effectiveness
No catalyst: 0.5 mL O₂/min
KI: 8.2 mL O₂/min
FeCl₃: 12.8 mL O₂/min
MnO₂: 15.6 mL O₂/min

Experiment 2: Temperature effects were studied by conducting reactions at 10°C, 20°C, 30°C, 40°C, and 50°C using MnO₂ as the catalyst.

Experiment 3: The concentration of hydrogen peroxide was varied (1%, 3%, 6%, 9%, 12%) while keeping other factors constant to determine the relationship between reactant concentration and reaction rate.

PASSAGE VI
Ecological Succession Study

Ecologists studied primary succession on recently exposed volcanic rock to understand how communities change over time. They monitored species diversity and biomass over a 50-year period.

Stage 1 (Years 0-5): Pioneer species including lichens and mosses colonize bare rock. Total species count: 12, Total biomass: 2 kg/hectare

Stage 2 (Years 6-15): Grasses and small shrubs establish. Total species count: 28, Total biomass: 15 kg/hectare

Stage 3 (Years 16-35): Small trees and larger shrubs dominate. Total species count: 45, Total biomass: 125 kg/hectare

Stage 4 (Years 36-50): Mature forest development begins. Total species count: 67, Total biomass: 380 kg/hectare

Figure 2: The researchers also measured soil depth, which increased from 0 cm initially to 15 cm after 50 years, with the most rapid increase occurring during years 10-25.

Additional Data: pH levels changed from 6.2 (acidic, due to lichen acids) in early stages to 7.1 (neutral) in later stages as organic matter accumulated.
