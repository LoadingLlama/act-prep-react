Calculating areas and volumes represents one of the most frequently tested geometry topics on the ACT, requiring you to apply area formulas for two-dimensional shapes, volume formulas for three-dimensional solids, and triangle properties to solve complex problems. These concepts appear in approximately 20% of ACT Math questions and form the foundation for understanding spatial relationships, optimization problems, and real-world applications.

1. Triangle Areas and Properties

Basic Triangle Area Formula

The fundamental formula for the area of any triangle is:

Area = (1/2) × base × height

The base can be any side of the triangle, and the height is the perpendicular distance from that base to the opposite vertex. This perpendicular relationship is critical—the height must form a 90° angle with the base.

Example: A triangle has a base of 12 cm and a height of 8 cm. What is its area?

Area = (1/2) × 12 × 8 = (1/2) × 96 = 48 cm²

Note: The height must be perpendicular to the base you choose. If the triangle is tilted, the height may fall outside the triangle itself, but it still must form a right angle with the base.

Right Triangle Properties

For right triangles, finding area is particularly straightforward because the two legs automatically serve as base and height—they're already perpendicular to each other.

Right triangle area = (1/2) × leg₁ × leg₂

Example: A right triangle has legs of 5 inches and 12 inches. What is its area?

Area = (1/2) × 5 × 12 = (1/2) × 60 = 30 square inches

Common mistake: Don't use the hypotenuse in the area formula. The hypotenuse is never perpendicular to another side, so it can't serve as base or height.

Special Right Triangles

Two special right triangles appear frequently on the ACT: the 30-60-90 triangle and the 45-45-90 triangle. Knowing their side ratios helps you quickly find areas.

45-45-90 triangle: The two legs are equal, and the hypotenuse is leg × √2. If each leg is x, then:

Area = (1/2) × x × x = x²/2

Example: A 45-45-90 triangle has legs of 6 units each. What is its area?

Area = 6²/2 = 36/2 = 18 square units

30-60-90 triangle: If the shortest leg (opposite the 30° angle) is x, then the longer leg is x√3, and the hypotenuse is 2x. Area:

Area = (1/2) × x × x√3 = (x²√3)/2

Example: A 30-60-90 triangle has a shortest leg of 4 cm. What is its area?

Shorter leg = 4 cm
Longer leg = 4√3 cm
Area = (1/2) × 4 × 4√3 = 8√3 cm²

Equilateral Triangle Area

An equilateral triangle has all sides equal and all angles equal to 60°. If each side is s, the area formula is:

Area = (s²√3)/4

This formula comes from splitting the equilateral triangle into two 30-60-90 triangles and using their properties.

Example: An equilateral triangle has sides of 10 inches. What is its area?

Area = (10²√3)/4 = (100√3)/4 = 25√3 square inches

Approximation: √3 ≈ 1.732, so 25√3 ≈ 43.3 square inches

Heron's Formula (Advanced)

When you know all three sides of a triangle but not the height, use Heron's formula:

First find the semi-perimeter: s = (a + b + c)/2

Then: Area = √[s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)]

Example: A triangle has sides 5, 6, and 7. What is its area?

s = (5 + 6 + 7)/2 = 18/2 = 9
Area = √[9(9-5)(9-6)(9-7)]
Area = √[9 × 4 × 3 × 2]
Area = √216 = 6√6 ≈ 14.7 square units

2. Areas of Common Shapes

Rectangles and Squares

Rectangle area = length × width

Square area = side²

A square is a special rectangle where length equals width, so the formula simplifies to side squared.

Example: A rectangle is 15 feet long and 8 feet wide. What is its area?

Area = 15 × 8 = 120 square feet

Example: A square has sides of 9 meters. What is its area?

Area = 9² = 81 square meters

Parallelograms

Parallelogram area = base × height

Like triangles, the height must be perpendicular to the base. Don't confuse the slant side length with the height.

Example: A parallelogram has a base of 10 cm and a perpendicular height of 6 cm. The slant sides are 8 cm long. What is the area?

Area = 10 × 6 = 60 cm²

Note: The 8 cm slant side is NOT used in the area calculation—only the perpendicular height matters.

Trapezoids

A trapezoid has one pair of parallel sides (called bases). The area formula is:

Area = (1/2) × (base₁ + base₂) × height

This formula averages the two parallel bases and multiplies by the perpendicular height.

Example: A trapezoid has parallel bases of 8 inches and 12 inches, with a height of 5 inches. What is its area?

Area = (1/2) × (8 + 12) × 5
Area = (1/2) × 20 × 5
Area = 50 square inches

Memory trick: Average the bases, then multiply by height.

Circles

Circle area = πr² (where r is the radius)

If you're given the diameter (d) instead of radius, remember that r = d/2, so:

Circle area = π(d/2)² = πd²/4

Example: A circle has a radius of 7 cm. What is its area?

Area = π(7)² = 49π cm²
Area ≈ 49 × 3.14159 ≈ 153.9 cm²

ACT tip: The test often leaves answers in terms of π (like 49π), so don't approximate unless necessary.

Composite Figures

Many ACT problems involve composite figures—shapes made by combining or subtracting simpler shapes. The strategy is:

  1. Break the figure into recognizable shapes (rectangles, triangles, circles, etc.)
  2. Find the area of each component
  3. Add areas for combined shapes, subtract for removed sections

Example: A figure consists of a rectangle (10 × 6) with a semicircle (radius 3) attached to one of the shorter sides. What is the total area?

Rectangle area = 10 × 6 = 60
Semicircle area = (1/2)πr² = (1/2)π(3)² = 4.5π ≈ 14.1
Total area = 60 + 14.1 = 74.1 square units (or 60 + 4.5π in exact form)

3. Volumes of Three-Dimensional Solids

Rectangular Prisms and Cubes

Rectangular prism volume = length × width × height

Cube volume = side³

A cube is a special rectangular prism where all edges are equal.

Example: A box is 12 inches long, 8 inches wide, and 5 inches tall. What is its volume?

Volume = 12 × 8 × 5 = 480 cubic inches

Example: A cube has edges of 4 feet. What is its volume?

Volume = 4³ = 64 cubic feet

Cylinders

Cylinder volume = πr²h (where r is radius and h is height)

Think of this as the area of the circular base (πr²) multiplied by the height.

Example: A cylindrical water tank has a radius of 5 meters and a height of 12 meters. What is its volume?

Volume = π(5)²(12) = π(25)(12) = 300π m³
Volume ≈ 942.5 m³

Spheres

Sphere volume = (4/3)πr³

This formula is usually provided on the ACT, but memorizing it saves time.

Example: A sphere has a radius of 6 inches. What is its volume?

Volume = (4/3)π(6)³ = (4/3)π(216) = 288π cubic inches
Volume ≈ 904.8 cubic inches

Comparison: A sphere's volume is roughly 2/3 that of the cube that would contain it.

Cones

Cone volume = (1/3)πr²h

Notice this is exactly 1/3 the volume of a cylinder with the same radius and height.

Example: An ice cream cone has a radius of 3 cm and a height of 10 cm. What is its volume?

Volume = (1/3)π(3)²(10) = (1/3)π(9)(10) = 30π cm³
Volume ≈ 94.2 cm³

Pyramids

Pyramid volume = (1/3) × base area × height

The base can be any polygon. Calculate the base area first, then multiply by (1/3)h.

Example: A pyramid has a square base with sides of 8 feet and a height of 12 feet. What is its volume?

Base area = 8² = 64 square feet
Volume = (1/3) × 64 × 12 = (1/3) × 768 = 256 cubic feet

Surface Area vs. Volume

Don't confuse volume (the space inside a 3D object, measured in cubic units) with surface area (the total area of all faces, measured in square units).

Key surface area formulas:

  • Rectangular prism: SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
  • Cube: SA = 6s²
  • Cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh (two circles plus the lateral surface)
  • Sphere: SA = 4πr²

Example: A cube has edges of 5 cm. What is its surface area?

SA = 6(5)² = 6(25) = 150 cm²

Note: Each of the 6 faces has area 25 cm², so we multiply by 6.

4. Advanced Applications and Problem-Solving

Scaling Effects on Area and Volume

When you scale a figure by a factor of k:

  • All linear dimensions (lengths, widths, heights) multiply by k
  • Area multiplies by k²
  • Volume multiplies by k³

Example: A rectangle has area 20 square inches. If you double all dimensions, what is the new area?

Scaling factor k = 2
New area = original area × k² = 20 × 2² = 20 × 4 = 80 square inches

Example: A cube has volume 27 cubic feet. If you triple all edges, what is the new volume?

Scaling factor k = 3
New volume = 27 × 3³ = 27 × 27 = 729 cubic feet

Optimization Problems

The ACT often asks you to maximize or minimize area or volume given constraints.

Example: You have 40 feet of fence to enclose a rectangular garden. What dimensions give maximum area?

Perimeter = 40, so 2l + 2w = 40, which gives l + w = 20
If l = x, then w = 20 - x
Area = x(20 - x) = 20x - x²

Maximum occurs when the garden is a square: l = w = 10 feet
Maximum area = 10 × 10 = 100 square feet

General principle: For a fixed perimeter, a square always has the maximum area among rectangles.

Area of Shaded Regions

These problems require subtracting one area from another:

  1. Find the area of the outer figure
  2. Find the area of the inner figure (the unshaded part)
  3. Subtract: Shaded area = Outer area - Inner area

Example: A circle with radius 8 cm has a smaller circle with radius 5 cm removed from its center. What is the shaded area?

Outer circle area = π(8)² = 64π cm²
Inner circle area = π(5)² = 25π cm²
Shaded area = 64π - 25π = 39π cm²
Shaded area ≈ 122.5 cm²

Working Backward from Area or Volume

Sometimes you're given the area or volume and must find a dimension.

Example: A circle has area 49π square inches. What is its radius?

πr² = 49π
r² = 49
r = 7 inches

Example: A rectangular prism has volume 120 cubic feet, length 5 feet, and width 4 feet. What is its height?

5 × 4 × h = 120
20h = 120
h = 6 feet

Real-World Applications

ACT problems often present area and volume in practical contexts:

  • Paint coverage: How many cans of paint for a wall?
  • Filling containers: How much water fits in a tank?
  • Land area: How many acres in a plot?
  • Material costs: Cost per square foot of flooring?

Example: A cylindrical water tank has radius 4 feet and height 10 feet. If 1 cubic foot holds 7.5 gallons, how many gallons does the tank hold?

Volume = πr²h = π(4)²(10) = 160π cubic feet
Volume ≈ 502.7 cubic feet
Gallons = 502.7 × 7.5 ≈ 3,770 gallons

Common Mistakes and Time-Saving Tips

Mistake 1: Using diameter instead of radius (or vice versa). Always check whether you're given r or d.

Mistake 2: Forgetting the (1/2) in triangle and trapezoid formulas. These formulas always include a factor of 1/2.

Mistake 3: Confusing area (square units) with volume (cubic units). Check the units in the answer choices.

Mistake 4: Using slant height instead of perpendicular height. Height must always be perpendicular to the base.

Tip 1: Draw and label diagrams. Even if a figure is provided, add the given measurements to clarify.

Tip 2: Leave answers in terms of π when possible. It's often faster and more accurate.

Tip 3: Memorize the "1/3 rule" for pyramids and cones. Both are exactly 1/3 the volume of the corresponding prism or cylinder.

Tip 4: For composite figures, sketch the breakdown. Draw lines separating the figure into simpler shapes, then label each piece.
