Beyond the major grammar categories, the ACT English Test includes several miscellaneous grammar topics that appear regularly but don't fit into larger categories. These include adjective vs. adverb usage, double negatives, idioms and prepositions, and comparison forms. While each topic appears less frequently than commas or verbs, together they account for 8-12 questions per test. Mastering these miscellaneous rules ensures you catch errors that other students miss.

1. Adjectives vs. Adverbs

Adjectives modify nouns and pronouns, while adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. The ACT frequently tests whether you can distinguish between these forms, particularly after linking verbs and with words like "good/well" and "bad/badly."

Basic Adjective and Adverb Usage

Use adjectives to describe nouns. Use adverbs (usually ending in -ly) to describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.

✓ Adjective (describes noun): She is a careful driver.

✓ Adverb (describes verb): She drives carefully.

After Linking Verbs: Use Adjectives

After linking verbs (is, seems, appears, feels, tastes, smells, sounds, looks, becomes, remains), use adjectives because they describe the subject, not the verb.

✓ Correct: The soup tastes delicious. (adjective describes "soup")

✓ Correct: She feels confident. (adjective describes "she")

✗ Wrong: The soup tastes deliciously.

→ Adverb incorrectly used after linking verb

Good vs. Well, Bad vs. Badly

"Good" and "bad" are adjectives. "Well" and "badly" are usually adverbs. Exception: "well" as an adjective means "healthy."

✓ Adjective: She is a good singer. / She feels good. / She feels well (healthy).

✓ Adverb: She sings well. / He performed badly.

Memory Trick: "I feel good" = I'm happy (emotion). "I feel well" = I'm healthy (physical state). Both are correct but mean different things!

Real vs. Really, Sure vs. Surely

"Real" and "sure" are adjectives; "really" and "surely" are adverbs. When intensifying an adjective or verb, use the adverb form.

✓ Correct: The test was really difficult. (modifies adjective "difficult")

✓ Correct: I am sure about the answer. (adjective after linking verb)

2. Comparisons and Superlatives

When comparing two things, use the comparative form. When comparing three or more things, use the superlative form. The ACT tests whether you know when to use -er/-est versus more/most, and whether you avoid double comparisons.

Comparative (Two Items) vs. Superlative (Three or More)

Use -er or "more" for comparing two items. Use -est or "most" for comparing three or more items.

✓ Comparative: Of the two students, Maria is taller.

✓ Superlative: Of all the students, Maria is tallest.

✗ Wrong: Of the two students, Maria is tallest.

→ Use comparative for two items

When to Use -er/-est vs. More/Most

One-syllable adjectives generally use -er/-est. Two-syllable adjectives vary. Three+ syllable adjectives use more/most.

One syllable: tall → taller → tallest / fast → faster → fastest

Two syllables (varies): happy → happier → happiest / careful → more careful → most careful

Three+ syllables: beautiful → more beautiful → most beautiful

Avoid Double Comparisons

Never use both -er and "more" together, or both -est and "most" together.

✗ Wrong: This is the most easiest problem.

✗ Wrong: She is more smarter than her brother.

✓ Correct: This is the easiest problem.

✓ Correct: She is smarter than her brother.

Irregular Comparatives and Superlatives

Some common adjectives have irregular comparative and superlative forms that must be memorized.

good → better → best

bad → worse → worst

much/many → more → most

little → less → least

3. Double Negatives and Negative Words

Using two negative words in the same clause creates a double negative, which is grammatically incorrect in standard English. The ACT tests whether you can identify and eliminate double negatives while maintaining the intended meaning.

Common Negative Words

Recognizing negative words helps you avoid using two in one clause.

Negative words: no, not, never, none, nothing, nobody, no one, nowhere, neither, nor, hardly, scarcely, barely

Identifying Double Negatives

When two negative words appear in the same clause, they create a double negative error.

✗ Double negative: I don't have no money.

✗ Double negative: She can't hardly wait.

✗ Double negative: They didn't do nothing wrong.

✓ Correct: I don't have any money. / I have no money.

✓ Correct: She can hardly wait. / She can't wait.

✓ Correct: They didn't do anything wrong. / They did nothing wrong.

Hardly, Scarcely, and Barely

These words already convey negative meaning, so don't use them with other negatives.

✗ Wrong: I can't barely see the board.

✗ Wrong: We didn't scarcely have time.

✓ Correct: I can barely see the board.

✓ Correct: We scarcely had time.

Quick Fix: When you spot a double negative, remove one negative word OR change the second negative to its positive counterpart (no → any, nothing → anything, nobody → anybody).

4. Idioms and Prepositional Phrases

Idioms are expressions that require specific word combinations. The ACT frequently tests prepositional idioms—phrases where only one preposition is correct. These cannot be learned through rules; you must develop an ear for what sounds right through reading and practice.

Common Prepositional Idioms

Here are frequently tested idioms where the preposition must be exact:

different from (not "than" or "to")

interested in (not "with" or "about")

independent of (not "from")

concerned about or with (both acceptable)

capable of (not "to")

responsible for (not "of")

result in or from (different meanings: "in" = cause, "from" = caused by)

agree with (person) or to (plan)

More Common Idioms

✓ superior to (not "than")

✓ prefer X to Y (not "over" or "than")

✓ regard as (not "to be")

✓ prohibit from (not "to")

✓ credit with (not "for")

✓ native to (not "of")

Between vs. Among

Use "between" for two items or when items are distinct. Use "among" for three or more items when they form a group.

✓ Between: The choice is between college and work.

✓ Among: She distributed the prizes among all the students.

Less vs. Fewer, Amount vs. Number

Use "fewer" and "number" with countable nouns. Use "less" and "amount" with uncountable nouns.

✓ Countable: Fewer students attended. / A large number of books.

✓ Uncountable: Less water is needed. / A large amount of time.

ACT Strategy: When you see an underlined preposition, ask: "Does this sound right?" Trust your ear—if a phrase sounds awkward, test the other answer choices for the standard idiom.
