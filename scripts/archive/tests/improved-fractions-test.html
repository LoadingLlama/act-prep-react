Fractions represent parts of a whole, and they're essential for the ACT Math section. In this lesson, we'll build your understanding step-by-step, starting with the basics and working up to all four operations. By the end, you'll handle any fraction problem with confidence!

Understanding Fractions: The Foundation

A fraction has two parts:

  • Numerator (top): How many parts you have
  • Denominator (bottom): How many equal parts make the whole

For example, 3/4 means "3 out of 4 equal parts."

Real-world example: If you slice a pizza into 4 equal pieces and eat 3, you've eaten 3/4 of the pizza. The numerator (3) is how many slices you ate, and the denominator (4) is the total number of slices.

Equivalent Fractions

Before we learn operations, we need to understand that fractions can look different but have the same value. If you multiply or divide both the numerator and denominator by the same number, the fraction's value stays the same.

Example: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8

This concept is crucial for adding and subtracting fractions!

Operation 1: Multiplying Fractions (Easiest!)

Multiplication is the simplest fraction operation: just multiply straight across.

Rule: (a/b) × (c/d) = (a × c)/(b × d)

Why it works: When you take "a fraction OF a fraction," you're finding a part of a part. Multiplying the numerators gives you the parts you're taking, and multiplying the denominators gives you the new total.

Example: 2/3 × 4/5

Step 1: Multiply numerators: 2 × 4 = 8

Step 2: Multiply denominators: 3 × 5 = 15

Answer: 8/15

Operation 2: Dividing Fractions

Division uses a simple trick: flip the second fraction and multiply!

Rule: (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c)

Remember: "Keep, Change, Flip"

  • Keep the first fraction the same
  • Change division to multiplication
  • Flip the second fraction (reciprocal)

Example: 2/3 ÷ 4/5

Step 1: Keep the first: 2/3

Step 2: Change ÷ to ×

Step 3: Flip the second: 5/4

Step 4: Multiply: 2/3 × 5/4 = 10/12 = 5/6

Operation 3: Adding Fractions (Needs Common Denominator)

You can only add fractions when they have the same denominator. Think of it like this: you can't add apples and oranges—you need the same "pieces."

The Challenge: 1/4 + 2/3

These have different denominators (4 and 3), so we can't add them yet. We need to convert them to equivalent fractions with the same denominator.

Step 1: Find a common denominator. We need a number that both 4 and 3 divide into evenly. The smallest is 12 (since 3 × 4 = 12).

Step 2: Convert both fractions to twelfths.

  • 1/4 = ?/12 → Multiply top and bottom by 3: (1×3)/(4×3) = 3/12
  • 2/3 = ?/12 → Multiply top and bottom by 4: (2×4)/(3×4) = 8/12

Step 3: Add the numerators. Now they have the same denominator:

3/12 + 8/12 = (3+8)/12 = 11/12

Important: The denominator stays the same when adding—only add the numerators!

Operation 4: Subtracting Fractions

Subtraction works exactly like addition—you need a common denominator first, then subtract the numerators.

Example: 5/6 - 1/4

Step 1: Find common denominator: 12

Step 2: Convert: 5/6 = 10/12 and 1/4 = 3/12

Step 3: Subtract: 10/12 - 3/12 = 7/12

Simplifying Fractions

Always simplify your answer to lowest terms by dividing both numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor (GCF).

Example: 20/120

Step 1: Find the GCF of 20 and 120 → It's 20

Step 2: Divide both by 20: (20÷20)/(120÷20) = 1/6

ACT Calculator Tip

Here's a time-saving strategy: convert fractions to decimals on your calculator!

Example: For 3/7 + 2/5, type: (3÷7) + (2÷5) = 0.829...

Then match this decimal to the answer choices. This is especially helpful for complex fractions or when you're short on time!

Example Problem

Example 1

Problem: What is 5/8 × 4/15?

A. 1/6
B. 1/3
C. 2/3
D. 9/23

Solution: Multiply numerators and denominators, then simplify.

Step 1: Multiply across: (5×4)/(8×15) = 20/120

Step 2: Simplify by dividing by GCF of 20:

20/120 = (20÷20)/(120÷20) = 1/6

Answer: A

Key Takeaway

Quick Reference:

  • Multiply: Straight across (easiest!)
  • Divide: Keep-Change-Flip, then multiply
  • Add/Subtract: Get common denominator first, then add/subtract numerators only
  • Simplify: Divide by GCF to reduce to lowest terms

Practice these operations in order from easiest to hardest. Master multiplication first, then division, and finally addition/subtraction. With repetition, you'll solve fraction problems quickly and confidently!
