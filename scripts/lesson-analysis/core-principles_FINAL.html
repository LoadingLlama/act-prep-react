The ACT Reading section tests your ability to apply seven core principles that separate high-scoring students from those who struggle. Master these principles to dramatically improve both speed and accuracy.

1. Every Answer Must Be Supported by the Passage

The most fundamental rule: every correct answer must have direct textual support.

The ACT only cares what the passage says—not what you know. If a passage claims something factually wrong, answer based on the passage. Outside knowledge is irrelevant—only passage evidence matters.

Example 1: Evidence-Based Answering

Question: "The author's primary purpose in discussing the artist's early work is to..."

Wrong approach: "I remember this artist's early work was revolutionary..." (relying on outside knowledge)

Right approach: Find where the passage discusses early work. Read exactly what it says about the purpose. Match your answer to the passage evidence only.

2. Use Process of Elimination

Crossing out wrong answers is often faster than finding the right answer directly.

Mark clearly wrong answers with an X. Narrow to 2-3 choices before deep analysis. Compare remaining choices directly.

Common Wrong Answer Types

Too extreme: Uses "always," "never," "only" when passage is nuanced.

Opposite: Says the reverse of what passage states.

Out of scope: Mentions topics passage doesn't discuss.

Distortion: Twists passage meaning slightly.

Example 2: Eliminating Wrong Answers

Passage: "Some scientists believe climate change may contribute to more frequent hurricanes."

Question: "According to the passage, the relationship between climate change and hurricanes is..."

❌ "Climate change always causes more hurricanes" (too extreme—passage says "may contribute")

❌ "Scientists have proven the connection" (opposite—passage says "believe," not "proven")

❌ "Rising ocean temperatures increase hurricane strength" (out of scope—passage doesn't mention ocean temperatures)

✓ "Climate change is thought by some to potentially increase hurricane frequency" (matches passage exactly)

3. Read Actively, Not Passively

Active reading means engaging with the text, not just scanning words.

Active Reading Techniques

Ask "Why did the author include this?" as you read. Note main idea of each paragraph mentally. Track author's tone (positive, negative, neutral). Underline key claims or transitions.

4. Answer What's Asked, Not What You Think

Many students answer the question they expect instead of the actual question.

Reread the question stem before selecting an answer. Check: "Does this answer what was asked?" Watch for: "primarily," "best," "most," "EXCEPT".

Example 3: Answering the Right Question

Question: "What does the author suggest about renewable energy adoption?"

Passage: "The slow adoption of renewable energy reflects both technological challenges and resistance from established energy industries. Despite clear environmental benefits, the transition requires significant infrastructure investment."

❌ "Renewable energy provides environmental benefits" (this answers what the passage states, not what it suggests)

✓ "Multiple barriers are slowing the transition to renewable energy" (this is the implied meaning—what the passage suggests)

5. Manage Your Time Strategically

You have 35 minutes for 4 passages (40 questions). Allocate time wisely.

Target: 8-9 minutes per passage. 3-4 minutes reading, 5-6 minutes answering. Skip difficult questions—return later if time permits. Never spend 2+ minutes on one question.

6. Use Line References to Save Time

When a question references specific lines, ALWAYS use them.

Go directly to those lines. Read 1-2 sentences before and after for context. Don't search the whole passage when given line numbers.

7. Trust the Passage Over Your Gut

Your intuition can mislead you. The passage is always right.

If an answer "sounds good" but you can't find support, it's wrong. If an answer seems weird but passage supports it, it's correct. Verify every answer against passage text.

Example 4: Passage vs. Intuition

Passage: "While solar energy reduces carbon emissions, its manufacturing process creates significant toxic waste and requires rare earth minerals extracted through environmentally damaging mining."

Question: "The passage suggests that solar energy is..."

❌ "An environmentally friendly solution to climate change" (your intuition about solar—not what passage says)

✓ "Beneficial for reducing emissions but problematic in manufacturing and sourcing" (what the passage actually says)

Key Takeaways

✓Every answer must have direct passage support—no outside knowledge

✓Eliminate wrong answers first before choosing the right one

✓Read actively by asking why the author included each part

✓Always answer what's asked, not what you expect the question to be
