The ACT Reading section includes one paired passage set consisting of two shorter passages (Passage A and Passage B) addressing related topics from different perspectives. Master systematic strategies for reading efficiently, organizing information to prevent confusion, and approaching comparison questions methodically.

1. Understanding Paired Passage Structure and Question Types

Paired passages follow a predictable structure and generate specific question types that differ from single-passage questions.

The Paired Passage Format

The ACT consistently includes one paired passage set, typically in social science or humanities:

  • Passage A: Approximately 400-450 words
  • Passage B: Approximately 400-450 words
  • Combined total: Roughly 800-900 words (slightly shorter than single full passage)
  • 10 questions total: Same as single passages

Passages address related topics but present different perspectives, focus on different aspects, or argue different positions. Common relationships:

  • Contrasting perspectives: Same topic, different conclusions
  • Complementary focuses: Related aspects (historical/contemporary, theoretical/practical)
  • Different scopes: Broad overview vs. specific case study
  • Debate or dialogue: Opposing sides of argument or controversy

Three Categories of Questions

Paired passage questions fall into three distinct categories:

  • Passage A only (typically 2-3 questions): Ask exclusively about A, can answer without reading B
    • Stems: "According to Passage A..." or "The author of Passage A suggests..."
  • Passage B only (typically 2-3 questions): Ask exclusively about B
    • Stems: "In Passage B, the author argues..." or "According to Passage B..."
  • Comparison/synthesis (typically 4-5 questions): Require understanding both passages
    • Stems: "Both passages discuss..." "Unlike Passage A, Passage B..." "Both authors would agree..."

Common Comparison Question Patterns

  • Agreement: "Both authors would most likely agree that..."
  • Disagreement: "The authors disagree about..." or "Unlike A, B believes..."
  • Perspective: "How would B author respond to A's claim in lines 15-17?"
  • Relationship: "Passage B relates to Passage A by..."
  • Shared topic: "Both passages discuss..."
  • Unique focus: "Which is discussed in A but not in B?"

Why Students Find Paired Passages Challenging

  • Information confusion—mixing up which passage said what
  • Mental fatigue—reading and comprehending two passages requires sustained attention
  • Comparison complexity—analyzing relationships is cognitively demanding
  • Time pressure perception—two passages in 9 minutes creates anxiety
  • Reading inefficiency—reading both completely then having to reread extensively

2. The Strategic Reading Approach for Paired Passages

Unlike single passages where you read straight through, paired passages benefit from a strategic approach that organizes information and prevents confusion.

The Sequential Method: Read A, Answer A, Read B, Answer All

Most effective strategy for most students:

  • Step 1: Read Passage A completely with active reading strategies
  • Step 2: Answer all Passage A only questions (skip comparison questions)
  • Step 3: Read Passage B completely, noting how B relates to A
  • Step 4: Answer Passage B only questions, then tackle comparison questions

Advantages: Not holding both passages in memory simultaneously (reduces cognitive load), answer while passage details are fresh, read B with A in mind (makes comparison easier).

Active Notation to Prevent Confusion

Biggest challenge: remembering which passage said what. Combat through systematic notation:

  • Label key ideas with A or B—mark significant points to track source
  • Note main idea of each passage separately—quick phrase capturing central point
  • Mark points of clear agreement or disagreement
  • Highlight unique topics—if A discusses something B never mentions, mark as unique to A
  • Use different marking symbols—straight underlines for A, wavy for B

Identifying the Relationship While Reading

As you read Passage B, actively identify the relationship rather than treating them as independent texts. Ask:

  • Do they agree or disagree on main point?
  • Do they focus on different aspects of same topic?
  • Does one provide example or case study of what the other discusses generally?
  • Do they represent different perspectives or stakeholders?

By the time you finish B, you should articulate in one sentence how they relate: "A argues for X while B argues against X" or "Both support X but emphasize different reasons."

Example 1: Sequential Method in Action

Step 1 - Read Passage A: Discussion of how social media platforms affect political discourse, arguing they increase polarization.

  • Note main idea: "Social media increases polarization"
  • Mark key evidence with "A"

Step 2 - Answer Passage A Questions: Questions 21-23 ask about A only. Answer while A is fresh (takes ~2 minutes).

Step 3 - Read Passage B: Same topic but arguing social media can promote diverse viewpoints if used thoughtfully.

  • While reading, note: "B disagrees with A—says social media can reduce polarization with right approach"
  • Mark key evidence with "B"

Step 4 - Answer Remaining Questions: Questions 24-26 ask about B only—answer these. Questions 27-30 are comparison questions. Now you've engaged deeply with both and can tackle efficiently.

Advantage: Never holding both full passages in working memory simultaneously; answering while information is fresh; building understanding progressively.

3. Strategies for Comparison Questions

Comparison questions require synthesizing information from both passages and understanding their relationships. Specific strategies help you answer efficiently and accurately.

Agreement Questions: Finding Common Ground

When questions ask "Both authors would agree that..." or "Both passages discuss...", identify claims supported by both passages despite any differences.

Strategy:

  • Evaluate each answer against both passages—both must support it
  • Look for foundational agreements—even when disagreeing on conclusions, often agree on basic facts
  • Distinguish between agreement and emphasis—both might mention without equal emphasis
  • Beware partial agreement—wrong answers include claims one strongly supports but other only weakly mentions
  • Verify by pointing to specific locations in both passages

Disagreement Questions: Identifying Contrasts

Questions asking "The authors disagree about..." or "Unlike Passage A, Passage B..." require identifying genuine differences in position or emphasis.

Strategy:

  • Distinguish between disagreement and different focus—true disagreement means opposing positions on same issue
  • Look for explicit contrasts—clearest disagreements involve passages making opposite claims about same specific issue
  • Verify both sides—confirm A actually supports one position and B supports the opposite
  • Check for silence vs. opposition—if A discusses something B doesn't mention, that's not disagreement

Example: If A says "Policy X successfully reduced unemployment" and B says "Policy X failed to reduce unemployment," that's clear disagreement. But if A discusses unemployment while B discusses inflation, that's different focus, not disagreement.

Response Questions: Predicting Reactions

Questions like "How would the author of Passage B respond to the claim in Passage A?" require predicting reactions based on actual passage content.

Strategy:

  • Identify specific claim in referenced lines—what exactly is A saying?
  • Determine B author's relevant position—what does B say about this topic?
  • Predict response type—agree, disagree, qualify/nuance, or provide additional evidence?
  • Match tone and strength—if B takes strong positions, response should reflect that strength
  • Base responses on actual B content—don't speculate about what author might believe

Relationship Questions: Describing How Passages Connect

Questions asking "Passage B relates to Passage A by..." or "Compared to Passage A, Passage B..." require understanding structural or rhetorical relationship. Common relationships:

  • Opposing arguments: Opposite positions on debatable issue
  • Supporting through example: One provides specific example of general principle
  • Extending or elaborating: One provides additional information building on the other
  • Contrasting perspectives: Different viewpoints on same phenomenon
  • Problem and solution: One describes problem, other proposes solutions

Example 2: Comparison Question Types

Context: Passage A argues standardized testing improves educational accountability; Passage B argues standardized testing narrows curriculum and harms learning.

Agreement question: "Both authors would agree that..."

  • ❌ "Standardized testing has no place in education" (only B would agree)
  • ❌ "Standardized testing effectively measures student learning" (only A would agree)
  • ✓ "Standardized testing has significant effects on educational practices" (both discuss major impacts, even though they evaluate differently)

Disagreement question: "The authors disagree about..."

  • ✓ "Whether standardized testing ultimately benefits students" (A says yes, B says no—clear opposition)

Response question: "How would B author respond to A's claim that testing improves accountability?"

  • ❌ "By agreeing that accountability is important" (doesn't address testing claim)
  • ✓ "By arguing that accountability comes at the cost of educational quality" (acknowledges A's point but counters with B's concern)

4. Time Management and Advanced Tactics

Paired passages present unique time management challenges. Advanced strategies help you work efficiently without sacrificing accuracy.

Optimal Time Allocation

For paired passages, target approximately 8-9 minutes total (similar to single passages):

  • 2-2.5 minutes: Reading Passage A (shorter than full passages)
  • 1.5-2 minutes: Answering Passage A questions (typically 2-3 questions)
  • 2-2.5 minutes: Reading Passage B (noting relationships to A)
  • 2.5-3 minutes: Answering B and comparison questions (typically 5-7 remaining)

Paired passages shouldn't take significantly more time than single passages because total word count is similar.

Efficient Information Retrieval for Comparison Questions

Comparison questions often require verifying information in both passages. Strategies for efficient retrieval:

  • Use your margin notes—if you labeled key ideas with A or B, quickly locate without rereading
  • Remember the structure—each passage's main argument typically appears early (first 1-2 paragraphs)
  • Use process of elimination—if certain A doesn't support answer, eliminate without checking B
  • Leverage earlier questions—single-passage questions already answered reinforce understanding

When to Skip and Return

Some comparison questions are more difficult and time-consuming. Strategic skipping improves overall performance:

  • Skip questions requiring extensive passage searching—if requires rereading multiple paragraphs in both, skip temporarily
  • Skip questions where uncertain after reasonable effort—if thought about 45+ seconds and can't decide, guess, mark for review, move on
  • Return to skipped questions if time permits—after completing all others, may have 1-2 minutes remaining
  • Don't skip questions where making progress—if methodically working through and narrowing down, continue

Building Paired Passage Stamina

Paired passages require sustained focus to track two texts and analyze relationships. Building stamina through practice improves performance:

  • Practice paired passages specifically—regularly work through sets in official ACT materials
  • Analyze your errors—determine whether from misunderstanding one passage, confusing which said what, or misidentifying relationship
  • Time yourself—practice completing sets within 8-9 minutes
  • Experiment with notation systems—try different ways to find what works best
  • Review successful attempts—when you do well, reflect on what worked to replicate strategies

Example 3: Time Management in Practice

Paired Passage Timeline (9 minutes total):

0:00-2:15 - Read Passage A (450 words), noting main argument: "Urban gardens improve community health." Mark key evidence with "A" in margin.

2:15-3:45 - Answer Questions 11-13 (Passage A only). Q11: Main idea of A. Q12: Detail from A. Q13: Author's purpose in A. (1.5 minutes for 3 questions)

3:45-6:00 - Read Passage B (450 words), noting it discusses urban gardens but emphasizes environmental benefits over health benefits. Note: "B agrees gardens good but different focus—environment not health." Mark key evidence with "B."

6:00-7:15 - Answer Questions 14-16 (Passage B only). Q14: Main point of B. Q15: What B suggests. Q16: Detail from B. (1.25 minutes for 3 questions)

7:15-9:00 - Answer Questions 17-20 (Comparison). Q17: Both passages discuss... Q18: Unlike A, B emphasizes... Q19: How would B author respond to A's health claims? Q20: Passages relate by... (1.75 minutes for 4 questions)

Total: 9 minutes for 10 questions. Efficient notation and sequential method prevented confusion and excessive rereading.
