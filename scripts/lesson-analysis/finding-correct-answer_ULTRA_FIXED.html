Three essential strategies help you find correct answers consistently: prediction before looking at choices, matching technique, and comparison methodology. Master these to avoid traps and improve accuracy under time pressure.

1. Strategy 1: Predict Before Looking at Choices

Formulate your own answer before reading the multiple-choice options.

Why Prediction Works

  • Prevents confirmation bias—accepting answers that sound good without verifying
  • Anchors you to passage content before seeing misleading options
  • Builds a mental filter for evaluating choices objectively

How to Predict

  • Read the question carefully
  • Locate the relevant passage section
  • Formulate your answer in your own words
  • Find the choice that best matches (wording will differ)

When Prediction Works Best

  • Detail questions: "According to the passage..."
  • Cause-effect questions: "The author suggests that X resulted in..."
  • Vocabulary questions: "As used in line 42, 'revolutionary' most nearly means..."

Example 1: Prediction in Action

Passage excerpt: "Seeking formal training unavailable in his rural hometown, he relocated to Paris in 1890."

Question: "The passage indicates that the primary reason for the artist's move to Paris was to..."

Without prediction:

  • "All these sound reasonable. Paris was a cultural center, so maybe that's why?"

With prediction:

  • You predict: "to get better art education"
  • You find: "receive professional instruction in painting techniques"
  • ✓ Perfect match—select with confidence

2. Strategy 2: Use the Matching Technique

Compare each answer choice to the passage to find the one that accurately reflects the text.

Understanding Paraphrasing

Correct answers rarely use exact passage words—they paraphrase using synonyms and restructured sentences.

  • "Extremely difficult" → "very challenging"
  • "Rapidly increased" → "grew quickly"
  • Focus on semantic equivalence, not word-for-word matching

Key Elements to Verify

  • Subject/actor: Who is performing the action?
  • Action/verb: What is happening?
  • Cause and effect: What led to what?
  • Timing: When did it occur?
  • Degree: How much or to what extent?

Example 2: Matching Technique

Passage (lines 45-47): "The scientist's innovative methodology revolutionized data collection, enabling researchers to gather information with unprecedented accuracy and speed."

Question: "According to lines 45-47, the scientist's new method..."

  • ✓ "Significantly improved the precision and efficiency of information gathering"
    • Paraphrases "unprecedented accuracy and speed"
    • Paraphrases "revolutionized data collection"
  • ❌ "Was the first method ever used to collect data"
    • Distorts "innovative" into extreme claim
    • Not supported by text

3. Strategy 3: Compare Close Answers

When narrowed to 2-3 choices, identify the critical difference and determine which the passage supports.

Finding the Critical Difference

The critical difference is the specific distinction that determines which answer is correct:

  • Degree: "some" vs. "many" vs. "all"
  • Tone: "criticized" vs. "questioned" vs. "praised"
  • Scope: "the first paragraph" vs. "the entire passage"
  • Timing: "before" vs. "after" vs. "during"

Example 3: Comparison Methodology

You've eliminated A and B. Comparing remaining choices:

Choice C: "The researcher's findings challenged previous assumptions."

Choice D: "The researcher's findings completely disproved previous assumptions."

Critical difference: "challenged" vs. "completely disproved"

Passage says: "These results called into question several long-held beliefs, though further research would be needed to definitively resolve the matter."

  • ✓ Choice C: "Called into question" = "challenged"
  • ❌ Choice D: "Further research needed" contradicts "completely disproved"

The "More Complete" Answer

When both answers are accurate, choose the more complete one (but don't add information not in the passage).

4. Combining All Three Strategies

Use these strategies together for maximum effectiveness.

The Integrated Approach

  • Step 1: Predict your answer
  • Step 2: Match choices to your prediction and passage
  • Step 3: Compare close answers if needed

Adapting to Question Types

  • Detail questions: Prediction → Matching
  • Main idea questions: Matching (less prediction)
  • Inference questions: All three (often need comparison)
  • Vocabulary questions: Prediction → Matching

Example 4: Full Integration

Question: "The passage indicates that the scientist's most significant contribution was..."

Step 1 - Predict: "Creating a vaccine" (passage emphasized vaccine development)

Step 2 - Match: Choice A says "developing an immunization treatment" (vaccine = immunization)

Step 3 - Verify: Passage: "Her groundbreaking vaccine represented the pinnacle of her career"

Answer: A (Total time: 35 seconds)

Building Speed Through Practice

  • Start untimed—focus on accuracy first
  • Once you hit 90%+ accuracy, add timing
  • After 4-6 weeks, strategies become automatic
  • Your speed will actually increase (fewer mistakes = less rethinking)

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Predict your answer before looking at choices to avoid confirmation bias
  • ✓Prediction works best for detail, cause-effect, and vocabulary questions
  • ✓Correct answers paraphrase—look for similar meaning, not identical words
  • ✓Verify subject/actor, action/verb, cause-effect, timing, and degree when matching
  • ✓Identify the critical difference when comparing close answers
  • ✓Critical differences are often degree, tone, scope, or timing
  • ✓Choose the "best" answer—the one most strongly supported by the passage
  • ✓Combine all three: predict, match, then compare if needed
  • ✓Practice until automatic—accuracy first, then speed
