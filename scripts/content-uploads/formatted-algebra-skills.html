Many ACT questions test your core algebra skills. To answer these questions correctly, you need to isolate variables, solve equations, and manipulate expressions with confidence. Take your time, write out each step, and use your calculator to avoid silly mistakes like mental math errors or forgotten negative signs.

1. PEMDAS (Order of Operations)

In algebra, order of operations is critical. PEMDAS is an acronym that helps you remember the correct sequence:

  • P – Parentheses: Complete calculations inside parentheses first
  • E – Exponents: Next, complete exponents and square roots
  • MD – Multiplication and Division: Work left-to-right
  • AS – Addition and Subtraction: Work left-to-right

The most common mistake: students forget to work left-to-right during multiplication and division. This is where the ACT loves to trick you.

  • Example: 16 ÷ 4 × 2 − 3
    • Correct: 16 ÷ 4 × 2 − 3 = 4 × 2 − 3 = 8 − 3 = 5
    • Incorrect (common error): 16 ÷ 4 × 2 − 3 = 16 ÷ 8 − 3 = 2 − 3 = −1

Example 1: Order of Operations

What is the value of the expression 22 + (−4)² ÷ 2 × (5 + 10)?

A. 285
B. 142
C. 120
D. 72

Solution:

  • Parentheses: 22 + (−4)² ÷ 2 × 15
  • Exponents: 22 + 16 ÷ 2 × 15
  • Multiply/Divide (left-to-right): 22 + 16 ÷ 2 × 15 = 22 + 8 × 15 = 22 + 120
  • Addition: 22 + 120 = 142

Answer: B

2. Negative Numbers

The ACT really loves questions with negative numbers. These are prime opportunities for simple mistakes. Let's review the most common pitfalls.

Subtracting Negative Numbers

When subtracting a negative, the two negatives become a positive. The key mistake: forgetting to distribute the negative sign to all terms.

  • Example: 5x − (2x − 5)
    • Incorrect: 5x − 2x − 5 = 3x − 5
    • Correct: 5x − 2x − (−5) = 5x − 2x + 5 = 3x + 5

Negative Numbers and Exponents

−2² is NOT the same as (−2)²

  • −2² = −4 (square 2 first, then apply negative sign)
  • (−2)² = 4 (square the entire negative number)
  • Always use parentheses when entering negative numbers into your calculator!

Example 2: Distributing Negatives

Which of the following expressions is equivalent to 2(a + b) − 3(a − 4b)?

A. −a + 14b
B. −a + 6b
C. −a − 3b
D. −a − 9b

Solution:

  • Distribute: 2a + 2b − 3a − 3(−4b)
  • Simplify: 2a + 2b − 3a + 12b
  • Combine like terms: −a + 14b

Answer: A

3. Combining Like Terms

Whenever possible, combine like terms to simplify equations. This speeds up your solution and reduces errors.

Example 3: Solving with Like Terms

If 2(3x − 6) + 3y = 6x − y + 8, what is the value of y?

A. 5
B. 3
C. 1
D. −1

Solution:

  • Distribute: 6x − 12 + 3y = 6x − y + 8
  • The 6x terms cancel: −12 + 3y = −y + 8
  • Add y and 12 to both sides: 4y = 20
  • Divide by 4: y = 5

Answer: A

4. Cross Multiplying Fractions

When two fractions are equal, cross multiply to eliminate the fractions.

If x/y = z/w, then xw = yz

Example 4: Cross Multiplication

If x/4 = 6/7, what is the value of x?

A. 14/3
B. 24/7
C. 24/7
D. 3

Solution:

  • Cross multiply: 7x = 24
  • Divide by 7: x = 24/7

Answer: B (or C, since they're the same)

5. Squaring Both Sides Correctly

When working with square roots, you often need to square both sides. The critical rule: square the entire side, not individual terms.

  • Correct: (√x)² = (x − 2)²
  • Incorrect: x = x² − 4
  • Always check for extraneous solutions by plugging answers back into the original equation

Example 5: Square Root Equations

What value(s) of x solve the equation √x = x − 2?

A. 1
B. 4
C. 2
D. 1, 4

Solution:

  • Square both sides: x = (x − 2)(x − 2) = x² − 4x + 4
  • Rearrange: 0 = x² − 5x + 4
  • Factor: 0 = (x − 4)(x − 1), so x = 1 or x = 4
  • Check x = 1: √1 = 1 − 2 → 1 = −1 ✗ (extraneous)
  • Check x = 4: √4 = 4 − 2 → 2 = 2 ✓

Answer: B

6. Algebra with Inequality Signs

Treat inequalities like regular equations, but remember one critical rule:

When multiplying or dividing by a negative number,
flip the direction of the inequality sign
  • Example: −5x − 3 > 11
    • −5x > 14
    • x < −14/5 (flipped when dividing by −5)

Example 6: Inequality with Variables

Which of the following inequalities is equivalent to 2x − 6y < 4x − 14?

A. x > 6y − 14
B. x > 3y − 7
C. x < −3y + 7
D. x > −3y + 7

Solution:

  • Subtract 4x from both sides and add 6y: 2x − 4x < 6y − 14
  • Simplify: −2x < 6y − 14
  • Divide by −2 (flip sign): x > (6y − 14)/(−2)
  • Simplify: x > −3y + 7

Answer: D

7. Taking Square Roots of Variables

When taking the square root of a squared variable, you must account for both positive and negative solutions.

If x² = 9, then x = ±3
Not just x = 3!

Important: This only applies to variables. The square root of just a number is always positive: √9 = 3 (not ±3).

Example 7: Square Root with Variables

What value(s) of x are solutions to the equation x² + 10 = 46?

A. −6
B. 6
C. 0, 6
D. −6, 6

Solution:

  • Subtract 10: x² = 36
  • Take square root: √x² = ±√36
  • x = ±6, so x = −6 or x = 6

Answer: D

8. ACT Strategy Tips

Tip 1: Use Your Calculator

  • Your calculator follows PEMDAS automatically
    • Enter complex expressions directly
    • Always use parentheses for negative numbers
    • Double-check you entered the expression correctly

Tip 2: Write Out Every Step

  • Don't try to do multiple steps in your head
    • Write each line of algebra clearly
    • This prevents sign errors and dropped terms
    • Makes it easy to find mistakes if you need to check

Tip 3: Backsolve When Possible

  • For equations with numerical answer choices:
    • Plug each answer choice into the original equation
    • Find which one makes the equation true
    • Often faster and more accurate than solving algebraically

Key Takeaways

  • ✓PEMDAS: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiply/Divide (left-to-right), Add/Subtract (left-to-right)
  • ✓Distribute negative signs to ALL terms: 5x − (2x − 5) = 5x − 2x + 5
  • ✓−2² ≠ (−2)²: Use parentheses for negative numbers with exponents
  • ✓When multiplying/dividing inequalities by negatives, flip the sign
  • ✓Square entire sides, not individual terms: (√x)² = (x − 2)²
  • ✓Taking square root of x² gives ±value: if x² = 9, then x = ±3
