Understanding angles and lines is fundamental to success on the ACT Math section. These concepts appear in approximately 6-8 questions on every test, making them one of the highest-yield topics to master. Whether dealing with parallel lines, triangles, or polygons, a solid grasp of angle relationships will save you time and boost your score.

1. Intersecting Lines and Angle Relationships

When two lines intersect, they create four angles. Understanding the relationships between these angles is crucial for solving ACT geometry problems quickly.

  • Vertical angles are equal:
    • Opposite angles formed by intersecting lines are always congruent
    • If one angle is 50°, the angle across from it is also 50°
  • Adjacent angles are supplementary:
    • Angles that share a side add up to 180°
    • If you know one angle, you can find all the others
  • All four angles around a point sum to 360°

2. Parallel Lines Cut by a Transversal

When parallel lines are intersected by another line (called a transversal), special angle relationships emerge. The ACT loves testing these relationships because many students find the terminology confusing.

The Simple Rule

  • Instead of memorizing terms like "alternate interior angles" and "corresponding angles," remember this: When two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, you get two sets of identical angles
    • Four angles will be equal to each other (the "small" angles)
    • Four angles will be equal to each other (the "large" angles)
    • Any "small" angle + any "large" angle = 180°

Quick Identification

  • On the ACT, if you see two parallel lines with a third line crossing them:
    • Find any angle that you know
    • All acute angles are equal to each other
    • All obtuse angles are equal to each other
    • Each acute angle + each obtuse angle = 180°

3. Triangle Angle Relationships

The most important rule about triangles: the sum of interior angles in any triangle is always 180°. This rule appears on almost every ACT test.

Special Triangle Types

  • Isosceles triangles have two equal sides and two equal angles
    • The angles opposite the equal sides are equal
    • If two angles are marked equal, the sides opposite them are also equal
  • Equilateral triangles have three equal sides and three 60° angles
  • Right triangles have one 90° angle
    • The other two angles must sum to 90°
    • Common right triangle angle pairs: 30°-60°, 45°-45°

Example 1: Triangle with Parallel Lines

In the figure below, line AB is parallel to line CD. A triangle is formed with angles 34° and 90°. If x° is the alternate interior angle to the third angle of the triangle, what is x?

A. 34
B. 40
C. 48
D. 56
E. 62

Solution:

  • Find the third angle in the triangle: 180° − 34° − 90° = 56°
  • Since x and the third angle are alternate interior angles (and the lines are parallel), they must be equal: x = 56°

Answer: D

4. Interior Angles of Polygons

The ACT frequently tests your ability to find the sum of interior angles in polygons. While you should memorize common polygons, there's one formula that works for all of them:

Sum of Interior Angles = 180°(n − 2)
where n = number of sides

Common Polygons to Memorize

  • Triangle (3 sides): 180°
  • Quadrilateral (4 sides): 360°
    • Includes squares, rectangles, parallelograms, trapezoids
  • Pentagon (5 sides): 540°
  • Hexagon (6 sides): 720°

Example 2: Pentagon with Variable Angles

A pentagon has angles measuring x°, 1.3x°, 1.4x°, 1.2x°, and 1.1x°. What is the value of x?

A. 72
B. 88
C. 90
D. 105
E. 120

Solution:

  • Use the formula: Sum = 180°(5 − 2) = 180°(3) = 540°
  • Set up equation: x° + 1.3x° + 1.4x° + 1.2x° + 1.1x° = 540°
  • Combine like terms: 6x° = 540°, so x = 90

Answer: C

5. ACT Strategy Tips

Here are three powerful strategies to use on angle problems:

Tip 1: Mark Up Your Test

  • When you find one angle, immediately mark any equal angles in the figure
    • Label vertical angles with the same value
    • Mark corresponding angles when you see parallel lines
    • Write supplementary angle values next to each other

Tip 2: Trust the Figure (Usually)

  • All ACT figures are drawn to scale unless they explicitly state "Note: Figure not drawn to scale"
    • You can estimate angles and eliminate obviously wrong answers
    • If an angle looks obtuse, it's probably greater than 90°
    • Use your pencil to compare angle sizes visually

Tip 3: Extend Parallel Lines

  • When parallel lines don't extend all the way across the figure:
    • Use your pencil to extend them through the entire diagram
    • This makes it much easier to identify corresponding and alternate angles
    • Works especially well with parallelograms and trapezoids
