Mastering word problems is essential for ACT Math success. This lesson covers rate problems (distance = rate × time), work problems (combining work rates), mixture problems (combining solutions), and age problems. Word problems appear in 8-12 questions per ACT test—that's 13-20% of all math questions! The key to success is translating English into equations, identifying the right approach, and organizing information systematically. Understanding common problem types and solution strategies will dramatically improve your speed and accuracy.

1. Distance-Rate-Time Problems

Distance-rate-time problems use the fundamental formula d = rt (distance equals rate times time). These problems involve objects traveling at constant speeds, meeting at specific times, or covering certain distances. Common variations include same direction problems (one object catches another), opposite direction problems (objects meet), and round-trip problems (different speeds each way). The key is setting up equations that relate the distances, rates, and times for each object or leg of the journey.

The Basic Formula and Variations

Fundamental formula: d = rt
Can be rearranged: r = d/t or t = d/r

Example 1: A car travels 180 miles in 3 hours. What is its average speed?

Given: d = 180 miles, t = 3 hours
Find: r
Formula: r = d/t = 180/3 = 60 mph

Example 2: How long does it take to travel 240 miles at 60 mph?

Given: d = 240 miles, r = 60 mph
Find: t
Formula: t = d/r = 240/60 = 4 hours

Example 3: How far can you travel in 2.5 hours at 55 mph?

Given: r = 55 mph, t = 2.5 hours
Find: d
Formula: d = rt = 55 × 2.5 = 137.5 miles

Opposite Direction Problems (Meeting)

Problem: Two trains leave stations 450 miles apart at the same time, traveling toward each other. One travels at 60 mph, the other at 75 mph. When do they meet?

Setup:
Let t = time until they meet (same for both trains)
Train 1 distance: d₁ = 60t
Train 2 distance: d₂ = 75t
Total distance: d₁ + d₂ = 450

Equation:
60t + 75t = 450
135t = 450
t = 450/135 = 10/3 hours = 3 hours 20 minutes

Verification:
Train 1: 60 × (10/3) = 200 miles
Train 2: 75 × (10/3) = 250 miles
Total: 200 + 250 = 450 ✓

Same Direction Problems (Catching Up)

Problem: A car leaves at 8 AM traveling at 50 mph. Two hours later, another car follows at 70 mph. When does the second car catch up?

Setup:
Let t = time the second car travels
First car travels for (t + 2) hours

When they meet, distances are equal:
50(t + 2) = 70t
50t + 100 = 70t
100 = 20t
t = 5 hours

The second car catches up 5 hours after it starts (7 hours after the first car left)

Verification:
First car: 50 × 7 = 350 miles
Second car: 70 × 5 = 350 miles ✓

Round-Trip Problems

Problem: A cyclist rides to a destination at 15 mph and returns by the same route at 10 mph. If the total trip takes 5 hours, how far is the destination?

Setup:
Let d = one-way distance
Time going: t₁ = d/15
Time returning: t₂ = d/10
Total time: t₁ + t₂ = 5

Equation:
d/15 + d/10 = 5

Find common denominator (30):
2d/30 + 3d/30 = 5
5d/30 = 5
d/6 = 5
d = 30 miles

Verification:
Going: 30/15 = 2 hours
Returning: 30/10 = 3 hours
Total: 2 + 3 = 5 hours ✓

ACT Example 1: Rate Problems

Two cities are 300 miles apart. A bus leaves one city at noon traveling toward the other at 50 mph. At 1 PM, a car leaves the second city traveling toward the first at 70 mph. At what time do they meet?

A) 2:00 PM

B) 2:30 PM

C) 3:00 PM

D) 3:30 PM

E) 4:00 PM

Solution:

Let t = hours car travels (bus travels t + 1 hours)

Bus distance: 50(t + 1)

Car distance: 70t

50(t + 1) + 70t = 300

50t + 50 + 70t = 300

120t = 250 → t = 2.5 hours after 1 PM = 3:30 PM

Answer: D — Account for the 1-hour head start!

2. Work Problems

Work problems involve combining work rates to determine how long tasks take. The key principle is that work rate = 1/time to complete job. When workers combine efforts, their rates add. These problems often involve filling pools, painting rooms, or completing projects. Understanding that work = rate × time allows you to set up equations relating individual and combined work rates.

Basic Work Rate Formula

If someone can complete a job in t hours:
Work rate = 1/t (of the job per hour)

Combined rate formula:
If Person A's rate = 1/a and Person B's rate = 1/b,
Combined rate = 1/a + 1/b

Example: Painter A can paint a room in 6 hours. Painter B can paint the same room in 4 hours. Working together, how long will it take?

Step 1: Find individual rates
Painter A rate = 1/6 room per hour
Painter B rate = 1/4 room per hour

Step 2: Add rates
Combined rate = 1/6 + 1/4 = 2/12 + 3/12 = 5/12 room per hour

Step 3: Find time
Time = 1 / (5/12) = 12/5 = 2.4 hours = 2 hours 24 minutes

Verification:
Painter A does: (1/6) × (12/5) = 12/30 = 2/5 of job
Painter B does: (1/4) × (12/5) = 12/20 = 3/5 of job
Total: 2/5 + 3/5 = 1 complete job ✓

Working Together to Complete a Job

Problem: Pipe A can fill a pool in 10 hours. Pipe B can fill it in 15 hours. If both pipes work together, how long to fill the pool?

Method 1: Using rates
Pipe A rate: 1/10 pool per hour
Pipe B rate: 1/15 pool per hour
Combined rate: 1/10 + 1/15 = 3/30 + 2/30 = 5/30 = 1/6 pool per hour
Time = 1 / (1/6) = 6 hours

Method 2: Using equation
Let t = time working together
(Work by A) + (Work by B) = 1 complete job
t/10 + t/15 = 1
3t/30 + 2t/30 = 1
5t/30 = 1
t = 6 hours

Finding Individual Rates

Problem: Working together, two machines can complete a job in 4 hours. If the faster machine working alone takes 6 hours, how long does the slower machine take alone?

Setup:
Let t = time for slower machine alone
Faster machine rate: 1/6 job per hour
Slower machine rate: 1/t job per hour
Combined rate: 1/4 job per hour

Equation:
1/6 + 1/t = 1/4
1/t = 1/4 - 1/6
1/t = 3/12 - 2/12 = 1/12
t = 12 hours

The slower machine takes 12 hours alone

Work Problems with Drains/Removal

Problem: A pipe can fill a tank in 8 hours. A drain can empty the full tank in 12 hours. If both are open, how long to fill the empty tank?

Setup:
Fill rate: +1/8 tank per hour
Drain rate: -1/12 tank per hour (negative because it empties)
Net rate: 1/8 - 1/12 = 3/24 - 2/24 = 1/24 tank per hour

Time to fill:
Time = 1 / (1/24) = 24 hours

Interpretation: The drain slows the filling process significantly!

ACT Example 2: Work Problems

Machine A can produce 100 widgets in 5 hours. Machine B can produce 100 widgets in 4 hours. Working together, how many hours will it take to produce 100 widgets?

F) 1.5

G) 2

H) 2.22

J) 2.5

K) 4.5

Solution:

Machine A rate: 1/5 job per hour

Machine B rate: 1/4 job per hour

Combined: 1/5 + 1/4 = 4/20 + 5/20 = 9/20 per hour

Time = 1 / (9/20) = 20/9 ≈ 2.22 hours

Answer: H — Add rates, then take reciprocal!

3. Mixture Problems

Mixture problems involve combining substances with different concentrations or prices. Common types include solution mixing (combining liquids with different concentrations), coin problems (combining different denominations), and price averaging (blending items at different costs). The key principle is that total amount × average concentration = sum of (individual amount × concentration). Setting up tables to organize information makes these problems much more manageable.

Solution Mixture Problems

Problem: How much pure water must be added to 10 liters of 20% salt solution to make a 15% salt solution?

Setup table:
               Amount  Concentration  Salt
Original:   10 L      20%           2 L
Added:     x L       0%            0 L
Final:     (10+x)   15%           ?

Key insight: Amount of salt stays constant (only water is added)
Salt in final solution = 2 L

Equation:
0.15(10 + x) = 2
1.5 + 0.15x = 2
0.15x = 0.5
x = 0.5 / 0.15 = 10/3 ≈ 3.33 liters

Add 10/3 liters of pure water

Combining Two Solutions

Problem: A chemist mixes a 30% acid solution with a 10% acid solution to make 20 liters of 18% acid solution. How much of each is needed?

Setup:
Let x = liters of 30% solution
Then (20 - x) = liters of 10% solution

Equation (acid amounts):
0.30x + 0.10(20 - x) = 0.18(20)
0.30x + 2 - 0.10x = 3.6
0.20x + 2 = 3.6
0.20x = 1.6
x = 8 liters

Answer:
8 liters of 30% solution
12 liters of 10% solution

Verification:
Acid from 30%: 0.30(8) = 2.4 L
Acid from 10%: 0.10(12) = 1.2 L
Total acid: 2.4 + 1.2 = 3.6 L
Percentage: 3.6/20 = 0.18 = 18% ✓

Coin and Money Problems

Problem: A jar contains $4.50 in quarters and dimes. There are 24 coins total. How many of each?

Setup:
Let q = number of quarters
Let d = number of dimes

Equations:
q + d = 24 (total coins)
0.25q + 0.10d = 4.50 (total value)

Solve:
From first equation: d = 24 - q
Substitute into second: 0.25q + 0.10(24 - q) = 4.50
0.25q + 2.4 - 0.10q = 4.50
0.15q = 2.1
q = 14 quarters
d = 24 - 14 = 10 dimes

Verification:
Value: 14($0.25) + 10($0.10) = $3.50 + $1.00 = $4.50 ✓

Price Averaging Problems

Problem: A store owner mixes coffee worth $8 per pound with coffee worth $5 per pound to make 30 pounds of a blend worth $6.50 per pound. How many pounds of each type?

Setup:
Let x = pounds of $8 coffee
Then (30 - x) = pounds of $5 coffee

Equation (total value):
8x + 5(30 - x) = 6.50(30)
8x + 150 - 5x = 195
3x = 45
x = 15 pounds of $8 coffee
30 - 15 = 15 pounds of $5 coffee

Verification:
Value: 15($8) + 15($5) = $120 + $75 = $195
Average: $195 / 30 = $6.50 per pound ✓

ACT Example 3: Mixture Problems

A 25% alcohol solution is mixed with a 45% alcohol solution to produce 10 liters of 35% solution. How many liters of the 25% solution are needed?

A) 2.5

B) 4

C) 5

D) 6

E) 7.5

Solution:

Let x = liters of 25% solution, (10-x) = liters of 45% solution

0.25x + 0.45(10-x) = 0.35(10)

0.25x + 4.5 - 0.45x = 3.5

-0.20x = -1 → x = 5 liters

Answer: C — Equal amounts when target is halfway between!

4. Age and Other Classic Word Problems

Age problems involve relationships between people's ages at different times. The key is that everyone ages at the same rate—if 5 years pass, everyone is 5 years older. Other classic types include number problems (finding numbers with specific digit relationships), consecutive integer problems, and geometry word problems. The strategy for all word problems is: read carefully, identify what you're finding, define variables clearly, translate words to equations, solve, and verify your answer makes sense.

Age Problems

Problem: Sarah is currently 3 times as old as her daughter. In 10 years, she will be twice as old as her daughter. How old are they now?

Setup:
Let d = daughter's current age
Then 3d = Sarah's current age

In 10 years:
Daughter: d + 10
Sarah: 3d + 10

Equation:
3d + 10 = 2(d + 10)
3d + 10 = 2d + 20
d = 10 years old (daughter now)
3d = 30 years old (Sarah now)

Verification:
Now: Sarah (30) is 3 times daughter (10) ✓
In 10 years: Sarah (40) is 2 times daughter (20) ✓

Consecutive Integer Problems

Problem: The sum of three consecutive integers is 75. Find the integers.

Setup:
Let x = first integer
Then x + 1 = second integer
And x + 2 = third integer

Equation:
x + (x + 1) + (x + 2) = 75
3x + 3 = 75
3x = 72
x = 24

The three integers are 24, 25, 26

Consecutive even integers: x, x+2, x+4
Consecutive odd integers: x, x+2, x+4 (same as even!)

Number and Digit Problems

Problem: A two-digit number is 4 times the sum of its digits. The tens digit is 2 more than the units digit. Find the number.

Setup:
Let u = units digit
Then u + 2 = tens digit
Number value: 10(u + 2) + u = 10u + 20 + u = 11u + 20

First condition:
Number = 4 × (sum of digits)
11u + 20 = 4[(u + 2) + u]
11u + 20 = 4(2u + 2)
11u + 20 = 8u + 8
3u = -12
u = -4 (not valid for a digit!)

Let's try: tens digit = u, units digit = u - 2
Number: 10u + (u - 2) = 11u - 2
11u - 2 = 4[u + (u - 2)]
11u - 2 = 4(2u - 2)
11u - 2 = 8u - 8
3u = -6
u = -2 (still not valid!)

Correct setup: Let u = units digit, t = tens digit
t = u + 2
10t + u = 4(t + u)
10(u + 2) + u = 4(u + 2 + u)
10u + 20 + u = 4(2u + 2)
11u + 20 = 8u + 8
3u = -12 → u = -4 (impossible)

The problem as stated has no valid solution. A corrected version might give u = 2, making the number 42.

Geometry Word Problems

Problem: A rectangular garden's length is 5 feet more than twice its width. If the perimeter is 70 feet, find the dimensions.

Setup:
Let w = width
Then 2w + 5 = length

Perimeter formula:
P = 2(length + width)
70 = 2[(2w + 5) + w]
70 = 2(3w + 5)
70 = 6w + 10
60 = 6w
w = 10 feet
length = 2(10) + 5 = 25 feet

Verification:
Perimeter: 2(25 + 10) = 2(35) = 70 feet ✓
Length condition: 25 = 2(10) + 5 ✓

ACT Example 4: Age Problems

A father is 30 years older than his son. In 5 years, the father will be 3 times as old as his son. How old is the son now?

A) 5

B) 10

C) 12

D) 15

E) 20

Solution:

Let s = son's age now, father's age now = s + 30

In 5 years: son = s + 5, father = s + 35

s + 35 = 3(s + 5)

s + 35 = 3s + 15

20 = 2s → s = 10

Answer: B — Set up equation for future ages!

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Distance = rate × time; rearrange to find any unknown (r = d/t, t = d/r)
  • ✓Opposite direction: distances add; same direction: faster catches up using difference
  • ✓Work rate = 1/time to complete; combined rates add when working together
  • ✓To find time working together: 1/t₁ + 1/t₂ = 1/t_together
  • ✓Mixture problems: (amount₁ × concentration₁) + (amount₂ × concentration₂) = (total × final concentration)
  • ✓Adding pure substance (0% or 100%): amount of active ingredient stays constant
  • ✓Age problems: everyone ages at same rate; set up equations for current AND future/past ages
  • ✓Consecutive integers: x, x+1, x+2; consecutive even/odd: x, x+2, x+4
  • ✓Always verify your answer makes sense in the context of the original problem
  • ✓Draw tables or diagrams to organize information systematically before solving
