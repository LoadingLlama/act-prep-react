Water knowledge questions test your understanding of water's unique physical and chemical properties, aquatic ecosystem characteristics, water quality parameters and their significance, and hydrological cycles and processes. Water appears in numerous ACT science passages spanning chemistry (solutions, properties), biology (aquatic life, osmosis), Earth science (water cycle, weather), and environmental science (pollution, conservation). This lesson teaches you essential water concepts that repeatedly appear on the ACT, explains how water's molecular structure creates its special properties, provides context for interpreting aquatic ecosystem data, and shows you how to recognize when water's unique characteristics are relevant to understanding experimental results or environmental observations.

1. Water's Molecular Structure and Physical Properties

Water's formula H₂O represents a bent molecular structure that creates polarity, which in turn explains many of water's unique properties.

Polarity and Hydrogen Bonding

Water is polar due to its molecular geometry: (1) Structure: The H₂O molecule has a bent shape (not linear). Oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, so electron density concentrates near oxygen, creating a partial negative charge (δ-) on oxygen and partial positive charges (δ+) on hydrogens. (2) Hydrogen bonding: The partially positive hydrogen of one water molecule attracts the partially negative oxygen of another water molecule, forming a hydrogen bond. This is a strong intermolecular force (though weaker than covalent bonds within molecules). (3) Consequences: Hydrogen bonding causes water molecules to stick together (cohesion) and to other polar surfaces (adhesion). This explains water's high surface tension, capillary action in plants, and ability to climb up narrow tubes. (4) ACT context: Passages may describe experiments where water's polarity matters—for example, why water dissolves salt (ionic compound) but not oil (nonpolar), or why insects can walk on water (surface tension from hydrogen bonding). Understanding that "polar molecules attract other polar molecules" helps predict solubility and behavior.

High Specific Heat Capacity and Heat of Vaporization

Water has unusually high thermal properties: (1) High specific heat (4.18 J/g°C): Water requires a lot of energy to change temperature. This means water heats up slowly and cools down slowly, providing temperature stability. Coastal climates are moderated by oceans absorbing and releasing heat gradually. (2) High heat of vaporization (2260 J/g): Converting liquid water to vapor requires substantial energy. This makes evaporation an effective cooling mechanism—when sweat evaporates, it removes significant heat from skin. (3) Cause: Hydrogen bonds must be broken to heat water or evaporate it, requiring extra energy. (4) ACT applications: Passages about climate (oceans stabilize temperature), biology (sweating cools organisms), or environmental science (water bodies buffer against temperature extremes). If a question asks why temperature changed less in water than in sand under the same heat input, the answer relates to water's high specific heat. If asked why evaporation cools, it's because energy is absorbed during phase change.

Density Anomaly: Ice Floats

Water's density behavior is unusual: (1) Most substances: Solids are denser than liquids (molecules pack closer when solid), so solids sink in their own liquids. (2) Water is different: Ice is less dense than liquid water, so ice floats. Maximum density of water is at 4°C, not at its freezing point (0°C). (3) Cause: When water freezes, hydrogen bonds form a crystalline lattice with more space between molecules than in liquid water, decreasing density. (4) Biological importance: Ice floating on lakes insulates the liquid water below, allowing aquatic life to survive winter. If ice sank, lakes would freeze solid from bottom up, killing most organisms. (5) ACT context: Passages about aquatic ecosystems in winter, or questions asking why ice forms on top of ponds. The answer relates to density differences. Also, water's expansion when freezing can crack rocks (mechanical weathering) or burst pipes—density anomaly has physical consequences.

Universal Solvent Properties

Water is called the "universal solvent" because it dissolves many substances: (1) Polar and ionic substances dissolve: Water's polarity allows it to surround and separate ions (like Na⁺ and Cl⁻ from salt) or dissolve polar molecules (like sugar). The partially negative oxygen attracts cations; partially positive hydrogens attract anions. (2) Nonpolar substances don't dissolve: "Like dissolves like"—polar water doesn't mix with nonpolar substances like oils, fats, or waxes. These substances are hydrophobic (water-fearing). (3) Biological significance: Water transports nutrients and wastes in organisms because it can dissolve so many different chemicals. Blood plasma is mostly water carrying dissolved oxygen, glucose, ions, proteins (some), and waste products. (4) Environmental significance: Water dissolves pollutants, minerals, gases (like oxygen and CO₂). This makes it essential for life but also means pollution spreads easily through water systems. (5) ACT applications: Questions about which substances dissolve in water (ionic salts, polar molecules yes; oils no), or why water is crucial for transporting materials in living systems. If comparing solvents, remember water dissolves polar/ionic substances best.

Example: Water's Properties in ACT Questions

Question Type 1: Solubility
"Which substance would dissolve most readily in water?"
Choices: (A) Table salt (NaCl) (B) Vegetable oil (C) Wax (D) Butter
Answer: (A) NaCl is ionic, dissolves in polar water. Others are nonpolar/hydrophobic.

Question Type 2: Temperature Stability
"Why do coastal areas have smaller temperature fluctuations than inland areas?"
Answer: Water's high specific heat—oceans absorb heat without large temperature changes, moderating nearby air temperature.

Question Type 3: Ice and Aquatic Life
"How does ice formation on a lake's surface help fish survive winter?"
Answer: Ice is less dense than water, so it floats. The ice layer insulates liquid water below, preventing the entire lake from freezing and allowing fish to survive in liquid water beneath the ice.

Question Type 4: Hydrogen Bonding
"What causes water to have high surface tension?"
Answer: Hydrogen bonding between water molecules creates cohesive forces that resist surface disruption, producing surface tension.

2. Water Quality Parameters and Their Significance

ACT passages about water quality measure various parameters that indicate ecological health and suitability for organisms.

Dissolved Oxygen (DO) and Its Importance

Dissolved oxygen is oxygen gas (O₂) dissolved in water: (1) Measurement: Typically measured in mg/L or ppm (parts per million). Healthy aquatic systems have 5-14 mg/L DO. Below 5 mg/L stresses fish; below 2-3 mg/L causes fish kills. (2) Factors affecting DO: (a) Temperature—cold water holds more oxygen than warm water. (b) Photosynthesis—plants produce oxygen during the day, increasing DO. (c) Decomposition—bacteria consuming organic matter use oxygen, decreasing DO. (d) Turbulence—moving water absorbs oxygen from air; still water has less. (3) Pollution impact: Organic pollution (sewage, agricultural runoff) promotes bacterial growth that depletes oxygen (eutrophication). Low DO indicates poor water quality. (4) ACT scenarios: Tables showing DO levels at different times (higher during day due to photosynthesis, lower at night when respiration continues without photosynthesis). Or comparing DO in polluted vs. clean streams (polluted has lower DO). Questions ask what low DO indicates (pollution/decomposition) or which organisms tolerate low DO (some invertebrates, not most fish).

pH Levels in Aquatic Systems

pH measures acidity/basicity: (1) Scale: pH 7 is neutral, <7 is acidic, >7 is basic (alkaline). Each pH unit represents a 10-fold change in [H⁺]. Natural water systems are typically pH 6.5-8.5. (2) Factors affecting pH: (a) Dissolved CO₂ forms carbonic acid, lowering pH. (b) Limestone/carbonate rocks increase pH (basic). (c) Acid rain (from sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides) lowers pH. (d) Photosynthesis removes CO₂, potentially raising pH. (3) Biological significance: Most aquatic organisms require pH 6.5-8.5. Extreme pH (very acidic or very basic) denatures proteins and disrupts metabolic processes. Acid rain lowering lake pH below 5 kills fish and amphibians. (4) Buffering: Some water systems have high buffering capacity (resist pH change) due to carbonates or other buffer compounds. Soft water (low mineral content) has poor buffering and is vulnerable to acid rain. (5) ACT context: Passages about acid rain effects, or comparing lakes with different buffering capacities. Questions might ask why pH dropped after industrial pollution (acid rain introduction) or why one lake's pH changed less than another's (better buffering).

Turbidity, Suspended Solids, and Water Clarity

Turbidity measures water cloudiness: (1) Causes: Suspended particles like silt, algae, organic matter, microscopic organisms. Erosion, runoff, algae blooms, and pollution increase turbidity. (2) Measurement: Often in NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units). Clear water has <5 NTU; turbid water >50 NTU. (3) Effects on ecosystems: (a) Reduces light penetration, limiting photosynthesis by aquatic plants. Less plant growth means less oxygen production and less food for herbivores. (b) Clogs fish gills, reducing oxygen uptake. (c) Interferes with predator vision (fish can't see prey). (d) Indicates erosion or pollution. (4) Related concept—Secchi disk: A tool to measure water clarity. Lower the disk into water until it disappears; greater depth = clearer water. (5) ACT scenarios: Comparing turbidity before/after rain (increased after due to runoff), or relating turbidity to plant growth (higher turbidity correlates with less plant growth due to reduced light). Questions ask what high turbidity indicates (erosion, algae bloom, pollution) or effects on aquatic life (reduced photosynthesis, stressed fish).

Nutrient Levels: Nitrogen and Phosphorus

Nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus) are essential but harmful in excess: (1) Sources: Agricultural fertilizer runoff, sewage, animal waste. (2) Eutrophication: Excess nutrients cause algae blooms. Algae grow rapidly, covering the surface. When algae die, bacteria decompose them, consuming large amounts of oxygen. This creates hypoxic (low oxygen) zones, killing fish and other aerobic organisms. (3) Indicators: High nitrogen (nitrate, ammonia) and phosphorus levels indicate nutrient pollution. Normal levels: nitrate <10 mg/L, phosphorus <0.1 mg/L in lakes. Eutrophic waters exceed these significantly. (4) Effects: Loss of biodiversity (sensitive species die, only pollution-tolerant species survive), foul odors, unsuitability for drinking or recreation. (5) ACT context: Passages comparing water quality near agricultural areas (high nutrients) vs. forested areas (low nutrients). Questions about why algae bloomed (nutrient increase from fertilizer runoff), or why fish died after algae bloom (oxygen depletion during decomposition). Understanding the nutrient → algae bloom → oxygen depletion → fish kill sequence is key.

Example: Water Quality Interpretation

Scenario: Table shows data from two streams

Stream A (upstream, forested area):
DO: 8.5 mg/L, pH: 7.2, Turbidity: 3 NTU, Nitrate: 1.5 mg/L

Stream B (downstream, near farms):
DO: 3.2 mg/L, pH: 7.8, Turbidity: 28 NTU, Nitrate: 15 mg/L

Analysis:
1. Stream B has lower DO (3.2 vs 8.5) → Poor oxygen, likely stressed fish
2. Stream B has higher turbidity (28 vs 3) → Cloudy water, possibly from erosion/algae
3. Stream B has higher nitrate (15 vs 1.5) → Nutrient pollution from farm fertilizer

Question: Why is Stream B's DO lower?
Answer: Agricultural runoff increased nitrates → algae bloom → bacterial decomposition of dead algae consumes oxygen → DO drops

Question: Which stream has better water quality?
Answer: Stream A—higher DO, lower turbidity, lower nitrates indicate healthier ecosystem

3. Aquatic Ecosystems and Zones

Understanding aquatic ecosystem structure helps interpret passages about organism distribution and environmental gradients in water bodies.

Lake Zones: Littoral, Limnetic, Profundal, Benthic

Lakes are divided into zones based on depth and light: (1) Littoral zone: Shallow, near shore. Sunlight reaches bottom. Has rooted plants, high biodiversity. Warm, nutrient-rich. (2) Limnetic zone: Open water, surface to depth where light penetrates (photic zone). Plankton (algae, tiny organisms) dominate. Primary productivity occurs here. (3) Profundal zone: Deep water below light penetration (aphotic zone). No photosynthesis. Decomposers and fish that feed on detritus. Cooler, lower oxygen than surface. (4) Benthic zone: Bottom sediments, any depth. Decomposers, burrowing organisms, sediment-dwelling invertebrates. (5) ACT applications: Questions about where photosynthesis occurs (littoral and limnetic—where light reaches), where decomposition is highest (benthic—organic matter settles), or which zone has most plant species (littoral—shallow enough for rooted plants). If a passage presents data collected at different depths, understand that shallow areas differ from deep areas in light, oxygen, temperature, and organisms.

Marine Zones: Intertidal, Neritic, Oceanic

Ocean zones are defined by depth and proximity to shore: (1) Intertidal zone: Area between high and low tide. Organisms must tolerate exposure to air (during low tide) and submersion (during high tide). Extreme conditions, high stress, specialized organisms. (2) Neritic zone: Shallow ocean over continental shelf (shoreline to ~200 m depth). Sunlight reaches bottom. High productivity, coral reefs, kelp forests. Rich fisheries. (3) Oceanic zone: Open ocean beyond continental shelf. Deep water. Further divided: (a) Photic zone (0-200 m, light penetrates, photosynthesis occurs). (b) Aphotic zone (below 200 m, no light, no photosynthesis, organisms depend on detritus or chemosynthesis). (4) Depth and pressure: Deep ocean has high pressure, low temperature, no light. Specialized organisms (bioluminescent, high-pressure adaptations). (5) ACT scenarios: Comparing organism abundance by zone (neritic has more diversity than deep oceanic due to light/nutrients), or asking why certain organisms live in specific zones (intertidal organisms must tolerate desiccation).

Thermal Stratification in Lakes

Thermal stratification creates distinct temperature layers: (1) Summer stratification: (a) Epilimnion (top layer)—warm, well-mixed by wind, high oxygen. (b) Thermocline (middle layer)—rapid temperature drop with depth. (c) Hypolimnion (bottom layer)—cold, still, oxygen can be depleted if organic matter decomposes. (2) Winter stratification: In temperate zones, surface water cools. Water is densest at 4°C. In winter, 4°C water sinks to bottom, 0°C ice forms on top. This inverse stratification protects bottom-dwelling organisms. (3) Turnover: In spring and fall, temperature equalizes, and wind mixes the entire lake, redistributing oxygen and nutrients. This is called lake turnover. (4) Biological implications: During summer stratification, hypolimnion can become anoxic (no oxygen) if isolated from surface oxygen. Fall turnover replenishes oxygen. (5) ACT context: Graphs showing temperature vs. depth in summer (distinct layers) vs. fall (uniform temperature). Questions about when oxygen is replenished in deep water (during turnover) or why fish avoid deep water in summer (low oxygen in hypolimnion).

Freshwater vs. Marine Ecosystem Differences

Key differences between freshwater and marine systems: (1) Salinity: Freshwater <0.5 ppt (parts per thousand); marine ~35 ppt. Organisms are adapted to one or the other. Marine fish drink water and excrete salt; freshwater fish do the opposite. (2) Osmosis: In freshwater, water enters organisms' cells (hypotonic environment). In seawater, water leaves cells (hypertonic environment). Organisms have osmoregulation adaptations. (3) Biodiversity: Marine systems have higher overall biodiversity, but freshwater has high diversity relative to volume (only 2.5% of Earth's water is fresh). (4) Nutrient cycling: Freshwater systems often nutrient-limited (especially phosphorus). Marine systems vary—coastal areas nutrient-rich, open ocean often nutrient-poor except upwelling zones. (5) ACT applications: Questions about why a fish died when moved from freshwater to saltwater (osmoregulation failure), or comparing pollution impacts (freshwater systems more vulnerable to eutrophication due to limited dilution compared to vast oceans).

Example: Aquatic Zones and Stratification

Question 1: Lake zones
"In which zone would you find the highest diversity of rooted plants?"
Answer: Littoral zone—shallow enough for light to reach bottom, allowing rooted plants to photosynthesize

Question 2: Summer stratification
Data shows lake in August: Surface temp 24°C, 10m depth 15°C, 20m depth 8°C
"Why might fish avoid depths below 15m in summer?"
Answer: Hypolimnion (deep layer) has low oxygen during summer stratification because decomposition consumes oxygen and no mixing occurs with oxygen-rich surface water

Question 3: Marine zones
"Which zone experiences the most variable environmental conditions?"
Answer: Intertidal zone—organisms exposed to air at low tide (desiccation risk, temperature extremes) and submerged at high tide (waves, predators)

Question 4: Freshwater vs. Marine
"A marine fish placed in freshwater would likely:"
Answer: Absorb too much water and swell (freshwater is hypotonic relative to fish's body fluids, causing water to enter cells via osmosis)

4. Hydrological Cycle and Water Movement

The water cycle describes water movement through Earth's systems, appearing in Earth science and environmental passages.

Major Processes: Evaporation, Condensation, Precipitation

The water cycle involves continuous movement: (1) Evaporation: Liquid water (oceans, lakes, rivers) becomes water vapor using solar energy. Higher temperature and wind increase evaporation rate. (2) Transpiration: Plants release water vapor from leaves. Combined with evaporation, called evapotranspiration. (3) Condensation: Water vapor cools and forms liquid droplets (clouds, fog). Requires condensation nuclei (dust particles) and cooling (rising air, contact with cold surfaces). (4) Precipitation: Water falls as rain, snow, sleet, hail when droplets become too heavy to remain suspended. (5) Infiltration and runoff: Precipitation either soaks into ground (infiltration, recharging groundwater) or flows over surface (runoff into streams/lakes). (6) ACT applications: Diagrams showing cycle components. Questions ask what increases evaporation (higher temperature, more surface area), or what determines whether water infiltrates or runs off (soil type, vegetation, slope).

Groundwater: Aquifers, Recharge, and Wells

Groundwater is water stored underground: (1) Aquifers: Underground layers of permeable rock/sediment holding water. Water fills pore spaces between particles. (2) Water table: Upper surface of saturated zone. Below the water table, all spaces are filled with water. Above it, spaces contain air and some water. (3) Recharge: Process of water infiltrating soil and refilling aquifers. Recharge occurs where precipitation exceeds evapotranspiration and runoff, especially in permeable soils. (4) Wells: Holes drilled below the water table to extract groundwater. Over-pumping lowers the water table, potentially drying up wells. (5) Contamination: Pollutants can infiltrate and contaminate aquifers. Groundwater moves slowly, so contamination persists for long periods. (6) ACT scenarios: Questions about where water comes from in wells (aquifer below water table), or why groundwater contamination is serious (slow movement means pollutants remain for years, affecting drinking water).

Surface Water Movement: Runoff, Streams, Watersheds

Surface water systems transport water across land: (1) Runoff: Water flowing over land surface toward streams/rivers/lakes. Caused by precipitation exceeding infiltration capacity. Increases with impermeable surfaces (pavement), steep slopes, and heavy rain. (2) Watersheds (drainage basins): Area of land where all water drains to a common outlet (river, lake). Dividing ridges separate watersheds. Everything happening in a watershed affects downstream water quality. (3) Stream characteristics: (a) Headwaters (source)—small, clear, cold, high oxygen, often in mountains. (b) Middle reaches—wider, moderate flow, diverse habitats. (c) Mouth—wide, slow, often turbid, warmer, lower oxygen. (4) Human impacts: Deforestation increases runoff (less infiltration), urbanization creates impermeable surfaces (more runoff, more flooding), dams alter flow patterns. (5) ACT context: Maps of watersheds, questions about why pollution in one area affects water miles downstream (watershed connectivity), or why urban areas have more flooding (increased runoff from pavement).

Water Residence Time and Distribution

Residence time is how long water remains in a reservoir: (1) Ocean residence time: ~3,000 years (vast volume, slow cycling). (2) Groundwater: Weeks to 10,000 years (depending on aquifer depth and flow rate). (3) Lakes: Months to years. (4) Rivers: Days to weeks (constantly flowing). (5) Atmosphere: ~9 days (water vapor condenses quickly). (6) Distribution: 97% of Earth's water is saltwater (oceans). Of the 3% freshwater, ~69% is ice (glaciers, ice caps), ~30% is groundwater, <1% is surface water (lakes, rivers) and atmosphere. (7) Implications: Freshwater for human use is limited. Groundwater contamination is serious due to long residence time (pollutants persist). (8) ACT applications: Questions about which reservoir has the most water (oceans), or which reservoir cycles fastest (atmosphere/rivers), or why groundwater pollution is long-lasting (slow movement, long residence time).

Example: Water Cycle and Movement

Question 1: Evaporation factors
"Which condition would increase evaporation rate from a lake?"
Choices: (A) Lower temperature (B) Higher humidity (C) Increased wind speed (D) Cloud cover
Answer: (C) Wind increases evaporation by removing vapor-saturated air from surface

Question 2: Groundwater
"A well stops producing water during a drought. Why?"
Answer: Reduced precipitation → less recharge → water table drops below well depth

Question 3: Watershed
"Agricultural fertilizer applied in the northern part of a watershed is detected in a lake to the south. Why?"
Answer: Runoff carries fertilizer through the watershed to the lake (all water in a watershed drains to common outlet)

Question 4: Residence time
"Why does groundwater contamination persist longer than river contamination?"
Answer: Groundwater has much longer residence time (slow movement through aquifer) compared to rivers (fast-flowing), so pollutants remain for years vs. days/weeks
