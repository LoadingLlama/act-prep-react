Number substitution is a powerful test-taking strategy that transforms abstract algebraic problems into concrete arithmetic by plugging in your own numbers for variables. Instead of manipulating variables and expressions symbolically, you choose simple, strategic numbers to substitute, work through the problem with those numbers, and then test which answer choice produces the same result. This technique is particularly effective for problems with variables in answer choices, complex expressions, and word problems where the algebraic setup is unclear.

1. When to Use Number Substitution

Identifying the Right Problems

Number substitution is most effective when:

Answer choices contain variables: If all answer choices include x, y, n, or other variables, this is the clearest signal to use number substitution.

Example answer choices:
A. 2x + 3
B. 3x + 2
C. 2x - 3
D. 3x - 2
E. x + 5

Since all choices contain x, pick a value for x, work through the problem, and see which expression gives you the correct result.

The problem involves complex expressions: When the problem requires manipulating complicated algebraic expressions, substituting numbers can simplify the work.

The algebraic approach is unclear: If you're unsure how to set up the algebra, number substitution provides a concrete alternative.

The problem asks "which expression": Questions asking you to identify an equivalent expression or formula are perfect for this strategy.

When NOT to Use Number Substitution

This strategy is less effective when:

  • Answer choices are all numbers: Use working backwards instead
  • The algebraic solution is straightforward: Simple one-step or two-step algebra is faster than substitution
  • The problem involves geometric reasoning: Unless it's asking for an algebraic expression, geometry problems need direct solving
  • Testing would require extensive calculation: If substituting numbers creates complicated arithmetic, algebra may be more efficient

The Golden Rule: Choose Strategic Numbers

The success of number substitution depends on choosing good numbers to substitute. Follow these principles:

Use simple, easy-to-calculate numbers: Numbers like 2, 3, 5, 10, or 100 make arithmetic quick and reduce errors.

Avoid 0 and 1 (usually): These numbers have special properties (0 times anything is 0, 1 times anything is itself) that can make different expressions produce the same result, preventing you from distinguishing answer choices.

Respect problem constraints: If the problem says "positive integer" or "even number," choose a number that fits. If it mentions "price in dollars," use a reasonable price.

Use 100 for percent problems: When dealing with percentages, 100 is often the simplest choice because calculating percentages of 100 is easy.

For multiple variables, choose different values: If the problem has x and y, make them different numbers (like x = 3 and y = 5) so you can track them separately.

2. Step-by-Step Process

The Five-Step Method

Step 1: Choose your number(s). Select simple, strategic values for the variable(s) in the problem, respecting any constraints.

Step 2: Work through the problem with your numbers. Substitute your chosen values into the problem and solve it using arithmetic to get a concrete numerical answer. This is your "target value."

Step 3: Label your target. Write down or circle this result—it's what you're looking for in the answer choices.

Step 4: Test each answer choice. Substitute your chosen number(s) into each answer choice expression and calculate the result.

Step 5: Find the match. The answer choice that produces your target value is correct.

Complete example:

Problem: If the cost of n notebooks is 3n + 5 dollars, what is the cost of 7 more notebooks than n?

A. 3n + 12
B. 3n + 26
C. 10n + 5
D. 21n + 35
E. 3n + 7

Step 1: Choose a number for n. Let n = 4 (simple number, easy to work with).

Step 2: Work through the problem.
- n notebooks cost 3n + 5 = 3(4) + 5 = 12 + 5 = $17
- "7 more notebooks than n" means 4 + 7 = 11 notebooks total
- Cost of 11 notebooks: 3(11) + 5 = 33 + 5 = $38

Step 3: Label target. We're looking for $38.

Step 4 & 5: Test answer choices (where n = 4):
A. 3(4) + 12 = 12 + 12 = 24 ❌
B. 3(4) + 26 = 12 + 26 = 38 ✓
C. 10(4) + 5 = 40 + 5 = 45 ❌
D. 21(4) + 35 = 84 + 35 = 119 ❌
E. 3(4) + 7 = 12 + 7 = 19 ❌

Answer: B

Staying Organized

To avoid errors, maintain clear organization:

  • Write down your substitution: Note "Let x = 3" clearly so you don't forget what value you're using
  • Show your arithmetic: Work through calculations step-by-step rather than doing everything mentally
  • Circle your target value: Make it easy to see what you're looking for when testing answer choices
  • Test choices systematically: Go through A, B, C, D, E in order to avoid missing the correct answer

What If Two Choices Match?

Occasionally, two answer choices might produce your target value. This usually means you chose an unfortunate number (often 0, 1, or a number that makes certain terms cancel). If this happens:

  • Pick a different number: Choose a new value for the variable and test only the two remaining answer choices with this new number
  • Choose a more complex number: Use a number like 3, 5, or 7 rather than 2, or use a larger number like 10
  • Only one will match with the new number: That's your answer

In practice, choosing good numbers from the start (avoiding 0 and 1) usually prevents this issue.

3. Common Problem Types

Expression Translation Problems

These problems describe a situation in words and ask which expression represents it:

Problem: Sam has x dollars. He spends $8 and then doubles what remains. Which expression represents the amount he has now?

F. 2x - 8
G. 2x - 16
H. 2(x - 8)
J. x - 16
K. 8 - 2x

Using substitution:
Let x = 20 (Sam starts with $20)
- He spends $8: 20 - 8 = 12 remaining
- Doubles what remains: 12 × 2 = 24
- Target: $24

Testing choices (where x = 20):
F. 2(20) - 8 = 40 - 8 = 32 ❌
G. 2(20) - 16 = 40 - 16 = 24 ✓
H. 2(20 - 8) = 2(12) = 24 ✓

Two choices match! Choose a new number.

Let x = 10
- He spends $8: 10 - 8 = 2 remaining
- Doubles what remains: 2 × 2 = 4
- Target: $4

G. 2(10) - 16 = 20 - 16 = 4 ✓
H. 2(10 - 8) = 2(2) = 4 ✓

Still matching! Let x = 15
- He spends $8: 15 - 8 = 7 remaining
- Doubles what remains: 7 × 2 = 14
- Target: $14

G. 2(15) - 16 = 30 - 16 = 14 ✓
H. 2(15 - 8) = 2(7) = 14 ✓

Note: G and H are actually algebraically equivalent: 2(x - 8) = 2x - 16. Both are correct! The test would not present both as options. If you encounter this, either could be the answer.

Answer: H (or G if H weren't available)

Percent Problems

For percent problems, using 100 as your base value makes calculations simple:

Problem: A store increases the price of an item by 20% and then decreases it by 20%. If the original price was p dollars, which expression represents the final price?

A. p
B. 0.96p
C. 1.04p
D. 0.80p
E. 1.20p

Using substitution with p = 100:
- Original price: $100
- Increase by 20%: $100 + $20 = $120
- Decrease by 20% (of the new price): $120 - (0.20 × $120) = $120 - $24 = $96
- Target: $96

Testing choices (where p = 100):
A. 100 ❌
B. 0.96(100) = 96 ✓
C. 1.04(100) = 104 ❌
D. 0.80(100) = 80 ❌
E. 1.20(100) = 120 ❌

Answer: B

Average Problems

Problems involving averages and unknowns work well with number substitution:

Problem: The average of three numbers is x. If two of the numbers are 15 and 21, what is the third number in terms of x?

F. x - 36
G. 3x - 36
H. x - 12
J. 3x - 12
K. x + 36

Using substitution:
Let x = 20 (average is 20)
- Average of three numbers is 20, so sum = 20 × 3 = 60
- Two numbers are 15 and 21: 15 + 21 = 36
- Third number: 60 - 36 = 24
- Target: 24

Testing choices (where x = 20):
F. 20 - 36 = -16 ❌
G. 3(20) - 36 = 60 - 36 = 24 ✓
H. 20 - 12 = 8 ❌
J. 3(20) - 12 = 60 - 12 = 48 ❌
K. 20 + 36 = 56 ❌

Answer: G

Problems with Multiple Variables

When the problem involves two or more variables, choose different values for each:

Problem: If a rectangle has length x and width y, which expression represents the perimeter?

A. xy
B. 2xy
C. x + y
D. 2x + 2y
E. x² + y²

Using substitution:
Let x = 5 and y = 3
- Perimeter = 2(length) + 2(width) = 2(5) + 2(3) = 10 + 6 = 16
- Target: 16

Testing choices (where x = 5, y = 3):
A. 5 × 3 = 15 ❌
B. 2 × 5 × 3 = 30 ❌
C. 5 + 3 = 8 ❌
D. 2(5) + 2(3) = 10 + 6 = 16 ✓
E. 5² + 3² = 25 + 9 = 34 ❌

Answer: D

4. Advanced Strategies and Tips

Special Number Considerations

Certain problem types benefit from specific number choices:

For fraction problems: Choose numbers that are multiples of the denominators to avoid messy arithmetic.

Example: If the problem involves "one-third of x," choose x = 6, 9, 12, or another multiple of 3 to get whole number results.

For consecutive integer problems: Use actual consecutive integers like 5, 6, 7 or 10, 11, 12.

For even/odd problems: Respect the constraint—if x must be even, use 2, 4, 6, etc.

For digit problems: If the problem discusses digits of a number, choose a two-digit number like 23 where you can easily identify which digit is which.

When 0 and 1 Are Actually Useful

While generally avoided, there are rare cases where 0 or 1 is appropriate:

  • Use 0 when testing for constant terms: If you suspect an expression has a constant (like the "+5" in "3x + 5"), substituting 0 isolates that constant
  • Use 1 when checking coefficients: Substituting 1 can help you see the coefficient structure of an expression
  • Never use them as your first choice: Only use 0 or 1 if you're testing a specific hypothesis or if you've already found the answer with another number and want to verify

Combining Number Substitution with Other Strategies

Number substitution can work alongside other techniques:

With estimation: After substituting and finding your target, you can often estimate which answer choices are in the right range before calculating all of them.

With elimination: If answer choices have obviously wrong forms (wrong operations, wrong signs), eliminate those before testing.

With algebraic reasoning: You can use number substitution to check an algebraic solution or to guide your algebraic setup.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Choosing numbers that violate constraints. Always check if the problem specifies "positive," "even," "integer," etc., and choose accordingly.

Mistake 2: Forgetting what you substituted. Write down "x = 3" or "p = 100" clearly so you don't mix up values mid-problem.

Mistake 3: Making arithmetic errors. Double-check your calculations, especially when testing answer choices. A small error can lead you to select the wrong answer.

Mistake 4: Stopping after finding one match without checking others. While you should find only one match, occasionally an arithmetic error makes you think you've found it when you haven't. If time permits, verify by testing one more choice.

Time Management

Number substitution is generally quick, but optimize your efficiency:

  • Choose numbers you can work with mentally: If you can do most arithmetic in your head, you'll save significant time
  • Use scratch paper effectively: Write your substitution, target value, and testing clearly to avoid confusion
  • Skip complicated answer choices initially: If one answer choice looks much more complicated than others, test the simpler ones first
  • Budget 30-60 seconds: Most number substitution problems should take under a minute once you're practiced

Practice Example: Complex Multi-Variable Problem

Problem: A painter charges a flat fee of f dollars plus h dollars per hour. If she works for n hours, what is her total charge in dollars?

A. fhn
B. f + h + n
C. f + hn
D. fn + h
E. n(f + h)

Using substitution:
Let f = 50 (flat fee = $50)
Let h = 20 (hourly rate = $20/hour)
Let n = 3 (works 3 hours)

- Flat fee: $50
- Hourly charge: $20 × 3 hours = $60
- Total charge: $50 + $60 = $110
- Target: $110

Testing choices (where f = 50, h = 20, n = 3):
A. 50 × 20 × 3 = 3,000 ❌
B. 50 + 20 + 3 = 73 ❌
C. 50 + (20 × 3) = 50 + 60 = 110 ✓
D. (50 × 3) + 20 = 150 + 20 = 170 ❌
E. 3(50 + 20) = 3(70) = 210 ❌

Answer: C
