Parallel structure questions make up about 7-9% of the ACT English Test—roughly 5-7 questions per test! These questions test whether items in a list or comparison are written with the same grammatical form.

1. What is Parallel Structure?

  • Parallel structure is the repetition of a chosen grammatical form within a sentence as items are listed or compared
    • The items in a list can be words, phrases, or clauses
    • Whenever we see items in a list or a comparison between items, we must make sure they have parallel structure
  • Items in any list must be written with the same grammatical form
    • Items must be all nouns, all verbs, all adjectives, etc.
    • When lists are written with proper parallel structure, the sentence flows more smoothly and sounds better
    • Many students can "hear" which answer choice is correct without even realizing they're being tested on parallel structure

Examples of Parallel Structure Errors

  • ❌ Incorrect: "To protect her fragile hands, Julia will not rake the leaves, wash the dishes, and has decided she will not take out the trash."
    • List items: "rake the leaves" (verb phrase), "wash the dishes" (verb phrase), "has decided she will not take out the trash" (clause)
    • Not parallel! The third item is much longer and has a different structure
  • ✓ Correct: "To protect her fragile hands, Julia will not rake the leaves, wash the dishes, or take out the trash."
    • All three items are now verb phrases: "rake," "wash," "take out"
    • Parallel!

2. Parallel Structure with Lists

  • No matter how simple or complex the items in the list are, they must be parallel
    • Lists can contain nouns, verb phrases, adjectives, or entire clauses
    • All items must match in grammatical form

Lists of Nouns or Noun Phrases

  • ❌ Incorrect: "Elon Musk is known for self-driving cars and pioneering the SpaceX program."
    • "Self-driving cars" (noun phrase) vs. "pioneering the SpaceX program" (verb phrase)
    • Not parallel!
  • ✓ Correct: "Elon Musk is known for self-driving cars and the SpaceX program."
    • Both are noun phrases: "self-driving cars" and "the SpaceX program"
  • ✓ Also correct: "Elon Musk is known for manufacturing self-driving cars and pioneering the SpaceX program."
    • Both are verb phrases: "manufacturing" and "pioneering"

Lists of Verb Phrases

  • ❌ Incorrect: "After calling in sick to school, Charlie hoped to get excused from class, receiving an extension on his homework assignment, and spend the afternoon watching movies at home."
    • "To get excused" (infinitive), "receiving" (gerund), "spend" (base verb)
    • Not parallel!
  • ✓ Correct: "After calling in sick to school, Charlie hoped to get excused from class, receive an extension on his homework assignment, and spend the afternoon watching movies at home."
    • All three use the base verb form after "hoped to": "get," "receive," "spend"
    • Parallel!

Lists of 3+ vs. Lists of 2

  • Lists of 3 or more items are easier to spot
    • You will be able to more obviously "hear" which answer choice is correct or incorrect
    • Example: "She made her bed, cleaned the shelves, and swept the floor" (all past tense verbs)
  • Lists of 2 are more difficult to spot and can trick you
    • They can often sound good even if they're not parallel
    • Be extra aware of parallel structure anytime you see a list of 2 on the ACT
    • ❌ Incorrect: "It's harder to lift the couch by myself than lifting it with some friends."
    • ✓ Correct: "It's harder to lift the couch by myself than to lift it with some friends." (both use "to lift")

3. Special Cases: Correlative Conjunctions

  • In addition to lists, there are certain special cases for parallel structure
    • These are officially called correlative conjunctions
    • They must have the same grammatical structure for both parts of the conjunction

The Four Main Correlative Conjunctions

  • Neither ___ nor ___
    • Example: "The news report is neither accurate nor fair."
    • Both blanks must have the same form (both adjectives here)
  • Either ___ or ___
    • Example: "You can either call me or text me."
    • Both blanks must match (both verb phrases here)
  • Not only ___ but also ___
    • This is the most commonly tested correlative conjunction on the ACT
    • Example: "My mother not only took away my keys but also locked them in the safe."
    • Both blanks must match (both verb phrases here)
  • Both ___ and ___
    • Example: "Both a scholar and an athlete, Sylvia got a scholarship to row crew in college."
    • Both blanks must match (both noun phrases here)

How to Check Parallel Structure with Correlative Conjunctions

  • Identify what comes immediately after the first part of the conjunction
    • What type of word or phrase follows "not only," "either," "neither," or "both"?
  • Check what comes immediately after the second part
    • What type of word or phrase follows "but also," "or," "nor," or "and"?
  • Make sure both parts match in grammatical form
    • If the first part has a verb phrase, the second must have a verb phrase
    • If the first part has a noun, the second must have a noun

4. Common Parallel Structure Patterns

  • Watch for these common patterns on the ACT
    • Lists connected by "and" or "or"
    • Comparisons using "than"
    • Correlative conjunctions (neither/nor, either/or, not only/but also, both/and)

Strategy for Parallel Structure Questions

  • Step 1: Identify that there's a list or comparison
    • Look for words like "and," "or," "than," or correlative conjunctions
  • Step 2: Find all the items in the list or comparison
    • How many items are there? What are they?
  • Step 3: Check the grammatical form of each item
    • Is the first item a noun? Verb? Adjective? Phrase? Clause?
    • Are all the other items the same type?
  • Step 4: Choose the answer that makes all items match
    • All items must have the same grammatical structure
    • Trust your ear—parallel structure usually "sounds right"

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Parallel structure is the repetition of a chosen grammatical form within a sentence as items are listed or compared. Items in a list must all be the same type—all nouns, all verbs, all adjectives, etc. When lists have proper parallel structure, sentences flow smoothly and sound better.
  • ✓Lists of 3 or more items are easier to spot because you can clearly hear when one item doesn't match the others. Lists of 2 are trickier and can sound correct even when they're not parallel, so pay extra attention to comparisons using "than" and two-item lists connected by "and" or "or."
  • ✓Correlative conjunctions (neither/nor, either/or, not only/but also, both/and) require parallel structure on both sides. Whatever grammatical form comes after the first part must match the grammatical form that comes after the second part. "Not only/but also" is the most commonly tested on the ACT.
  • ✓To check parallel structure, identify all items in the list or comparison, determine the grammatical form of each item, and make sure they all match. Trust your ear—parallel structure usually sounds right, and non-parallel structure often sounds awkward or choppy.
