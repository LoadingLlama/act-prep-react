Counting problems ask "how many" ways something can occur. The ACT tests factorials, the fundamental counting principle, permutations, and combinations.

1. Factorials

  • The factorial function, symbolized by "!", multiplies an integer by every integer below it:
    • n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 2 × 1
    • Example: 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120
    • Example: 3! = 3 × 2 × 1 = 6
    • Special case: 0! = 1 (by definition)
  • Most ACT factorial questions are simple calculations:
    • Example: 2! + 4! = ?
    • 2! = 2 × 1 = 2
    • 4! = 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24
    • 2! + 4! = 2 + 24 = 26
  • Calculator tip:
    • Most calculators have a "!" button
    • Check your calculator manual if unsure
    • Saves time on larger factorials like 7! or 10!
  • Simplifying factorial fractions (advanced):
    • Example: (3! × n!)/(n+1)!
    • Expand: (6 × n × (n-1) × ... × 1)/((n+1) × n × (n-1) × ... × 1)
    • Cancel common terms: n! cancels out
    • Result: 6/(n+1)

2. Fundamental Counting Principle

  • The fundamental counting principle: If one event can occur in m ways and another can occur in n ways, then both can occur in m × n ways
    • Multiply the number of choices for each decision
    • This is the MOST common counting method on the ACT
    • Works when order matters AND when order doesn't matter (for independent choices)
  • Example: Creating outfits
    • You have 3 shirts, 4 pairs of shoes, and 5 pairs of pants
    • How many different outfits can you create?
    • Total outfits = 3 × 4 × 5 = 60
  • Example: Lock codes
    • A lock code has 3 numbers (0-9) and 2 letters (A-Z)
    • How many different codes are possible?
    • Numbers: 10 choices for each digit = 10 × 10 × 10 = 1,000
    • Letters: 26 choices for each letter = 26 × 26 = 676
    • Total codes = 1,000 × 676 = 676,000
  • When choices decrease (without replacement):
    • Example: Selecting 3 people from a group of 10 for 1st, 2nd, 3rd place
    • 1st place: 10 choices
    • 2nd place: 9 choices (one person already selected)
    • 3rd place: 8 choices
    • Total arrangements = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720

3. Permutations (Order Matters)

  • Permutations count arrangements where ORDER MATTERS:
    • Different orderings count as different outcomes
    • Example: ABC is different from BAC
    • Used for rankings, positions, seating arrangements
  • The permutation formula:
    • nPr = n!/(n-r)!
    • n = total number of items
    • r = number of items being selected
    • nPr = number of ways to arrange r items from n items
  • Example: How many ways can you arrange 3 books from a shelf of 5 books?
    • n = 5 books total, r = 3 books selected
    • 5P3 = 5!/(5-3)! = 5!/2!
    • 5P3 = (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1)/(2 × 1)
    • 5P3 = 120/2 = 60 arrangements
  • Shortcut using fundamental counting principle:
    • Same problem: 5 books, select 3
    • 1st position: 5 choices
    • 2nd position: 4 choices
    • 3rd position: 3 choices
    • Total = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 (same answer, faster!)

4. Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter)

  • Combinations count selections where ORDER DOESN'T MATTER:
    • Different orderings count as the SAME outcome
    • Example: ABC is the same as BAC is the same as CAB
    • Used for groups, committees, selections
  • The combination formula:
    • nCr = n!/(r!(n-r)!)
    • n = total number of items
    • r = number of items being selected
    • nCr = number of ways to select r items from n items
  • Example: How many ways can you select 3 books from a shelf of 5 books?
    • n = 5 books, r = 3 books (order doesn't matter)
    • 5C3 = 5!/(3! × 2!)
    • 5C3 = (5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1)/((3 × 2 × 1) × (2 × 1))
    • 5C3 = 120/(6 × 2) = 120/12 = 10
  • Key difference from permutations:
    • Same problem with permutations gave 60 arrangements
    • With combinations, we get 10 selections
    • Combinations are ALWAYS smaller (or equal) to permutations
    • We divide by r! to remove duplicate orderings

5. When to Use Each Method

  • Use Fundamental Counting Principle when:
    • Making multiple independent choices
    • Creating combinations of items (outfits, meals, codes)
    • This is the FASTEST method—use it whenever possible!
  • Use Permutations when:
    • ORDER MATTERS in the selection
    • Words: "arrange," "order," "rank," "position"
    • Examples: Race finish order, seating arrangements, passwords
  • Use Combinations when:
    • ORDER DOESN'T MATTER in the selection
    • Words: "group," "committee," "team," "select," "choose"
    • Examples: Choosing committee members, selecting toppings, forming groups
  • Quick decision tree:
    • Question asks "how many outfits/meals/codes?" → Fundamental Counting Principle (multiply choices)
    • Question asks "arrange" or "order" → Permutation (order matters)
    • Question asks "select" or "choose a group" → Combination (order doesn't matter)
  • Common ACT trick:
    • The ACT will often make permutation and combination both answer choices
    • Read carefully: does order matter?
    • If you select people for a "committee," order doesn't matter (combination)
    • If you select people for "president, VP, secretary," order matters (permutation)
