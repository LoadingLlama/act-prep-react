Mastering arcs and sectors requires understanding the relationship between central angles, arc length, and sector area in circles. These concepts appear in approximately 8% of ACT Math questions and connect to proportional reasoning, circle properties, and radian measure.

1. Circle Basics and Central Angles

Essential Circle Terminology

Before working with arcs and sectors, review these fundamental circle terms:

Radius (r): Distance from center to any point on the circle.

Diameter (d): Distance across the circle through the center. Always d = 2r.

Circumference (C): Perimeter of the circle. Formula: C = 2πr or C = πd.

Area (A): Space inside the circle. Formula: A = πr².

Example: A circle has radius 6 cm. Find its diameter, circumference, and area.

Diameter = 2(6) = 12 cm
Circumference = 2π(6) = 12π cm ≈ 37.7 cm
Area = π(6)² = 36π cm² ≈ 113.1 cm²

Central Angles

A central angle has its vertex at the center of the circle, with its sides (radii) extending to the circumference. Central angles are measured in degrees, with a full circle containing 360°.

Key principle: A central angle's measure equals the measure of the arc it intercepts.

Example: If a central angle measures 60°, the arc it intercepts also measures 60°.

Inscribed Angles vs. Central Angles

An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle (not at the center). The inscribed angle theorem states:

Inscribed angle = (1/2) × Central angle (when both intercept the same arc)

Example: A central angle measures 80°. An inscribed angle intercepts the same arc. What is the inscribed angle?

Inscribed angle = (1/2) × 80° = 40°

Angles in a Full Circle

A complete circle contains 360 degrees or 2π radians. This fundamental relationship underlies all arc and sector calculations.

Common angle conversions:

  • 90° = π/2 radians (quarter circle)
  • 180° = π radians (semicircle)
  • 270° = 3π/2 radians (three-quarter circle)
  • 360° = 2π radians (full circle)

2. Arc Length

Arc Length Formula Using Degrees

An arc is a portion of the circle's circumference. To find arc length when the central angle is in degrees:

Arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr

Or equivalently: Arc length = (θ/360°) × C

This formula represents the fraction of the circle (θ/360°) multiplied by the total circumference.

Example: A circle has radius 10 inches. What is the arc length for a central angle of 72°?

Arc length = (72°/360°) × 2π(10)
Arc length = (1/5) × 20π
Arc length = 4π inches ≈ 12.6 inches

Interpretation: The arc is 1/5 of the total circumference (because 72° is 1/5 of 360°).

Arc Length Formula Using Radians

When the central angle is in radians, the arc length formula simplifies beautifully:

Arc length = rθ (where θ is in radians)

This elegant formula makes radian calculations much faster.

Example: A circle has radius 8 cm. What is the arc length for a central angle of π/3 radians?

Arc length = 8 × (π/3) = 8π/3 cm ≈ 8.4 cm

Working Backward from Arc Length

Sometimes you're given the arc length and must find the central angle or radius.

Example: An arc length is 15π cm on a circle with radius 18 cm. What is the central angle in degrees?

Using arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr:
15π = (θ/360°) × 2π(18)
15π = (θ/360°) × 36π
15 = (θ/360°) × 36
θ/360° = 15/36 = 5/12
θ = 360° × (5/12) = 150°

Major and Minor Arcs

Minor arc: The shorter arc between two points on a circle (central angle < 180°).

Major arc: The longer arc between two points (central angle > 180°).

Semicircle: When the central angle is exactly 180°, both arcs are equal.

Example: Two points on a circle are connected by a minor arc with central angle 100°. What is the central angle of the major arc?

Major arc angle = 360° - 100° = 260°

Verification: Minor + Major = 100° + 260° = 360° ✓

3. Sector Area

Sector Area Formula Using Degrees

A sector is a "slice" of a circle, like a piece of pie. It's bounded by two radii and an arc. The sector area formula:

Sector area = (θ/360°) × πr²

This represents the fraction of the circle (θ/360°) multiplied by the total circle area.

Example: A circle has radius 12 feet. What is the area of a sector with central angle 45°?

Sector area = (45°/360°) × π(12)²
Sector area = (1/8) × 144π
Sector area = 18π ft² ≈ 56.5 ft²

Check: 45° is 1/8 of 360°, so the sector is 1/8 of the total circle area.

Sector Area Formula Using Radians

When using radians, the sector area formula is:

Sector area = (1/2)r²θ (where θ is in radians)

Example: A circle has radius 5 meters. Find the sector area for a central angle of 2π/3 radians.

Sector area = (1/2)(5)²(2π/3)
Sector area = (1/2)(25)(2π/3)
Sector area = 25π/3 m² ≈ 26.2 m²

Sector Perimeter

The perimeter of a sector includes the arc length plus two radii:

Sector perimeter = arc length + 2r

Example: A sector has radius 9 cm and central angle 60°. What is its perimeter?

Arc length = (60°/360°) × 2π(9) = (1/6) × 18π = 3π cm
Sector perimeter = 3π + 2(9) = 3π + 18 cm ≈ 27.4 cm

Segment Area

A segment is the region between a chord and the arc it cuts off. To find segment area:

Segment area = Sector area - Triangle area

The triangle is formed by the two radii and the chord.

Example: A segment is cut from a circle of radius 8 by a central angle of 90°. Find the segment area.

Sector area = (90°/360°) × π(8)² = (1/4) × 64π = 16π
Triangle area = (1/2)(8)(8) = 32 (it's a right triangle with the two radii as legs)
Segment area = 16π - 32 ≈ 50.3 - 32 = 18.3 square units

4. Applications and Problem-Solving

Proportional Reasoning with Circles

The key insight for arc and sector problems is that everything scales proportionally:

Angle/360° = Arc/Circumference = Sector area/Circle area

Example: A sector has area equal to 1/6 of the circle's total area. What is the central angle?

Since sector/circle = 1/6, we have:
θ/360° = 1/6
θ = 360°/6 = 60°

Clock Problems

Clocks provide practical arc and sector applications. Key facts:

  • Hour hand moves 360°/12 hours = 30° per hour
  • Minute hand moves 360°/60 minutes = 6° per minute
  • Hour hand also moves 0.5° per minute

Example: At 3:00, what is the angle between the hour and minute hands?

Hour hand position: 3 × 30° = 90°
Minute hand position: 0° (at 12)
Angle between them: 90° - 0° = 90°

Pizza and Pie Problems

Real-world sector problems often involve dividing circular objects into equal pieces.

Example: A 16-inch diameter pizza is cut into 8 equal slices. What is the area of one slice?

Radius = 16/2 = 8 inches
Total pizza area = π(8)² = 64π square inches
One slice = 64π/8 = 8π ≈ 25.1 square inches

Alternative: Central angle per slice = 360°/8 = 45°
Sector area = (45°/360°) × 64π = (1/8) × 64π = 8π ✓

Arc Length in Real-World Contexts

Arc length appears in problems involving circular tracks, wheels, and paths.

Example: A wheel with radius 14 inches rotates through 120°. How far does a point on the edge travel?

Arc length = (120°/360°) × 2π(14)
Arc length = (1/3) × 28π
Arc length = 28π/3 inches ≈ 29.3 inches

Common Mistakes and Time-Saving Tips

Mistake 1: Using diameter instead of radius. All formulas use radius r, not diameter d.

Mistake 2: Mixing degrees and radians. Check which unit the angle is in and use the appropriate formula.

Mistake 3: Forgetting to multiply by the fraction. Arc length is not 2πr—it's (θ/360°) × 2πr.

Tip 1: Recognize common fractions. 90° = 1/4 circle, 120° = 1/3 circle, 60° = 1/6 circle, 45° = 1/8 circle.

Tip 2: Leave answers in terms of π. It's faster and more accurate than approximating.

Tip 3: Use proportional reasoning. If you know one relationship, you can often solve without formulas.

Tip 4: Draw a diagram. Visualizing the sector makes the problem much clearer.

  • ✓ All arc and sector calculations use proportional reasoning: angle/360° = arc/circumference = sector/circle area
  • ✓ Sector perimeter includes arc length plus two radii
  • ✓ Segment area = sector area - triangle area (for the region between chord and arc)
  • ✓ Minor arcs have central angles less than 180°; major arcs greater than 180°
  • ✓ Common angle fractions: 90° = 1/4, 120° = 1/3, 60° = 1/6, 45° = 1/8 of a circle
  • ✓ Always use radius (not diameter) in arc and sector formulas
