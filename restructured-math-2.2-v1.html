Geometry questions involving areas, volumes, and triangles appear on every ACT Math test, accounting for approximately 15-20% of all questions. Success on these problems requires memorizing key formulas and knowing when to apply specific triangle rules like Pythagorean theorem and special right triangle ratios.

1. Area Formulas for Common Shapes

  • You MUST memorize these area formulas—they are NOT provided on the ACT:
    • Triangle: A = ½bh (where b = base, h = height)
    • Rectangle: A = lw (where l = length, w = width)
    • Square: A = s² (where s = side length)
    • Parallelogram: A = bh (base times height, NOT side lengths)
    • Trapezoid: A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h (average of the two bases times height)
    • Circle: A = πr² (where r = radius)
    • Kite: A = ½d₁d₂ (half the product of the diagonals)
  • Common mistakes to avoid:
    • For parallelograms, use the HEIGHT (perpendicular distance), not the slanted side length
    • For circles, make sure you have the radius, not the diameter (if given diameter, divide by 2)
    • For triangles, the height must be perpendicular to the base
  • Perimeter formulas you should know:
    • Rectangle: P = 2l + 2w
    • Square: P = 4s
    • Circle (circumference): C = 2πr or C = πd

2. Volume Formulas and Unit Conversions

  • Essential volume formulas to memorize:
    • Rectangular Prism (box): V = lwh
    • Cube: V = s³
    • Right Cylinder: V = πr²h
    • Any 3D shape: V = Bh (where B = area of base, h = height)
  • Surface area formulas (less common but important):
    • Rectangular Prism: SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh
    • Cube: SA = 6s²
  • CRITICAL: Unit conversions—The ACT frequently tests these, and students make costly mistakes:
    • Always convert units BEFORE calculating area or volume
    • 1 yard = 3 feet
    • 1 foot = 12 inches
    • 1 square yard = 9 square feet (not 3!)
    • 1 square foot = 144 square inches (not 12!)
    • 1 cubic yard = 27 cubic feet (not 3!)
    • 1 cubic foot = 1,728 cubic inches (not 12!)
  • Why you must convert FIRST:
    • Example: A field is 136 yards × 93 yards. To find area in square feet:
    • ✓ CORRECT: Convert to feet first: (136 × 3) × (93 × 3) = 408 × 279 = 113,832 ft²
    • ✗ WRONG: Calculate area then convert: 136 × 93 = 12,648, then × 9 = wrong answer!

3. Right Triangles and Pythagorean Theorem

  • The Pythagorean theorem applies ONLY to right triangles:
    • a² + b² = c²
    • Where a and b are the legs (the two shorter sides)
    • c is the hypotenuse (the longest side, opposite the right angle)
  • Pythagorean triples—memorize these to save time:
    • 3-4-5 triangle: If you see sides of 3 and 4, the hypotenuse is 5
    • 5-12-13 triangle: If you see sides of 5 and 12, the hypotenuse is 13
    • These can be scaled up: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 10-24-26, etc.
    • If you recognize a Pythagorean triple, you can skip the calculation entirely
  • When to use Pythagorean theorem:
    • Finding the third side of a right triangle when you know two sides
    • Finding distances in coordinate geometry problems
    • Solving problems with rectangles (diagonals create right triangles)
    • Working with circles and tangent lines

4. Special Right Triangles (30-60-90 and 45-45-90)

  • 45-45-90 triangle (also called a right isosceles triangle):
    • Side ratio: x : x : x√2
    • The two legs are equal (both length x)
    • The hypotenuse is x√2 (one leg times √2)
    • If the leg is 5, the hypotenuse is 5√2
    • If the hypotenuse is 10, each leg is 10/√2 = 5√2
  • 30-60-90 triangle:
    • Side ratio: x : x√3 : 2x
    • Short leg (opposite 30°): x
    • Long leg (opposite 60°): x√3
    • Hypotenuse (opposite 90°): 2x
    • KEY RULE: The short leg is always half the hypotenuse
    • If the hypotenuse is 10, the short leg is 5, and the long leg is 5√3
  • How to recognize special right triangles:
    • Look for angle measurements (45°, 30°, 60°)
    • Look for the phrase "right isosceles triangle" (45-45-90)
    • Look for equilateral triangles split in half (creates 30-60-90)
    • If you see √2 or √3 in the answer choices, it's likely a special right triangle
  • When to use special right triangles instead of Pythagorean theorem:
    • When you see the special angles (30°, 45°, 60°)
    • When the answer choices contain √2 or √3
    • Special triangles are FASTER than using a² + b² = c²

5. Test-Taking Tips for Geometry Problems

  • Draw and label diagrams
    • If no diagram is provided, draw one yourself
    • Label all given measurements directly on the diagram
    • Mark right angles with a small square
    • Write formulas next to the shapes as you work
  • Look for Pythagorean triples first
    • Before using a² + b² = c², check if the sides are a Pythagorean triple
    • Common ones: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, and their multiples
    • This saves time and prevents calculation errors
  • Use substitution for complex problems
    • If a problem uses variables (like "a field is l feet by w feet"), pick simple numbers
    • Example: Let l = 4 and w = 3, then solve with those specific values
    • This makes algebraic area/volume problems much easier to visualize
  • Convert units before calculating
    • NEVER calculate area in yards and then convert to square feet
    • ALWAYS convert the length and width first, then calculate
    • Write out the conversion as a separate step to avoid mistakes
  • Memorize formulas NOW, not the night before
    • Make flashcards for all area and volume formulas
    • Practice drawing and labeling 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 triangles from memory
    • Review Pythagorean triples until you can recognize them instantly
