Understanding lines and linear equations is essential for success on the ACT Math section, requiring mastery of slope calculations, equation forms, and graphing techniques. These concepts appear in approximately 12% of ACT Math questions and connect to coordinate geometry, systems of equations, and function analysis.

1. Slope and Rate of Change

Calculating Slope from Two Points

The slope of a line measures its steepness and direction. Given two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂), the slope formula is:

m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)

This formula represents "rise over run"—the vertical change divided by the horizontal change.

Example: Find the slope of the line passing through (2, 5) and (8, 17).

m = (17 - 5)/(8 - 2) = 12/6 = 2

Interpretation: For every 1 unit you move right, the line rises 2 units.

Positive, Negative, Zero, and Undefined Slopes

The sign and value of slope tell you about a line's behavior:

Positive slope (m > 0): Line rises from left to right. As x increases, y increases.

Negative slope (m < 0): Line falls from left to right. As x increases, y decreases.

Zero slope (m = 0): Horizontal line. y-value never changes, regardless of x.

Undefined slope: Vertical line. x-value never changes; division by zero in slope formula.

Examples:
Points (3, 4) and (7, 10): m = (10-4)/(7-3) = 6/4 = 3/2 (positive slope)
Points (1, 8) and (5, 2): m = (2-8)/(5-1) = -6/4 = -3/2 (negative slope)
Points (2, 6) and (9, 6): m = (6-6)/(9-2) = 0/7 = 0 (horizontal line)
Points (4, 3) and (4, 9): m = (9-3)/(4-4) = 6/0 = undefined (vertical line)

Slope as Rate of Change

In word problems, slope represents rate of change. The units of slope are (units of y)/(units of x).

Example: A car travels 150 miles in 3 hours and 270 miles in 6 hours. What is the rate of change (speed)?

Points: (3, 150) and (6, 270) where x = hours and y = miles
m = (270 - 150)/(6 - 3) = 120/3 = 40 miles per hour

Interpretation: The car travels at 40 mph.

Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Parallel lines have the same slope: If line 1 has slope m₁ and line 2 has slope m₂, then lines are parallel when m₁ = m₂.

Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals: If m₁ × m₂ = -1, the lines are perpendicular.

Example: A line has slope 2/3. What is the slope of a line parallel to it? Perpendicular to it?

Parallel slope: 2/3 (same slope)
Perpendicular slope: -3/2 (negative reciprocal)

Verification: (2/3) × (-3/2) = -6/6 = -1 ✓

2. Equation Forms of Lines

Slope-Intercept Form

y = mx + b

This is the most common form. Here, m is the slope and b is the y-intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis).

Example: Write the equation of a line with slope 3 and y-intercept 5.

y = 3x + 5

Reading from the equation: m = 3, b = 5
The line crosses the y-axis at (0, 5) and rises 3 units for every 1 unit right.

Point-Slope Form

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

Use this form when you know the slope m and a point (x₁, y₁) on the line. It's especially useful for writing equations quickly.

Example: Write the equation of a line with slope -2 passing through (3, 7).

y - 7 = -2(x - 3)

Converting to slope-intercept form:
y - 7 = -2x + 6
y = -2x + 13

Standard Form

Ax + By = C

In standard form, A, B, and C are integers, and typically A is positive. This form is useful for finding intercepts quickly.

To find intercepts from standard form:

  • x-intercept: Set y = 0 and solve for x
  • y-intercept: Set x = 0 and solve for y

Example: Find the intercepts of 3x + 4y = 12.

x-intercept: Set y = 0
3x + 4(0) = 12 → 3x = 12 → x = 4
Point: (4, 0)

y-intercept: Set x = 0
3(0) + 4y = 12 → 4y = 12 → y = 3
Point: (0, 3)

Converting Between Forms

You should be comfortable converting between all three forms:

Example: Convert 2x + 3y = 15 to slope-intercept form.

3y = -2x + 15
y = (-2/3)x + 5

Reading the equation: slope = -2/3, y-intercept = 5

3. Graphing Lines

Graphing from Slope-Intercept Form

Given y = mx + b:

  1. Plot the y-intercept at (0, b)
  2. Use the slope m to find a second point: move "rise" units up/down and "run" units right
  3. Draw a line through the two points

Example: Graph y = (3/4)x - 2.

Step 1: Plot y-intercept at (0, -2)
Step 2: Slope = 3/4 means rise 3, run 4
From (0, -2), move up 3 and right 4 to get (4, 1)
Step 3: Draw line through (0, -2) and (4, 1)

Graphing from Two Points

If you're given two points:

  1. Plot both points on the coordinate plane
  2. Draw a straight line connecting them
  3. Extend the line in both directions

If you need the equation, first calculate the slope, then use point-slope form with either point.

Graphing Horizontal and Vertical Lines

Horizontal lines: Form y = k (where k is a constant). All points have the same y-coordinate.

Vertical lines: Form x = h (where h is a constant). All points have the same x-coordinate.

Examples:
y = 3 is a horizontal line through (0, 3), parallel to the x-axis
x = -2 is a vertical line through (-2, 0), parallel to the y-axis

Reading Information from Graphs

When given a graph of a line, you can determine:

  • y-intercept: Where the line crosses the y-axis
  • x-intercept: Where the line crosses the x-axis
  • Slope: Pick two clear points and use the slope formula
  • Direction: Positive slope rises left to right; negative slope falls

Example: A line passes through (0, 4) and (2, 8). What is its equation?

y-intercept: b = 4
Slope: m = (8 - 4)/(2 - 0) = 4/2 = 2
Equation: y = 2x + 4

4. Applications and Problem-Solving

Writing Equations from Word Problems

Many ACT problems describe a linear relationship in words. Look for:

  • Initial value: This is the y-intercept (b)
  • Rate of change: This is the slope (m)
  • Variable relationships: Identify what x and y represent

Example: A phone plan costs $25 per month plus $0.10 per text message. Write an equation for total cost C based on number of texts t.

Initial value (monthly fee): b = 25
Rate of change (per text): m = 0.10
Equation: C = 0.10t + 25

Interpretation: With 100 texts, C = 0.10(100) + 25 = $35

Finding Where Lines Intersect

The intersection point of two lines satisfies both equations simultaneously. This is a system of equations (covered more in Unit 4).

Example: Where do y = 2x + 1 and y = -x + 7 intersect?

Set the equations equal:
2x + 1 = -x + 7
3x = 6
x = 2

Substitute back: y = 2(2) + 1 = 5
Intersection point: (2, 5)

Verification: Check in second equation: y = -(2) + 7 = 5 ✓

Distance and Midpoint on Lines

Distance formula between points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Midpoint formula:

M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)

Example: Find the distance and midpoint between (1, 2) and (7, 10).

Distance:
d = √[(7-1)² + (10-2)²] = √[36 + 64] = √100 = 10

Midpoint:
M = ((1+7)/2, (2+10)/2) = (8/2, 12/2) = (4, 6)

Linear Inequalities

Linear inequalities like y > 2x + 3 represent regions on the coordinate plane:

  • Solid line: Use for ≤ or ≥ (points on the line are included)
  • Dashed line: Use for < or > (points on the line are excluded)
  • Shading: Shade above the line for y > or y ≥; below for y < or y ≤

Example: Graph y ≤ -x + 4.

Step 1: Graph y = -x + 4 with a solid line (because of ≤)
Step 2: Shade below the line (y is less than or equal to)
Test point: Check (0, 0): Is 0 ≤ 4? Yes, so (0, 0) should be in the shaded region ✓

Common Mistakes and Tips

Mistake 1: Calculating slope incorrectly. Always use (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁), not (x₂ - x₁)/(y₂ - y₁).

Mistake 2: Confusing slope and y-intercept. In y = mx + b, m is slope and b is y-intercept.

Mistake 3: Forgetting negative reciprocals for perpendicular lines. If slope is 3, perpendicular slope is -1/3, not -3.

Tip 1: Use point-slope form for quick equation writing. It saves time when you know a point and the slope.

Tip 2: Check your answer. Plug one of the given points into your equation to verify.

Tip 3: Memorize special cases. Horizontal lines: y = k, slope = 0. Vertical lines: x = h, slope = undefined.
