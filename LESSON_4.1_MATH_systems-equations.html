Systems of equations involve finding values that satisfy two or more equations simultaneously. On the ACT, you'll encounter systems of linear equations, systems with quadratics, and word problems that require setting up and solving systems. Mastering substitution, elimination, and graphical methods will enable you to solve these problems efficiently and accurately.

1. Understanding Systems of Equations

What is a System of Equations?

A system of equations is a set of two or more equations that share the same variables. The solution to a system is the set of values that makes all equations true simultaneously.

Example system:

  • 2x + y = 7
  • x - y = 2

The solution (x, y) = (3, 1) satisfies both equations because 2(3) + 1 = 7 and 3 - 1 = 2.

Types of Solutions

One solution: The lines intersect at exactly one point (most common on the ACT).

No solution: The lines are parallel and never intersect (inconsistent system).

Infinitely many solutions: The lines are identical; every point on the line is a solution (dependent system).

Examples:

One solution: x + y = 5 and x - y = 1 → Solution: (3, 2)

No solution: 2x + y = 3 and 2x + y = 5 → Parallel lines, no intersection

Infinitely many solutions: x + y = 4 and 2x + 2y = 8 → Same line

2. The Substitution Method

When to Use Substitution

Use substitution when one equation is already solved for a variable, or can easily be solved for a variable.

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Solve one equation for one variable in terms of the other.

Step 2: Substitute this expression into the other equation.

Step 3: Solve the resulting equation for the remaining variable.

Step 4: Substitute back to find the other variable.

Step 5: Check your solution in both original equations.

Example: Solve the system:
y = 2x - 1
3x + y = 14

Step 1: First equation is already solved for y.

Step 2: Substitute y = 2x - 1 into the second equation:
3x + (2x - 1) = 14

Step 3: Solve for x:
5x - 1 = 14
5x = 15
x = 3

Step 4: Substitute x = 3 back into y = 2x - 1:
y = 2(3) - 1 = 6 - 1 = 5

Solution: (x, y) = (3, 5)

Check in second equation: 3(3) + 5 = 9 + 5 = 14 ✓

More Complex Example

Example: Solve the system:
2x + 3y = 8
x - y = 3

Step 1: Solve the second equation for x:
x = y + 3

Step 2: Substitute into the first equation:
2(y + 3) + 3y = 8

Step 3: Solve for y:
2y + 6 + 3y = 8
5y + 6 = 8
5y = 2
y = 2/5

Step 4: Find x:
x = y + 3 = 2/5 + 3 = 2/5 + 15/5 = 17/5

Solution: (x, y) = (17/5, 2/5)

Check: 2(17/5) + 3(2/5) = 34/5 + 6/5 = 40/5 = 8 ✓

3. The Elimination Method

When to Use Elimination

Use elimination when both equations are in standard form (Ax + By = C) and neither variable is already isolated. Elimination is often faster than substitution for these systems.

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Align the equations vertically by variables.

Step 2: Multiply one or both equations by constants so that one variable has opposite coefficients.

Step 3: Add the equations to eliminate one variable.

Step 4: Solve for the remaining variable.

Step 5: Substitute back to find the other variable.

Example: Solve the system:
2x + y = 7
x - y = 2

Step 1: Equations are already aligned.

Step 2: Notice that y has opposite coefficients (+1 and -1), so no multiplication needed.

Step 3: Add the equations:
(2x + y) + (x - y) = 7 + 2
3x = 9

Step 4: Solve for x:
x = 3

Step 5: Substitute x = 3 into first equation:
2(3) + y = 7
6 + y = 7
y = 1

Solution: (x, y) = (3, 1)

Example Requiring Multiplication

Example: Solve the system:
3x + 2y = 12
5x - 3y = 1

Strategy: Eliminate y by making coefficients opposite.
Multiply first equation by 3: 9x + 6y = 36
Multiply second equation by 2: 10x - 6y = 2

Add the equations:
(9x + 6y) + (10x - 6y) = 36 + 2
19x = 38
x = 2

Substitute x = 2 into first equation:
3(2) + 2y = 12
6 + 2y = 12
2y = 6
y = 3

Solution: (x, y) = (2, 3)

Check in second equation: 5(2) - 3(3) = 10 - 9 = 1 ✓

4. Systems with Non-Linear Equations

Linear and Quadratic Systems

When one equation is linear and one is quadratic, substitution is typically the best method. Solve the linear equation for one variable, then substitute into the quadratic equation.

Example: Solve the system:
y = x² - 4
y = 2x + 1

Step 1: Both equations are solved for y, so set them equal:
x² - 4 = 2x + 1

Step 2: Rearrange to standard form:
x² - 2x - 5 = 0

Step 3: Use the quadratic formula:
x = (2 ± √(4 + 20))/2 = (2 ± √24)/2 = (2 ± 2√6)/2 = 1 ± √6

Step 4: Find corresponding y values:
For x = 1 + √6: y = 2(1 + √6) + 1 = 3 + 2√6
For x = 1 - √6: y = 2(1 - √6) + 1 = 3 - 2√6

Solutions: (1 + √6, 3 + 2√6) and (1 - √6, 3 - 2√6)

Systems with Two Quadratics

For systems with two quadratic equations, look for opportunities to eliminate terms through addition or subtraction.

Example: Solve the system:
x² + y² = 25
x² - y² = 7

Add the equations:
2x² = 32
x² = 16
x = ±4

Subtract the equations:
2y² = 18
y² = 9
y = ±3

Four solutions: (4, 3), (4, -3), (-4, 3), (-4, -3)

Check (4, 3): 16 + 9 = 25 ✓ and 16 - 9 = 7 ✓

5. Word Problems and Applications

Setting Up Systems from Word Problems

Step 1: Identify the unknowns and assign variables.

Step 2: Translate each condition into an equation.

Step 3: Solve the system using substitution or elimination.

Step 4: Check that your answer makes sense in context.

Example: Adult tickets cost $12 and child tickets cost $8. A family buys 7 tickets for a total of $68. How many of each type did they buy?

Let: a = adult tickets, c = child tickets

Equation 1 (total tickets): a + c = 7
Equation 2 (total cost): 12a + 8c = 68

Solve equation 1 for a: a = 7 - c

Substitute into equation 2:
12(7 - c) + 8c = 68
84 - 12c + 8c = 68
84 - 4c = 68
-4c = -16
c = 4

Find a: a = 7 - 4 = 3

Answer: 3 adult tickets and 4 child tickets

Check: 3 + 4 = 7 ✓ and 12(3) + 8(4) = 36 + 32 = 68 ✓

Mixture Problems

Example: A chemist needs to mix a 20% acid solution with a 50% acid solution to create 30 liters of a 35% acid solution. How much of each should be used?

Let: x = liters of 20% solution, y = liters of 50% solution

Equation 1 (total volume): x + y = 30
Equation 2 (acid content): 0.20x + 0.50y = 0.35(30)

Simplify equation 2: 0.20x + 0.50y = 10.5

Solve equation 1 for y: y = 30 - x

Substitute:
0.20x + 0.50(30 - x) = 10.5
0.20x + 15 - 0.50x = 10.5
-0.30x = -4.5
x = 15

Find y: y = 30 - 15 = 15

Answer: 15 liters of each solution

Distance-Rate-Time Problems

Example: A boat travels 36 miles downstream in 2 hours. The return trip upstream takes 3 hours. Find the speed of the boat in still water and the speed of the current.

Let: b = boat speed in still water, c = current speed

Downstream: (b + c) × 2 = 36
Upstream: (b - c) × 3 = 36

Simplify:
b + c = 18
b - c = 12

Add equations:
2b = 30
b = 15

Find c: c = 18 - 15 = 3

Answer: Boat speed = 15 mph, current speed = 3 mph

6. Special Cases and Problem-Solving Strategies

Recognizing No Solution Systems

If elimination leads to a false statement like 0 = 5, the system has no solution (parallel lines).

Example:
2x + y = 3
4x + 2y = 10

Multiply first equation by 2: 4x + 2y = 6
Second equation: 4x + 2y = 10

Subtracting gives: 0 = -4 (False!)

Conclusion: No solution (parallel lines)

Recognizing Infinite Solutions

If elimination leads to a true statement like 0 = 0, the system has infinitely many solutions (same line).

Example:
x + 2y = 4
2x + 4y = 8

Multiply first equation by 2: 2x + 4y = 8
Second equation: 2x + 4y = 8

Subtracting gives: 0 = 0 (True!)

Conclusion: Infinitely many solutions (same line)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Sign errors. When distributing a negative or multiplying equations, track signs carefully.

Mistake 2: Forgetting to substitute back. After finding one variable, always substitute to find the other.

Mistake 3: Not checking the solution. Always verify your answer satisfies both original equations.

Mistake 4: Choosing the wrong method. Use substitution when a variable is isolated; use elimination when equations are in standard form.

ACT-Specific Tips

Tip 1: Look for answer choices that can help you work backwards or eliminate options.

Tip 2: If asked for x + y or 2x - y, consider whether you can find this directly without solving for individual variables.

Tip 3: For graphical questions, visualize where lines might intersect based on their slopes and intercepts.

Tip 4: When time is limited, elimination is often faster than substitution for standard-form equations.
