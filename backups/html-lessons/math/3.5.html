Inequalities appear 2-4 times per ACT Math test, testing your ability to solve algebraic inequalities and graph them on number lines or coordinate planes. The most important rule: flip the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number.

1. Solving Algebraic Inequalities

  • Treat inequality signs (<, >, ≤, ≥) like an equals sign:
    • Use all normal algebra rules: add, subtract, combine like terms
    • Isolate the variable just like solving equations
    • The inequality sign stays the same for most operations
  • CRITICAL RULE: Flip the sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative
    • This is the #1 mistake students make with inequalities!
    • Example: -2x + 5 < 11
    • Subtract 5: -2x < 6
    • Divide by -2: x > -3 (sign flipped from < to >!)
  • When NOT to flip the sign:
    • ONLY flip when you multiply or divide BOTH SIDES by a negative
    • Don't flip just because you see a negative number somewhere!
    • Example: 3x + 6 > x - 4
    • Combine: 2x > -10
    • Divide by 2 (positive): x > -5 (sign stays the same!)
  • Step-by-step process:
    • Step 1: Move all variable terms to one side
    • Step 2: Move all number terms to the other side
    • Step 3: Combine like terms
    • Step 4: Divide to isolate variable (flip sign if dividing by negative!)

2. Graphing Inequalities on a Number Line

  • Two types of circles on number lines:
    • Open circle (○): Use for > and < (strict inequalities)
    • The endpoint is NOT included in the solution
    • Example: x > 3 has an open circle at 3
    • Solid circle (●): Use for ≥ and ≤ (inclusive inequalities)
    • The endpoint IS included in the solution
    • Example: x ≥ 3 has a solid circle at 3
  • Which direction to shade:
    • Greater than (> or ≥): Shade to the RIGHT
    • Example: x ≥ 10/3 → solid circle at 10/3, shade right
    • Less than (< or ≤): Shade to the LEFT
    • Example: x < -5 → open circle at -5, shade left
  • Quick memory trick:
    • The inequality sign "points" to the smaller values
    • So x > 3 means x is to the RIGHT of 3 (bigger values)
    • And x < 3 means x is to the LEFT of 3 (smaller values)

3. Graphing Linear Inequalities in the Coordinate Plane

  • Three-step process for graphing linear inequalities:
    • Step 1: Rearrange into y = mx + b form (slope-intercept)
    • Step 2: Plot the line (dashed or solid based on inequality)
    • Step 3: Shade the solution region (above or below the line)
  • Dashed vs. solid lines:
    • Dashed line: Use for > and < (strict inequalities)
    • Points ON the line are NOT part of the solution
    • Solid line: Use for ≥ and ≤ (inclusive inequalities)
    • Points ON the line ARE part of the solution
  • Where to shade:
    • Greater than (> or ≥): Shade ABOVE the line
    • Example: y ≥ 2x + 3 → solid line, shade above
    • Less than (< or ≤): Shade BELOW the line
    • Example: y < -x + 2 → dashed line, shade below
  • Example: Graph 2x + 3y ≥ 6
    • Step 1: Solve for y: 3y ≥ -2x + 6 → y ≥ (-2/3)x + 2
    • Step 2: Draw solid line (because of ≥)
    • Step 3: Shade above the line (because of ≥)

4. Systems of Inequalities and Advanced Graphing

  • Systems of inequalities (multiple inequalities on same graph):
    • Graph each inequality separately on the same coordinate plane
    • The solution is where the shaded regions OVERLAP
    • Example: y < 2x - 4 AND y > -(1/3)x - 2
    • Graph both lines, shade both regions, find the overlap
  • Parabola inequalities (follow same rules as lines):
    • Greater than (> or ≥): Shade ABOVE the parabola
    • Less than (< or ≤): Shade BELOW the parabola
    • Use dashed curve for > and <, solid curve for ≥ and ≤
  • Circle and ellipse inequalities (different shading rules):
    • Greater than (> or ≥): Shade OUTSIDE the circle/ellipse
    • Example: (x-2)² + (y-1)² > 4 → shade outside the circle
    • Less than (< or ≤): Shade INSIDE the circle/ellipse
    • Example: (x-2)²/4 + (y-1)²/9 ≤ 1 → shade inside the ellipse
  • When you'll see advanced inequalities:
    • Parabola/circle/ellipse inequalities appear in the LAST 10 questions
    • These are for students aiming for 30+ scores
    • Master linear inequalities first before worrying about these!

5. Test-Taking Tips for Inequalities

  • Write "FLIP SIGN" when dividing by negatives
    • This is the most common mistake on inequality questions
    • Circle any negative you're dividing by to remind yourself
    • Double-check the final sign before selecting your answer
  • Use test points to check your graph shading
    • Pick a point in the shaded region (like (0,0) if it's not on the line)
    • Plug it into the original inequality
    • If it makes the inequality true, you shaded correctly!
    • If false, you shaded the wrong side
  • Remember: Open vs solid circles/lines
    • < and > → open circle/dashed line
    • ≤ and ≥ → solid circle/solid line
    • ACT answer choices often differ only in this detail!
  • For systems, look for the overlap
    • The solution to a system is where ALL shaded regions overlap
    • This is usually the darkest shaded area on the graph
    • Test a point in the overlap to verify it satisfies all inequalities
  • Circle/ellipse shading is opposite of parabola/line shading
    • Lines/parabolas: > means shade above
    • Circles/ellipses: > means shade outside
    • This catches many students—don't mix them up!
