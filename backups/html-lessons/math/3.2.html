Fractions appear frequently on the ACT Math test, both as standalone problems and within larger algebra questions. This chapter covers ALL the fundamental techniques you need to answer any fraction question quickly and efficiently—from combining fractions with variables to using your calculator to convert between fractions and decimals.

1. Combining Fractions (Adding and Subtracting)

  • To add or subtract fractions, you MUST have the same denominator:
    • You cannot add 1/2 + 2/5 directly—the denominators are different
    • First find the least common denominator (LCD)
    • Then add or subtract the numerators while keeping the denominator the same
  • Quick trick for finding the least common denominator:
    • Multiply the denominators together
    • Example: For 1/2 + 2/5, LCD = 2 × 5 = 10
    • This always works, even if it's not the smallest possible denominator
    • On the ACT, any common denominator is fine—you don't need the smallest one
  • Step-by-step process for combining fractions:
    • Example: 1/2 + 2/5
    • Step 1: Find LCD = 2 × 5 = 10
    • Step 2: Convert each fraction: 1/2 = 5/10, and 2/5 = 4/10
    • Step 3: Add numerators: 5/10 + 4/10 = 9/10
  • Fractions with variables in denominators (harder but same concept):
    • Example: 1/x + 3/(x-4)
    • LCD = x(x-4) [multiply the denominators]
    • Multiply first fraction by (x-4)/(x-4): (x-4)/[x(x-4)]
    • Multiply second fraction by x/x: 3x/[x(x-4)]
    • Add: (x-4+3x)/[x(x-4)] = (4x-4)/[x(x-4)]

2. Dividing Fractions ("Keep, Flip, Change")

  • The "Keep, Flip, Change" method for dividing fractions:
    • Keep the first fraction exactly as it is
    • Flip the second fraction (take the reciprocal)
    • Change the division sign to multiplication
    • Example: (2/3) ÷ (4/5) = (2/3) × (5/4) = 10/12 = 5/6
  • This works even when dividing a fraction by a whole number:
    • Example: (2/3) ÷ 3
    • Write 3 as 3/1, then flip to get 1/3
    • Result: (2/3) × (1/3) = 2/9
  • The reciprocal of a fraction:
    • Flip the numerator and denominator
    • Reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3
    • Reciprocal of 5 is 1/5 (since 5 = 5/1)
    • Reciprocal of x/(x-2) is (x-2)/x
  • Why "keep, flip, change" works:
    • Dividing by a number is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal
    • Example: 10 ÷ 2 = 10 × (1/2) = 5
    • Same principle applies to fractions

3. Simplifying Fractions (Critical Rules!)

  • Rule: You can ONLY simplify if you can divide ALL terms by the same number
    • Example: (2x+10)/4 CAN be simplified (divide all by 2) → (x+5)/2
    • Example: (8x+3)/4 CANNOT be simplified (can't divide 3 by 4)
    • Common mistake: (2x+10)/4 ≠ (x+10)/2 ❌ (You MUST divide ALL terms!)
  • When you CANNOT simplify, split the fraction instead:
    • Example: (8x+3)/4 = 8x/4 + 3/4 = 2x + 3/4
    • You CAN split numerators: (a+b)/c = a/c + b/c ✓
    • You CANNOT split denominators: a/(b+c) ≠ a/b + a/c ❌
  • Simplifying with variables in the denominator:
    • Example: 3/(6x+30) CAN be simplified → 3/[3(2x+10)] → 1/(2x+10)
    • Example: 3/(6x+10) CANNOT be simplified (can't divide 10 by 3)
    • Same rule: ALL terms must be divisible by the same number
  • CRITICAL: You cannot split denominators!
    • 3/(6x+10) ≠ 3/6x + 3/10 ❌ WRONG!
    • This is one of the most common mistakes students make
    • When in doubt, leave the denominator together

4. Getting Rid of Fractions (Multiply by LCD)

  • For equations with only numbers in denominators, eliminate fractions:
    • Find the least common multiple (LCM) of all denominators
    • Multiply EVERY term by the LCM
    • All fractions will disappear, leaving only whole numbers
    • Then solve the equation normally
  • Example: (1/4)x + (1/2) = (3/8)x
    • LCM of 4, 2, and 8 is 8
    • Multiply all terms by 8: (1/4)x×8 + (1/2)×8 = (3/8)x×8
    • Simplify: 2x + 4 = 3x
    • Solve: x = 4
  • More complex example with subtraction:
    • Example: (3x/5) - (2x/6) - (1/2) = (x/10)
    • LCM of 5, 6, 2, and 10 is 30
    • Multiply all terms by 30: 18x - 10x - 15 = 3x
    • Simplify: 8x - 15 = 3x
    • Solve: 5x = 15, so x = 3
  • When to use this method:
    • If you struggle with fractions, ALWAYS use this method!
    • It turns difficult fraction problems into easy whole number problems
    • Works great when ALL denominators are numbers (not variables)

5. Calculator Strategies for Fractions

  • Strategy #1: Turn fractions into decimals
    • Example: (3/4)x - (2/5) = (1/2)x becomes 0.75x - 0.4 = 0.5x
    • Solve: 0.75x = 0.5x + 0.4 → 0.25x = 0.4 → x = 1.6
    • Then convert 1.6 back to a fraction if needed, or check answer choices
    • This avoids dealing with fractions entirely!
  • Strategy #2: Convert decimals to fractions using your calculator
    • TI-83/84: Enter decimal → Press MATH → Select "Frac" → Press ENTER
    • TI-30XS: Enter decimal → Press 2nd → Press PRB → Select F◂▸D → Press ENTER
    • If you have a different calculator, YouTube has tutorials for your model
  • Strategy #3: Use parentheses properly!
    • ALWAYS put the entire numerator in parentheses
    • ALWAYS put the entire denominator in parentheses
    • Example: To enter (2x+1)/(x-3), type: (2x+1)÷(x-3)
    • Without parentheses, calculator follows order of operations incorrectly
  • Strategy #4: Compare answer choices as decimals
    • If you solved to get x = 1.6 (decimal)
    • And answer choices are fractions like 8/5, 5/3, 13/10, etc.
    • Enter each fraction in calculator to see which equals 1.6
    • 8/5 = 1.6 ✓ That's your answer!
  • Strategy #5: Backsolving
    • If the question asks "what is the value of x?" with answer choices
    • Plug each answer choice back into the original equation
    • Use your calculator to check which one makes the equation true
    • This is often faster than solving algebraically!

6. Test-Taking Tips for Fractions

  • Use your calculator strategically
    • For numerical fractions: let calculator do the work
    • For algebraic fractions: write out steps carefully
    • Save mental energy for the hard problems
  • Use substitution for "equivalent" questions
    • If asked "which expression is equivalent," plug in a test value
    • Example: If the original has x, try x = 2
    • Calculate both expressions—they should give the same answer
    • This is often faster than algebraic manipulation
  • Remember the "cannot split denominators" rule
    • a/(b+c) ≠ a/b + a/c ❌ This is ALWAYS wrong!
    • But (a+b)/c = a/c + b/c ✓ Splitting numerators is OK
    • This mistake appears in wrong answer choices frequently
  • When in doubt, eliminate fractions
    • If all denominators are numbers, multiply by LCM
    • If dealing with fractions makes you nervous, turn them into decimals
    • Both methods turn hard problems into easy ones
  • Master "Keep, Flip, Change" for division
    • This simple rule handles ALL fraction division
    • Keep first, flip second, change to multiplication
    • Works every time, no exceptions

Key Takeaways

  • ✓To eliminate fractions from equations, multiply all terms by the LCM—this turns fraction problems into whole number problems
  • ✓Use your calculator to convert between fractions and decimals, backsolve with answer choices, and always use parentheses around numerators and denominators
