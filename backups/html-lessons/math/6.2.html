Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring. The ACT tests basic probability calculations, independent events, and dependent events (with and without replacement).

1. Basic Probability Formula

  • The fundamental probability formula:
    • Probability = Desired Outcomes ÷ Total Possible Outcomes
    • Always expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage
    • Probability values range from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain)
  • Example: A bag contains 38 candies: 18 blue, 10 red, 5 yellow, 3 green, 2 pink
    • What is the probability of selecting a yellow candy?
    • Desired outcomes = 5 (yellow candies)
    • Total outcomes = 38 (total candies)
    • Probability = 5/38
  • Finding probability of NOT selecting something:
    • Count everything that ISN'T the unwanted outcome
    • Example: Probability of NOT selecting blue candy
    • Blue candies = 18, so NOT blue = 38 - 18 = 20
    • Probability = 20/38 = 10/19

2. Multiple Events (AND Rule)

  • CRITICAL: When events happen in sequence, MULTIPLY their probabilities
    • The word "and" means multiply
    • P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
    • Common mistake: Adding probabilities instead of multiplying
  • Example: Select a red candy AND THEN a green candy
    • First selection: P(red) = 10/38
    • Second selection: P(green) = 3/37 (only 37 left after first pick)
    • P(red AND green) = (10/38) × (3/37) = 30/1406 = 15/703
  • Without replacement vs. with replacement:
    • Without replacement: Total decreases after each selection
    • After picking first candy, only 37 remain (as shown above)
    • With replacement: Total stays the same (item returned)
    • If candy is replaced: P(red AND green) = (10/38) × (3/38)

3. Multiple Possibilities (OR Rule)

  • When you want probability of one event OR another event:
    • The word "or" means add
    • P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
    • This applies when events are mutually exclusive (can't both happen)
  • Example: Probability of selecting red OR yellow candy
    • P(red) = 10/38
    • P(yellow) = 5/38
    • P(red OR yellow) = 10/38 + 5/38 = 15/38
  • Quick method when events don't overlap:
    • Count total desired outcomes directly
    • Red or yellow = 10 + 5 = 15 candies total
    • Probability = 15/38 (same result, faster!)

4. Probability with Geometric Scenarios

  • Some ACT probability questions use area or length:
    • Probability = Desired Area ÷ Total Area
    • Or: Probability = Desired Length ÷ Total Length
    • Same concept as basic probability, just with geometric measurements
  • Example: Dartboard with target circle inside square board
    • Square board: 10 inches × 10 inches = 100 square inches
    • Target circle: radius 3 inches, area = π(3)² = 9π square inches
    • Probability of hitting target = 9π/100
  • Example: Random point on a line segment
    • Line segment from 0 to 20
    • What's probability the point is between 5 and 12?
    • Desired length = 12 - 5 = 7
    • Total length = 20
    • Probability = 7/20

5. Common Probability Scenarios on the ACT

  • Selecting from a group:
    • Marbles in a bag
    • Cards from a deck
    • Students in a class
    • Count favorable outcomes and divide by total
  • Multiple selections:
    • ALWAYS check: with or without replacement?
    • Without replacement is more common on ACT
    • Remember to decrease the total for each subsequent pick
  • At least one success:
    • Often easier to find probability of NO successes first
    • Then: P(at least one) = 1 - P(none)
    • Example: P(at least one red) = 1 - P(no reds selected)
  • Complementary events:
    • P(event) + P(NOT event) = 1
    • If P(rain) = 0.3, then P(no rain) = 0.7
    • Use this when "NOT" probability is easier to calculate

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Basic probability = Desired Outcomes ÷ Total Outcomes; probability of NOT occurring = count everything that isn't the unwanted outcome
  • ✓Multiple events use AND rule (multiply probabilities); remember to adjust totals for "without replacement" scenarios
  • ✓Multiple possibilities use OR rule (add probabilities); for mutually exclusive events, you can count desired outcomes directly
  • ✓Geometric probability uses area or length ratios; complementary events: P(event) + P(NOT event) = 1
