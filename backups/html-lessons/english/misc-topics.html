Miscellaneous grammar questions make up about 8-10% of the ACT English Test—roughly 6-8 questions per test! This chapter covers commonly confused words, active vs. passive voice, and other important grammar rules that don't fit into other categories.

1. Commonly Confused Words

  • The ACT tests many commonly confused words
    • The most common is "affect" vs. "effect"
    • If you're unsure, go with your gut—you'll usually be correct

Affect vs. Effect

  • Affect = verb meaning to act on or produce a change in
    • Example: "Sunshine and palm trees affect my mood positively."
  • Effect = noun meaning the result of a change
    • Example: "Sunshine and palm trees have a positive effect on my mood."

Other Commonly Confused Words

  • Accept (receive) vs. Except (exclude)
  • Advice (noun - recommendation) vs. Advise (verb - to recommend)
  • Lie (to lie down) vs. Lay (to place)
  • Lose (to misplace) vs. Loose (not tight)
  • Passed (moved beyond) vs. Past (gone by in time)
  • Principal (head of school) vs. Principle (a rule)
  • Their (possessive) vs. There (location) vs. They're (they are)
  • To (toward) vs. Too (also) vs. Two (2)

2. Than vs. Then

  • Than is ONLY used for comparisons
    • Example: "I think that chocolate with almonds is much better than ice cream."
  • Then has multiple meanings
    • Used when dealing with time: "First we eat, then we clean up."
    • Means "in addition to": "I need to call my mother, and then we can leave."
    • Used with if/then statements: "If it rains, then we'll stay inside."
  • Quick rule: For a comparison, use "than." Otherwise, use "then."

3. Have vs. Of

  • Many people mistakenly use "could of" or "would of"
    • This is ALWAYS incorrect!
    • The correct forms are "could have" or "would have"
  • The words "could," "should," "would," and "might" should always be followed by "have," not "of"
    • The reason for this mistake: The contraction "could've" (short for "could have") is pronounced like "could of"
    • ❌ Incorrect: "If we left the show earlier, we could of avoided the traffic."
    • ✓ Correct: "If we left the show earlier, we could have avoided the traffic."

4. Countable vs. Non-countable Adjectives

  • A countable noun is something you can count
    • Examples: bottles of milk, hours, trophies, calories
  • A non-countable noun is something that cannot be expressed in plural form
    • Examples: time, air, milk, significance

Which Adjectives to Use

  • For COUNTABLE nouns, use: number, many, fewer, few
    • Example: "The number of ingredients" (you can count ingredients)
    • Example: "How many trophies" (you can count trophies)
    • Example: "Fewer calories" (you can count calories)
    • Example: "A few minutes" (you can count minutes)
  • For NON-COUNTABLE nouns, use: amount, much, less, little
    • Example: "The amount of time" (you can't count time itself)
    • Example: "How much significance" (you can't count significance)
    • Example: "Less whole milk" (you can't count milk itself, only containers)
    • Example: "A little time" (you can't count time itself)

5. Active Voice vs. Passive Voice

  • Active voice means the subject performs the verb's action
    • Example: "I picked up the pencil." (I performed the action)
    • Example: "The police chief announced the list of suspects." (The police chief performed the action)
  • Passive voice means the subject is acted upon by the verb
    • Example: "The pencil was picked up by me." (The pencil didn't do the action)
    • Example: "The list of suspects was announced by the police chief." (The list didn't do the action)
  • On the ACT, NEVER select an answer choice in passive voice
    • Active voice is always better than passive voice
    • Active voice is clearer, more direct, and stronger

6. Prepositional Idioms

  • Idioms are expressions stated in a certain way because it's widely accepted practice
    • Certain words must be paired with certain prepositions to be correct
    • These questions don't come up often on the ACT
  • When prepositional idioms appear, rely on what sounds best
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Bobby was afraid by the dark."
    • ✓ Correct: "Bobby was afraid of the dark."
  • Common prepositional idioms to know
    • Afraid of (not afraid by)
    • Anxious about (not anxious of)
    • Different from (not different than)
    • Responsible for (not responsible of)

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓"Affect" is a verb (to produce a change), while "effect" is a noun (the result of a change). "Than" is only for comparisons, while "then" is for time sequences or if/then statements. Always use "could have" not "could of"—the same applies to should, would, and might.
  • ✓For countable nouns (things you can count), use number, many, fewer, and few. For non-countable nouns (things you can't count in plural form), use amount, much, less, and little. For example: "fewer calories" but "less milk."
  • ✓Active voice (where the subject performs the action) is always better than passive voice (where the subject is acted upon) on the ACT. Choose "I picked up the pencil" over "The pencil was picked up by me."
  • ✓Prepositional idioms pair specific words with specific prepositions (like "afraid of" not "afraid by"). These questions are rare—when they appear, trust what sounds right. Don't try to memorize every idiom; focus on mastering other topics first.
