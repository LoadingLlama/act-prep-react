To conquer the ACT English Test, we first need to be able to identify the different parts of a sentence and know the rules for how they can and cannot be combined.

1. Understanding Clauses and Phrases

A clause is a group of words that contains both a subject and a verb. There are two types of clauses:

Independent Clauses

An independent clause can stand as a sentence by itself. It contains a complete thought with a subject and verb.

How to Identify Independent Clauses

Read the sentence and see if you can stop talking at the end. If you can stop, it's a complete sentence and an independent clause.

Examples of Independent Clauses:

  • The dog chased its tail.
  • Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast.
  • He picked it up.
  • The excited child opened up his present.

Dependent Clauses

A dependent clause cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. These clauses become dependent when you add a subordinating conjunction to the front.

Common Subordinating Conjunctions:

  • after
  • although
  • as
  • because
  • before
  • even though
  • if
  • in order to
  • once
  • since
  • that
  • though
  • unless
  • until
  • whatever
  • when
  • whenever
  • whether
  • where
  • while

How These Words Create Dependent Clauses:

  • Independent: The dog chased its tail. → Dependent: While the dog chased its tail...
  • Independent: Monique made scones. → Dependent: Although Monique made scones...
  • Independent: He picked it up. → Dependent: Since he picked it up...

Phrases

A phrase lacks a subject or verb (or both). Phrases provide additional information but cannot stand alone as sentences.

Examples of Phrases:

  • Chasing its tail... (missing subject - who is chasing?)
  • Making homemade scones... (missing subject - who is making?)
  • Picking it up... (missing subject - who is picking?)
  • Excited to open his present... (missing verb - what happened?)

Quick Test: Independent vs. Dependent vs. Phrase

Step 1: Read the sentence out loud. Can you stop talking at the end? → Yes = Independent Clause

Step 2: If you need to keep talking, cover the first word and read again. Is it now a complete sentence? → Yes = Dependent Clause | No = Phrase

2. Avoiding Sentence Fragments

A sentence fragment is missing a subject or verb (or both) and does not express a complete thought. Fragments cannot stand alone.

Common Fragment Errors:

❌ Incorrect: The student running to get to class on time.

Problem: Missing a verb - what did the student do?

✓ Correct: The student running to get to class on time dropped her water bottle.

❌ Incorrect: Excited to go to the beach and surf.

Problem: Missing a subject - who is excited?

✓ Correct: My little brother Shaun is excited to go to the beach and surf.

3. Five Golden Rules for Compound Sentences

Compound sentences contain multiple clauses (more than one subject and verb). On the ACT, multiple clauses must follow one of these 5 rules:

Rule #1: Two Separate Sentences (Period)

Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats.

Two independent clauses separated by a period.

Rule #2: Comma + FANBOYS

Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats.

Use comma + FANBOYS to join two independent clauses.

Memorize FANBOYS:

For • And • Nor • But • Or • Yet • So

Rule #3: Semicolon Between Clauses

Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats.

A semicolon works like a period on the ACT.

Rule #4: Dependent Clause + Comma + Independent Clause

While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats.

Dependent clause followed by comma, then independent clause.

Rule #5: Independent Clause + Dependent Clause (No Comma)

Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats.

Independent clause straight into dependent clause - no comma needed.

⚠️ Critical: Memorize All 5 Rules!

The ACT will use answer choices that "look" or "sound" correct but break these rules. Knowing them cold is essential!

4. Avoiding the Comma Splice Error

A comma splice occurs when you use only a comma to join two independent clauses. This is always incorrect on the ACT.

Common Comma Splice Errors:

❌ Incorrect: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.

Two independent clauses joined by only a comma = comma splice!

How to Fix Comma Splices (Using All 5 Rules):

  • Rule #1: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red. They are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #2: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, but they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #3: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red; they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #4: While it is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #5: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red though they are actually colorblind.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Independent clauses can stand alone; dependent clauses and phrases cannot
  • ✓Memorize the 5 golden rules for compound sentences and the 7 FANBOYS words
  • ✓A comma by itself CANNOT join two independent clauses (that's a comma splice!)
  • ✓Sentence fragments are missing a subject or verb and must be fixed
  • ✓Read sentences out loud to identify whether clauses are independent or dependent
