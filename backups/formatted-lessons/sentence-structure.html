Chapter 1: Sentence Structure

To conquer the ACT English Test, we first need to be able to identify the different parts of a sentence and know the rules for how they can and cannot be combined.

Clauses and Phrases

A clause is a group of words that contains both a subject (who or what is doing the action) and a verb (the action or state of being).

There are two types of clauses:

1. Independent Clauses

An independent clause can stand as a sentence by itself. It expresses a complete thought and has both a subject and a verb.

How to identify: Read the sentence out loud. If you can stop talking at the end and it feels complete, it's an independent clause.

Examples:

  • The dog chased its tail.
  • Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast.
  • He picked it up.
  • The excited child opened up his present.

2. Dependent Clauses

A dependent clause has a subject and a verb BUT cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. It needs to be attached to an independent clause.

Clauses become dependent when you add a subordinating conjunction to the front. Common subordinating conjunctions include:

after, although, as, because, before, even though, if, in order to, once, since, that, though, unless, until, whatever, when, whenever, where, whether, while

Notice how adding these words turns independent clauses into dependent clauses:

  • While the dog chased its tail...
  • Although Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast...
  • Since he picked it up...
  • When the excited child opened up his present...

3. Phrases

A phrase lacks a subject or verb or both. A phrase does not express a complete thought and can never stand alone as a sentence.

Examples:

  • Chasing its tail... (missing subject - WHO is chasing?)
  • Making some homemade peach scones for breakfast... (missing subject)
  • Picking it up... (missing subject)
  • Excited to open up his present... (missing subject)

TIP - How to tell the difference:
• Read the sentence out loud. If you can stop at the end, it's an independent clause.
• If you need to keep talking, it's either a dependent clause or a phrase.
• To distinguish between dependent clause and phrase: cover the first word. If you then have a complete sentence, it's a dependent clause. If not, it's a phrase.

Practice Exercise: Identify if each is an Independent Clause (I), Dependent Clause (D), or Phrase (P).

  1. A reference to Canadian politics in the 1980s
  2. The new clothing brand that makes clothing entirely from recycled plastic bottles is popular
  3. He created the new pair of shoes specifically for a special client with size 17 feet
  4. Because the water bottle was manufactured with two layers of stainless steel
  5. Illuminating the crime scene with a flashlight
  6. Forgetting his calculator at home was a big mistake
  7. Even though potted succulents grow far better in summer than winter
  8. Running out of time to complete his sculpture for the spring art show
  9. With no regard for his own health and safety
  10. Rain is in the forecast for the next three days

Answers:

  1. P - No verb (what about this reference?)
  2. I - Complete sentence with subject ("brand") and verb ("is")
  3. I - Complete sentence with subject ("He") and verb ("created")
  4. D - "Because" makes it dependent; cover "because" and you have a complete sentence
  5. P - No subject (WHO is illuminating?)
  6. I - Complete sentence; "Forgetting..." is the subject, "was" is the verb
  7. D - "Even though" makes it dependent
  8. P - No subject (WHO is running?)
  9. P - Prepositional phrase with no subject or verb
  10. I - Complete sentence with subject ("Rain") and verb ("is")

Sentence Fragments

A complete sentence must contain both a subject and a verb. Sentence fragments are missing a subject or a verb or both and do not express a complete thought. Sentence fragments cannot stand by themselves and are ALWAYS incorrect on the ACT.

Example 1:

Incorrect: The student running to get to class on time.

Problem: Missing a verb - what did the student do?

Correct: The student running to get to class on time dropped her water bottle.

Example 2:

Incorrect: Excited to go to the beach and surf.

Problem: Missing a subject - who is excited?

Correct: My little brother Shaun is excited to go to the beach and surf.

The 5 Golden Rules for Combining Independent Clauses

On the ACT, you'll often see compound sentences (sentences with multiple clauses). There are exactly 5 ways to correctly combine two independent clauses:

Rule #1: Two separate sentences (period)

Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats.

Rule #2: Comma + FANBOYS

Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats.

The FANBOYS are seven coordinating conjunctions: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

Rule #3: Semicolon between 2 independent clauses

Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats.

Note: A semicolon = a period on the ACT. Both sides must be complete sentences.

Rule #4: Dependent clause followed by independent clause (comma)

While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats.

Rule #5: Independent clause straight into dependent clause (no comma)

Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats.

WARNING: You must memorize these 5 rules! The ACT will try to trick you with answer choices that "look" or "sound" correct but actually break one of these rules.

Comma Splice

A comma splice occurs when you join two independent clauses with ONLY a comma (no FANBOYS). This is ALWAYS incorrect.

Example:

Incorrect: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.

Problem: Two independent clauses joined by only a comma = comma splice!

We can fix this comma splice using any of our 5 rules:

  • Rule #1: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red. They are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #2: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, but they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #3: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red; they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #4: While it is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.
  • Rule #5: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red though they are actually colorblind.

Practice Problems

For each question, select all answer choices that correctly complete the sentence.

1. Hockey is my favorite __________ I cannot skate very well.

  • a. sport, although
  • b. sport, but
  • c. sport even though

2. While penguins are clumsy __________ amazingly graceful in water.

  • a. on land, they are
  • b. on land they are
  • c. on land; they are

3. Swimming is a great __________ works all the muscle groups in the body.

  • a. workout; it
  • b. workout because it
  • c. workout, for it

4. __________ I still prefer it.

  • a. My friends say the extra hot salsa is too spicy,
  • b. Although my friends say the extra hot salsa is too spicy,
  • c. My friends say the extra hot salsa is too spicy, but

Answers and Explanations:

1. Answers: b and c

  • b. sport, but - Correct (Rule #2: comma + FANBOYS)
  • c. sport even though - Correct (Rule #5: independent + dependent, no comma)
  • a. sport, although - Incorrect (creates comma splice)

2. Answer: a only

  • a. on land, they are - Correct (Rule #4: dependent + comma + independent)
  • b. on land they are - Incorrect (missing comma after dependent clause)
  • c. on land; they are - Incorrect (can't use semicolon when first part is dependent)

3. Answers: All are correct!

  • a. workout; it - Correct (Rule #3: semicolon)
  • b. workout because it - Correct (Rule #5: independent + dependent)
  • c. workout, for it - Correct (Rule #2: comma + FANBOYS "for")

4. Answers: b and c

  • b. Although my friends say... - Correct (Rule #4: dependent + comma + independent)
  • c. ...too spicy, but - Correct (Rule #2: comma + FANBOYS)
  • a. ...too spicy, - Incorrect (comma splice!)

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Independent clauses can stand alone; dependent clauses and phrases cannot
  • ✓Memorize the 5 rules for combining independent clauses
  • ✓A comma by itself cannot join two independent clauses (comma splice)
  • ✓Sentence fragments are missing a subject or verb and cannot stand alone
  • ✓FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So - memorize these!
