Chapter 5: Pronouns

Pronouns are words that stand in for a person, place, or thing. On the ACT, you will need to know how to select the proper pronoun based on case ("he" vs. "him" or "who" vs. "whom"), make sure that a pronoun agrees with the word it is replacing, and spot ambiguous pronouns.

Pronoun Case

Pronouns have two cases: subject and object. The pronoun's role in the sentence dictates which type is correct.

Subject pronouns: I, we, you, he/she/it, they, who

Object pronouns: me, us, you, him/her/it, them, whom

For simple questions, trust your ear:

  • They went to the street festival. (subject)
  • The man handed us a stack of flyers. (object)

Pronouns in lists: Ignore the other items in the list and just read the pronoun to tell which is correct.

  • The policeman asked to speak to my friends and me.
  • His girlfriend and he went to the hockey game.

Pronouns in prepositional phrases: Always use the object case.

  • The bet between Joe and me was for just five dollars.
  • A great rivalry exists among Tim, Scott, and him.

Who vs. Whom

Here's the trick you need to memorize:

If you can replace the word with "he," "she," or "they," use "who."

If you can replace the word with "him," "her," or "them," use "whom."

Examples:

  • Who stole the apple? (He stole it)
  • To whom should I address this letter? (Address it to him)

Quick tip: If the word right after is a verb, use "who." If the word right after is a noun, use "whom."

Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns (his, hers, its, theirs, ours, yours) do NOT use apostrophes.

Important distinctions:

  • its (possessive) vs. it's (it is)
  • your (possessive) vs. you're (you are)
  • their (possessive) vs. they're (they are)
  • whose (possessive) vs. who's (who is)

Pronoun Agreement

Pronouns must agree with the antecedent (the noun that the pronoun is replacing) in terms of plurality and gender.

Examples:

  • Each attendee raised his or her drink for a toast. (singular antecedent)
  • The guests raised their drinks for a toast. (plural antecedent)
  • The entire team went on stage to pick up its trophy. (team is singular)

Ambiguous Pronouns

An ambiguous pronoun is one where we don't know exactly who or what it refers to. These are always incorrect.

Incorrect: Stephen smiled at his father as he threw the ball. (Who threw it?)

Correct: Stephen smiled at his father as his father threw the ball.

Watch out for pronouns like: he, him, his, she, her, they, them, their, it, its, this, that, these, those, some, none

Practice Problem 1

The restaurant manager asked that an order be set aside for (him/himself) after he put cheese on a plate for (him/himself).

Solution:

The answers are him and himself. Use "him" when someone else is setting aside the order. Use "himself" when he's doing it for himself (reflexive).

Practice Problem 2

Terry, (who/whom) is the top ranked basketball recruit, always dominates the player (who/whom) he is guarded by.

Solution:

The answers are who and whom. First blank: "who is" (verb after). Second blank: "whom he is guarded by" (noun after).

Practice Problem 3

When the owner of Pizza Port greets a customer, he always smiles.

A. NO CHANGE
B. greets a customer, the owner always smiles.
C. greets a customer, they always smile.
D. greets customers, he always smiles.

Solution:

The answer is B. "He" is ambiguous - we don't know if it refers to the owner or the customer. Option B clarifies by repeating "the owner."
