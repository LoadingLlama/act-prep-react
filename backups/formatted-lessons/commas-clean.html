Chapter 2: Commas

Commas are one of the most tested punctuation marks on the ACT. Understanding when to use and when NOT to use commas is essential for success.

Lists (Series)

Use commas to separate items in a series (a list of three or more items). The Oxford comma (the comma before "and" or "or") is optional on the ACT.

Correct: I bought apples, oranges, and bananas. (with Oxford comma)

Correct: I bought apples, oranges and bananas. (without Oxford comma)

Incorrect: I bought apples oranges and bananas. (missing necessary commas)

When you have only TWO items joined by "and" or "or", do NOT use a comma before the conjunction.

Correct: I bought apples and oranges.

Incorrect: I bought apples, and oranges.

Essential vs. Non-Essential Information

Non-essential information can be removed from the sentence without changing its fundamental meaning. It requires commas on both sides.

Essential information is necessary to identify what you're talking about and does NOT use commas.

To determine if information is essential, ask: "Do I need this information to know WHICH ONE I'm talking about?"

  • Essential: The student who won the prize was excited. (need to know WHICH student)
  • Non-essential: Sarah, who won the prize, was excited. (already know it's Sarah)
  • Essential: Students who study regularly tend to perform better. (only talking about students WHO STUDY)
  • Non-essential: The students, who study regularly, tend to perform better. (talking about ALL students)

Introductory Words and Phrases

Use a comma after introductory elements that come before the main clause.

  • Introductory words: However, the experiment yielded unexpected results.
  • Introductory phrases: After the storm, we assessed the damage.
  • Introductory dependent clauses: When the bell rang, students rushed to class.

Commas with Coordinate Adjectives

Coordinate adjectives are two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun. They can be separated by commas.

If you can put "and" between the adjectives OR reverse their order and the sentence still makes sense, use a comma.

  • Coordinate: It was a long, difficult test. ("long and difficult" works)
  • Cumulative: She wore a beautiful red dress. ("beautiful and red" doesn't work)

Addresses and Dates

Use commas to separate elements in addresses and dates. When these appear in the middle of a sentence, put a comma AFTER the last element too.

  • Address: She moved to Denver, Colorado, last year.
  • Full date: The event on July 4, 2023, was cancelled.
  • Month-Year only: She graduated in May 2022 from Harvard. (no comma)

Practice Problem 1

The famous scientist Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes made groundbreaking discoveries.

A. NO CHANGE
B. scientist, Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes,
C. scientist Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes,
D. scientist, Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes

Solution:

The answer is C. "Marie Curie" is essential (we need it to know which scientist), so no commas. "Who won two Nobel Prizes" is non-essential (we already know it's Marie Curie), so it needs commas on both sides.

Practice Problem 2

Running through the park on a sunny morning the jogger noticed the cherry blossoms.

A. NO CHANGE
B. morning, the jogger
C. morning the jogger,
D. morning, the jogger,

Solution:

The answer is B. "Running through the park on a sunny morning" is an introductory participial phrase. It needs a comma after it before the main clause begins.

Practice Problem 3

Students who arrive late to the exam will not receive extra time.

A. NO CHANGE
B. Students, who arrive late to the exam,
C. Students who arrive late to the exam,
D. Students, who arrive late to the exam

Solution:

The answer is A. "Who arrive late to the exam" is essential information - we need it to know WHICH students won't receive extra time. Essential information doesn't use commas.
