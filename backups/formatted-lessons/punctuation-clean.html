Chapter 3: Punctuation

Beyond commas and basic sentence structure, the ACT tests several other punctuation marks. Master these rules to tackle even the most complex punctuation questions.

Colons

A colon can be used to introduce a list, an example, an explanation, a clarification, or a definition.

Three basic rules for colons:

1. The part before a colon must be an independent clause.

Incorrect: The slide showing the most popular car colors in 2018: white, gray, and black.

Correct: The slide shows the most popular car colors in 2018: white, gray, and black.

2. Everything after a colon must be only what's being introduced.

Incorrect: Will brought his lunch to the beach: a roast beef sandwich, and he brought his friend Joey a turkey club.

Correct: Will brought his lunch to the beach: a roast beef sandwich.

3. NEVER use colons with "including," "such as," or "for example."

Incorrect: I went to the grocery store to pick up some items, including: chicken, cheese, and onions.

Correct: I went to the grocery store to pick up some items, including chicken, cheese, and onions.

Semicolons

A semicolon is the same as a period on the ACT. Both sides must be independent clauses.

Correct: The storm was approaching; we decided to cancel the picnic.

Correct: She studied hard; therefore, she passed the exam.

Dashes

Dashes can function like unnecessary information commas, parentheses, or colons.

Most commonly, 2 dashes function like commas or parentheses to separate non-essential information:

Correct: Residents of Washington D.C. — the capital of the United States — are still trying to get representation in Congress.

Important: You cannot mix punctuation. It must be a pair of commas, a pair of dashes, or a pair of parentheses.

Incorrect: Deep-dish pizza — a famous meal in Chicago, does not exist in Italy.

Correct: Deep-dish pizza — a famous meal in Chicago — does not exist in Italy.

A single dash can act as a colon (following all colon rules):

Correct: You will need the following ingredients — milk, butter, flour, and eggs.

Apostrophes

Apostrophes have two functions: possession and contractions.

Possession Rules:

  • Singular nouns: add 's (the dog's collar)
  • Plural nouns ending in s: add apostrophe after s (the dogs' collars)
  • Plural nouns not ending in s: add 's (the children's playground)

Possessive Pronouns (no apostrophe):

  • its (possessive) vs. it's (it is)
  • your (possessive) vs. you're (you are)
  • their (possessive) vs. they're (they are)
  • whose (possessive) vs. who's (who is)

Quotation Marks

Quotation marks are used for direct quotes.

Spoken quotes use commas:

Correct: Abigail said, "I will not pay until the painting is completed."

Correct: "The thunderstorm last night woke me up," Paul whispered.

Unspoken quotes have NO commas:

Incorrect: My grandfather described the shells as, "beautiful souvenirs from a past life."

Correct: My grandfather described the shells as "beautiful souvenirs from a past life."

Practice Problem 1

The museum displayed ancient artifacts, including: pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.

A. NO CHANGE
B. artifacts including pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.
C. artifacts: pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.
D. artifacts, including pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.

Solution:

The answer is D. Never use a colon with "including." The phrase "including" already introduces the list, so we just need commas.

Practice Problem 2

The teams captain announced the new strategy.

A. NO CHANGE
B. team's captain
C. teams' captain
D. teams's captain

Solution:

The answer is B. We're talking about one team's captain (singular possessive). Add 's to the singular noun "team."

Practice Problem 3

The professor — who had taught for thirty years, was retiring.

A. NO CHANGE
B. professor, who had taught for thirty years,
C. professor — who had taught for thirty years —
D. professor who had taught for thirty years

Solution:

The answer is C. When using dashes for non-essential information, you must use them in pairs. Don't mix dashes with commas.
