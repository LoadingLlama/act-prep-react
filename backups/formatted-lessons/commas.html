Chapter 2: Commas

Commas are one of the most tested punctuation marks on the ACT. Understanding when to use (and when NOT to use) commas is essential for success on the English section.

1. Lists (Series)

Use commas to separate items in a series (a list of three or more items). The Oxford comma (the comma before "and" or "or" in a list) is optional on the ACT - both versions are considered correct.

Examples:

  • Correct: I bought apples, oranges, and bananas. (with Oxford comma)
  • Correct: I bought apples, oranges and bananas. (without Oxford comma)
  • Incorrect: I bought apples oranges and bananas. (missing necessary commas)

Important: When you have only TWO items joined by "and" or "or", do NOT use a comma before the conjunction.
Correct: I bought apples and oranges.
Incorrect: I bought apples, and oranges.

2. Essential vs. Non-Essential Information

This is one of the most important comma rules on the ACT. Non-essential information (also called non-restrictive) can be removed from the sentence without changing its fundamental meaning. It requires commas on both sides. Essential information (also called restrictive) is necessary to identify what you're talking about and does NOT use commas.

The "Which One?" Test:

To determine if information is essential, ask yourself: "Do I need this information to know WHICH ONE I'm talking about?"

Examples:

  • Essential (no commas): The student who won the prize was excited.
    → We need "who won the prize" to know WHICH student
  • Non-essential (with commas): Sarah, who won the prize, was excited.
    → We already know it's Sarah; the prize information is extra
  • Essential (no commas): Students who study regularly tend to perform better.
    → We're only talking about the students WHO STUDY REGULARLY
  • Non-essential (with commas): The students, who study regularly, tend to perform better.
    → We're talking about ALL the students (and they happen to study regularly)

Key Rule: If removing the information would make it unclear what or who you're talking about, it's ESSENTIAL → no commas. If the sentence is still clear without it, it's NON-ESSENTIAL → use commas.

3. Introductory Words and Phrases

Use a comma after introductory elements that come before the main clause of the sentence. These include introductory words, phrases, and dependent clauses.

Types of Introductory Elements:

  • Introductory words: However, Therefore, Furthermore, Moreover, Nevertheless
    Example: However, the experiment yielded unexpected results.
  • Introductory phrases: Prepositional phrases, participial phrases
    Example: After the storm, we assessed the damage.
    Example: Running late for class, Maria forgot her textbook.
  • Introductory dependent clauses: Clauses beginning with subordinating conjunctions
    Example: When the bell rang, students rushed to their next class.
    Example: Because it was raining, the game was postponed.

4. Commas with Coordinate Adjectives

Coordinate adjectives are two or more adjectives that equally modify a noun. They can be separated by commas. Cumulative adjectives build on each other and should NOT be separated by commas.

The "And" Test:

If you can put "and" between the adjectives OR reverse their order and the sentence still makes sense, use a comma. Otherwise, don't.

Examples:

  • Coordinate (use comma): It was a long, difficult test.
    → "long and difficult test" ✓ "difficult, long test" ✓
  • Cumulative (no comma): She wore a beautiful red dress.
    → "beautiful and red dress" ✗ "red beautiful dress" ✗

5. Addresses and Dates

Use commas to separate elements in addresses and dates. When these appear in the middle of a sentence, remember to put a comma AFTER the last element too.

Examples:

  • Address: She moved to Denver, Colorado, last year.
    → Comma after city AND after state
  • Full date: The event on July 4, 2023, was cancelled.
    → Comma after day AND after year
  • Month-Year only: She graduated in May 2022 from Harvard.
    → NO comma between month and year when day is omitted

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

The famous scientist Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes made groundbreaking discoveries in radioactivity.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. scientist, Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes,
C. scientist Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes,
D. scientist, Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C

"Marie Curie" is essential (we need it to know WHICH scientist), so no commas around it. "Who won two Nobel Prizes" is non-essential (we already know we're talking about Marie Curie), so it needs commas on both sides.

Problem 2:

Running through the park on a sunny morning the jogger noticed the beautiful cherry blossoms.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. morning, the jogger
C. morning the jogger,
D. morning, the jogger,

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

"Running through the park on a sunny morning" is an introductory participial phrase. It needs a comma after it before the main clause begins. No comma is needed after "jogger" because the sentence continues with the main verb.

Problem 3:

The conference will be held on September 15, 2024 at the convention center.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. September 15, 2024, at
C. September 15 2024, at
D. September, 15, 2024 at

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

When a full date (month, day, year) appears in the middle of a sentence, you need commas after the day AND after the year. The year is treated as a parenthetical element.

Problem 4:

The museum displayed ancient fragile pottery from the Ming dynasty.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. ancient, fragile pottery
C. ancient, fragile, pottery
D. ancient fragile, pottery

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

"Ancient" and "fragile" are coordinate adjectives (you can say "ancient and fragile pottery" or "fragile, ancient pottery"). They need a comma between them. No comma after "fragile" because "pottery" is the noun being modified.

Problem 5:

Students who arrive late to the exam will not receive extra time.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. Students, who arrive late to the exam,
C. Students who arrive late to the exam,
D. Students, who arrive late to the exam

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A

"Who arrive late to the exam" is essential information - we need it to know WHICH students won't receive extra time (only the late ones, not all students). Essential information doesn't use commas.

Key Takeaways

  1. Lists: Use commas between items in a series of three or more. Only two items = no comma before "and/or".
  2. Essential vs. Non-essential: Use the "Which one?" test. Essential info (needed to identify) = no commas. Non-essential (extra info) = commas on both sides.
  3. Introductory elements: Use a comma after introductory words, phrases, or clauses.
  4. Coordinate adjectives: If you can add "and" between them or reverse them, use a comma.
  5. Dates and addresses: Comma after each element, including a comma AFTER the last element when it appears mid-sentence.
  6. Remember: When in doubt, read the sentence aloud. Natural pauses often (but not always) indicate where commas belong.

ACT Tip: The ACT loves to test essential vs. non-essential information. Always ask yourself: "Do I need this information to know which one I'm talking about?" This single question will help you solve many comma problems correctly.
