Chapter 3: Punctuation

Beyond commas and basic sentence structure, the ACT tests several other punctuation marks. Master these rules to tackle even the most complex punctuation questions.

1. Colons

A colon (:) introduces a list, example, explanation, clarification, or definition. To use a colon correctly, you must follow three essential rules.

The Three Colon Rules:

  1. The part before the colon must be an independent clause.
    If it can't stand alone as a complete sentence, the colon is wrong.
    ✗ The ingredients include: flour, sugar, and eggs.
    ✓ The recipe requires three ingredients: flour, sugar, and eggs.
  2. Everything after the colon must be ONLY what's being introduced.
    The sentence cannot continue to unrelated topics.
    ✗ He brought his lunch: a sandwich, and he also brought water.
    ✓ He brought his lunch: a turkey sandwich with mustard.
  3. NEVER use colons with "including," "such as," or "for example."
    These phrases already introduce information, so a colon is redundant.
    ✗ I enjoy many sports, including: tennis, soccer, and swimming.
    ✓ I enjoy many sports, including tennis, soccer, and swimming.
    ✗ We visited several cities: such as Paris, Rome, and London.
    ✓ We visited several cities: Paris, Rome, and London.

Correct Colon Examples:

  • List: She packed three essentials: water, a map, and sunscreen.
  • Single item: He wanted just one thing: respect.
  • Explanation: The results were clear: exercise improves mental health.
  • Definition: She experienced synesthesia: a condition where senses blend together.

Advanced Rule: A colon can join two independent clauses when the second clause explains, defines, or clarifies the first. This is rare on the ACT but good to know.
Example: She learned an important lesson: honesty is always the best policy.

2. Semicolons

A semicolon (;) connects two closely related independent clauses. On the ACT, treat a semicolon exactly like a period - both sides must be complete sentences.

Semicolon Rules:

  • Both sides must be independent clauses (complete sentences)
  • The clauses should be closely related in meaning
  • Can be used with transitional phrases like "however," "therefore," "moreover"

Examples:

  • ✓ The storm was approaching; we decided to cancel the picnic.
  • ✓ She studied hard; therefore, she passed the exam.
  • ✗ Running through the park; the jogger saw a deer. (first part is not a complete sentence)

3. Dashes

Dashes (—) are versatile punctuation marks that can function like commas, parentheses, or colons.

Three Uses of Dashes:

  1. Paired dashes for non-essential information (like commas or parentheses):
    The capital city — Washington D.C. — attracts millions of tourists annually.
    Important: Don't mix punctuation! Use either two commas, two dashes, or parentheses — never mix them.
    ✗ The movie — which was three hours long, felt even longer.
    ✓ The movie — which was three hours long — felt even longer.
  2. Single dash as a colon (following all colon rules):
    She had one goal — graduating with honors.
  3. Single dash for emphasis at the end (rare on ACT):
    He finally understood the problem — or so he thought.

4. Apostrophes

Apostrophes have two main functions: showing possession and forming contractions.

Possession Rules:

  • Singular nouns: Add 's
    Example: The dog's collar (one dog)
  • Plural nouns ending in s: Add apostrophe after the s
    Example: The dogs' collars (multiple dogs)
  • Plural nouns not ending in s: Add 's
    Example: The children's playground

Possessive Pronouns (NO apostrophes!):

  • its (possessive) vs. it's (it is)
  • your (possessive) vs. you're (you are)
  • their (possessive) vs. they're (they are)
  • whose (possessive) vs. who's (who is)

Pro Tip: To choose between possessive and contraction, expand the contraction. If "it is" or "you are" makes sense, use the apostrophe. Otherwise, use the possessive form.

5. Quotation Marks

Quotation marks are used for direct quotes and to indicate special usage of words.

Quotation Mark Rules:

  • Spoken quotes use commas:
    She said, "I'll be there at noon."
    "The test was difficult," he whispered.
  • Unspoken quotes have NO commas:
    ✗ He described it as, "a complete disaster."
    ✓ He described it as "a complete disaster."
  • Special usage (technical terms, sarcasm, unusual meaning):
    The "fresh" fish smelled terrible.
    Scientists defined the term "quantum entanglement."

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

The museum displayed ancient artifacts, including: pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. artifacts including pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.
C. artifacts: pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.
D. artifacts, including pottery, jewelry, and scrolls.

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: D

Never use a colon with "including." The phrase "including" already introduces the list, so we just need commas. "Including pottery, jewelry, and scrolls" is non-essential information that needs commas around it.

Problem 2:

The teams captain announced the new strategy.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. team's captain
C. teams' captain
D. teams's captain

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

We're talking about one team's captain (singular possessive). Add 's to the singular noun "team" to show possession.

Problem 3:

The experiment failed, however the researchers learned valuable lessons.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. failed; however, the
C. failed however, the
D. failed, however; the

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

We have two independent clauses. "However" is a transitional word, not a FANBOYS conjunction, so we need a semicolon before it and a comma after it.

Problem 4:

The professor — who had taught for thirty years, was retiring.

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. professor, who had taught for thirty years,
C. professor — who had taught for thirty years —
D. professor who had taught for thirty years

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C

When using dashes for non-essential information, you must use them in pairs. Don't mix dashes with commas. The information about teaching for thirty years is non-essential (we already know which professor), so it needs to be set off with matching punctuation.

Problem 5:

The manager described the new policy as, "revolutionary and game-changing."

Options:

A. NO CHANGE
B. as "revolutionary and game-changing."
C. as: "revolutionary and game-changing."
D. as "revolutionary and game-changing".

Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B

This is an unspoken quote - the manager isn't directly speaking these words in dialogue. Unspoken quotes don't use commas before the quotation marks. The period goes outside the quotation marks in American English.

Key Takeaways

  1. Colons: Must have an independent clause before them. Never use with "including," "such as," or "for example."
  2. Semicolons: Treat like periods - both sides must be complete sentences.
  3. Dashes: Can replace commas, parentheses, or colons. When paired, don't mix with other punctuation.
  4. Apostrophes: Show possession or contractions. Remember: its/it's, your/you're, their/they're.
  5. Quotation marks: Spoken quotes need commas; unspoken quotes don't.

ACT Strategy: When you see punctuation in the answer choices, identify what's on each side of the punctuation mark. Ask yourself: Are both sides independent clauses? Is one side dependent? This will quickly eliminate wrong answers.
