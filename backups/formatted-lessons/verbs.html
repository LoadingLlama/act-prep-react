Chapter 4: Verbs

Verbs questions test you on two major topics - subject-verb agreement and verb tense. The subject-verb agreement questions are trickier, as it can be difficult to find the subject and "hear" which verb is correct.

Subject-Verb Agreement

Whenever you see a verbs question on the ACT, your first job is to find the subject. The subject must agree with the verb. If the subject is singular, the verb must be singular. If the subject is plural, the verb must be plural.

Simple examples:

  • Cats love to sit in empty boxes. (plural subject, plural verb)
  • My dog barks at the mailman every day. (singular subject, singular verb)

The ACT increases difficulty by putting distance between the subject and verb. There are 3 ways the ACT will try to trick you:

1. Unnecessary information phrases between subject and verb

Cross out any unnecessary information phrases to find the subject.

Tortoises, known to live to over 150 years old, are sold at the local pet shop.

2. Prepositional phrases between subject and verb (most common)

Prepositional phrases never contain the subject or verb. Cross them out to find the subject.

The entire group of students is organizing a bake sale.

The pod of dolphins hunts as a team.

3. Group nouns that sound plural but are singular

Group nouns (class, committee, team) are singular even though the group consists of more than one person/thing.

The sheep herd waits to be sheared.

The jury agrees that the defendant deserves jail time.

Helpful Tricks

Read the subject and verb side-by-side: Once you find the subject and verb, read them next to each other and ignore the information in between.

Plug in "he/she/it" for singular, "they" for plural: Sometimes it's difficult to hear which verb matches with the subject. For singular words, plug in "he," "she," or "it." For plural words, plug in "they."

Verb Tense

For questions on verb tense, trust your ear. Read the previous sentences and look for other verbs. Make sure to match the tense in the rest of the paragraph.

Examples:

  • Last year, Margot designed a new line of summer dresses. (past tense)
  • Before he votes tomorrow, Dale will research each candidate's positions. (future tense)
  • The referee stopped the fight after the boxers refused to follow the rules. (past tense)

On the ACT, do not pick a complicated tense (has run, had ran, had been running) when a simple tense works.

Perfect Tense

We use the perfect tense when there are multiple timelines in a sentence. The perfect tense uses "has," "have," or "had" with the verb.

  • Many surfers enjoy paddling out, but few have ridden waves over ten feet tall.
  • The bison had been eating when the lions began their attack.

Irregular Verbs

For many irregular verbs, there is an "a" version and a "u" version. Remember this simple rule:

With "has," "have," or "had" → Use the "u" version

Without "has," "have," or "had" → Use the "a" version

Common irregular verbs:

  • begin → began (past) → has/have/had begun (perfect)
  • drink → drank (past) → has/have/had drunk (perfect)
  • swim → swam (past) → has/have/had swum (perfect)
  • sing → sang (past) → has/have/had sung (perfect)

Practice Problem 1

The entire class of first graders (cheer/cheers) when the ice cream truck arrives.

Solution:

The answer is cheers. "Class" is the subject, which is singular (it's a group noun). Cross out the prepositional phrase "of first graders" to see: "The entire class cheers."

Practice Problem 2

Before I woke up, Chris had already (swam/swum) twenty laps.

Solution:

The answer is swum. We have "had" before the verb, so we need the "u" version for the perfect tense: "had swum."

Practice Problem 3

In-n-Out, known for its animal style burgers, and Five Guys always tops the annual list of best burgers.

A. NO CHANGE
B. Five Guys, always topping
C. Five Guys always top
D. Five Guys, always top

Solution:

The answer is C. The subjects are "In-n-Out and Five Guys" (two restaurants = plural). We need the plural verb "top." Option C is the only one with the correct verb and no unnecessary comma.
