Sentence structure questions make up 15-20% of the ACT English Test—that's 11-15 questions per test! To master these questions, you must understand how to identify and properly combine clauses, fix fragments, and avoid comma splices.

1. Clauses, Phrases, and Fragments

Independent Clauses

  • An independent clause can stand as a sentence by itself
    • Always has a subject and a verb
    • Read the sentence out loud—if you can stop talking at the end, it's an independent clause
    • Examples: "The dog chased its tail" / "He picked it up"

Dependent Clauses

  • A dependent clause cannot stand alone as a complete sentence
    • Most often created by adding a subordinating conjunction to the front
    • Common subordinating conjunctions: after, although, as, because, before, even though, if, in order to, once, since, that, though, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whether, while, whatever
    • Examples: "While the dog chased its tail..." / "Because he picked it up..."
  • How to identify: If you feel like you need to keep talking after reading it, it's dependent
    • Cover the first word and read the rest—if it becomes a complete sentence, the original was a dependent clause
    • If it's still incomplete after covering the first word, it's a phrase

Phrases

  • A phrase lacks a subject or verb or both
    • Does not express a complete thought and can never stand alone
    • Provides additional descriptive information in a sentence
    • Examples: "Chasing its tail..." / "Excited to open up his present..."
    • All missing the subject—we don't know who is doing the action

Sentence Fragments

  • A sentence fragment is missing a subject or verb or both
    • Cannot stand by itself as a complete sentence
    • Usually sounds plain wrong when you read it aloud
    • Incorrect: "The student running to get to class on time" (missing verb)
    • Correct: "The student running to get to class on time dropped her water bottle"

2. Five Types of Compound Sentences

  • On the ACT, multiple clauses must always appear as one of these 5 types
    • The ACT tests these rules constantly—memorize all 5!
    • Answer choices that "sound good" but break these rules are wrong

Type 1: Two Separate Independent Clauses with Periods

  • Structure: Independent clause + period + Independent clause
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats."

Type 2: Comma + FANBOYS Joining 2 Independent Clauses

  • Structure: Independent clause + comma + FANBOYS + Independent clause
    • FANBOYS = For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So (memorize these!)
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats."
    • CRITICAL: Must have BOTH the comma AND the FANBOYS word

Type 3: Semicolon Between 2 Independent Clauses

  • Structure: Independent clause + semicolon + Independent clause
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats."
    • On the ACT, a semicolon functions the same as a period
    • Both sides MUST be independent clauses

Type 4: Dependent Clause Followed by Independent Clause (Comma)

  • Structure: Dependent clause + comma + Independent clause
    • Example: "While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats."
    • When dependent clause comes BEFORE independent, use a comma

Type 5: Independent Clause Straight Into Dependent Clause (No Comma)

  • Structure: Independent clause + no comma + Dependent clause
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats."
    • When independent clause comes BEFORE dependent, NO comma

3. Comma Splices

  • A comma splice joins two independent clauses with only a comma—this is ALWAYS incorrect
    • Incorrect: "It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind"
    • A comma by itself CANNOT join two independent clauses
    • Most common error students make with sentence structure

Five Ways to Fix a Comma Splice

  • Use the 5 compound sentence rules to fix comma splices
    • Rule #1 (Period): "...color red. They are actually colorblind."
    • Rule #2 (Comma + FANBOYS): "...color red, but they are actually colorblind."
    • Rule #3 (Semicolon): "...color red; they are actually colorblind."
    • Rule #4 (Dependent → Independent): "While it is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind."
    • Rule #5 (Independent → Dependent): "It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red though they are actually colorblind."

4. Spotting and Solving Sentence Structure Questions

How to Identify Sentence Structure Questions

  • Punctuation variations in answer choices
    • Periods, semicolons, commas, and FANBOYS appear in different positions
    • If you see these variations, it's a sentence structure question
  • Subject or verb forms vary between choices
    • One choice says "drove" while another says "driving"
    • Some choices have subjects/verbs, others don't
    • Watch for comma splices in these questions

Three-Step Approach to Sentence Structure Questions

  • Step 1: Find where the sentence is being "split"
    • Look for the punctuation point in the underlined portion
  • Step 2: Look left and right of the split point
    • Identify whether each side is independent, dependent, or a phrase
    • Read each side separately to test if it can stand alone
  • Step 3: Apply the sentence structure rules
    • Use the 5 compound sentence types to determine the correct answer
    • Eliminate choices that break the rules

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Independent clauses can stand alone as complete sentences, while dependent clauses (starting with subordinating conjunctions) and phrases cannot—test by reading aloud to see if you can stop talking at the end
  • ✓There are exactly 5 ways to combine clauses correctly: (1) period between independent clauses, (2) comma + FANBOYS between independent clauses, (3) semicolon between independent clauses, (4) dependent + comma + independent, (5) independent + no comma + dependent
  • ✓A comma by itself CANNOT join two independent clauses—this creates a comma splice which is always incorrect—fix using one of the 5 compound sentence rules
  • ✓To solve sentence structure questions: (1) find the split point, (2) identify whether each side is independent, dependent, or a phrase, (3) apply the appropriate rule—look for punctuation variations in answer choices as clues
