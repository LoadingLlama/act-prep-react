Understanding comma rules is one of the most important skills for the ACT English Test. Commas appear more frequently than any other punctuation mark on the ACT, and the test focuses on four essential patterns that account for nearly all comma questions. Mastering these four rules—introductory elements, nonessential information, compound sentences, and series and lists—will give you the confidence to handle comma questions quickly and accurately, turning what seems like endless punctuation confusion into a systematic, testable skill.

1. Commas with Introductory Elements

When a sentence begins with introductory words, phrases, or clauses, you need a comma to separate them from the main part of the sentence. This rule applies to dependent clauses, prepositional phrases, and transition words that appear at the beginning.

Introductory Dependent Clauses

When a dependent clause starts a sentence (beginning with words like "when," "if," "because," "although," "since," "while"), place a comma after the entire clause before the independent clause begins.

✗ Wrong: Although she studied for hours she still felt unprepared.

✓ Correct: Although she studied for hours, she still felt unprepared.

Key Pattern: [Dependent clause starting with subordinating conjunction] + COMMA + [Independent clause]

Introductory Phrases

Longer introductory phrases (generally 4+ words) need a comma. This includes prepositional phrases, participial phrases, and infinitive phrases at the start of sentences.

✓ Correct: In the early morning hours, the city streets were empty.

✓ Correct: Running through the park, she finally felt free.

✓ Correct: To understand the problem, we need more information.

Introductory Words and Transitions

Single transition words or short phrases like "however," "therefore," "moreover," "consequently," "for example," and "in fact" need commas when they start sentences.

✓ Correct: However, the results were inconclusive.

✓ Correct: For example, the data shows a clear trend.

✓ Correct: In fact, the opposite is true.

Short Introductory Elements (Special Case)

Very short introductory elements (1-3 words) typically don't need commas, unless omitting the comma causes confusion. On the ACT, if you see a short phrase without a comma, it's usually correct.

✓ Correct: Yesterday we visited the museum. (no comma needed)

✓ Correct: At noon the bell rang. (no comma needed)

2. Commas with Nonessential Information

Nonessential information (also called "nonrestrictive" elements) provides extra details that can be removed without changing the core meaning of the sentence. These elements must be set off with commas. This is one of the most heavily tested comma rules on the ACT.

Identifying Nonessential vs. Essential Information

The key test: Can you remove the element without changing what the sentence identifies or means? If yes, it's nonessential and needs commas. If removing it changes the meaning or makes the sentence unclear, it's essential and should NOT have commas.

✓ Nonessential: My sister, who lives in Boston, is visiting next week.

→ "who lives in Boston" is extra information; you can remove it

✓ Essential: Students who study regularly perform better on tests.

→ "who study regularly" identifies WHICH students; removing it changes meaning (NO commas)

Appositives (Renaming Phrases)

An appositive renames or describes a nearby noun. If the appositive provides nonessential extra detail, set it off with commas.

✓ Correct: Dr. Martinez, a renowned cardiologist, gave the keynote speech.

✓ Correct: The novel Pride and Prejudice, written by Jane Austen, remains popular today.

Parenthetical Expressions

Parenthetical expressions interrupt the main sentence with extra commentary or asides. Common examples include "in fact," "of course," "by the way," "for instance," "however," "therefore," and "I believe."

✓ Correct: The experiment, in fact, produced unexpected results.

✓ Correct: This solution, I believe, addresses the core issue.

Critical Rule: Nonessential elements need BOTH commas (one before and one after), unless they end the sentence. Never use just one comma—it's either both or neither.

3. Commas in Compound Sentences

When joining two independent clauses (complete sentences) with a coordinating conjunction, place a comma before the conjunction. The seven coordinating conjunctions are: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so (remember: FANBOYS).

The Basic Pattern

Independent clause + COMMA + FANBOYS + independent clause

✓ Correct: She completed her homework, and she studied for the test.

✓ Correct: The weather was terrible, but we decided to go anyway.

✓ Correct: I could watch the movie, or I could finish my reading.

When NOT to Use a Comma with Conjunctions

If the conjunction joins two verbs (compound predicate) but NOT two independent clauses, do not use a comma. This is a common ACT trap.

✗ Wrong: She completed her homework, and studied for the test.

✓ Correct: She completed her homework and studied for the test.

→ "studied for the test" is NOT a complete sentence (no subject), so NO comma

Testing the Pattern

To determine if you need a comma before a conjunction, check what comes after it. Can the words after the conjunction stand alone as a complete sentence? If yes, use a comma. If no, don't.

Test this sentence: The team practiced for hours and won the championship.

→ After "and": "won the championship" = incomplete (no subject) = NO comma needed

Test this sentence: The team practiced for hours, and they won the championship.

→ After "and": "they won the championship" = complete sentence = comma needed

4. Commas in Series and Lists

When listing three or more items in a series, separate them with commas. The ACT typically includes the Oxford comma (the comma before "and" or "or" in a list), though occasionally you'll see it omitted in the original passage.

Basic Series Format

Item 1, item 2, and item 3

✓ Correct: I need to buy apples, oranges, and bananas.

✓ Correct: The movie was exciting, suspenseful, and unpredictable.

Series of Phrases or Clauses

Lists can contain phrases or even short clauses, not just single words. The comma pattern remains the same.

✓ Correct: She enjoys reading novels, watching documentaries, and hiking in the mountains.

Two-Item Lists (Special Case)

When you have only TWO items joined by "and" or "or," you don't use a comma (unless they're independent clauses—see Rule 3).

✗ Wrong: I need apples, and oranges.

✓ Correct: I need apples and oranges.

Coordinate Adjectives

When two or more adjectives equally modify the same noun, separate them with commas. Test: Can you insert "and" between them or reverse their order? If yes, use a comma.

✓ Correct: It was a dark, stormy night.

→ "dark and stormy night" or "stormy, dark night" both work = comma needed

✓ Correct: She wore a beautiful silk dress.

→ "beautiful and silk dress" sounds wrong; adjectives build on each other = NO comma

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Introductory elements: Use a comma after dependent clauses, long phrases (4+ words), and transition words at the start of sentences
  • ✓Nonessential information: Set off extra details with TWO commas (before and after) unless the element ends the sentence; test by removing the element—if the core meaning stays the same, it's nonessential
  • ✓Compound sentences: Place a comma before FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) only when joining two independent clauses; if what follows the conjunction can't stand alone as a sentence, don't use a comma
  • ✓Series and lists: Use commas to separate three or more items (including the Oxford comma before "and" or "or"); with only two items, no comma is needed unless they're independent clauses
  • ✓Common ACT traps: Watch for commas incorrectly placed before conjunctions joining verbs (not clauses), single commas around nonessential elements (need both), and missing commas after introductory dependent clauses
