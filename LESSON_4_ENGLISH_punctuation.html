Advanced punctuation mastery is essential for success on the ACT English Test. Beyond commas and periods, the ACT tests your understanding of semicolons, colons, dashes, and apostrophes. These punctuation marks appear in multiple questions on every test, and understanding their precise rules eliminates confusion and builds confidence. Once you master these four categories, you'll approach punctuation questions systematically rather than relying on what "sounds right."

1. Semicolons: Connecting Related Ideas

Semicolons serve two main purposes on the ACT: joining independent clauses and separating complex items in a list. Both uses appear frequently, and understanding when to use (and not use) semicolons is critical for avoiding comma splices and run-on sentences.

Joining Two Independent Clauses

Use a semicolon to connect two closely related independent clauses (complete sentences). Both sides must be able to stand alone as sentences.

✓ Correct: The experiment failed; we needed to adjust our hypothesis.

✓ Correct: She studied every night; her dedication was impressive.

Key Rule: Think of semicolons as stronger periods. They can ONLY connect two independent clauses—never an independent clause to a dependent clause or fragment.

Semicolons with Transitional Words

When using transitional words like "however," "therefore," "moreover," "nevertheless," "consequently," or "furthermore" between two independent clauses, use a semicolon before the transition and a comma after it.

✓ Correct: The weather was terrible; however, we continued hiking.

✓ Correct: Sales increased dramatically; therefore, we expanded production.

✗ Wrong: The weather was terrible, however, we continued hiking.

→ This creates a comma splice; need semicolon before "however"

Separating Complex Items in a List

When list items themselves contain commas, use semicolons to separate the major items for clarity.

✓ Correct: We visited Portland, Oregon; Austin, Texas; and Denver, Colorado.

Common Semicolon Errors on the ACT

The ACT loves to test incorrect semicolon use, particularly connecting an independent clause to a dependent clause or fragment.

✗ Wrong: I studied all night; because I had an exam the next day.

→ "because I had an exam the next day" is a dependent clause; use a comma, not semicolon

2. Colons: Introducing Information

Colons introduce information: they signal that what follows will explain, illustrate, or list what was just mentioned. The ACT tests whether you understand the two key rules for proper colon use.

Rule #1: Independent Clause Before the Colon

The text before a colon must be a complete sentence (independent clause). This is the most commonly tested colon rule on the ACT.

✓ Correct: I need three things from the store: milk, eggs, and bread.

→ "I need three things from the store" is a complete sentence

✗ Wrong: I need: milk, eggs, and bread.

→ "I need" is NOT a complete sentence; remove the colon

What Can Follow a Colon

After a colon, you can have a list, an explanation, an example, a quote, or even another independent clause. The key is what comes BEFORE the colon must be complete.

✓ List: She visited three countries: France, Italy, and Spain.

✓ Explanation: The reason was obvious: nobody had studied.

✓ Independent clause: Remember this: practice makes perfect.

Colons vs. Semicolons

Both require an independent clause before them, but their purposes differ: semicolons connect two equal ideas, while colons introduce or explain.

✓ Semicolon (equal ideas): She loves reading; he prefers movies.

✓ Colon (explanation): She loves reading: it helps her relax.

3. Dashes: Adding Emphasis and Interruption

Dashes (—) emphasize information or set off interruptions in sentences. They function similarly to commas for nonessential information but add stronger emphasis. On the ACT, dashes must be used in pairs (like parentheses) unless the emphasized information ends the sentence.

Setting Off Nonessential Information

Use a pair of dashes to emphasize nonessential information in the middle of a sentence. This creates more dramatic emphasis than commas.

✓ Correct: The results—which surprised everyone—changed our entire approach.

✓ Correct: My friend—the one from college—moved to Seattle.

Critical Rule: Like nonessential commas, dashes must come in pairs (one before, one after) unless the emphasized element ends the sentence. Never mix punctuation types—use both dashes or both commas, not one of each.

Ending a Sentence with Emphasis

When the emphasized information comes at the end, use just one dash before it.

✓ Correct: She finally achieved her goal—admission to medical school.

✓ Correct: The answer was simple—practice more.

Dashes vs. Commas vs. Parentheses

All three can set off nonessential information, but they differ in emphasis: dashes add emphasis, commas are neutral, and parentheses minimize importance.

✓ Emphasize (dashes): The results—absolutely shocking—changed everything.

✓ Neutral (commas): The results, which were surprising, changed everything.

✓ Minimize (parentheses): The results (see page 10) changed everything.

Common ACT Dash Errors

The most common error is using only one dash when two are needed, or mixing dashes with commas for the same element.

✗ Wrong: The experiment—which took three years, produced groundbreaking results.

→ Mixing dash and comma; use both dashes or both commas

4. Apostrophes: Possession and Contraction

Apostrophes serve two main purposes: showing possession and forming contractions. The ACT tests both uses heavily, particularly the distinction between possessive forms and common homophones like "its/it's" and "your/you're."

Possession: Singular Nouns

For singular nouns, add apostrophe + s to show possession, even if the noun ends in "s."

✓ Correct: the student's backpack

✓ Correct: James's car

✓ Correct: the boss's office

Possession: Plural Nouns

For plural nouns ending in "s," add only an apostrophe after the "s." For irregular plurals not ending in "s," add apostrophe + s.

✓ Regular plural: the students' backpacks

✓ Regular plural: the teachers' lounge

✓ Irregular plural: the children's toys

Contractions: Replacing Letters

Apostrophes replace missing letters in contractions. This is where confusion with possessive pronouns occurs.

✓ Correct: it's = it is / it has

✓ Correct: you're = you are

✓ Correct: they're = they are

✓ Correct: who's = who is / who has

Common Homophone Errors

The ACT frequently tests confusion between contractions and possessive pronouns. Remember: possessive pronouns NEVER use apostrophes.

it's (contraction) = it is / it has → It's raining outside.

its (possessive) = belonging to it → The dog wagged its tail.

you're (contraction) = you are → You're doing great!

your (possessive) = belonging to you → Your test is on Monday.

they're (contraction) = they are → They're arriving soon.

their (possessive) = belonging to them → Their books are here.

there (location) = in that place → Put it over there.

Quick Test: For it's/its and similar pairs, try substituting "it is" or "it has." If the substitution works, use the apostrophe (it's). If not, use the possessive form without an apostrophe (its).

When NOT to Use Apostrophes

Never use apostrophes to form simple plurals or with possessive pronouns.

✗ Wrong: The 1990's were memorable. → ✓ Correct: The 1990s were memorable.

✗ Wrong: That book is her's. → ✓ Correct: That book is hers.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Semicolons: Connect two independent clauses or separate complex list items; both sides must be able to stand alone as complete sentences; commonly used with transitional words like "however" and "therefore"
  • ✓Colons: Introduce lists, explanations, or examples; what comes BEFORE the colon must be an independent clause; what follows can be a list, explanation, quote, or even another independent clause
  • ✓Dashes: Emphasize or set off nonessential information; must come in pairs (like parentheses) unless the emphasized element ends the sentence; never mix dashes with commas for the same element
  • ✓Apostrophes for possession: Singular nouns add 's (student's, James's); plural nouns ending in "s" add only ' (students'); irregular plurals add 's (children's)
  • ✓Apostrophes for contractions vs. possessive pronouns: it's = it is/has (its = possessive); you're = you are (your = possessive); they're = they are (their = possessive, there = location); who's = who is/has (whose = possessive)
