The parallel structure rule is one of the most frequently tested grammar concepts on the ACT English section. Parallelism ensures that items in a list or comparison are written in the same grammatical form, creating sentences that flow smoothly and clearly. When elements within a sentence have consistent structure, the writing becomes more readable and professional. Mastering parallel structure will help you quickly identify errors that many students miss, especially in lists of two items and complex comparisons.

1. What is parallel structure?

Parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for items that are joined in a sentence. When you list multiple items, compare two things, or use correlative conjunctions, each element must match in structure. This grammatical consistency makes sentences clearer and more elegant.

Items in a list can be nouns, verbs, phrases, or clauses. The key is that all items in the same list must use the same form. If the first item is a verb in the infinitive form, all other items must also be infinitives. If the first item is a noun phrase, all other items must be noun phrases.

  • Key advantages of understanding parallel structure:
    • Many students can "hear" parallelism errors without formally analyzing grammar
    • Correct answers often sound smoother and more natural
    • This is one of the most predictable question types on the ACT
  • When to watch for parallel structure:
    • Any list of two or more items joined by "and," "or," or commas
    • Comparisons using "than," "more," "less," "better," or "worse"
    • Correlative conjunctions: "either...or," "neither...nor," "not only...but also," "both...and"
    • Series of actions or descriptions in a sentence

Example 1: Basic parallel structure in lists

To prepare for the marathon, Elena committed to running every morning, eating a balanced diet, and she would get eight hours of sleep each night.

A. NO CHANGE
B. getting eight hours of sleep each night.
C. to get eight hours of sleep each night.
D. sleep for eight hours each night.
E. would sleep eight hours each night.

Solution:

  • Identify the list: The sentence lists three things Elena committed to doing.
  • Check the first two items for their grammatical form:
    • "running every morning" (gerund phrase)
    • "eating a balanced diet" (gerund phrase)
  • The third item must also be a gerund phrase to maintain parallelism.
  • Choice A ("she would get eight hours") includes a subject and verb - not parallel.
  • Choice B ("getting eight hours of sleep each night") matches the gerund form perfectly.
  • Choice C uses an infinitive ("to get") - not parallel with gerunds.
  • Choices D and E use different verb forms - not parallel.

Answer: B

2. How to identify and fix parallelism errors

Follow these four steps to identify and correct parallel structure errors efficiently:

Step 1: Locate the list or comparison

  • Look for connecting words like "and," "or," and commas that join items together.
    • Lists of three or more items are easy to spot
    • Lists of two items are harder to notice but equally important
  • Watch for comparison words: "than," "more than," "less than," "better than," "worse than."
    • These signal that two things are being compared
    • The items being compared must be grammatically parallel
  • Identify correlative conjunctions: "either...or," "neither...nor," "not only...but also," "both...and."
    • The elements following each part must be parallel
    • If "both" is followed by a noun, then "and" must also be followed by a noun

Step 2: Identify the grammatical form of the first item

  • Determine what type of grammatical structure the first item uses:
    • Noun: "courage"
    • Noun phrase: "the red car"
    • Gerund (verb + -ing): "running"
    • Infinitive: "to run"
    • Past participle: "broken"
    • Verb phrase: "has been running"
    • Prepositional phrase: "in the morning"
  • The first item usually establishes the pattern that all other items must follow.
  • Pay special attention to whether the item includes articles ("a," "an," "the") or prepositions - these often need to be repeated or omitted consistently.

Step 3: Check that all other items match the same form

  • Compare each subsequent item to the first item's structure.
    • If the first item is "to swim," check that other items are also infinitives ("to run," "to jump")
    • If the first item is "swimming," check that other items are also gerunds ("running," "jumping")
  • Watch for sneaky variations that break parallelism:
    • Adding a subject to one item but not others
    • Switching from gerunds to infinitives
    • Changing from nouns to verb phrases
  • Trust your ear - non-parallel structures often "sound wrong" even if you can't immediately identify why.

Step 4: For comparisons, ensure you're comparing equivalent things

  • Make sure you're comparing "apples to apples," not "apples to oranges."
    • Wrong: "The population of California is larger than Texas." (Compares population to state)
    • Right: "The population of California is larger than that of Texas." (Compares population to population)
  • Look for "that of," "those of," "that in," or "those at" in answer choices - these phrases often signal comparison questions.
    • "That of" stands in for a singular noun already mentioned
    • "Those of" stands in for a plural noun already mentioned
  • Alternatively, you can repeat the noun or use a possessive form to maintain parallel structure.

Example 2: Correlative conjunctions and parallel structure

The new policy not only requires employees to submit timesheets by Friday but also they must attend a mandatory training session each month.

A. NO CHANGE
B. requires them to attend a mandatory training session each month.
C. requiring them to attend a mandatory training session each month.
D. the attendance of a mandatory training session each month.
E. attendance at a mandatory training session each month.

Solution:

  • Identify the correlative conjunction: "not only...but also."
  • What follows "not only"? The verb "requires" plus an object.
  • The structure after "not only" is: "requires employees to submit timesheets by Friday."
  • What follows "but also" must be parallel to what follows "not only."
  • Choice A ("they must attend") introduces a new subject and verb - not parallel.
  • Choice B ("requires them to attend") matches the verb structure perfectly: "requires employees to submit" and "requires them to attend."
  • Choice C uses a gerund ("requiring") - not parallel with the base verb "requires."
  • Choices D and E use noun phrases instead of verb phrases - not parallel.

Answer: B

3. Common parallelism mistakes

Understanding common parallelism errors will help you spot and correct them more quickly on test day. These mistakes appear frequently on the ACT, so recognizing them gives you a significant advantage.

Mistake 1: Mixing gerunds and infinitives

  • Incorrect: "Sarah enjoys hiking, swimming, and to bike."
  • Correct: "Sarah enjoys hiking, swimming, and biking."
  • The first two items are gerunds (hiking, swimming), so the third must also be a gerund (biking), not an infinitive (to bike).

Mistake 2: Adding extra subjects or helping verbs

  • Incorrect: "The coach demanded that players arrive early, practice hard, and they should maintain good grades."
  • Correct: "The coach demanded that players arrive early, practice hard, and maintain good grades."
  • The first two items are simple verbs without additional subjects or helping verbs, so the third item should match this pattern.

Mistake 3: Lists of two items

  • Incorrect: "It's easier to ride a bike than walking."
  • Correct: "It's easier to ride a bike than to walk."
  • Lists of two are particularly tricky because non-parallel versions may sound acceptable to your ear.
  • Always check that both items use the exact same grammatical structure.

Mistake 4: Faulty comparisons

  • Incorrect: "The climate of Seattle is rainier than Portland."
  • Correct: "The climate of Seattle is rainier than that of Portland."
  • The incorrect version compares "climate" to "Portland" (a city), not to Portland's climate.
  • Use "that of," "those of," or repeat the noun to fix this error.

Mistake 5: Inconsistent prepositions or articles

  • Incorrect: "The package will arrive in the morning or afternoon."
  • Correct: "The package will arrive in the morning or in the afternoon."
  • Also correct: "The package will arrive in the morning or the afternoon."
  • If you include a preposition or article before the first item, you should either repeat it before each item or omit it before all items after the first.

Mistake 6: Breaking parallelism with correlative conjunctions

  • Incorrect: "Either you can submit the report today or waiting until Monday."
  • Correct: "Either you can submit the report today or you can wait until Monday."
  • Also correct: "You can either submit the report today or wait until Monday."
  • The placement of the first conjunction word matters - what comes after "either" must be parallel to what comes after "or."

Example 3: Complex parallel structure with comparisons

The museum's collection of Renaissance paintings is more impressive than the Modern Art Museum.

A. NO CHANGE
B. than that of the Modern Art Museum.
C. than the Modern Art Museum's.
D. compared to the Modern Art Museum.
E. in comparison with the Modern Art Museum.

Solution:

  • Identify what is being compared: "the museum's collection of Renaissance paintings."
  • The comparison word is "than" - this signals a comparison question.
  • Choice A compares "the museum's collection" to "the Modern Art Museum" (the building/institution) - not parallel.
  • We need to compare the first museum's collection to the second museum's collection.
  • Choice B ("that of the Modern Art Museum") correctly uses "that of" to stand for "the collection of" - parallel and clear.
  • Choice C ("the Modern Art Museum's") is grammatically correct because the possessive implies "the Modern Art Museum's collection" - this is also parallel.
  • However, Choice B is clearer and more explicit.
  • Choice D changes "more impressive than" to "compared to," which alters the sentence meaning - not the best choice.
  • Choice E similarly changes the comparison structure unnecessarily.
  • Between B and C, both are technically correct, but B is the strongest choice because "that of" clearly establishes the comparison between collections.

Answer: B

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Items in any list must use the same grammatical form - all nouns, all gerunds, all infinitives, or all verb phrases
  • ✓Watch for correlative conjunctions like "either...or," "neither...nor," "not only...but also," and "both...and" - elements after each part must be parallel
  • ✓In comparisons, make sure you're comparing equivalent things - use "that of" or "those of" when necessary to maintain parallel structure
  • ✓Lists of two items are harder to spot than lists of three or more - train your ear to recognize when two elements don't match in structure
  • ✓If you see "that of," "those of," or similar phrases in answer choices, the question is testing comparison parallelism
  • ✓Trust your ear - sentences with correct parallel structure flow smoothly and naturally, while non-parallel structures often sound awkward or choppy
