Mean, median, and mode are tested in 2-4 questions on every ACT Math test, making them essential statistics concepts to master. The ACT tests your ability to calculate the mean (average), find the median (middle value), identify the mode (most frequent value), and understand when each measure is most appropriate. Common traps include confusing which formula to use, forgetting to sort data before finding the median, or not recognizing the impact of outliers on these measures—mastering these fundamentals ensures you can quickly analyze any data set.

1. Mean (Arithmetic Average)

Calculating the Mean

The mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of values: Mean = (Sum of all values) ÷ (Number of values).
Example: For the data set {3, 7, 9, 11, 15}, the mean is (3 + 7 + 9 + 11 + 15) ÷ 5 = 45 ÷ 5 = 9.

Finding a Missing Value

If you know the mean and all but one value, you can find the missing value. Multiply the mean by the number of values to get the total sum, then subtract the known values.
Example: Four numbers have a mean of 12, and three of them are 8, 10, and 14. The sum is 4 × 12 = 48. The fourth number is 48 - (8 + 10 + 14) = 48 - 32 = 16.

Example 1: Finding the Mean

The test scores for five students are 78, 82, 85, 90, and 95. What is the mean score?

A. 82
B. 84
C. 85
D. 86
E. 88

Solution:

Sum: 78 + 82 + 85 + 90 + 95 = 430. Mean: 430 ÷ 5 = 86. The answer is D.

2. Median (Middle Value)

Finding the Median

First, arrange the data in ascending order. If there is an odd number of values, the median is the middle value. If there is an even number of values, the median is the average of the two middle values.
Example (odd): {3, 7, 9, 11, 15} → median is 9.
Example (even): {3, 7, 9, 11} → median is (7 + 9) ÷ 2 = 8.

When to Use Median vs. Mean

The median is less affected by outliers (extreme values) than the mean. In data sets with outliers, the median often provides a better measure of central tendency. The mean is more sensitive to every value in the data set.

Example 2: Finding the Median

The ages of six people are 22, 25, 28, 30, 35, and 40. What is the median age?

F. 28
G. 29
H. 30
J. 32
K. 35

Solution:

The data is already in order. There are 6 values (even), so the median is the average of the 3rd and 4th values: (28 + 30) ÷ 2 = 29. The answer is G.

3. Mode (Most Frequent Value)

Identifying the Mode

The mode is the value that appears most frequently in a data set. A data set can have one mode (unimodal), more than one mode (bimodal or multimodal), or no mode (if all values appear with equal frequency).
Example: {2, 4, 4, 5, 7, 9} → mode is 4.
Example: {3, 3, 5, 7, 7} → modes are 3 and 7 (bimodal).

Mode in Context

The mode is useful for categorical data (e.g., favorite colors, most common shoe size) where mean and median are not meaningful. It tells you the most typical or popular item.

Example 3: Finding the Mode

The number of books read by students in a class: 3, 5, 5, 7, 8, 8, 8, 10, 12. What is the mode?

A. 5
B. 7
C. 8
D. 10
E. 12

Solution:

Count the frequency: 3 appears once, 5 twice, 7 once, 8 three times, 10 once, 12 once. The value 8 appears most frequently (3 times). The answer is C.

4. Comparing Mean, Median, and Mode

Effect of Outliers

An outlier is an extremely high or low value. Outliers pull the mean in their direction but do not affect the median or mode as much.
Example: {1, 2, 3, 4, 100}. Mean = 22 (affected by 100), Median = 3 (not affected).

Symmetry and Skewness

Symmetric distribution: Mean ≈ Median ≈ Mode.
Right-skewed (positively skewed): Mean > Median (pulled by high outliers).
Left-skewed (negatively skewed): Mean < Median (pulled by low outliers).

Choosing the Best Measure

Use the mean for symmetric data without outliers. Use the median when there are outliers or skewed data. Use the mode for categorical data or to find the most common value.

Example 4: Effect of Outliers on Mean vs. Median

The salaries in a small company are $30,000, $32,000, $35,000, $38,000, and $200,000. Which measure of central tendency best represents the typical salary?

F. Mean, because it includes all values
G. Median, because it is not affected by the outlier
H. Mode, because it shows the most common salary
J. All three are equally good
K. None of these measures are useful

Solution:

Mean = 67,000 (pulled up by $200,000 outlier). Median = 35,000 (middle value). No mode (all values unique). The median better represents the typical salary. The answer is G.
