Verb questions are among the most frequently tested topics on the ACT English section, accounting for approximately 10-15% of all grammar questions. Mastering verb rules means understanding three critical concepts: subject-verb agreement, verb tense, and verb form. The ACT deliberately makes these questions challenging by separating subjects from verbs with distracting phrases, using compound subjects, and testing irregular verb forms. However, once you learn to identify the true subject of a sentence and recognize timing clues in the surrounding context, verb questions become straightforward and highly predictable.

1. What are the key verb rules?

Subject-verb agreement means the verb must match the number of the subject. If the subject is singular, the verb must be singular. If the subject is plural, the verb must be plural. This rule sounds simple, but the ACT complicates it by inserting prepositional phrases, clauses, and other distracting elements between the subject and verb.

The three most common ways the ACT tests subject-verb agreement are:

  • Prepositional phrases between subject and verb - The most common trick. Prepositional phrases never contain the subject or verb.
    • Example: The box of chocolates (is/are) on the table.
    • Cross out "of chocolates" to find the subject "box" (singular)
    • Correct answer: is
  • Unnecessary descriptive phrases - These phrases add detail but never contain the subject.
    • Example: The dogs, covered in mud after playing in the rain, (needs/need) a bath.
    • Cross out "covered in mud after playing in the rain"
    • Correct answer: need
  • Collective nouns that sound plural but are singular - Group words like committee, team, class, family, and audience are grammatically singular.
    • Example: The committee (vote/votes) on the proposal tomorrow.
    • Committee is singular even though it contains multiple people
    • Correct answer: votes

Verb tense refers to when an action occurs: past, present, or future. The ACT tests whether you can identify the correct tense based on context clues in surrounding sentences. Unlike subject-verb agreement questions where you cannot trust your ear, tense questions require you to read for context and select the verb that matches the timeline established in the passage.

  • Simple tenses - Past, present, and future
    • Past: walked, ran, studied
    • Present: walks, runs, studies
    • Future: will walk, will run, will study
  • Perfect tenses - Used when multiple timelines exist in a sentence
    • Present perfect: has walked, have run
    • Past perfect: had walked, had run
    • Future perfect: will have walked, will have run
  • Conditional tenses - Describe what would or could happen
    • If I study, I will pass (probable)
    • If I studied, I would pass (improbable)
    • If I had studied, I would have passed (impossible)

Verb form refers to using the correct version of irregular verbs. Many common English verbs have irregular past and perfect forms that do not follow standard patterns. The ACT frequently tests whether you know the difference between past tense forms (swam, began, rang) and perfect tense forms (swum, begun, rung).

Example 1: Subject-verb agreement with prepositional phrases

The collection of rare stamps from various countries around the world, including several from the 1800s, [1] in a secure vault at the museum.

A. are stored
B. were stored
C. is stored
D. have been stored
E. store

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the subject - Cross out all prepositional phrases and descriptive phrases
    • "of rare stamps" is a prepositional phrase (cross out)
    • "from various countries around the world" is a prepositional phrase (cross out)
    • "including several from the 1800s" is a descriptive phrase (cross out)
    • The true subject is "collection" (singular)
  • Step 2: Determine if the subject is singular or plural
    • "Collection" is a singular collective noun
    • Test: Replace with "it" - "It is stored" sounds correct
  • Step 3: Eliminate answers with plural verbs
    • Choice A: "are stored" is plural (eliminate)
    • Choice E: "store" is plural (eliminate)
  • Step 4: Choose the correct tense
    • The sentence describes a current state (where the collection is now)
    • Choice B: "were stored" is past tense (eliminate)
    • Choice D: "have been stored" suggests an action that started in the past and continues, but the sentence describes a simple present state (eliminate)
    • Choice C: "is stored" is present tense and singular ✓

Answer: C

2. Advanced verb techniques

Once you master basic subject-verb agreement and verb tense, the ACT tests more advanced concepts. These questions require you to apply multiple rules simultaneously and recognize subtle distinctions between verb forms.

Technique 1: Reading subjects and verbs side-by-side

After identifying the subject and crossing out intervening phrases, read the subject and verb together as if nothing separates them. This technique makes incorrect verbs immediately obvious to your ear.

  • Original sentence: The paintings in the gallery that were created by local artists (is/are) available for purchase.
  • Cross out phrases: The paintings in the gallery that were created by local artists (is/are) available for purchase.
  • Read side-by-side: "The paintings are" ✓ vs. "The paintings is" ✗
  • Correct answer: are

Technique 2: Using pronoun substitution

When you cannot "hear" which verb sounds correct, substitute the subject with a simple pronoun. Use "he," "she," or "it" for singular subjects and "they" for plural subjects. This technique clarifies agreement immediately.

  • Original sentence: Each of the swimmers who completed the race (win/wins) a medal.
  • Identify subject: "Each" (singular)
  • Substitute pronoun: "He wins a medal" ✓ vs. "He win a medal" ✗
  • Correct answer: wins

Technique 3: The "a" versus "u" rule for irregular verbs

Many irregular verbs have two forms: an "a" version (swam, began, rang, sang, drank) and a "u" version (swum, begun, rung, sung, drunk). The rule is simple: use the "u" version with "has," "have," or "had" (perfect tense), and use the "a" version without these helping verbs (simple past tense).

  • With has/have/had - Use the "u" version
    • Correct: I have swum across the lake.
    • Incorrect: I have swam across the lake.
  • Without has/have/had - Use the "a" version
    • Correct: I swam across the lake yesterday.
    • Incorrect: I swum across the lake yesterday.

Common irregular verbs following this pattern:

  • begin → began (simple past) → begun (perfect)
  • drink → drank (simple past) → drunk (perfect)
  • sink → sank (simple past) → sunk (perfect)
  • swim → swam (simple past) → swum (perfect)
  • run → ran (simple past) → run (perfect)
  • sing → sang (simple past) → sung (perfect)
  • ring → rang (simple past) → rung (perfect)

Technique 4: Identifying verb question types

Recognizing whether a question tests subject-verb agreement or verb tense determines your approach. Subject-verb agreement questions always include singular and plural versions of verbs in the answer choices (is/are, was/were, has/have). Verb tense questions include different tenses but do not include both singular and plural options.

  • Subject-verb agreement signals:
    • Answer choices include is/are, was/were, has/have, or singular vs. plural verb forms
    • Strategy: Find the subject, determine if it is singular or plural, eliminate wrong number
  • Verb tense signals:
    • Answer choices show different tenses (walked, walks, will walk) but not singular/plural options
    • Strategy: Read surrounding sentences for context clues, match the established timeline

Example 2: Perfect tense with multiple timelines

By the time the rescue team arrived at the mountain base camp, the climbers [2] waiting in the snow for over six hours.

A. are
B. were
C. had been
D. will be
E. have been

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify multiple timelines
    • First event: The climbers were waiting (this happened first)
    • Second event: The rescue team arrived (this happened second)
    • The sentence describes two past events, with one occurring before the other
  • Step 2: Recognize the need for perfect tense
    • When two past events occur at different times, use past perfect (had + past participle) for the earlier event
    • The key phrase "by the time" signals that perfect tense is needed
  • Step 3: Eliminate incorrect tenses
    • Choice A: "are" is present tense (eliminate)
    • Choice B: "were" is simple past tense, but we need past perfect to show the waiting happened before the arrival (eliminate)
    • Choice D: "will be" is future tense (eliminate)
    • Choice E: "have been" is present perfect, which describes past actions continuing to the present, not two past events (eliminate)
    • Choice C: "had been" is past perfect, correctly showing the waiting occurred before the arrival ✓
  • Step 4: Verify with context
    • "By the time the rescue team arrived" establishes a past moment in time
    • "had been waiting for over six hours" correctly shows the waiting started before that moment and continued until it

Answer: C

3. Common verb mistakes

Even strong writers make predictable errors with verbs. The ACT exploits these common mistakes by including them as answer choices. Recognizing these patterns helps you avoid traps and select correct answers confidently.

Mistake 1: Trusting your ear on subject-verb agreement

The biggest mistake students make is relying on what sounds right without identifying the true subject. When phrases separate the subject from the verb, incorrect options often sound more natural because we are used to hearing grammatically incorrect sentences in casual speech.

  • Incorrect but sounds right: The group of tourists were taking photographs.
  • Correct: The group of tourists was taking photographs.
  • Why the error occurs: "tourists" is closest to the verb, so "were" sounds correct, but "group" is the singular subject

Mistake 2: Choosing complicated tenses when simple tenses work

The ACT prefers simple, direct language. If a simple past, present, or future tense clearly conveys the timing, choose it over unnecessarily complicated perfect or progressive tenses.

  • Unnecessarily complicated: Yesterday, I was having lunch with my friend.
  • Better: Yesterday, I had lunch with my friend.
  • When to use complex tenses: Only when multiple timelines exist or when an action started in the past and continues to the present

Mistake 3: Confusing "would have" with "would of"

In speech, "would have" sounds like "would of," leading many students to write it incorrectly. The correct form always uses "have," never "of."

  • Incorrect: If I had studied harder, I would of passed the test.
  • Correct: If I had studied harder, I would have passed the test.
  • Also applies to: could have (not could of), should have (not should of), might have (not might of)

Mistake 4: Mixing up irregular verb forms

Using the wrong form of irregular verbs is extremely common because these verbs do not follow standard conjugation patterns. The most common errors involve using simple past forms where perfect forms are needed, or vice versa.

  • Incorrect: The bell had already rang when I arrived to class.
  • Correct: The bell had already rung when I arrived to class.
  • Rule: With "had," use the perfect form (rung), not the simple past form (rang)

Mistake 5: Mismatching verb tense with passage context

Every verb tense question requires reading beyond the immediate sentence. Students who focus only on the sentence containing the blank often choose verbs that are grammatically correct in isolation but inconsistent with the passage timeline.

  • Strategy: Always read the previous sentence and following sentence to determine the established timeline
  • Look for other verbs in surrounding sentences as timing clues
  • When all other verbs are past tense, your answer should usually be past tense too

Example 3: Compound subject and irregular verb form

The museum director explained that both the Renaissance paintings and the ancient Greek sculpture [3] from private collectors before the exhibit opened last month.

A. was acquired
B. were acquired
C. has been acquired
D. have been acquired
E. had been acquired

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the compound subject
    • The subject has two parts connected by "and": "the Renaissance paintings" and "the ancient Greek sculpture"
    • When two subjects are joined by "and," they form a plural compound subject
    • Therefore, the verb must be plural
  • Step 2: Eliminate singular verbs
    • Choice A: "was acquired" is singular (eliminate)
    • Choice C: "has been acquired" is singular (eliminate)
  • Step 3: Analyze the timeline
    • The sentence contains two past events with different timings
    • First event: The art was acquired from collectors (happened first)
    • Second event: The exhibit opened last month (happened second)
    • The word "before" explicitly signals that acquisition occurred before the opening
  • Step 4: Recognize the need for past perfect
    • When one past event occurs before another past event, use past perfect for the earlier event
    • Choice B: "were acquired" is simple past tense, which does not show that acquisition happened before the opening (eliminate)
    • Choice D: "have been acquired" is present perfect, which describes actions from past to present, but both events already happened (eliminate)
    • Choice E: "had been acquired" is past perfect plural, correctly showing the acquisition happened before the exhibit opened ✓
  • Step 5: Verify subject-verb agreement and tense together
    • "The paintings and sculpture" (plural) + "had been acquired" (past perfect plural) = grammatically correct
    • Timeline: acquisition → exhibit opening (past perfect → simple past) = logically correct

Answer: E

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Always cross out prepositional phrases and descriptive phrases to find the true subject before checking verb agreement
  • ✓Collective nouns like committee, team, family, and jury are grammatically singular even though they contain multiple people
  • ✓For verb tense questions, read surrounding sentences to identify context clues and match the established timeline
  • ✓Use past perfect tense when one past event occurred before another past event, signaled by words like "before," "by the time," or "already"
  • ✓For irregular verbs, use the "u" version (swum, begun, rung) with has/have/had, and use the "a" version (swam, began, rang) without these helping verbs
  • ✓Identify question type first: if answer choices show singular and plural options, test subject-verb agreement; if answer choices show only different tenses, focus on timeline context
