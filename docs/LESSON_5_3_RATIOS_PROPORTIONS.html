Ratios and proportions appear in 3-5 questions on every ACT Math test, often disguised in word problems involving mixtures, rates, scale factors, and similar figures. The ACT tests your ability to set up and solve proportions using cross-multiplication, work with direct and inverse variation, and apply ratio reasoning to real-world scenarios. Common traps include confusing part-to-part with part-to-whole ratios or setting up proportions incorrectly—mastering these fundamentals ensures you can confidently tackle any ratio problem the ACT throws at you.

1. Understanding Ratios

A ratio compares two quantities using division. Ratios can be written as fractions, with colons, or with the word "to": 3/4, 3:4, or "3 to 4" all represent the same ratio.

Writing and Simplifying Ratios

To write a ratio, place the first quantity in the numerator and the second in the denominator. Simplify ratios like fractions by dividing both parts by their GCD. For example, the ratio of 12 to 18 is 12/18 = 2/3. Ratios should typically be simplified to lowest terms unless the problem specifies otherwise.

Part-to-Part vs. Part-to-Whole Ratios

Part-to-part ratios compare two parts of a whole. If boys to girls is 3:2, there are 3 boys for every 2 girls. Part-to-whole ratios compare one part to the total. With 3 boys and 2 girls, the ratio of boys to total students is 3:5.

Using Ratio Multipliers

If a ratio is a:b, the actual quantities are ax and bx for some multiplier x. For example, if boys:girls = 3:2 and there are 40 total students, then 3x + 2x = 40, so 5x = 40, giving x = 8. Therefore: 3(8) = 24 boys and 2(8) = 16 girls.

Example 1: Ratio Problem

The ratio of cats to dogs in a shelter is 5:3. If there are 24 dogs, how many cats are there?

A) 30

B) 35

C) 40

D) 45

E) 48

Solution:

Step 1: Set up using multiplier: cats = 5x, dogs = 3x.

Step 2: We know dogs = 24, so 3x = 24, giving x = 8.

Step 3: Calculate cats: 5x = 5(8) = 40.

Answer: C — There are 40 cats.

2. Solving Proportions

A proportion states that two ratios are equal: a/b = c/d. Proportions are essential for solving many ACT word problems involving rates, scaling, and similar figures.

Cross-Multiplication Method

To solve a proportion, use cross-multiplication: if a/b = c/d, then ad = bc. For example, solve 3/7 = x/21: cross-multiply to get 3(21) = 7x, so 63 = 7x, giving x = 9. This method works because you're multiplying both sides by bd.

Setting Up Proportions

When setting up a proportion, keep the same units in corresponding positions. If comparing miles to hours, write (miles₁/hours₁) = (miles₂/hours₂). For example, "If 5 apples cost $3, how much do 12 apples cost?" Set up: 5 apples/$3 = 12 apples/$x, giving x = $7.20.

Scale Factor Problems

In scale problems, all corresponding lengths have the same ratio. If a model car has scale 1:24, and the model is 6 inches long, the real car is 6 × 24 = 144 inches = 12 feet long. Map scales work similarly: if 1 inch = 50 miles, then 3.5 inches = 175 miles.

Example 2: Proportion Word Problem

If 8 workers can complete a job in 12 days, how many days will it take 6 workers (working at the same rate)?

A) 9

B) 12

C) 14

D) 16

E) 18

Solution:

Step 1: This is inverse proportion: fewer workers means more days. Use workers × days = constant.

Step 2: Set up: 8 workers × 12 days = 6 workers × x days.

Step 3: Solve: 96 = 6x, so x = 16 days.

Answer: D — 6 workers will take 16 days.

3. Direct and Inverse Variation

Variation describes how one quantity changes in response to another. Understanding direct and inverse variation helps you set up correct equations for relationship problems.

Direct Variation (Direct Proportion)

Direct variation: y varies directly with x means y = kx for some constant k. When x doubles, y doubles. When x triples, y triples. Example: "y varies directly with x, and y = 15 when x = 3." Find k: 15 = k(3), so k = 5. Equation: y = 5x.

Inverse Variation (Inverse Proportion)

Inverse variation: y varies inversely with x means y = k/x or xy = k. When x doubles, y is halved. When x triples, y becomes one-third. Example: "y varies inversely with x, and y = 8 when x = 5." Find k: 8 = k/5, so k = 40. Equation: y = 40/x.

Recognizing Variation Types

Direct variation: quantities increase together or decrease together. Graph is a line through the origin. Inverse variation: as one increases, the other decreases. Graph is a hyperbola. Look for keywords: "varies directly" or "proportional to" suggests direct, "varies inversely" or "inversely proportional" suggests inverse.

Example 3: Inverse Variation

y varies inversely with x. If y = 12 when x = 4, what is y when x = 8?

A) 3

B) 6

C) 12

D) 16

E) 24

Solution:

Step 1: Inverse variation means xy = k (constant).

Step 2: Find k using given values: (4)(12) = 48, so k = 48.

Step 3: When x = 8: 8y = 48, so y = 6.

Answer: B — When x doubles from 4 to 8, y halves from 12 to 6.

4. Rate Problems

Rate problems involve relationships between distance, rate (speed), and time, or similar quantity-rate-time scenarios. The fundamental formula and its variations appear frequently on the ACT.

Distance = Rate × Time

The basic formula is d = rt. Rearrange for rate: r = d/t. Rearrange for time: t = d/r. For example, if you travel 150 miles in 3 hours, your rate is 150/3 = 50 mph. Units must be consistent: if rate is mph, distance should be miles and time should be hours.

Work Rate Problems

Work problems use the same structure: Work = Rate × Time. If a person completes a job in h hours, their work rate is 1/h of the job per hour. When people work together, add their rates. For example, if Person A does a job in 6 hours and Person B in 4 hours, working together: combined rate = 1/6 + 1/4 = 5/12 of the job per hour.

Average Speed

Average speed = total distance / total time. Do NOT average the speeds! For example, traveling 60 mph for 2 hours then 40 mph for 1 hour: Total distance = 60(2) + 40(1) = 160 miles. Total time = 3 hours. Average speed = 160/3 ≈ 53.3 mph (not (60+40)/2 = 50).

Example 4: Rate Problem

A train travels 240 miles at a constant speed. If the trip takes 4 hours, what is the train's speed in miles per hour?

A) 40 mph

B) 50 mph

C) 60 mph

D) 70 mph

E) 80 mph

Solution:

Step 1: Use formula: rate = distance / time.

Step 2: Substitute values: rate = 240 miles / 4 hours = 60 mph.

Answer: C — The train travels at 60 miles per hour.
