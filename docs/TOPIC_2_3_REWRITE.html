Understanding transitions is one of the most essential skills for scoring well on the ACT English Test. Transition words and phrases connect ideas between sentences and paragraphs, creating a smooth logical flow in writing. Instead of testing you on obscure grammar rules, transition questions require you to read for context and understand the logical relationship between ideas. This makes transitions one of the most strategic areas to master, since recognizing these relationships can help you answer questions quickly and confidently.

1. What are transitions on the ACT?

Transitions are words or phrases that show the relationship between two ideas. On the ACT English Test, you will be asked to choose the transition that best connects two sentences or clauses based on their logical relationship. All answer choices in these questions are grammatically correct, so you must read the surrounding sentences carefully to determine which transition makes the most sense in context.

The ACT tests transitions in two primary ways. Understanding these two question formats will help you approach each question strategically and avoid common mistakes.

Format 1: All four choices are transitions

When all four answer choices contain different transition words, you are being tested purely on selecting the correct logical relationship. Each choice will be grammatically correct but will express a different relationship between ideas.

  • Examples of these questions include:
    • Choosing between "however," "furthermore," "for example," and "therefore"
    • Selecting the right contrast word: "nevertheless," "on the other hand," "in contrast," or "despite this"
    • Picking the correct additive transition: "additionally," "moreover," "in fact," or "indeed"

Format 2: Three choices are transitions, one has no transition

When one answer choice contains no transition word at all, you must decide both which transition is correct and whether a transition is even necessary. Most often in these questions, the correct answer is the choice without any transition, as many sentences flow naturally without explicit connecting words.

  • Key indicators that no transition is needed:
    • The second sentence continues the same idea without contrasting or adding to it
    • The relationship between sentences is already clear from context
    • Adding a transition word would create redundancy or awkwardness

Categories of transition relationships

The ACT tests your ability to recognize different logical relationships between ideas. Understanding these categories will help you quickly identify which type of transition fits the context.

  • Additive transitions provide additional support or evidence:
    • additionally, furthermore, moreover, in addition, also, besides, in fact
  • Resulting transitions connect a cause to its consequence:
    • therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, hence, accordingly
  • Contrasting transitions focus on differences between ideas:
    • however, nevertheless, in contrast, on the other hand, although, despite this
  • Agreement transitions focus on similarities:
    • likewise, similarly, correspondingly, by the same token
  • Example transitions link general ideas to specific examples:
    • for example, for instance, specifically, namely, in this case
  • Sequential transitions connect events based on when they occur:
    • first, next, then, finally, subsequently, meanwhile, before, after
  • Summary transitions provide a summary of previous ideas:
    • in conclusion, in summary, ultimately, in other words, briefly
  • Emphasis transitions add emphasis to previous claims:
    • indeed, in fact, clearly, obviously, above all

Example 1: Identifying contrasting transitions

From a distance, the ocean looked pretty calm today even though a storm had arrived last night. Under the circumstances, the scene was much more chaotic with big waves and strong riptides.

Which transition word is the most logical in context?

A. NO CHANGE
B. For instance,
C. Up close,
D. Therefore,
E. Similarly,

Solution:

  • First, identify the relationship between the two sentences. The first sentence says the ocean looked "calm" from a distance. The second sentence says the scene was actually "chaotic" with waves and riptides.
  • This is a contrasting relationship. The first sentence establishes an observation from one perspective, and the second sentence contrasts it with a different perspective.
  • Notice the first sentence mentions "from a distance." This directly contrasts with choice C, "up close," which creates a clear spatial contrast.
  • Eliminate the other choices:
    • Choice A ("under the circumstances") is vague and does not establish a clear relationship
    • Choice B ("for instance") introduces an example, but the second sentence is not an example of the first
    • Choice D ("therefore") suggests a result, but the chaos is not a result of the calm appearance
    • Choice E ("similarly") suggests agreement, but the sentences are contrasting

Answer: C

2. How to choose the right transition

Choosing the correct transition requires a systematic approach that focuses on understanding the relationship between ideas rather than simply recognizing grammatically correct options. Follow these four steps to identify the right transition efficiently and accurately.

Step 1: Ignore the current transition

  • Cover up or mentally block out the underlined transition word in the passage. The passage may contain an incorrect transition, so reading it first can bias your understanding of the relationship.
  • Read the sentence before and the sentence after without looking at the transition. This allows you to form an independent understanding of how the ideas relate.

Step 2: Determine the logical relationship

  • Ask yourself: How do these two ideas connect? Look for clues in the content:
    • Do they contrast with each other? (look for opposing words or ideas)
    • Does the second sentence add to or support the first? (look for similar ideas building on each other)
    • Does the second sentence show a result or consequence of the first? (look for cause-effect patterns)
    • Does the second sentence provide an example of the first? (look for general statements followed by specifics)
    • Does the second sentence follow the first in time? (look for chronological sequences)
  • In your mind, describe the relationship in simple terms before looking at the answer choices. For example: "These ideas contrast," or "This is a cause and effect relationship."

Step 3: Match the relationship to answer choices

  • Look at the answer choices and identify which transition matches the logical relationship you identified. Use your knowledge of transition categories to eliminate wrong answers quickly.
  • If you are unsure between two choices, plug each one into the passage and read the sentences aloud mentally. The correct transition will sound natural and clearly express the intended relationship.

Step 4: Consider whether a transition is necessary

  • If one answer choice contains no transition word, evaluate whether the sentences flow naturally without one. Many sentences connect clearly through context alone and do not require explicit transition words.
  • Choose the option with no transition if:
    • The second sentence continues the same thought without a shift in direction
    • The relationship is already obvious from context
    • Adding any transition word would make the sentence awkward or redundant

Example 2: Determining when no transition is needed

Stepping out on the island's port on Monday, the new couple debuted their own classic take on beach attire. As a result, the actor opted for a classic white tee and board shorts while his model counterpart wore a floral print coverup and strappy sandals.

Which transition word, if any, is the most logical in context?

A. NO CHANGE
B. In other words,
C. Consequently,
D. Specifically,
E. DELETE the underlined portion

Solution:

  • Step 1: Ignore the current transition "as a result" and read both sentences. The first sentence introduces the couple debuting their beach attire. The second sentence describes specifically what each person wore.
  • Step 2: Determine the relationship. The second sentence is not a result or consequence of the first. Instead, it provides specific details about what "classic take on beach attire" means. This is a clarification or specification relationship.
  • Step 3: Evaluate each choice:
    • Choice A ("as a result") suggests consequence, which is incorrect
    • Choice B ("in other words") suggests rephrasing, but we are not restating the same idea
    • Choice C ("consequently") also suggests consequence, which is incorrect
    • Choice D ("specifically") could work since the second sentence provides specific details
    • Choice E (delete the transition) might work if the relationship is clear without it
  • Step 4: Read the sentences without any transition. "Stepping out on the island's port on Monday, the new couple debuted their own classic take on beach attire. The actor opted for a classic white tee and board shorts..." This flows naturally. The second sentence clearly describes what the couple wore without needing a transition word.
  • While "specifically" could work, deleting the transition entirely is better because the relationship is already obvious from context. The ACT favors conciseness when meaning is clear.

Answer: E

3. Common transition mistakes

Many students make predictable errors when answering transition questions. Understanding these common mistakes will help you avoid traps and choose the correct answer with confidence. The key is recognizing that transitions are about logical meaning, not just grammatical correctness.

Mistake 1: Choosing the transition that sounds familiar

  • Students often select the transition word that sounds most common or natural in isolation, such as "however" or "therefore," without considering the actual relationship between the sentences.
  • Always base your choice on the specific logical relationship in the passage, not on which transition sounds best in general. Every transition is context-dependent.

Mistake 2: Not reading the sentence before the transition

  • Many students only read the sentence containing the underlined transition, which makes it impossible to understand the relationship between ideas.
  • You must read both the sentence before and the sentence after the transition. Transitions connect two ideas, so you need to understand both sides of the connection.
  • Sometimes you may need to read even more context, especially if the passage is building a complex argument over multiple sentences.

Mistake 3: Adding unnecessary transitions

  • When faced with a choice to delete the transition, students often assume that having a transition word is always better. This is not true. Many sentences flow perfectly without explicit transitions.
  • If the relationship between sentences is already clear from context, adding a transition word can create redundancy or awkwardness.
  • Remember: The ACT values conciseness. When meaning is clear without a transition, choose the option that deletes it.

Mistake 4: Confusing similar transition types

  • Some transitions appear similar but express different relationships. For example:
    • "For example" introduces a specific instance of a general idea
    • "For instance" also introduces an example and is interchangeable with "for example"
    • "In other words" restates the same idea in different language
    • "In fact" emphasizes or strengthens a previous claim
  • Pay careful attention to the subtle differences between similar transition words. Understanding these distinctions will help you eliminate wrong answers quickly.

Mistake 5: Letting the current transition influence your choice

  • If the passage contains an incorrect transition, reading it first can bias your understanding of the relationship and lead you to choose "NO CHANGE" incorrectly.
  • Always ignore the underlined transition initially and form your own understanding of how the sentences connect. Then evaluate all answer choices equally, including "NO CHANGE."

Example 3: Avoiding common transition traps

The Renaissance was a period of extraordinary cultural achievement in Europe, producing masterpieces in art, literature, and science. However, Leonardo da Vinci created iconic works such as the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper during this era, and Michelangelo sculpted the famous statue of David.

Which transition word is the most logical in context?

A. NO CHANGE
B. For example,
C. Nevertheless,
D. As a result,
E. On the other hand,

Solution:

  • This question tests whether you can avoid Mistake 5: letting the current transition influence your choice. The passage contains "however," which suggests a contrast. Many students will keep this transition without analyzing the relationship.
  • Ignore "however" and read both sentences. The first sentence makes a general claim about the Renaissance producing masterpieces. The second sentence provides specific examples of those masterpieces by naming da Vinci and Michelangelo's works.
  • Determine the relationship: This is not a contrast. The second sentence supports and illustrates the first with concrete examples. This is an example relationship.
  • Evaluate each choice:
    • Choice A ("however") is incorrect because there is no contrast between the sentences
    • Choice B ("for example") is correct because the second sentence provides specific examples of Renaissance masterpieces
    • Choice C ("nevertheless") suggests contrast despite an obstacle, which does not fit
    • Choice D ("as a result") suggests the artists' works were a consequence of the Renaissance, which is illogical
    • Choice E ("on the other hand") also suggests contrast, which is incorrect
  • The correct answer is B. This question demonstrates why you must ignore the given transition and focus solely on the logical relationship between sentences.

Answer: B

  • ✓Always ignore the underlined transition first and determine the logical relationship between sentences independently
  • ✓Read the sentence before and the sentence after the transition to understand both sides of the connection
  • ✓Memorize the eight main transition categories: additive, resulting, contrasting, agreement, example, sequential, summary, and emphasis
  • ✓When one answer choice deletes the transition entirely, consider whether the relationship is already clear from context
  • ✓Do not choose transitions based on what sounds familiar or common; every transition must match the specific logical relationship in the passage
  • ✓Practice distinguishing between similar transition words that express subtly different relationships, such as "for example" versus "in other words"
