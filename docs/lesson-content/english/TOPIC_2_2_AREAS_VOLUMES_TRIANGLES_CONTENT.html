Concept 1: Area Formulas

Understanding area formulas is essential for ACT geometry. The ACT provides some formulas, but knowing when and how to apply them efficiently is key to solving problems quickly.

Key Area Formulas

  • Rectangle: A = length × width
  • Square: A = s² (where s = side length)
  • Triangle: A = ½ × base × height
  • Parallelogram: A = base × height
  • Trapezoid: A = ½ × (base₁ + base₂) × height
  • Circle: A = πr²

Example 1: Rectangle Area

Problem: A rectangular garden is 12 feet long and 8 feet wide. What is the area of the garden in square feet?

Solution:

Using the rectangle formula: A = length × width

A = 12 × 8 = 96 square feet

Answer: 96 square feet

Example 2: Triangle Area

Problem: A triangle has a base of 10 inches and a height of 7 inches. What is its area?

Solution:

Using the triangle formula: A = ½ × base × height

A = ½ × 10 × 7 = ½ × 70 = 35 square inches

Answer: 35 square inches

Concept 2: Volume Formulas

Volume measures three-dimensional space. The ACT frequently tests your ability to calculate volumes of common 3D shapes.

Key Volume Formulas

  • Rectangular Prism (Box): V = length × width × height
  • Cube: V = s³ (where s = edge length)
  • Cylinder: V = πr²h (where r = radius, h = height)
  • Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³
  • Cone: V = (1/3)πr²h
  • Pyramid: V = (1/3) × base area × height

Example 3: Rectangular Prism Volume

Problem: A box is 6 cm long, 4 cm wide, and 3 cm tall. What is its volume?

Solution:

V = length × width × height

V = 6 × 4 × 3 = 72 cm³

Answer: 72 cubic centimeters

Example 4: Cylinder Volume

Problem: A cylindrical water tank has a radius of 5 feet and a height of 12 feet. What is its volume in cubic feet?

Solution:

V = πr²h

V = π(5)²(12) = π(25)(12) = 300π cubic feet

Answer: 300π ft³ (or approximately 942.5 ft³)

Concept 3: Right Triangles and Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean theorem is one of the most important formulas on the ACT. It relates the three sides of a right triangle.

Pythagorean Theorem

For a right triangle with legs a and b, and hypotenuse c:

a² + b² = c²

The hypotenuse is always the longest side, opposite the right angle.

Example 5: Finding the Hypotenuse

Problem: A right triangle has legs of length 6 and 8. What is the length of the hypotenuse?

Solution:

Using a² + b² = c²:

6² + 8² = c²

36 + 64 = c²

100 = c²

c = 10

Answer: 10

Note: This is a 6-8-10 triangle, which is a multiple of the common 3-4-5 triangle!

Example 6: Finding a Leg

Problem: A right triangle has a hypotenuse of 13 and one leg of length 5. What is the length of the other leg?

Solution:

Using a² + b² = c²:

5² + b² = 13²

25 + b² = 169

b² = 144

b = 12

Answer: 12

This is the famous 5-12-13 Pythagorean triple!

Concept 4: Special Right Triangles

Two special right triangles appear frequently on the ACT. Memorizing their ratios saves valuable time.

45-45-90 Triangle

Side ratio: x : x : x√2

The legs are equal, and the hypotenuse is leg × √2

30-60-90 Triangle

Side ratio: x : x√3 : 2x

Short leg (opposite 30°) : Long leg (opposite 60°) : Hypotenuse

Example 7: 45-45-90 Triangle

Problem: In a 45-45-90 triangle, if each leg has length 7, what is the length of the hypotenuse?

Solution:

Using the 45-45-90 ratio x : x : x√2

Here x = 7, so hypotenuse = 7√2

Answer: 7√2 (or approximately 9.9)

Example 8: 30-60-90 Triangle

Problem: In a 30-60-90 triangle, if the short leg is 4, what is the length of the long leg and hypotenuse?

Solution:

Using the 30-60-90 ratio x : x√3 : 2x

Here x = 4 (short leg)

Long leg = 4√3

Hypotenuse = 2(4) = 8

Answer: Long leg = 4√3, Hypotenuse = 8

Concept 5: Common Pythagorean Triples

Recognizing Pythagorean triples can save you calculation time on the ACT. These are sets of three integers that satisfy a² + b² = c².

Common Pythagorean Triples

  • 3-4-5 (and multiples: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 15-20-25)
  • 5-12-13 (and multiples: 10-24-26)
  • 8-15-17
  • 7-24-25

Example 9: Recognizing Multiples

Problem: A right triangle has legs of 9 and 12. What is the hypotenuse?

Solution:

Recognize this as 3 times the 3-4-5 triangle

9 = 3 × 3, and 12 = 3 × 4

So hypotenuse = 3 × 5 = 15

Answer: 15

This is much faster than calculating 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225, √225 = 15!

Example 10: Complex Application

Problem: A rectangular prism (box) has dimensions 3 cm × 4 cm × 12 cm. What is the length of the space diagonal (the diagonal from one corner to the opposite corner through the interior)?

Solution:

Step 1: Find the diagonal of the base using the Pythagorean theorem

Base diagonal² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25

Base diagonal = 5 (this is the 3-4-5 triple!)

Step 2: Now use the base diagonal and the height to find the space diagonal

Space diagonal² = 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169

Space diagonal = 13 (this is the 5-12-13 triple!)

Answer: 13 cm

The space diagonal formula is d = √(l² + w² + h²) = √(9 + 16 + 144) = √169 = 13

ACT Strategy Tips

  • Always check if you can use a Pythagorean triple before calculating
  • When you see 45-45-90 or 30-60-90 mentioned, immediately think of the side ratios
  • For volume problems, carefully identify which dimension is which (radius vs. diameter, etc.)
  • Draw a diagram if one isn't provided – it helps visualize the problem
  • Remember that area is in square units and volume is in cubic units
