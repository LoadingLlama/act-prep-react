Concept 1: Understanding Fractions

Fractions represent parts of a whole. Mastering fraction operations is essential for ACT success, as they appear in algebra, geometry, and word problems.

Fraction Fundamentals

  • Numerator: The top number (how many parts you have)
  • Denominator: The bottom number (total number of equal parts)
  • Proper fraction: Numerator < Denominator (e.g., 3/4)
  • Improper fraction: Numerator ≥ Denominator (e.g., 7/4)
  • Mixed number: Whole number + fraction (e.g., 1 3/4)

Key point: A fraction represents division: 3/4 means 3 ÷ 4

Concept 2: Adding and Subtracting Fractions

To add or subtract fractions, you need a common denominator. This is one of the most frequently tested fraction skills on the ACT.

Adding/Subtracting with Common Denominators

When denominators are the same:

  • Keep the denominator
  • Add or subtract the numerators
  • Simplify if needed

a/c + b/c = (a+b)/c

Example 1: Adding with Common Denominators

Problem: 2/7 + 3/7

Solution:

Same denominator, so add numerators:

= (2 + 3)/7

Answer: 5/7

Adding/Subtracting with Different Denominators

When denominators are different:

  1. Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert each fraction to equivalent fractions with the LCD
  3. Add or subtract the numerators
  4. Simplify if needed

Example 2: Finding Common Denominators

Problem: 1/4 + 1/6

Solution:

Step 1: Find LCD of 4 and 6

Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16...

Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18...

LCD = 12

Step 2: Convert to equivalent fractions

1/4 = 3/12 (multiply top and bottom by 3)

1/6 = 2/12 (multiply top and bottom by 2)

Step 3: Add

3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12

Answer: 5/12

Example 3: Subtracting Fractions

Problem: 5/6 - 1/4

Solution:

Step 1: LCD of 6 and 4 is 12

Step 2: Convert

5/6 = 10/12

1/4 = 3/12

Step 3: Subtract

10/12 - 3/12 = 7/12

Answer: 7/12

Concept 3: Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is actually simpler than adding them - no common denominator needed!

Multiplication Rule

a/b × c/d = (a×c)/(b×d)

  • Multiply numerators together
  • Multiply denominators together
  • Simplify the result

Pro tip: Cancel common factors before multiplying to make calculations easier!

Example 4: Basic Multiplication

Problem: 2/3 × 3/5

Solution:

Notice the 3's cancel:

2/3 × 3/5 = (2×3)/(3×5)

We can cancel 3/3 = 1

= 2/5

Answer: 2/5

Canceling first saves time and reduces errors!

Example 5: Multiplying with Cancellation

Problem: 5/8 × 4/15

Solution:

Step 1: Look for common factors to cancel

5 and 15 have common factor 5

4 and 8 have common factor 4

Step 2: Cancel and multiply

5/8 × 4/15 = (1×1)/(2×3)

Answer: 1/6

Concept 4: Dividing Fractions

To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction.

Division Rule: "Keep, Change, Flip"

a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c

  1. Keep the first fraction
  2. Change division to multiplication
  3. Flip the second fraction (reciprocal)

Remember: The reciprocal of a/b is b/a

Example 6: Basic Division

Problem: 3/4 ÷ 2/5

Solution:

Step 1: Keep, change, flip

3/4 ÷ 2/5 = 3/4 × 5/2

Step 2: Multiply

= (3×5)/(4×2)

= 15/8

Step 3: Convert to mixed number

15 ÷ 8 = 1 remainder 7

Answer: 1 7/8

Example 7: Division with Cancellation

Problem: 5/6 ÷ 10/9

Solution:

Step 1: Keep, change, flip

5/6 ÷ 10/9 = 5/6 × 9/10

Step 2: Cancel before multiplying

5 and 10 share factor 5

6 and 9 share factor 3

= (1×3)/(2×2)

Answer: 3/4

Concept 5: Working with Mixed Numbers

Mixed numbers combine whole numbers and fractions. For calculations, convert them to improper fractions first.

Converting Mixed Numbers

Mixed to Improper:

  • Multiply whole number by denominator
  • Add the numerator
  • Place over original denominator
  • Example: 2 3/4 = (2×4 + 3)/4 = 11/4

Improper to Mixed:

  • Divide numerator by denominator
  • Quotient = whole number
  • Remainder = new numerator
  • Example: 11/4 = 2 3/4

Example 8: Adding Mixed Numbers

Problem: 2 1/3 + 1 1/2

Solution:

Step 1: Convert to improper fractions

2 1/3 = 7/3

1 1/2 = 3/2

Step 2: Find LCD (6) and add

7/3 = 14/6

3/2 = 9/6

14/6 + 9/6 = 23/6

Step 3: Convert back

23 ÷ 6 = 3 remainder 5

Answer: 3 5/6

Example 9: Multiplying Mixed Numbers

Problem: 1 2/5 × 2 1/3

Solution:

Step 1: Convert to improper fractions

1 2/5 = 7/5

2 1/3 = 7/3

Step 2: Multiply

7/5 × 7/3 = 49/15

Step 3: Convert to mixed number

49 ÷ 15 = 3 remainder 4

Answer: 3 4/15

Concept 6: Comparing Fractions and Calculator Techniques

Comparing fractions and using your calculator efficiently can save valuable time on the ACT.

Strategies for Comparing Fractions

  1. Common denominators: Convert to same denominator and compare numerators
  2. Cross multiplication: For a/b vs c/d, compare a×d with b×c
  3. Decimal conversion: Use calculator to convert to decimals
  4. Benchmark fractions: Compare to 1/2, 1, etc.

Example 10: Comparing Fractions with Cross Multiplication

Problem: Which is greater: 5/8 or 7/11?

Solution:

Method 1 - Cross Multiplication:

Compare 5×11 with 8×7

55 vs 56

Since 56 > 55, we have 7/11 > 5/8

Method 2 - Calculator:

5 ÷ 8 = 0.625

7 ÷ 11 ≈ 0.636

Answer: 7/11 is greater

Calculator Tips for Fractions

  • Convert to decimal: Just divide numerator by denominator
  • Use parentheses: (2/3) + (1/4) not 2/3+1/4
  • Mixed numbers: Enter as: 2 + 1/3 = 2.333...
  • Check answers: Convert answer choices to decimals for quick comparison
  • Simplifying: Find GCF by testing small primes (2, 3, 5, 7)

ACT Strategy Tips for Fractions

  • Convert mixed numbers first: Always change to improper fractions before multiplying or dividing
  • Look for cancellation opportunities: Cancel common factors before multiplying to simplify
  • Use your calculator wisely: Convert to decimals when comparing multiple fractions
  • Watch for trap answers: Common mistakes include wrong LCD or forgetting to simplify
  • Know your fraction-decimal equivalents: 1/2 = 0.5, 1/4 = 0.25, 1/3 ≈ 0.333, 2/3 ≈ 0.667, 3/4 = 0.75
  • Double-check signs: Pay special attention when subtracting fractions
  • Simplify final answers: ACT answer choices are always in simplest form
  • Don't rush the LCD: Taking 10 extra seconds to find the correct LCD saves time and prevents errors
  • Write out steps: Especially for complex problems with multiple operations
  • Test with easy numbers: If stuck, plug in simple values like 1 or 0 to eliminate wrong answers

Common Fraction Errors to Avoid

  • Adding/subtracting without common denominators: 1/2 + 1/3 ≠ 2/5
  • Adding denominators when multiplying: 2/3 × 3/4 ≠ 6/7
  • Forgetting to flip when dividing: Must use reciprocal
  • Canceling incorrectly: Only cancel factors, not terms being added
  • Converting mixed numbers incorrectly: Remember: whole × denominator + numerator
  • Not simplifying final answer: Always reduce to lowest terms

Quick Reference: Fraction Operations

Operation Rule Example
Addition Common denominator needed 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12
Subtraction Common denominator needed 3/4 - 1/2 = 3/4 - 2/4 = 1/4
