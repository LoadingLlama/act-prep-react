Understanding misplaced modifiers is one of the most essential skills for achieving a high score on the ACT English Test. A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that describes or provides additional information about another word in the sentence. When a modifier is separated from the word it describes, the sentence becomes confusing or takes on an unintended meaning. Mastering the rules for proper modifier placement will help you quickly identify these errors and select the correct answer choice on test day.

1. What Are Misplaced Modifiers?

A misplaced modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that is improperly separated from the noun or pronoun it is meant to modify. When a modifier is in the wrong position, it can accidentally describe the wrong word, leading to awkward, confusing, or even humorous sentences.

The key rule to remember is the "next-door neighbor rule": a modifier must be placed directly next to the word it modifies. If the modifier is separated from its target word, the sentence will have an incorrect or unintended meaning.

There are several types of misplaced modifiers that appear on the ACT:

  • Introductory modifiers: These appear at the beginning of a sentence followed by a comma. The first noun after the comma must be what the modifier describes.
  • Mid-sentence modifiers: These appear in the middle of a sentence, often set off by commas. They must directly follow the word they modify.
  • End-of-sentence modifiers: These appear at the end of a sentence and must directly follow the word they describe.
  • Dangling modifiers: A special type where the word being modified is missing entirely from the sentence, leaving the modifier with nothing to describe.

Consider this incorrect example: "Consistently erupting, Jack loves taking pictures of the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park." The introductory phrase "consistently erupting" is meant to describe the geyser, but it is mistakenly placed next to "Jack," making it sound like Jack is consistently erupting. The correct version would be: "Consistently erupting, the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park is one of Jack's favorite things to photograph."

Example 1: Introductory Modifier

Not popular since the early 1980s, the documentary about mullets explored the origins of the wildly popular hairstyle.

A. NO CHANGE
B. the documentary explored the origins of mullets, a wildly popular hairstyle not seen since the early 1980s.
C. mullets were the topic of a recently released documentary exploring the origins of the wildly popular hairstyle.
D. the origins of the wildly popular hairstyle known as mullets were explored in a recent documentary.
E. a recently released documentary explored the wildly popular hairstyle known as mullets and their origins.

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the introductory modifier. The phrase "not popular since the early 1980s" is the modifier at the beginning of the sentence.
  • Step 2: Determine what the modifier describes. The phrase describes something that was not popular since the early 1980s. That would be mullets (the hairstyle), not the documentary.
  • Step 3: Check which answer choice places the correct word immediately after the comma. In choice A, "the documentary" comes first, which is incorrect. In choice B, the structure is rearranged but still has issues with what follows the comma. In choice C, "mullets" comes immediately after the comma, which is correct.
  • Step 4: Verify the sentence makes logical sense. "Not popular since the early 1980s, mullets were the topic of a recently released documentary" correctly places the modifier next to what it describes.

Answer: C

2. How to Fix Misplaced Modifiers

Follow these proven techniques to identify and correct misplaced modifiers on the ACT:

Technique 1: Apply the Next-Door Neighbor Rule

For introductory modifiers separated by a comma, the first noun or pronoun after the comma must be the word the modifier describes. This is the most common type of misplaced modifier on the ACT.

  • Read the introductory phrase or clause before the comma
  • Ask yourself: "Who or what is this phrase describing?"
  • Check if that person, place, or thing appears immediately after the comma
  • If not, eliminate that answer choice and look for one that places the correct word first

Example: "Running from the police, Eric hopped the fence and hid underneath a car." The phrase "running from the police" describes Eric, and Eric is correctly placed immediately after the comma.

Technique 2: Reorder the Sentence Structure

When the modifier cannot easily be moved next to the word it describes, you may need to restructure the entire sentence. This often involves moving the modifier from the beginning to the middle or end of the sentence.

  • Identify the word being modified
  • Look for answer choices that place the modifier immediately before or after that word
  • For mid-sentence modifiers, place commas around the descriptive phrase
  • For end-of-sentence modifiers, place the modifier directly after the word it describes

Example: "The research team tracked the movements of the blue storks, which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year." The modifier "which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year" is correctly placed at the end of the sentence, immediately after "blue storks."

Technique 3: Convert the Phrase to a Clause

Another way to fix a misplaced modifier is to add a subject and verb to the modifying phrase, converting it into a dependent clause. This makes it clear exactly who or what is performing the action.

  • Add a subordinating conjunction such as "because," "since," "when," or "while"
  • Include a clear subject (often a pronoun like "it," "he," "she," or "they")
  • Add an appropriate verb to complete the clause

Example: "Since it is consistently erupting, Jack loves taking pictures of the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park." By adding "since" and "it is," we convert the phrase "consistently erupting" into a dependent clause that clearly describes the geyser.

Technique 4: Watch for Scrambled Answer Choices

On the ACT, misplaced modifier questions have a distinctive appearance that helps you recognize them quickly. Learning to spot these questions is half the battle.

  • All four answer choices are similar in length
  • The answer choices contain the same words arranged in different orders
  • The first word or phrase varies across the answer choices
  • When you spot these clues, immediately identify the modifier and determine what it describes

Example 2: Mid-Sentence Modifier

The research team, which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year, tracked the movements of the blue storks.

A. NO CHANGE
B. The research team tracked the movements of the blue storks, which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year.
C. The research team, which tracked the movements of the blue storks, noted that the birds arrived at their nesting grounds early this year.
D. Tracking the movements of the blue storks, the research team discovered they arrived at their nesting grounds early this year.
E. The blue storks, tracked by the research team, arrived at their nesting grounds early this year.

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the modifier. The clause "which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year" is the modifier set off by commas.
  • Step 2: Determine what the modifier describes. What arrived at nesting grounds early? The blue storks, not the research team.
  • Step 3: Apply the next-door neighbor rule. In choice A, the modifier comes right after "research team," which is incorrect. In choice B, the modifier comes right after "blue storks," which is correct.
  • Step 4: Verify the sentence is clear and logical. Choice B correctly places the descriptive clause at the end of the sentence, immediately after "blue storks."

Answer: B

3. Common Modifier Mistakes

Certain types of misplaced modifiers appear repeatedly on the ACT English Test. Understanding these common patterns will help you spot errors quickly and avoid traps in the answer choices.

Mistake 1: Assuming You Know What the Sentence Means

The biggest challenge with misplaced modifiers is that our brains automatically correct them when we read. We usually understand what the sentence is trying to say, even when it is grammatically incorrect. This makes misplaced modifiers difficult to spot unless you deliberately check modifier placement.

  • Always identify the modifier and consciously ask what it describes
  • Do not rely on your intuitive understanding of the sentence
  • Check that the modifier is next to the correct word, not just near it

Mistake 2: Ignoring Modifiers Without Commas

Not all modifiers are set off by commas. Some appear as relative clauses starting with "that" or "which," or as participial phrases ending in "-ing" or "-ed." These still follow the next-door neighbor rule.

  • Look for phrases starting with "that," "which," or "who"
  • Watch for words ending in "-ing" (walking, running, thinking) or "-ed" (caught, broken, designed)
  • Verify these modifying phrases are next to the word they describe

Example: "A tarantula bit one of my coworkers that had a huge, hairy body." The phrase "that had a huge, hairy body" should describe the tarantula, not the coworker. The correct version places the modifier next to "tarantula": "A tarantula that had a huge, hairy body bit one of my coworkers."

Mistake 3: Misplacing Prepositional Phrases

Prepositional phrases (such as "in the stands," "on the table," "with automatic parking") can also act as modifiers. These are tested less frequently on the ACT but can be tricky because they are easily overlooked.

  • Identify prepositional phrases that describe a noun
  • Check if the phrase is placed next to the correct word
  • Be especially careful when the prepositional phrase appears at the end of the sentence

Example: "Paul and his father talked about the baseball game in the stands." The prepositional phrase "in the stands" should describe where Paul and his father were located, not where the baseball game was. The correct version is: "In the stands, Paul and his father talked about the baseball game."

Mistake 4: Overlooking Modifiers in Parentheses

Modifying phrases set apart by parentheses are rare on the ACT, but they still follow the next-door neighbor rule. The word being modified must appear immediately before the opening parenthesis.

  • Identify what the parenthetical phrase is defining or describing
  • Check that this word appears directly before the opening parenthesis
  • Reorder the sentence if necessary to place the word in the correct position

Example: "The scientists tried to develop a new method to make their self-pollination study (where pollen from the same plant arrives at the stigma of the flower) completely free of pollen contamination." The parenthetical phrase defines "self-pollination," not "study," so the correct version is: "The scientists tried to develop a new method to study self-pollination (where pollen from the same plant arrives at the stigma of the flower) completely free of pollen contamination."

Mistake 5: Choosing Answer Choices Based on Flow Rather Than Grammar

Many students mistakenly treat misplaced modifier questions as questions about phrasing or sentence flow. While some answer choices may sound better than others, the correct answer is determined by grammar rules, not by personal preference.

  • Do not select an answer because it "sounds good" or "flows well"
  • Always apply the next-door neighbor rule systematically
  • Eliminate answer choices where the modifier is separated from the word it describes

Example 3: Recognizing the Question Type

Analyzing the ultraviolet camera footage, scientists discovered that fish communicate using UV coloration and signaling.

A. NO CHANGE
B. fish were found to communicate using UV coloration and signaling.
C. it was discovered by scientists that fish communicate using UV coloration and signaling.
D. UV coloration and signaling were discovered as a way fish communicate.
E. the discovery was made that fish communicate using UV coloration and signaling.

Solution:

  • Step 1: Recognize this is a misplaced modifier question. Notice that all answer choices are similar in length, contain the same words in different orders, and vary in what comes after the comma.
  • Step 2: Identify the introductory modifier. The phrase "analyzing the ultraviolet camera footage" is the modifier.
  • Step 3: Determine what the modifier describes. Ask: "Who or what is analyzing the ultraviolet camera footage?" The scientists are analyzing the footage, not the fish or the UV coloration.
  • Step 4: Check which answer choice places "scientists" immediately after the comma. Choice A places "scientists" right after the comma, which is correct. Choices B, C, D, and E all place different words or phrases after the comma, none of which are doing the analyzing.
  • Step 5: Verify the sentence makes sense. "Analyzing the ultraviolet camera footage, scientists discovered that fish communicate using UV coloration and signaling" correctly connects the modifier to the scientists who performed the action.

Answer: A

  • ✓For introductory modifiers before a comma, the first noun after the comma must be what the modifier describes
  • ✓Recognize misplaced modifier questions by looking for answer choices with similar length and scrambled word order
  • ✓Always identify the modifier first and ask yourself who or what it describes before selecting an answer
  • ✓Watch for modifiers without commas including relative clauses with that or which and participial phrases ending in -ing or -ed
  • ✓Do not rely on what sounds good or flows well because your brain automatically corrects misplaced modifiers when reading
