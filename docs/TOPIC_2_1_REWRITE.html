Eliminating redundancy and wordiness is one of the most important skills for achieving a high score on the ACT English Test. The test consistently rewards concise writing that expresses ideas using the fewest words possible without sacrificing clarity or grammatical correctness. Understanding how to identify and eliminate redundant and wordy language will help you answer these questions quickly and confidently, as the shortest grammatically correct answer is almost always right.

1. What is redundancy and wordiness?

Redundancy occurs when words or phrases repeat information that has already been stated or implied. Wordiness means using more words than necessary to express an idea. Both errors make writing less effective and are always incorrect on the ACT.

The ACT English Test expects you to recognize that good writing values economy of expression. When multiple answer choices convey the same meaning, the shortest grammatically correct option is virtually always the right answer. This principle applies consistently across redundancy and wordiness questions.

Types of redundancy

  • Direct repetition: Using two or more words that mean the same thing
    • Example: "returned back" (returning already means coming back)
    • Example: "advance planning" (all planning happens in advance)
  • Contextual redundancy: Repeating information already stated elsewhere in the sentence or passage
    • Example: "In Sweden, there are moose in the Swedish forests" (Sweden mentioned twice)
    • Example: "The July 4th event in July" (July mentioned twice)
  • Implied redundancy: Adding words that describe something already implied by definition
    • Example: "close proximity" (proximity already means closeness)
    • Example: "end result" (all results come at the end)

Types of wordiness

  • Unnecessarily complex phrases: Using long phrases when shorter words work better
    • Example: "due to the fact that" instead of "because"
    • Example: "in spite of the fact that" instead of "although"
    • Example: "at this point in time" instead of "now"
  • Overly formal or elaborate descriptions: Using inflated language when simple words suffice
    • Example: "cultural institution supporting intellectual endeavors" instead of "museum"
    • Example: "commence" instead of "begin"
  • Irrelevant information: Including details that do not relate to the main point
    • These answer choices are typically long and contain information that disrupts the flow of the passage
    • If information does not directly support the sentence's purpose, it should be eliminated

Example 1: Basic redundancy elimination

After completing the marathon, Sarah decided to rest and relax for the remainder of the day.

Which choice is the least redundant in context?

A. NO CHANGE
B. rest and relax for the day
C. rest for the remainder of the day
D. relax for the day
E. rest

Solution:

  • Choice A contains redundancy because "rest" and "relax" convey essentially the same meaning in this context. Using both words is unnecessary.
  • Choice B still includes both "rest" and "relax," maintaining the redundancy problem even though "remainder of" is shortened to just "the."
  • Choice C eliminates "relax" but keeps the phrase "remainder of the day," which is more wordy than necessary.
  • Choice D is concise and clear, using "relax" instead of the redundant pairing and simplifying "remainder of the day" to "the day." This is the shortest grammatically correct option.
  • Choice E is the shortest but may be too brief, eliminating the time reference entirely, which could change the meaning slightly depending on context.

Answer: D

2. How to identify redundant language

Identifying redundancy requires careful reading of both the underlined portion and the surrounding context. Follow these systematic techniques to spot redundant and wordy language:

Technique 1: Check for repeated meanings

  • Look for words that express the same concept or idea
  • Ask yourself: "If I remove one of these words, does the sentence still mean the same thing?"
  • Common redundant pairs to watch for:
    • "each and every"
    • "various different"
    • "past history"
    • "future plans"
    • "true facts"
    • "unexpected surprise"

Technique 2: Read the surrounding sentences

  • Always read the sentence before and after the underlined portion
  • Check if information is already mentioned elsewhere in the passage
  • If the same fact appears twice, the second mention is usually redundant
  • Pay special attention to:
    • Names and identifiers already introduced
    • Dates and times already mentioned
    • Locations already established
    • Descriptions already provided

Technique 3: Apply the definition test

  • Check if a modifier describes something already included in the definition of the word it modifies
  • Example: "circle around" is redundant because circling inherently means moving around something
  • Example: "collaborate together" is redundant because collaboration inherently means working together
  • Ask: "Does this word's definition already include the meaning of the modifier?"

Technique 4: Compare answer choice lengths

  • On redundancy and wordiness questions, the shortest answer is almost always correct
  • If the question asks "Which choice is the least redundant?" or "Which choice best avoids wordiness?", use length as a key indicator
  • Check that the shortest option is grammatically correct and preserves the intended meaning
  • Important exception: Do NOT automatically choose the shortest answer on grammar questions that do not specifically ask about redundancy or wordiness

Technique 5: Eliminate irrelevant information

  • Information is irrelevant if it does not directly relate to the topic being discussed
  • Irrelevant details often appear in longer answer choices
  • Ask: "Does this information help support the main point of this sentence or paragraph?"
  • If the detail interrupts the flow or distracts from the main idea, it should be removed

Example 2: Contextual redundancy

The July 4th "Walk for Wags" fundraising event for local dog shelters raised over ten thousand dollars this year.

Which choice is the least redundant in context?

A. NO CHANGE
B. this year for local dog shelters.
C. in July of this year.
D. for local dog shelters.
E. DELETE the underlined portion and end the sentence with a period.

Solution:

  • Apply Technique 2: Read the surrounding context. The sentence already states the event is "for local dog shelters" at the beginning, and it already mentions "July 4th" at the start.
  • Choice A is redundant because it repeats both pieces of information: "for local dog shelters" appears earlier in the sentence, and the year information is implied by the present tense and "July 4th" reference.
  • Choice B maintains the redundancy by repeating "for local dog shelters," which was already stated in the sentence.
  • Choice C is redundant because "July" is already mentioned at the beginning as "July 4th."
  • Choice D is redundant because "for local dog shelters" appears earlier in the same sentence.
  • Choice E eliminates the underlined portion entirely, which is the most concise option. The sentence reads: "The July 4th 'Walk for Wags' fundraising event for local dog shelters raised over ten thousand dollars." This preserves all necessary information without repetition.

Answer: E

3. Common redundancy patterns

The ACT English Test frequently tests the same redundancy and wordiness patterns. Recognizing these common patterns will help you identify errors more quickly and confidently.

Pattern 1: Action verb redundancy

  • Common errors:
    • "return back" → Use "return"
    • "repeat again" → Use "repeat"
    • "continue on" → Use "continue"
    • "ascend up" → Use "ascend"
    • "descend down" → Use "descend"
    • "merge together" → Use "merge"
  • These verbs already contain the meaning of the directional word that follows them

Pattern 2: Adjective-noun redundancy

  • Common errors:
    • "past history" → Use "history"
    • "future plans" → Use "plans"
    • "final outcome" → Use "outcome"
    • "end result" → Use "result"
    • "new innovation" → Use "innovation"
    • "added bonus" → Use "bonus"
  • The adjective describes something already inherent in the noun's definition

Pattern 3: Prepositional phrase wordiness

  • Replace wordy phrases with single words:
    • "due to the fact that" → Use "because"
    • "in spite of the fact that" → Use "although"
    • "at this point in time" → Use "now"
    • "in the event that" → Use "if"
    • "for the purpose of" → Use "to"
    • "in order to" → Use "to"
  • These phrases add unnecessary length without adding meaning

Pattern 4: Geographic and temporal redundancy

  • Watch for repeated location information:
    • "In Sweden, the Swedish forests..." → Remove "Swedish"
    • "Paris, the capital of France in France..." → Remove "in France"
  • Watch for repeated time information:
    • "The July 4th event in July..." → Remove "in July"
    • "At 3:00 p.m. in the afternoon..." → Use "at 3:00 p.m." or "in the afternoon"

Pattern 5: Double descriptors

  • Common errors using multiple words with similar meanings:
    • "each and every" → Use "each" or "every"
    • "various different" → Use "various" or "different"
    • "few and far between" → Use "rare"
    • "null and void" → Use "void"
    • "first and foremost" → Use "first" or "most important"

Pattern 6: Inflated vocabulary

  • Replace unnecessarily complex language with simpler alternatives:
    • "utilize" → Use "use"
    • "commence" → Use "begin"
    • "terminate" → Use "end"
    • "endeavor" → Use "try"
    • "facilitate" → Use "help"
    • "cultural institution supporting intellectual endeavors" → Use "museum"
  • The ACT rewards clear, direct language over unnecessarily formal vocabulary

Example 3: Multiple redundancy patterns

The historic museum houses rare artifacts that are displayed in glass cases for the purpose of preserving them for future generations to come.

Which choice best avoids wordiness and redundancy?

A. NO CHANGE
B. for the purpose of preserving them for the future.
C. in order to preserve them for future generations.
D. to preserve them for future generations.
E. to preserve them.

Solution:

  • Choice A contains multiple redundancy and wordiness problems:
    • Pattern 3: "for the purpose of" is wordy when "to" works better
    • Pattern 2: "future generations to come" is redundant because generations are inherently future, and "to come" adds no new information
  • Choice B improves slightly by removing "to come" but maintains the wordy phrase "for the purpose of" and the potentially redundant "for the future."
  • Choice C eliminates "for the purpose of" but still uses the wordy phrase "in order to" when "to" is sufficient.
  • Choice D correctly replaces the wordy phrases with the simple and concise "to." It keeps "future generations," which adds meaningful information about who will benefit from preservation.
  • Choice E is the shortest but may be too brief. While "to preserve them" is grammatically correct, it removes "future generations," which provides important context about the purpose of preservation. The phrase "future generations" is not redundant because it specifies who benefits from the preservation.
  • Choice D strikes the best balance: it eliminates all wordiness and redundancy while preserving meaningful information.

Answer: D

This example demonstrates why you should not always choose the absolute shortest answer. Choice E removes too much information, while Choice D achieves conciseness without sacrificing clarity or meaning. The key is to eliminate only redundant and wordy language while keeping information that serves a clear purpose.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓The shortest grammatically correct answer is almost always right on redundancy and wordiness questions
  • ✓Always read surrounding sentences to check for information already mentioned in context
  • ✓Watch for words that repeat meanings already implied by other words in the phrase
  • ✓Replace wordy prepositional phrases like "due to the fact that" with simple words like "because"
  • ✓Only use the "shortest answer" strategy when the question specifically asks about redundancy or wordiness
  • ✓Eliminate irrelevant information that does not directly support the sentence's main purpose
