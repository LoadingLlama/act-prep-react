The grammar review is your final step before mastering the ACT English Test. After learning individual grammar rules in Topics 1.1 through 1.8, you need a systematic way to review, reinforce, and retain everything you've learned. This comprehensive review guide brings together all major grammar concepts, provides strategic review methods, and helps you identify the most common mistakes students make when preparing for test day. Effective review transforms scattered knowledge into confident, automatic recognition of errors and corrections.

1. What Should You Review for the ACT?

A complete grammar review must cover all testable concepts from the ACT English Test. The test consistently evaluates eight major grammar categories, each with specific rules and patterns you need to recognize instantly.

Sentence Structure and Boundaries

Sentence structure forms the foundation of ACT grammar. You must understand how sentences connect and separate correctly.

  • Independent clauses: Complete thoughts with subject and verb that can stand alone
  • Dependent clauses: Incomplete thoughts that must attach to independent clauses
  • Comma splices: Incorrectly joining two independent clauses with only a comma
  • Run-on sentences: Two independent clauses joined without proper punctuation or conjunction
  • Sentence fragments: Incomplete sentences missing subject or verb, or dependent clauses standing alone

Punctuation Rules

The ACT tests specific punctuation marks in predictable ways. Master these six critical punctuation categories:

  • Commas: FANBOYS conjunctions, introductory elements, nonessential clauses, items in series
  • Semicolons: Joining independent clauses, separating complex list items
  • Colons: Introducing lists, explanations, or examples after independent clauses
  • Apostrophes: Showing possession and forming contractions
  • Dashes: Setting off parenthetical information or creating emphasis
  • Parentheses: Enclosing supplementary information or clarifications

Verb Agreement and Tense

Verbs must match their subjects in number and maintain consistent tense throughout passages.

  • Subject-verb agreement: Singular subjects take singular verbs; plural subjects take plural verbs
  • Tricky subjects: Collective nouns, indefinite pronouns, inverted sentences, compound subjects
  • Verb tense consistency: Maintaining proper timeline within and across paragraphs
  • Tense shifts: Knowing when tense changes are correct versus incorrect

Pronouns

Pronoun questions test whether pronouns clearly refer to specific nouns and use the correct case.

  • Pronoun-antecedent agreement: Pronouns must match their antecedents in number, gender, and person
  • Pronoun case: Subject pronouns (I, he, she), object pronouns (me, him, her), possessive pronouns (my, his, her)
  • Pronoun clarity: Ensuring pronouns have clear, unambiguous antecedents
  • Reflexive pronouns: Correct use of myself, himself, herself, themselves

Modifiers and Modification

Modifiers must be placed correctly to modify the intended words without creating confusion or ambiguity.

  • Misplaced modifiers: Modifying words placed too far from what they describe
  • Dangling modifiers: Opening phrases that don't logically connect to the sentence subject
  • Adjective and adverb forms: Using correct forms to modify nouns versus verbs
  • Comparative and superlative forms: Proper use of -er/-est and more/most

Parallel Structure

Parallelism requires items in lists, comparisons, and paired constructions to use matching grammatical forms.

  • Lists: All items must be nouns, verbs, phrases, or clauses of the same type
  • Correlative conjunctions: Both...and, either...or, not only...but also, neither...nor
  • Comparisons: Elements being compared must be grammatically equivalent

Conciseness and Style

The ACT rewards clear, direct writing without unnecessary words or phrases.

  • Redundancy: Eliminating words that repeat the same meaning
  • Wordiness: Choosing shorter, clearer expressions over lengthy ones
  • Precision: Using specific, accurate words instead of vague language
  • Active voice: Preferring active over passive constructions when possible

Transitions and Organization

Effective writing uses transition words to connect ideas logically and maintain clear organization.

  • Addition transitions: Additionally, furthermore, moreover
  • Contrast transitions: However, nevertheless, in contrast
  • Cause and effect: Therefore, consequently, as a result
  • Time sequence: First, then, finally, meanwhile
  • Sentence placement: Determining the most logical position for information

Example 1: Comprehensive Grammar Review

The scientist conducted extensive research, she published her findings in a prestigious journal.

A. NO CHANGE
B. research; she published
C. research, and she published
D. research she published
E. research and, she published

Solution:

  • Identify the problem: Two independent clauses ("The scientist conducted extensive research" and "she published her findings in a prestigious journal")
  • Choice A creates a comma splice by using only a comma to join independent clauses
  • Choice B correctly uses a semicolon to join two related independent clauses
  • Choice C is also grammatically correct, using comma + FANBOYS conjunction "and"
  • Choice D creates a run-on sentence with no punctuation or conjunction
  • Choice E incorrectly places comma before conjunction without placing one after
  • Between B and C, both are acceptable; choose based on style or other context

Answer: B (though C is also grammatically correct)

2. How to Review Effectively

Effective review requires more than passive rereading of rules. Use active strategies that strengthen recognition, speed, and accuracy on test day.

Strategy 1: Category-Based Practice

Review one grammar category at a time rather than mixing all concepts randomly. This focused approach builds stronger pattern recognition.

  • Day 1: Focus exclusively on sentence structure and comma splices
    • Complete 20-30 practice problems testing only these concepts
    • Review any mistakes immediately and identify the specific rule you missed
  • Day 2: Review punctuation rules systematically
    • Work through comma rules, then semicolons, then colons
    • Create quick reference notes for each punctuation mark's uses
  • Day 3-7: Continue through remaining categories
    • Verbs, pronouns, modifiers, parallelism, conciseness, transitions
    • Spend extra time on categories where you make the most errors
  • Day 8+: Mixed practice combining all categories
    • Take full ACT English practice sections under timed conditions
    • Track which categories still cause difficulty

Strategy 2: Error Log Method

Create a systematic record of every error you make during practice. This transforms mistakes into learning opportunities.

  • Record these details for each error:
    • Grammar category (sentence structure, punctuation, verbs, etc.)
    • Specific rule violated
    • Why you chose the wrong answer
    • The correct answer and explanation
  • Review your error log weekly:
    • Identify patterns in your mistakes
    • Focus additional practice on recurring error types
    • Track improvement over time

Strategy 3: Timed Drills

Speed matters on the ACT English Test. You have approximately 36 seconds per question to read, identify errors, and select answers.

  • Week 1-2: Practice without time pressure
    • Focus entirely on accuracy and understanding rules
    • Take as much time as needed to work through problems
  • Week 3-4: Introduce timing gradually
    • Set 45-second time limits per question
    • Build comfort working under mild time pressure
  • Week 5+: Full-speed practice
    • Work at actual test pace (36 seconds per question)
    • Complete full 45-minute practice sections regularly

Strategy 4: Self-Testing with Flashcards

Create flashcards for rules you struggle to remember. Active recall strengthens long-term retention better than passive review.

  • Front: Grammar rule or concept name ("When to use semicolons")
  • Back: Explanation and examples
  • Review flashcards daily, removing cards you consistently answer correctly
  • Keep difficult cards in rotation until they become automatic

Strategy 5: Teach the Concepts

Teaching grammar rules to someone else reveals gaps in your understanding and reinforces what you know.

  • Explain each major grammar category to a friend, family member, or study partner
  • Create your own example problems demonstrating each rule
  • If you struggle to explain a concept clearly, review that material again
  • Write out explanations as if teaching someone completely unfamiliar with grammar

Example 2: Strategic Review Application

Each of the students were required to submit their final project before the deadline, but only a few of them actually met the requirement on time.

A. NO CHANGE
B. Each of the students was required to submit his or her
C. Each of the students was required to submit their
D. Each of the students were required to submit his or her
E. All of the students were required to submit their

Solution:

  • Identify two potential errors: subject-verb agreement and pronoun-antecedent agreement
  • The subject is "each," which is singular despite "of the students" (prepositional phrase)
    • Singular subject requires singular verb "was," not "were"
  • The pronoun must agree with "each" (singular), not "students"
    • "Their" is plural and doesn't match singular "each"
    • "His or her" correctly matches singular antecedent
  • Choice A: Incorrect verb ("were") and pronoun ("their")
  • Choice B: Corrects both errors - "was" (correct verb) and "his or her" (correct pronoun)
  • Choice C: Corrects verb but not pronoun
  • Choice D: Corrects pronoun but not verb
  • Choice E: Changes meaning by switching to "all" instead of "each"

Answer: B

3. Common Review Mistakes

Students often undermine their review efforts by using ineffective strategies. Avoid these common pitfalls to maximize your preparation.

Mistake 1: Passive Reading Instead of Active Practice

Simply reading through grammar rules without applying them fails to build the skills you need for test day.

  • Problem: Students read rule explanations repeatedly without practicing problems
  • Solution: Spend 80% of review time on practice problems, only 20% on reading rules
  • Apply every rule immediately after learning it through targeted exercises

Mistake 2: Reviewing Only Correct Answers

Focusing only on what you get right provides false confidence and misses crucial learning opportunities.

  • Problem: Students check if answers are correct but don't analyze incorrect choices
  • Solution: For every error, identify the specific rule you violated and why the correct answer works
  • Review correct answers too - ensure you selected them for the right reasons
  • Understand why wrong answer choices are incorrect, not just which one is right

Mistake 3: Cramming Instead of Spacing

Massed practice (studying intensively in short periods) produces worse long-term retention than spaced repetition.

  • Problem: Studying grammar for eight hours the day before the test
  • Solution: Review consistently over weeks, with sessions spaced days apart
  • Return to previously learned material regularly to reinforce retention
  • Plan: 30-45 minutes daily for 4-6 weeks beats 10 hours in two days

Mistake 4: Ignoring Weak Areas

Students naturally gravitate toward concepts they already understand, avoiding difficult material that needs the most attention.

  • Problem: Practicing comma rules repeatedly while avoiding pronoun problems
  • Solution: Allocate practice time proportional to error frequency
  • Track which categories cause the most mistakes and triple practice time on those areas
  • Work through discomfort with difficult concepts - that's where score improvements happen

Mistake 5: Not Reviewing Under Realistic Conditions

Practicing without time limits or in comfortable environments fails to prepare you for actual test conditions.

  • Problem: Always practicing untimed or with music, phone nearby, or taking frequent breaks
  • Solution: Complete at least weekly full-length practice sections under exact test conditions
  • Simulate test day: quiet room, strict timing, no breaks, no external resources
  • Practice managing test anxiety and maintaining focus for 45 consecutive minutes

Mistake 6: Memorizing Examples Instead of Understanding Rules

The ACT presents grammar problems in countless variations. Memorizing specific examples won't help with novel questions.

  • Problem: Remembering that "Each of the students was" is correct without understanding why
  • Solution: Master the underlying principle - "each" is always singular regardless of prepositional phrases
  • Test your understanding by creating your own examples applying each rule
  • Explain rules in your own words rather than repeating memorized definitions

Example 3: Avoiding Review Mistakes

Walking through the forest, the trees seemed to whisper ancient secrets to Maria and I as we carefully made our way along the winding path.

A. NO CHANGE
B. the trees seemed to whisper ancient secrets to Maria and me
C. the trees seemed to whisper ancient secrets to me and Maria
D. Maria and I heard the trees seeming to whisper ancient secrets
E. we heard the trees seeming to whisper ancient secrets

Solution:

  • Identify two issues: possible dangling modifier and pronoun case error
  • Dangling modifier analysis:
    • "Walking through the forest" should modify the subject of the main clause
    • In choices A, B, and C, "the trees" are the subject - trees can't walk
    • Choices D and E fix this by making people the subject
  • Pronoun case analysis:
    • "Maria and I" functions as object of preposition "to"
    • Object pronouns required: "me," not "I"
    • Test by removing "Maria and": "to I" is clearly wrong; "to me" is correct
  • Choice A: Dangling modifier and wrong pronoun case
  • Choice B: Fixes pronoun but not modifier
  • Choice C: Fixes pronoun but not modifier (also poor style putting "me" first)
  • Choice D: Fixes modifier, but awkward phrasing with "seeming"
  • Choice E: Best answer - fixes modifier with clearest, most direct phrasing

Answer: E
