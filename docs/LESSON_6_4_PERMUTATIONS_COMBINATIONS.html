Permutations and combinations appear in 1-3 questions on the ACT, testing your ability to count arrangements and selections using the fundamental counting principle, permutation formula P(n,r), and combination formula C(n,r). The key decision is recognizing when order matters (use permutations) versus when order doesn't matter (use combinations)—choosing president, vice president, and secretary requires permutations (order matters), while choosing a 3-person committee requires combinations (order doesn't matter). Mastering this distinction and the formulas ensures you can tackle any counting problem.

1. Fundamental Counting Principle

The Principle

If one event can occur in m ways and a second event can occur in n ways, then the two events together can occur in m × n ways. This extends to any number of stages: multiply the number of choices at each stage.
Example: If you have 3 shirts and 4 pairs of pants, you can create 3 × 4 = 12 different outfits.

Example 1: Fundamental Counting Principle

A restaurant offers 5 appetizers, 8 main courses, and 3 desserts. How many different three-course meals can be ordered?

A. 16
B. 40
C. 64
D. 120
E. 240

Solution:

Number of meals = 5 × 8 × 3 = 120. The answer is D.

2. Permutations (Order Matters)

Permutations of n Objects

The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects in order is n! (n factorial), where n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 2 × 1.
Example: 5 books can be arranged on a shelf in 5! = 120 ways.

Permutations of r Objects from n

The number of ways to arrange r objects selected from n distinct objects is: P(n, r) = n! ÷ (n - r)!. This is also written as nPr or nPr.
Example: How many ways can you arrange 3 books selected from 5? P(5, 3) = 5! ÷ 2! = 120 ÷ 2 = 60.

Example 2: Permutations

In how many ways can a president, vice president, and secretary be chosen from a group of 8 people?

F. 24
G. 56
H. 336
J. 512
K. 40,320

Solution:

Order matters (president ≠ vice president). Use P(8, 3) = 8! ÷ 5! = (8 × 7 × 6 × 5!) ÷ 5! = 8 × 7 × 6 = 336. The answer is H.

3. Combinations (Order Does Not Matter)

Combinations of r Objects from n

The number of ways to select r objects from n distinct objects (where order does not matter) is: C(n, r) = n! ÷ (r! × (n - r)!). This is also written as nCr or nCr or "n choose r".
Example: How many ways can you choose 3 books from 5? C(5, 3) = 5! ÷ (3! × 2!) = 120 ÷ (6 × 2) = 10.

When to Use Permutations vs. Combinations

Use permutations when order matters (arranging, ranking, positions with different roles).
Use combinations when order does not matter (selecting, choosing, forming groups where all members are equivalent).

Example 3: Combinations

A committee of 4 people is to be selected from a group of 10 people. How many different committees can be formed?

A. 40
B. 210
C. 5,040
D. 10,000
E. 151,200

Solution:

Order does not matter (all committee members are equal). Use C(10, 4) = 10! ÷ (4! × 6!) = (10 × 9 × 8 × 7) ÷ (4 × 3 × 2 × 1) = 5040 ÷ 24 = 210. The answer is B.

4. Advanced Counting Problems

Permutations with Repetition

If there are n objects where some are identical, the number of distinct arrangements is: n! ÷ (n1! × n2! × ... × nk!), where n1, n2, ..., nk are the counts of each type of identical object.
Example: The word "MISSISSIPPI" has 11 letters: 1 M, 4 I's, 4 S's, 2 P's. Arrangements = 11! ÷ (1! × 4! × 4! × 2!).

Circular Permutations

The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects in a circle is (n - 1)!, since rotations of the same arrangement are considered identical.
Example: 5 people sitting around a circular table can be arranged in (5 - 1)! = 4! = 24 ways.

Example 4: Complex Counting Problem

How many distinct arrangements are there of the letters in the word "BANANA"?

F. 30
G. 60
H. 120
J. 360
K. 720

Solution:

"BANANA" has 6 letters: 3 A's, 2 N's, 1 B. Arrangements = 6! ÷ (3! × 2! × 1!) = 720 ÷ (6 × 2 × 1) = 720 ÷ 12 = 60. The answer is G.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Fundamental Counting Principle: If task 1 has m ways and task 2 has n ways, total = m × n
  • ✓Permutations: Order matters. P(n,r) = n!/(n−r)!. Arranging r items from n items
  • ✓Combinations: Order doesn't matter. C(n,r) = n!/[r!(n−r)!]. Selecting r items from n items
  • ✓Factorial: n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 1. Note: 0! = 1
  • ✓If problem asks "arrange" or "order", use permutations. If "select" or "choose", use combinations
  • ✓For problems with restrictions, calculate unrestricted then subtract forbidden cases
