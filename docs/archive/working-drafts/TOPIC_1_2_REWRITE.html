Understanding comma rules is one of the most important skills for the ACT English Test. Commas appear more frequently than any other punctuation mark, and knowing exactly when to use them helps you answer questions quickly and confidently. This skill is especially valuable because mastering four essential rules covers nearly all comma questions you'll encounter on test day.

1. What Are the 4 Essential Comma Rules?

The ACT tests four main comma rules. Master these four patterns, and you'll handle the vast majority of comma questions with ease.

Rule 1: Comma + FANBOYS

  • Use a comma and a FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) to join two independent clauses
    • Both parts must be complete sentences that can stand alone
    • The comma comes before the FANBOYS word
    • Example: "My alarm clock did not go off this morning, so I arrived late at school."

Rule 2: Dependent Clause + Comma + Independent Clause

  • When a dependent clause comes first, place a comma before the independent clause
    • Dependent clauses start with words like while, although, because, when, if, since
    • The comma separates the dependent clause from the independent clause
    • Example: "While electronic music has become very popular, many people still prefer classic rock."

Rule 3: Unnecessary Information Commas

  • Commas separate unnecessary information from the rest of the sentence
    • Information is unnecessary if you can remove it without changing the sentence's core meaning
    • Unnecessary information can appear before a comma, after a comma, or between two commas
    • Example: "Mrs. Ellison, who is known for giving pop quizzes, is my least favorite teacher."
    • Test: Cross out "who is known for giving pop quizzes" - the sentence still works

Rule 4: Listing Commas

  • Commas separate items in lists of three or more
    • Use commas to separate three or more items or adjectives
    • Lists of only two items never use commas
    • Example: "The group ordered coconut shrimp, hot wings, and onion rings."
    • Example with adjectives: "The old, limping dog still managed to complete the 3-mile hike."

Example 1: Applying the 4 Essential Rules

The basketball team, though did not mount a comeback this week.

A. NO CHANGE
B. team though,
C. team, though,
D. team though

Solution:

  • "Though" is unnecessary information - we can remove it and the sentence still works
  • Rule 3 requires unnecessary information between two commas when it appears in the middle of a sentence
  • Test: "The basketball team did not mount a comeback this week" still makes sense
  • Choice C correctly places commas around "though"

Answer: C

2. Advanced Comma Techniques

Beyond the four essential rules, understanding these advanced techniques helps you tackle the trickiest comma questions on the ACT.

The "Crossing-Out" Trick

  • For unnecessary information commas, cross out the information between (or before/after) the commas
    • If what remains is a complete sentence, the information is unnecessary
    • If the sentence breaks without it, the information is necessary (no commas)
    • This trick works for the vast majority of unnecessary information questions

The Names Rule

  • Whether names get commas depends on specificity
    • Non-specific identifier (friend, teacher, physicist): No commas around the name
    • Specific identifier (best friend, youngest sister, CEO): Commas around the name
    • Example (no commas): "My friend Kelly recently moved to London."
    • Example (with commas): "My best friend, Andrew, is a certified scuba diver."

"That" vs. "Which" Phrases

  • Phrases starting with "that" never get commas (necessary information)
    • Example: "The dogs that live down the street are always barking."
  • Phrases starting with "which" always get commas (unnecessary information)
    • Example: "My neighbor's dogs, which are always barking, love to play fetch."
    • Anytime you see "which," there must be a comma right before it

Prepositional Phrases

  • Prepositional phrases at the start of a sentence always get a comma after them
    • Example: "On my way to work, I listened to my new favorite podcast."
  • Prepositional phrases in the middle or end almost never get commas
    • They usually contain essential information specifying the noun
    • Example: "The clothing on the bed is for vacation."

Example 2: Using Advanced Techniques

My youngest sister Rebecca is studying marine biology at UC San Diego.

A. NO CHANGE
B. sister, Rebecca
C. sister, Rebecca,
D. sister Rebecca,

Solution:

  • Apply the Names Rule: "youngest sister" is a specific identifier (you can only have one youngest sister)
  • Since the identifier is specific to one person, the name "Rebecca" is unnecessary information
  • Unnecessary information in the middle of a sentence needs commas around it
  • Choice C correctly places commas before and after "Rebecca"

Answer: C

3. Common Comma Mistakes

Understanding comma rules isn't enough - you must recognize the most frequent errors that appear on the ACT. Avoid these common mistakes:

  • Using only one comma for unnecessary information - When unnecessary information appears in the middle, you need two commas (one before and one after)
  • Adding commas in two-item lists - Lists need three or more items to use commas
  • Confusing comma + FANBOYS with two-item lists - Check if the second half is an independent clause
  • Forgetting commas after introductory prepositional phrases
  • Placing commas around necessary information that specifies which noun you mean
  • Separating adjectives that build on each other (like "bright floral furniture")

Example 3: Spotting Common Errors

The debate team took first place in California, and then won the grand prize for the entire nation.

A. NO CHANGE
B. California and then won
C. California, and then, won
D. California and, then won

Solution:

  • Choice A uses comma + FANBOYS, but check if both halves are independent clauses
  • First half: "The debate team took first place in California" (independent)
  • Second half: "then won the grand prize" (NOT independent - missing subject)
  • This is actually a two-item list (took first place AND won grand prize), not two independent clauses
  • Two-item lists never use commas - Choice B is correct

Answer: B

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Master the four essential comma rules to handle the vast majority of comma questions
  • ✓Use the "crossing-out" trick to test if information is unnecessary
  • ✓The Names Rule depends on specificity - specific identifiers mean unnecessary names
  • ✓"That" phrases never get commas, "which" phrases always get commas
  • ✓Two-item lists never use commas - don't confuse them with comma + FANBOYS
  • ✓Prepositional phrases at the start always get a comma after them
