Understanding sentence structure is one of the most essential skills for the ACT English Test. Instead of memorizing countless grammar rules in isolation, mastering how clauses and phrases combine helps you spot errors quickly and choose correct answers confidently. This skill is especially valuable because sentence structure questions appear frequently, and recognizing the patterns makes solving them fast and accurate.

1. What Is Sentence Structure?

Sentence structure refers to how you combine subjects, verbs, and modifiers to create grammatically correct sentences. Every sentence is built from clauses (groups of words with a subject and verb) or phrases (groups of words without both a subject and verb). Knowing the difference determines whether you're writing complete sentences or creating errors.

  • Independent clauses:
    • Contain a subject and a verb
    • Express a complete thought
    • Can stand alone as complete sentences
    • Example: "The dog chased its tail."
  • Dependent clauses:
    • Contain a subject and a verb
    • Cannot stand alone (incomplete thought)
    • Usually start with subordinating conjunctions
    • Example: "While the dog chased its tail..."
  • Phrases:
    • Lack a subject or verb (or both)
    • Never express complete thoughts
    • Provide descriptive or modifying information
    • Example: "Chasing its tail..."

Common subordinating conjunctions that create dependent clauses include: while, although, because, when, if, since, before, after, unless, and though. When you see these words at the start of a clause, you know it cannot stand alone.

Example 1: Identifying Dependent vs. Independent Clauses

Although the bakery opens at 6 AM every morning. It always has a line of customers waiting outside.

A. NO CHANGE
B. morning, it
C. morning; it
D. morning it

Solution:

  • "Although the bakery opens at 6 AM every morning" is a dependent clause because it starts with "although"
  • Dependent clauses cannot stand alone as sentences - Choice A creates a sentence fragment
  • Must connect to the independent clause that follows
  • Choice B correctly uses a comma to join the dependent and independent clauses

Answer: B

2. The 5 Rules for Combining Clauses

On the ACT, when you combine multiple clauses, the sentence must follow one of five specific patterns. Master these five rules, and you'll spot sentence structure errors instantly.

Rule 1: Period (Two Separate Sentences)

  • Use a period to separate two independent clauses
    • Creates two complete, grammatically correct sentences
    • Most straightforward way to combine ideas
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats."

Rule 2: Comma + FANBOYS

  • Join two independent clauses with a comma followed by a coordinating conjunction
    • FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So
    • Both a comma AND the conjunction are required
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats."

Rule 3: Semicolon Between Independent Clauses

  • Use a semicolon to join two independent clauses
    • Functions exactly like a period on the ACT
    • Shows close relationship between ideas
    • No conjunction needed after semicolon
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats."

Rule 4: Dependent Clause + Comma + Independent Clause

  • When a dependent clause comes first, place a comma before the independent clause
    • Dependent clause starts with a subordinating conjunction
    • Comma is required to separate the clauses
    • Example: "While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats."

Rule 5: Independent Clause + Dependent Clause (No Comma)

  • When the independent clause comes first, no comma is needed before the dependent clause
    • The sentence flows naturally without punctuation
    • Common error: adding an unnecessary comma
    • Example: "Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats."

Example 2: Applying the 5 Rules

The famous scientist Marie Curie discovered radium, she won two Nobel Prizes for her groundbreaking research.

A. NO CHANGE
B. radium. She
C. radium, and she
D. radium; she

Solution:

  • Identify two independent clauses: "Marie Curie discovered radium" and "she won two Nobel Prizes"
  • Choice A creates a comma splice - joining independent clauses with only a comma (always incorrect)
  • Choice B uses Rule 1 (period between clauses) ✓
  • Choice C uses Rule 2 (comma + FANBOYS) ✓
  • Choice D uses Rule 3 (semicolon between clauses) ✓

Answer: B, C, or D (all three correctly fix the error)

3. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Understanding sentence structure isn't enough - you must recognize the most frequent errors that appear on the ACT. Avoid these common mistakes:

  • Comma splices - Joining two independent clauses with only a comma (no conjunction)
  • Sentence fragments - Punctuating dependent clauses or phrases as complete sentences
  • Using FANBOYS without a comma before it when joining independent clauses
  • Adding unnecessary commas when independent clause comes before dependent clause
  • Placing a comma after a semicolon (semicolons work alone, not with commas)
  • Forgetting the required comma when dependent clause comes first

Example 3: Spotting Common Errors

The student studied diligently for the final exam but she still felt nervous on test day.

A. NO CHANGE
B. exam, but she
C. exam. But she
D. exam; but she

Solution:

  • Two independent clauses: "The student studied diligently" and "she still felt nervous"
  • Choice A makes a common mistake: using FANBOYS ("but") without the required comma
  • Rule 2 requires BOTH comma + FANBOYS when joining independent clauses
  • Choice B correctly includes comma before "but"

Answer: B

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Master the five rules for combining clauses to spot sentence structure errors instantly
  • ✓Independent clauses express complete thoughts and can stand alone as sentences
  • ✓Dependent clauses start with subordinating conjunctions and cannot stand alone
  • ✓Comma splices (comma alone joining independent clauses) are always wrong on the ACT
  • ✓FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) require commas before them when joining clauses
  • ✓Semicolons function like periods and can replace them between independent clauses
