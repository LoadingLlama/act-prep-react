Systems of Equations

Understanding systems of equations, elimination method, and substitution method is essential for ACT Math success. These concepts appear in 4-6 questions per test—that's nearly 7% of all math questions! The ACT loves testing your ability to solve two equations simultaneously, especially in word problem contexts.

1. The Substitution Method

The substitution method involves solving one equation for a variable, then substituting that expression into the other equation. This method works particularly well when one equation is already solved for a variable or can easily be solved.

When to Use Substitution

  • Easy isolation: When one equation has a coefficient of 1 or −1
  • Already solved: When one equation is y = mx + b or x = (expression)
  • Complex elimination: When elimination would create messy fractions

Substitution Steps

  • Step 1: Solve one equation for one variable (choose the easiest one)
  • Step 2: Substitute that expression into the other equation
  • Step 3: Solve the resulting single-variable equation
  • Step 4: Substitute back to find the other variable
  • Step 5: Check your solution in both original equations

Solve the system: y = 2x + 3 and 3x + 2y = 16. What is the value of x?

A. −2
B. 1
C. 2
D. 3
E. 5

2. The Elimination Method

The elimination method (also called the addition method) involves adding or subtracting equations to eliminate one variable. This method is often faster and cleaner than substitution, especially when coefficients align nicely.

When to Use Elimination

  • Matching coefficients: When variables have the same or opposite coefficients
  • Easy multiplication: When you can multiply to create matching coefficients
  • Standard form: When both equations are in Ax + By = C form

Elimination Steps

  • Step 1: Align equations vertically with variables lined up
  • Step 2: Multiply one or both equations to create opposite coefficients for one variable
  • Step 3: Add the equations to eliminate one variable
  • Step 4: Solve the resulting single-variable equation
  • Step 5: Substitute back to find the other variable

ACT Strategy Tip

On the ACT, elimination is usually faster than substitution when both equations have integer coefficients. Look for opposite coefficients (like 3x and −3x) or coefficients that are multiples of each other (like 2x and 6x).

Solve the system: 2x + 3y = 12 and 4x − 3y = 6. What is the value of x + y?

A. 1
B. 2
C. 3
D. 4
E. 5

Solution:

  • Perfect setup for elimination: Notice +3y and −3y—they're opposites!
  • Add the equations: (2x + 3y) + (4x − 3y) = 12 + 6
  • The y terms cancel: 6x = 18
  • Solve: x = 3
  • Substitute x = 3 into first equation: 2(3) + 3y = 12 → 6 + 3y = 12 → 3y = 6 → y = 2
  • Find x + y: 3 + 2 = 5
  • Check: In second equation: 4(3) − 3(2) = 12 − 6 = 6 ✓

Answer: E

3. Word Problems with Systems

The ACT frequently tests systems of equations word problems. The key is translating the English sentences into mathematical equations. Look for relationships between two unknowns and set up two separate equations.

Common Word Problem Types

  • Age problems: Relationships between people's ages now and in the past/future
  • Money problems: Total cost and relationships between prices
  • Mixture problems: Combining two substances with different concentrations
  • Rate problems: Distance = rate × time with two moving objects

Translation Key Phrases

  • "The sum is..." → x + y = [number]
  • "Combined" or "together" → addition
  • "More than" → addition, "less than" → subtraction
  • "Twice as much as" → 2x or multiply by 2
  • "The difference is..." → x − y = [number]

At a school fundraiser, student tickets cost $5 and adult tickets cost $8. The school sold 120 tickets and collected $810 in total. How many student tickets were sold?

A. 30
B. 40
C. 50
D. 60
E. 70

Solution:

  • Define variables: Let s = student tickets, a = adult tickets
  • Equation 1 (total tickets): s + a = 120
  • Equation 2 (total money): 5s + 8a = 810
  • Use elimination: Multiply first equation by −5: −5s − 5a = −600
  • Add to second equation: (5s + 8a) + (−5s − 5a) = 810 + (−600)
  • Simplify: 3a = 210
  • Solve: a = 70 adult tickets
  • Substitute back: s + 70 = 120 → s = 50 student tickets
  • Check: 5(50) + 8(70) = 250 + 560 = 810 ✓

Answer: C

4. Special Cases and Graphical Interpretation

Sometimes systems have no solution (parallel lines) or infinitely many solutions (same line). Understanding the graphical interpretation helps you recognize these special cases quickly.

Graphical Interpretation

  • One solution: Lines intersect at one point (most common on ACT)
  • No solution: Lines are parallel (same slope, different y-intercepts)
  • Infinite solutions: Lines are identical (same slope, same y-intercept)

Recognizing Special Cases Algebraically

  • No solution: You get a false statement like 0 = 5 or 3 = 7
  • Infinite solutions: You get a true statement like 0 = 0 or 5 = 5
  • Example: If 2x + y = 5 and 4x + 2y = 10, the second is just the first doubled (infinite solutions)

The lines y = 2x − 3 and y = −x + 6 intersect at point (a, b). What is the value of a + b?

A. 4
B. 5
C. 6
D. 7
E. 8

Solution:

  • Both equations are solved for y: Perfect for substitution!
  • Set the expressions equal: 2x − 3 = −x + 6
  • Add x to both sides: 3x − 3 = 6
  • Add 3: 3x = 9
  • Solve: x = 3, so a = 3
  • Substitute into first equation: y = 2(3) − 3 = 6 − 3 = 3, so b = 3
  • Find a + b: 3 + 3 = 6
  • Verify: In second equation: y = −(3) + 6 = 3 ✓

Answer: C

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Use substitution when one equation is already solved for a variable or can easily be isolated
  • ✓Use elimination when coefficients are opposites or easy multiples—it's usually faster
  • ✓For word problems, define variables clearly and translate two relationships into two equations
  • ✓The intersection point (a, b) satisfies BOTH equations—use this to solve graphically
  • ✓Always check your solution in BOTH original equations to catch arithmetic errors
  • ✓Special cases: No solution (parallel lines) gives false statements like 0 = 5; infinite solutions (same line) gives true statements like 0 = 0
