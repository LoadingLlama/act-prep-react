Concept 1: Understanding Exponents

Exponents represent repeated multiplication. Understanding exponent rules is crucial for success on the ACT Math section, as these concepts appear in algebra, functions, and scientific notation problems.

Exponent Basics

  • Base: The number being multiplied (in 5³, base is 5)
  • Exponent/Power: How many times to multiply (in 5³, exponent is 3)
  • Meaning: 5³ = 5 × 5 × 5 = 125
  • Read as: "5 to the third power" or "5 cubed"

Key point: a^n means multiply 'a' by itself 'n' times

Concept 2: Product Rule for Exponents

When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents.

Product Rule

a^m × a^n = a^(m+n)

When bases are the same, add the exponents

Example: x³ × x⁵ = x^(3+5) = x⁸

Remember: This only works when the bases are identical!

Example 1: Product Rule Application

Problem: Simplify: 2³ × 2⁴

Solution:

Same base (2), so add exponents:

2³ × 2⁴ = 2^(3+4) = 2⁷

= 128

Answer: 2⁷ or 128

You can leave answer as 2⁷ unless asked to evaluate

Concept 3: Quotient Rule for Exponents

When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents.

Quotient Rule

a^m ÷ a^n = a^(m-n)

When bases are the same, subtract the exponents

Example: x⁷ ÷ x³ = x^(7-3) = x⁴

Example 2: Quotient Rule Application

Problem: Simplify: 5⁸ ÷ 5⁵

Solution:

Same base (5), so subtract exponents:

5⁸ ÷ 5⁵ = 5^(8-5) = 5³

= 125

Answer: 5³ or 125

Concept 4: Power Rule for Exponents

When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents.

Power Rule

(a^m)^n = a^(m×n)

When raising a power to a power, multiply exponents

Example: (x³)⁴ = x^(3×4) = x¹²

Also important:

  • (ab)^n = a^n × b^n (distribute exponent to each factor)
  • (a/b)^n = a^n / b^n (distribute exponent to numerator and denominator)

Example 3: Power Rule Application

Problem: Simplify: (3²)³

Solution:

Power to a power, so multiply exponents:

(3²)³ = 3^(2×3) = 3⁶

= 729

Answer: 3⁶ or 729

Example 4: Distributing Exponents

Problem: Simplify: (2x³)⁴

Solution:

Distribute the exponent to each factor:

(2x³)⁴ = 2⁴ × (x³)⁴

= 16 × x^(3×4)

= 16x¹²

Answer: 16x¹²

Concept 5: Zero and Negative Exponents

Zero and negative exponents follow specific rules that are frequently tested on the ACT.

Zero and Negative Exponent Rules

  • Zero exponent: a⁰ = 1 (for any nonzero a)
  • Negative exponent: a^(-n) = 1/a^n
  • Reciprocal: 1/a^(-n) = a^n

Key insight: Negative exponents mean "reciprocal" - flip the fraction!

Example 5: Zero and Negative Exponents

Problem: Simplify: 5⁰ + 2^(-3)

Solution:

Step 1: 5⁰ = 1 (anything to the zero power is 1)

Step 2: 2^(-3) = 1/2³ = 1/8

Step 3: Add

1 + 1/8 = 8/8 + 1/8 = 9/8

Answer: 9/8 or 1.125

Concept 6: Understanding Radicals (Roots)

Radicals are the opposite of exponents. The square root asks "what number squared gives this?"

Radical Basics

  • Square root: √a asks "what number squared equals a?"
  • Cube root: ∛a asks "what number cubed equals a?"
  • nth root: ⁿ√a asks "what number to the nth power equals a?"
  • Perfect squares: √1=1, √4=2, √9=3, √16=4, √25=5, √36=6, √49=7, √64=8, √81=9, √100=10

Remember: √(a×b) = √a × √b

Example 6: Simplifying Square Roots

Problem: Simplify: √48

Solution:

Step 1: Find the largest perfect square factor of 48

48 = 16 × 3 (16 is a perfect square)

Step 2: Split the radical

√48 = √(16 × 3) = √16 × √3

Step 3: Simplify

= 4√3

Answer: 4√3

Example 7: Operations with Radicals

Problem: Simplify: 3√2 + 5√2

Solution:

Like radicals can be combined (like combining like terms):

3√2 + 5√2 = (3 + 5)√2

Answer: 8√2

Can only combine radicals with the same radicand (number under the radical)

Concept 7: Rationalizing Denominators

Rationalizing means removing radicals from the denominator of a fraction.

Rationalization Strategy

  • Multiply numerator and denominator by the radical in the denominator
  • This creates a perfect square under the radical in the denominator
  • The denominator becomes a rational number

Example: 1/√2 = (1/√2) × (√2/√2) = √2/2

Example 8: Rationalizing a Denominator

Problem: Rationalize: 6/√3

Solution:

Step 1: Multiply by √3/√3

6/√3 × √3/√3

Step 2: Simplify

= (6√3)/(√3 × √3)

= (6√3)/3

Step 3: Reduce

= 2√3

Answer: 2√3

Concept 8: Fractional Exponents

Fractional exponents are another way to write radicals. This is a key connection tested on the ACT.

Fractional Exponent Rules

  • Basic rule: a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a
  • General form: a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(a^m) = (ⁿ√a)^m
  • Examples:
    • 16^(1/2) = √16 = 4
    • 8^(1/3) = ∛8 = 2
    • 27^(2/3) = (∛27)² = 3² = 9

Remember: The denominator is the root, the numerator is the power

Example 9: Evaluating Fractional Exponents

Problem: Evaluate: 64^(2/3)

Solution:

Method 1 - Root then Power:

64^(2/3) = (∛64)²

= 4²

= 16

Method 2 - Power then Root:

64^(2/3) = ∛(64²)

= ∛4096

= 16

Answer: 16

Tip: Root first is usually easier!

Example 10: Combining Exponent Rules

Problem: Simplify: (x^(1/2) × x^(1/3)) / x^(1/6)

Solution:

Step 1: Product rule for numerator (add exponents)

x^(1/2) × x^(1/3) = x^(1/2 + 1/3)

Need common denominator: 1/2 = 3/6, 1/3 = 2/6

= x^(3/6 + 2/6) = x^(5/6)

Step 2: Quotient rule (subtract exponents)

x^(5/6) / x^(1/6) = x^(5/6 - 1/6)

= x^(4/6)

Step 3: Simplify

= x^(2/3)

Answer: x^(2/3)

ACT Strategy Tips for Exponents and Roots

  • Memorize perfect squares: Know 1² through 15² by heart for quick recognition
  • Know your exponent rules cold: Product, quotient, and power rules are essential
  • Negative exponents = reciprocal: Move to opposite part of fraction and make positive
  • Zero exponent always equals 1: Don't overthink it - any nonzero number⁰ = 1
  • For fractional exponents: Take the root first (denominator), then apply the power (numerator)
  • Simplify radicals: Look for perfect square factors to simplify before calculating
  • Rationalize when needed: ACT answers never have radicals in denominators
  • Use your calculator wisely: For √ use the square root button; for other roots use a^(1/n)
  • Check your work: Plug back in to verify, especially with negative exponents
  • Watch for multiple steps: Complex problems often require 2-3 different exponent rules

Common Errors to Avoid

  • Don't add exponents when adding bases: 2³ + 2⁴ ≠ 2⁷ (it equals 8 + 16 = 24)
  • Don't multiply exponents when multiplying: x³ × x⁴ = x⁷, not x¹²
  • Don't distribute exponents to sums: (a + b)² ≠ a² + b² (use FOIL instead)
  • Remember the negative: (-2)⁴ = 16, but -2⁴ = -16
  • Can't combine different radicals: √2 + √3 ≠ √5
  • Fractional exponent order matters: In a^(m/n), n is the root, m is the power
