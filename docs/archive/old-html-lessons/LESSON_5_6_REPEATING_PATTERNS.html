Repeating patterns and cyclical sequences appear in 1-2 questions on the ACT, testing your ability to identify the cycle length, use modular arithmetic to find distant terms, and recognize patterns in geometric figures. These problems often ask "what is the 100th term?" when the pattern repeats every 4 values, requiring you to divide and use remainders rather than listing out 100 terms. Understanding how to quickly identify cycles and apply mod operations transforms these seemingly tedious problems into quick 30-second solutions.

1. Numeric Repeating Patterns

Cyclical Sequences

A cyclical sequence repeats a fixed set of values. For example: 2, 5, 8, 2, 5, 8, 2, 5, 8, ... The pattern repeats every 3 terms. To find the nth term, determine the position within the cycle using modular arithmetic: divide n by the cycle length and use the remainder.

Using Modular Arithmetic

If a pattern has cycle length k, compute n mod k (the remainder when n is divided by k). If the remainder is 0, use the kth term of the cycle; otherwise, use the term at position equal to the remainder.
Example: In the sequence 3, 7, 11, 3, 7, 11, ..., what is the 20th term? Cycle length is 3. 20 ÷ 3 = 6 remainder 2, so the 20th term is the 2nd term in the cycle: 7.

Example 1: Finding a Term in a Repeating Sequence

The sequence 4, 9, 1, 6, 4, 9, 1, 6, ... repeats every 4 terms. What is the 37th term?

A. 1
B. 4
C. 6
D. 9
E. Cannot be determined

Solution:

Cycle length is 4. Compute 37 mod 4: 37 ÷ 4 = 9 remainder 1. The remainder is 1, so the 37th term is the 1st term in the cycle: 4. The answer is B.

2. Patterns with Operations

Alternating Signs

Sequences may alternate between positive and negative values. Use (-1)n or (-1)n+1 to model alternating signs.
Example: 1, -1, 1, -1, 1, ... can be written as (-1)n+1 for the nth term.

Repeating Operations

Some patterns involve repeating a sequence of operations (add, subtract, multiply, divide). Track the cycle of operations and apply them step by step.
Example: Start with 5. Add 3, subtract 2, multiply by 2, repeat. The sequence is: 5, 8, 6, 12, 15, 13, 26, ...

Example 2: Alternating Signs and Powers

The sequence is defined by an = (-1)n × n2. What is the value of a5?

F. -25
G. 25
H. -5
J. 5
K. 0

Solution:

Substitute n = 5: a5 = (-1)5 × 52 = (-1) × 25 = -25. The answer is F.

3. Visual and Geometric Patterns

Shape Sequences

The ACT may show a sequence of shapes rotating, reflecting, or changing in size. Identify the transformation pattern and predict the next shape in the sequence.

Counting Patterns in Figures

Some problems ask you to count elements (dots, lines, tiles) in a sequence of figures and determine a formula for the nth figure. Look for arithmetic or geometric growth patterns.
Example: A sequence of squares made of dots: Figure 1 has 4 dots (1×1), Figure 2 has 9 dots (2×2), Figure 3 has 16 dots (3×3). The nth figure has (n+1)2 dots.

Example 3: Dots in a Pattern

A pattern of triangles is formed by dots. The first triangle has 3 dots, the second has 6 dots, the third has 10 dots. How many dots will the 5th triangle have?

A. 15
B. 18
C. 20
D. 21
E. 25

Solution:

The differences are: 6 - 3 = 3, 10 - 6 = 4. The differences increase by 1 each time. Next differences: 5, then 6. Fourth triangle: 10 + 5 = 15. Fifth triangle: 15 + 6 = 21. The answer is D. (This is also the formula for triangular numbers: n(n+1)/2.)

4. Problem-Solving Strategies

Identify the Cycle Length

First, determine how many terms appear before the pattern repeats. Write out enough terms to see the full cycle clearly.

Use Modular Arithmetic

Once you know the cycle length k, compute n mod k to find which term in the cycle corresponds to the nth term overall. This saves time on large values of n.

Look for Underlying Formulas

Some patterns can be expressed with formulas involving n, such as an = (-1)n × f(n) for alternating signs, or an = kn + c for arithmetic sequences. Recognizing these forms can speed up calculations.

Example 4: Complex Repeating Pattern

The sequence is: 2, 4, 8, 7, 2, 4, 8, 7, 2, 4, 8, 7, ... What is the sum of the 10th, 11th, and 12th terms?

F. 13
G. 14
H. 19
J. 21
K. 22

Solution:

Cycle length is 4: (2, 4, 8, 7). 10 mod 4 = 2 → 10th term is 4. 11 mod 4 = 3 → 11th term is 8. 12 mod 4 = 0 → 12th term is 7 (use the 4th term when remainder is 0). Sum: 4 + 8 + 7 = 19. The answer is H.
