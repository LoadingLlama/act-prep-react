Fractions

Mastering fractions is critical for ACT Math success—these concepts appear in 10-15 questions per test and are frequently combined with algebra, ratios, and percentages. Many students lose points on fraction problems not because the concepts are difficult, but because they rush through the steps or forget key rules. In this lesson, we'll build your confidence with clear explanations, step-by-step techniques, and strategic shortcuts that will help you solve fraction problems quickly and accurately on test day.

1. Adding and Subtracting Fractions

To add or subtract fractions, you must have a common denominator. Think of fractions as pieces of a pie—you can only add or subtract pieces if they're the same size.

When denominators are the same:

  • Simply add or subtract the numerators
  • Keep the denominator the same
  • Example: 3/7 + 2/7 = 5/7
  • Example: 5/9 - 2/9 = 3/9 = 1/3

When denominators are different:

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)—the smallest number both denominators divide into
  2. Convert each fraction to an equivalent fraction with the LCD
  3. Add or subtract the numerators
  4. Simplify if possible

ACT Strategy: When finding the LCD, start by checking if the larger denominator is a multiple of the smaller one. For example, with 1/4 + 1/12, check: is 12 divisible by 4? Yes! So LCD = 12. This saves time versus listing multiples.

What is 1/4 + 2/3?

A. 3/7
B. 5/12
C. 7/12
D. 11/12
E. 1

Solution:

Step 1: Find the LCD

Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16...

Multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12...

LCD = 12

Step 2: Convert each fraction

1/4 = 3/12 (multiply top and bottom by 3)

2/3 = 8/12 (multiply top and bottom by 4)

Step 3: Add the numerators

3/12 + 8/12 = 11/12

Answer: D

2. Multiplying Fractions

Multiplying fractions is simpler than adding or subtracting—no common denominator needed!

Formula: (a/b) × (c/d) = (a × c)/(b × d)

Steps:

  1. Multiply the numerators together
  2. Multiply the denominators together
  3. Simplify the result if possible

Cross-Canceling (Time-Saver!):

Before multiplying, you can simplify by canceling common factors between any numerator and any denominator (not just vertically). This is called cross-canceling, and it prevents you from working with large numbers.

Example: 3/8 × 4/9

  • Cancel: 3 and 9 both divide by 3 → becomes 1 and 3
  • Cancel: 4 and 8 both divide by 4 → becomes 1 and 2
  • Result: 1/2 × 1/3 = 1/6

ACT Strategy: Always look for opportunities to cross-cancel before multiplying. It's much easier to work with small numbers, and you'll avoid arithmetic errors when simplifying large fractions.

Simplify: 3/8 × 4/9

A. 1/6
B. 1/4
C. 1/3
D. 7/17
E. 12/72

Solution:

Method 1: Cross-Cancel First (Recommended)

Look for common factors diagonally:

  • 3 (top left) and 9 (bottom right): both divide by 3 → 1 and 3
  • 4 (top right) and 8 (bottom left): both divide by 4 → 1 and 2

Now multiply: 1/2 × 1/3 = 1/6

Method 2: Multiply Then Simplify

3/8 × 4/9 = 12/72

Simplify: 12/72 = 1/6 (divide by 12)

Cross-canceling saves time and reduces errors!

Answer: A

3. Dividing Fractions

To divide fractions, multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction. The reciprocal of a fraction is simply the fraction flipped upside down.

Formula: (a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c)

Steps:

  1. Keep the first fraction as is
  2. Change the division sign to multiplication
  3. Flip the second fraction (take its reciprocal)
  4. Multiply as normal

Memory trick: "Keep, Change, Flip" → Keep the first fraction, Change ÷ to ×, Flip the second fraction.

ACT Strategy: After flipping the second fraction, treat the problem as multiplication—including using cross-canceling to simplify. Division problems become multiplication problems in disguise!

What is 2/3 ÷ 4/5?

A. 5/6
B. 8/15
C. 10/12
D. 6/5
E. 8/5

Solution:

Step 1: Keep, Change, Flip

2/3 ÷ 4/5 becomes 2/3 × 5/4

Step 2: Multiply

2/3 × 5/4 = 10/12

Step 3: Simplify

10/12 = 5/6 (divide both by 2)

Alternative: Cross-cancel first

2/3 × 5/4 → cancel 2 and 4 (both ÷ 2) → 1/3 × 5/2 = 5/6

Answer: A

4. Mixed Numbers and Improper Fractions

A mixed number combines a whole number and a fraction, like 2⅓. An improper fraction has a numerator larger than or equal to the denominator, like 7/3.

Converting Mixed Number to Improper Fraction:

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
  2. Add the numerator
  3. Place the result over the original denominator
  4. Example: 2⅓ = (2 × 3 + 1)/3 = 7/3

Converting Improper Fraction to Mixed Number:

  1. Divide the numerator by the denominator
  2. The quotient becomes the whole number
  3. The remainder becomes the new numerator
  4. Example: 17/5 = 3⅖ (because 17 ÷ 5 = 3 remainder 2)

ACT Strategy: When multiplying or dividing mixed numbers, always convert to improper fractions first. Trying to multiply mixed numbers directly leads to errors. After your final calculation, check if the answer choices are in mixed number form—if so, convert your improper fraction back.

What is 2⅓ × 1½?

A. 2⅚
B. 3
C. 3½
D. 3⅚
E. 4

Solution:

Step 1: Convert to improper fractions

2⅓ = (2 × 3 + 1)/3 = 7/3

1½ = (1 × 2 + 1)/2 = 3/2

Step 2: Multiply

7/3 × 3/2 = 21/6

Step 3: Simplify

21/6 = 7/2 (divide by 3)

Step 4: Convert to mixed number

7/2 = 3½ (7 ÷ 2 = 3 remainder 1)

Answer: C

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Adding/subtracting requires common denominators; multiplying/dividing does not
  • ✓ To multiply fractions: multiply straight across, then simplify (or cross-cancel first!)
  • ✓ To divide fractions: Keep, Change, Flip—then multiply
  • ✓ Always convert mixed numbers to improper fractions before multiplying or dividing
  • ✓ Cross-canceling before multiplying saves time and reduces arithmetic errors
