Percentages appear on virtually every ACT Math test, accounting for 4-6 questions. Understanding how to convert between percentages, decimals, and fractions, as well as how to calculate percent increase, percent decrease, and successive percent changes, is essential for ACT success. Many students lose points on these "easy" questions due to careless conversion errors or forgetting that successive changes multiply rather than add—mastering the fundamentals ensures you don't leave points on the table.

1. Converting Between Forms

Being fluent in converting between percentages, decimals, and fractions helps you work with the most convenient form for each problem. These conversions are foundational skills for all percentage calculations.

Percent to Decimal and Decimal to Percent

To convert a percent to a decimal, divide by 100 (move decimal point two places left): 45% = 0.45, 8% = 0.08, 125% = 1.25. To convert a decimal to a percent, multiply by 100 (move decimal point two places right): 0.6 = 60%, 0.03 = 3%, 1.5 = 150%.

Percent to Fraction and Fraction to Percent

To convert percent to fraction, write over 100 and simplify: 25% = 25/100 = 1/4, 60% = 60/100 = 3/5. To convert fraction to percent, divide numerator by denominator and multiply by 100: 3/4 = 0.75 = 75%, 2/5 = 0.4 = 40%.

Common Percent-Fraction Equivalents

Memorizing common equivalents saves time: 1/2 = 50%, 1/4 = 25%, 3/4 = 75%, 1/5 = 20%, 2/5 = 40%, 3/5 = 60%, 4/5 = 80%, 1/3 ≈ 33.33%, 2/3 ≈ 66.67%, 1/8 = 12.5%, 3/8 = 37.5%, 5/8 = 62.5%, 7/8 = 87.5%.

Example 1: Conversion

What percent is equivalent to 7/20?

A) 14%

B) 28%

C) 35%

D) 70%

E) 140%

Solution:

Step 1: Divide numerator by denominator: 7 ÷ 20 = 0.35.

Step 2: Convert to percent by multiplying by 100: 0.35 × 100 = 35%.

Answer: C — 7/20 = 0.35 = 35%.

2. Finding Percentages

Calculating a percentage of a number is one of the most common percentage operations on the ACT. The key phrase "What is X% of Y?" translates to multiplication.

The Formula: "Of" Means Multiply

To find X% of Y, convert the percent to a decimal and multiply: X% of Y = (X/100) × Y. For example, "What is 30% of 80?" becomes 0.30 × 80 = 24. The word "of" in percentage problems almost always means multiply.

Finding What Percent One Number Is of Another

To find "What percent is A of B?", use the formula: (A/B) × 100%. For example, "24 is what percent of 60?" Calculate: (24/60) × 100% = 0.4 × 100% = 40%. This answers "24 is 40% of 60."

Finding the Whole When Given a Percent

If you know that A is X% of some unknown number, find the whole by dividing: Whole = A ÷ (X/100). For example, "35 is 25% of what number?" Calculate: 35 ÷ 0.25 = 140. Check: 25% of 140 = 0.25 × 140 = 35 ✓

Example 2: Finding a Percentage

45 is what percent of 150?

A) 20%

B) 25%

C) 30%

D) 33.33%

E) 40%

Solution:

Step 1: Use formula: (Part/Whole) × 100% = (45/150) × 100%.

Step 2: Simplify the fraction: 45/150 = 3/10 = 0.3.

Step 3: Convert to percent: 0.3 × 100% = 30%.

Answer: C — 45 is 30% of 150.

3. Percent Increase and Decrease

Percent change problems ask you to find how much something increased or decreased as a percentage of the original value. These problems appear in contexts like sales, discounts, population growth, and price changes.

Percent Increase Formula

Percent increase = (New − Old)/Old × 100%. The numerator is the amount of change (how much it increased), and the denominator is always the original value. For example, if a price increases from $50 to $65: Percent increase = (65 − 50)/50 × 100% = 15/50 × 100% = 30%.

Percent Decrease Formula

Percent decrease = (Old − New)/Old × 100%. Again, divide the change by the original value. For example, if a population decreases from 200 to 150: Percent decrease = (200 − 150)/200 × 100% = 50/200 × 100% = 25%.

Calculating New Value After Percent Change

If something increases by X%, multiply by (1 + X/100). If it decreases by X%, multiply by (1 − X/100). For example, a $120 item with 15% discount: New price = 120 × (1 − 0.15) = 120 × 0.85 = $102. Or, a $80 item with 25% markup: New price = 80 × (1 + 0.25) = 80 × 1.25 = $100.

Example 3: Percent Decrease

A shirt originally priced at $40 is on sale for $28. What is the percent discount?

A) 12%

B) 20%

C) 25%

D) 30%

E) 43%

Solution:

Step 1: Find the amount of decrease: $40 − $28 = $12.

Step 2: Use percent decrease formula: (Decrease/Original) × 100% = (12/40) × 100%.

Step 3: Simplify: 12/40 = 3/10 = 0.3, so 0.3 × 100% = 30%.

Answer: D — The discount is 30%.

4. Successive Percent Changes

When multiple percent changes occur in sequence, you cannot simply add the percentages. Each change applies to the result of the previous change, creating a compounding effect.

Why You Can't Add Percentages

A common mistake is adding successive percent changes. For example, increasing by 20% then decreasing by 20% does NOT return to the original value. The 20% decrease applies to the new (larger) value, so you lose more than you gained. Example: Start with 100. Increase 20%: 100 × 1.20 = 120. Decrease 20%: 120 × 0.80 = 96 (not back to 100).

Multiply the Factors

For successive percent changes, multiply the change factors. If something increases by A% then by B%, final value = original × (1 + A/100) × (1 + B/100). For example, $50 increased by 10% then 20%: Final = 50 × 1.10 × 1.20 = $66.

Mixed Increase and Decrease

When mixing increases and decreases, still multiply the factors. For a 25% increase followed by a 10% decrease: Factor = 1.25 × 0.90 = 1.125, which represents a net 12.5% increase. For example, $80 increased by 25% then decreased by 10%: Final = 80 × 1.25 × 0.90 = $90.

Example 4: Successive Changes

A stock price increases by 50% then decreases by 40%. If it started at $200, what is the final price?

A) $180

B) $200

C) $210

D) $220

E) $240

Solution:

Step 1: Apply 50% increase: $200 × 1.50 = $300.

Step 2: Apply 40% decrease to the new value: $300 × 0.60 = $180.

Step 3: Alternatively, multiply factors: $200 × 1.50 × 0.60 = $200 × 0.90 = $180.

Answer: A — Final price is $180 (net decrease of 10%).

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Percent means "per hundred": 25% = 25/100 = 0.25. Converting between forms is essential
  • ✓Percent change formula: ((New − Old) / Old) × 100%. Positive for increase, negative for decrease
  • ✓Successive changes multiply, they don't add: A 20% increase then 20% decrease ≠ 0% change
  • ✓When calculating percent of a number, convert to decimal first: 15% of 80 = 0.15 × 80 = 12
  • ✓Watch for the base: "20% more than 50" and "20% of 50" give different answers (60 vs 10)
  • ✓Percent problems can often be solved by setting up proportions: part/whole = percent/100
