Probability appears in 2-4 questions on the ACT, testing concepts from basic single-event probability to more complex compound events, independent vs. dependent events, conditional probability, and the addition and multiplication rules. The ACT loves to test whether you understand that independent events multiply (P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)) while mutually exclusive events add (P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)). Mastering the complement rule and recognizing when to add versus multiply probabilities eliminates the most common errors on these problems.

1. Basic Probability

Definition and Formula

The probability of an event is: P(event) = (Number of favorable outcomes) ÷ (Total number of possible outcomes).
Probabilities range from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). Example: The probability of rolling a 3 on a standard die is 1/6.

Complement Rule

The complement of an event A (denoted A') is the event that A does not occur. P(A') = 1 - P(A).
Example: If the probability of rain is 0.3, the probability of no rain is 1 - 0.3 = 0.7.

Example 1: Basic Probability

A bag contains 5 red marbles, 3 blue marbles, and 2 green marbles. If one marble is drawn at random, what is the probability that it is blue?

A. 1/10
B. 1/5
C. 3/10
D. 3/8
E. 1/3

Solution:

Total marbles = 5 + 3 + 2 = 10. Blue marbles = 3. P(blue) = 3/10. The answer is C.

2. Compound Events: Independent and Dependent

Independent Events

Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. For independent events A and B: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
Example: Rolling a die and flipping a coin are independent events.

Dependent Events

Two events are dependent if the occurrence of one affects the probability of the other. For dependent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A), where P(B|A) is the probability of B given that A has occurred.
Example: Drawing two cards from a deck without replacement—the first draw affects the second.

Example 2: Dependent Events

A box contains 4 red balls and 6 blue balls. Two balls are drawn without replacement. What is the probability that both balls are red?

F. 2/15
G. 4/25
H. 1/5
J. 2/5
K. 16/100

Solution:

P(first red) = 4/10. After drawing one red, 3 red and 9 total remain. P(second red | first red) = 3/9 = 1/3. P(both red) = (4/10) × (1/3) = 4/30 = 2/15. The answer is F.

3. Addition Rule and Mutually Exclusive Events

Mutually Exclusive Events

Events A and B are mutually exclusive (disjoint) if they cannot both occur at the same time. For mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).
Example: Rolling a 2 or a 5 on a die—these outcomes cannot occur simultaneously.

General Addition Rule

For any two events (whether or not they are mutually exclusive): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B). Subtract P(A and B) to avoid double-counting the overlap.

Example 3: Addition Rule

In a class, 60% of students play basketball, 40% play soccer, and 20% play both. What is the probability that a randomly selected student plays basketball or soccer?

A. 0.60
B. 0.70
C. 0.80
D. 1.00
E. 0.50

Solution:

P(basketball or soccer) = P(basketball) + P(soccer) - P(both) = 0.60 + 0.40 - 0.20 = 0.80. The answer is C.

4. Conditional Probability and Counting

Conditional Probability

The probability of event B given that event A has occurred is: P(B|A) = P(A and B) ÷ P(A). This formula adjusts the sample space to only include outcomes where A occurs.

Using Counting Techniques

Many probability problems can be solved using counting: compute the number of favorable outcomes and the total number of possible outcomes using combinations, permutations, or the fundamental counting principle.
Example: The probability of drawing a full house in poker can be calculated by counting the number of full house hands and dividing by the total number of 5-card hands.

Example 4: Conditional Probability

In a school, 30% of students are seniors, and 80% of seniors pass a test. What is the probability that a randomly selected student is a senior who passes the test?

F. 0.10
G. 0.18
H. 0.24
J. 0.30
K. 0.50

Solution:

P(senior and passes) = P(senior) × P(passes | senior) = 0.30 × 0.80 = 0.24. The answer is H.

  • ✓Independent events: Outcome of one doesn't affect the other. Multiply probabilities
