Understanding area, volume, and triangles is essential for ACT Math success. These concepts appear in 8-12 questions per test—that's nearly 20% of all math questions! While the ACT provides most formulas in a reference box, knowing when to use each formula and recognizing special patterns can save you valuable time and help you avoid common traps.

1. Area of Common Shapes

Area measures the space inside a two-dimensional shape, always expressed in square units (like ft² or cm²). The ACT tests your ability to identify which shape you're working with and apply the correct formula.

Key Formulas You'll Use

  • Rectangle: A = length × width (simplest area formula)
  • Triangle: A = ½ × base × height (half of a rectangle!)
  • Circle: A = πr² where r is the radius (distance from center to edge)
  • Trapezoid: A = ½ × (base₁ + base₂) × height (average the parallel sides)

ACT Strategy Tip

The triangle formula is literally half a rectangle's area—if you forget it, imagine putting two identical triangles together to form a rectangle. This visualization helps you remember and understand the formula, not just memorize it.

A circular garden has a diameter of 10 feet. What is the area of the garden in square feet?

A. 10π
B. 25π
C. 50π
D. 100π
E. 200π

Solution:

  • Common trap: The problem gives diameter (10 ft), but the formula uses radius!
  • Radius = diameter ÷ 2 = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 feet
  • Area = πr² = π(5)² = 25π square feet
  • Why keep π? ACT answers often leave π in the answer rather than calculating 3.14...

Answer: B

2. Volume of Three-Dimensional Shapes

Volume measures the space inside a three-dimensional object, expressed in cubic units (like m³ or in³). Think of volume as "how much water would fit inside." The ACT most frequently tests rectangular prisms (boxes), cylinders, and occasionally spheres or cones.

Essential Volume Formulas

  • Rectangular Prism (Box): V = length × width × height
  • Cylinder: V = πr²h (circle area times height)
  • Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³ (memorize this one—not in formula box!)
  • Cone: V = (1/3)πr²h (one-third of a cylinder)

Pattern Recognition

Notice that cylinder volume is just the circle's area (πr²) multiplied by the height. This makes sense: stack many circles on top of each other to build a cylinder! Similarly, a cone is exactly 1/3 of a cylinder with the same base and height.

A cylindrical water tank has a radius of 4 feet and a height of 10 feet. What is its volume in cubic feet?

A. 40π
B. 80π
C. 160π
D. 200π
E. 400π

Solution:

  • Volume of cylinder = πr²h
  • First, square the radius: r² = 4² = 16
  • Then multiply by height: V = π(16)(10) = 160π cubic feet
  • Order matters: Square first, then multiply by height—a common mistake is doing 4 × 10 first!

Answer: C

3. The Pythagorean Theorem

The Pythagorean theorem is one of the most tested concepts on the ACT. It relates the three sides of any right triangle: a² + b² = c², where c is always the hypotenuse (the longest side, opposite the right angle), and a and b are the two legs.

What This Really Means

The theorem tells us that if you square both legs and add them together, you get the square of the hypotenuse. This only works for right triangles (triangles with a 90° angle).

Pythagorean Triples (Time-Savers!)

Certain sets of three whole numbers always form right triangles. Memorizing these saves precious time:

  • 3-4-5 and multiples (6-8-10, 9-12-15, 15-20-25)
  • 5-12-13 and multiples (10-24-26)
  • 8-15-17 (appears less frequently)
  • 7-24-25 (rare but useful)

A right triangle has legs of length 9 and 12. What is the length of the hypotenuse?

A. 13
B. 15
C. 18
D. 21
E. 25

Solution:

  • Smart approach: Recognize this is 3 times the 3-4-5 triple!
  • 9 = 3 × 3, and 12 = 3 × 4
  • Therefore, hypotenuse = 3 × 5 = 15
  • Verify: 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225 = 15² ✓
  • Recognizing the triple saved us from doing the calculation!

Answer: B

4. Special Right Triangles

Two special triangles appear so frequently on the ACT that they deserve their own section. Unlike Pythagorean triples (which you discover), these triangles have fixed angle measurements that create predictable side ratios.

The 45-45-90 Triangle

This triangle has two 45° angles and one 90° angle, making it an isosceles right triangle. The side ratio is always x : x : x√2

  • Both legs are equal (that's what makes it isosceles)
  • Hypotenuse = leg × √2
  • If you cut a square diagonally, you get two 45-45-90 triangles!

The 30-60-90 Triangle

This triangle appears when you cut an equilateral triangle in half. The side ratio is always x : x√3 : 2x

  • Short leg (opposite 30°) : Long leg (opposite 60°) : Hypotenuse
  • If short leg = x, then long leg = x√3 and hypotenuse = 2x
  • The hypotenuse is always twice the short leg!

In a 30-60-90 triangle, the hypotenuse is 10. What is the length of the shorter leg?

A. 3
B. 5
C. 5√2
D. 5√3
E. 10√3

Solution:

  • The 30-60-90 ratio is x : x√3 : 2x
  • Hypotenuse = 2x = 10, so solve: x = 5
  • The shorter leg (opposite 30°) = x = 5
  • Key insight: The hypotenuse is always double the short leg in 30-60-90 triangles!

Answer: B
