Mastering lines, slope, and linear equations is crucial for ACT Math success. These concepts appear in 10-15 questions per test—that's about 25% of all math questions! Understanding slope, equations, and coordinate geometry will give you a massive advantage on test day.

1. Understanding Slope

Slope measures how steep a line is—specifically, how much the line rises (or falls) for every unit it moves horizontally. Think of slope as the "steepness" or "tilt" of a line.

Formula: m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)

This is often called "rise over run"—the vertical change divided by the horizontal change.

Types of Slope

  • Positive slope: Line goes uphill from left to right (↗)
  • Negative slope: Line goes downhill from left to right (↘)
  • Zero slope: Horizontal line (→) where y is constant
  • Undefined slope: Vertical line (↑) where x is constant

What is the slope of the line passing through the points (2, 5) and (6, 13)?

A. 1
B. 2
C. 3
D. 4
E. 8

Solution:

  • Use the slope formula: m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)
  • Identify the coordinates: (x₁, y₁) = (2, 5) and (x₂, y₂) = (6, 13)
  • Calculate: m = (13 - 5)/(6 - 2) = 8/4 = 2
  • Meaning: For every 1 unit right, the line goes up 2 units

Answer: B

2. Equations of Lines

Lines can be written in different equation forms. The ACT expects you to recognize and convert between these forms quickly.

Slope-Intercept Form (Most Common!)

y = mx + b

  • m = slope (the coefficient of x)
  • b = y-intercept (where the line crosses the y-axis)
  • Why it's useful: You can immediately see the slope and y-intercept!

Example: In y = 3x + 7, the slope is 3 and the y-intercept is 7.

Point-Slope Form

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

  • m = slope
  • (x₁, y₁) = a point on the line
  • When to use: You know the slope and one point on the line

A line has slope 2 and passes through the point (3, 8). What is the equation of the line in slope-intercept form?

A. y = 2x + 2
B. y = 2x + 3
C. y = 2x + 5
D. y = 2x + 8
E. y = 3x + 8

Solution:

  • We know m = 2, so the equation starts as y = 2x + b
  • Use the point (3, 8) to find b: substitute x = 3 and y = 8
  • 8 = 2(3) + b → 8 = 6 + b → b = 2
  • Therefore, the equation is y = 2x + 2

Answer: A

3. Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

The relationship between two lines' slopes tells you whether they're parallel or perpendicular. This is one of the ACT's favorite concepts!

Parallel Lines

Parallel lines never intersect and have the same slope.

  • If line 1 has slope m₁ and line 2 has slope m₂, they're parallel when m₁ = m₂
  • Example: y = 3x + 5 and y = 3x - 2 are parallel (both have slope 3)

Perpendicular Lines

Perpendicular lines intersect at a 90° angle. Their slopes are negative reciprocals.

  • If line 1 has slope m₁ and line 2 has slope m₂, they're perpendicular when m₁ × m₂ = -1
  • Quick trick: Flip the fraction and change the sign!
  • Example: If one slope is 2/3, a perpendicular slope is -3/2

A line has the equation y = 3x + 5. What is the slope of a line perpendicular to this line?

A. -3
B. -1/3
C. 1/3
D. 3
E. 5

Solution:

  • Original line has slope m = 3 (from y = 3x + 5)
  • To find perpendicular slope: flip and negate
  • 3 becomes 1/3, then negate to get -1/3
  • Check: 3 × (-1/3) = -1 ✓

Answer: B

4. Distance and Midpoint Formulas

These formulas help you work with points in the coordinate plane. They appear frequently on the ACT, especially in geometry problems.

Distance Formula

d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

This formula finds the straight-line distance between two points. It's actually just the Pythagorean theorem applied to coordinates!

Midpoint Formula

M = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)

The midpoint is simply the point exactly halfway between two points. Just average the x-coordinates and average the y-coordinates separately.

What is the distance between the points (1, 2) and (4, 6)?

A. 3
B. 4
C. 5
D. 6
E. 7

Solution:

  • Use distance formula: d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]
  • d = √[(4 - 1)² + (6 - 2)²] = √[3² + 4²]
  • d = √[9 + 16] = √25 = 5
  • Pro tip: This is a 3-4-5 Pythagorean triple in disguise!

Answer: C
