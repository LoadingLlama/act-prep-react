Concept 1: Circle Equations - Standard Form

The equation of a circle in the coordinate plane can be written in standard form, which immediately reveals the circle's center and radius. Understanding this form is crucial for quickly analyzing circles on the ACT.

Standard Form of a Circle

The standard form equation of a circle with center (h, k) and radius r is:

(x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²

  • Center: (h, k)
  • Radius: r (or radius = √r² if you need to take the square root)
  • Special case: Circle centered at origin (0, 0): x² + y² = r²

Important: Note the minus signs in the formula! (x - h) means the center's x-coordinate is h, not -h.

Example 1: Identifying Center and Radius

Problem: What are the center and radius of the circle with equation (x - 3)² + (y + 5)² = 49?

Solution:

Compare to standard form: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²

From (x - 3)²: h = 3

From (y + 5)² = (y - (-5))²: k = -5

From r² = 49: r = 7

Answer: Center = (3, -5), Radius = 7

Watch the signs! (y + 5) means subtract -5, so the y-coordinate is -5.

Example 2: Writing Circle Equations

Problem: Write the equation of a circle with center (2, -4) and radius 6.

Solution:

Use the standard form: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²

Center (h, k) = (2, -4), so h = 2 and k = -4

Radius r = 6, so r² = 36

Substituting: (x - 2)² + (y - (-4))² = 36

Answer: (x - 2)² + (y + 4)² = 36

Concept 2: Circle Equations - General Form

Circles can also be written in general form, which is an expanded version of the standard form. You need to be able to convert between the two forms using a technique called completing the square.

General Form of a Circle

x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0

To convert from general form to standard form:

  1. Group x terms and y terms together
  2. Complete the square for x terms: add (D/2)² inside and outside
  3. Complete the square for y terms: add (E/2)² inside and outside
  4. Rearrange to get (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²

Note: The coefficients of x² and y² must both be 1. If not, factor them out first!

Example 3: Converting General Form to Standard Form

Problem: Find the center and radius of the circle x² + y² - 6x + 8y - 11 = 0.

Solution:

Step 1: Group x and y terms

(x² - 6x) + (y² + 8y) = 11

Step 2: Complete the square for x terms

x² - 6x: take half of -6, which is -3, then square it: (-3)² = 9

Add 9 inside the parentheses and to the right side

Step 3: Complete the square for y terms

y² + 8y: take half of 8, which is 4, then square it: (4)² = 16

Add 16 inside the parentheses and to the right side

Step 4: Simplify

(x² - 6x + 9) + (y² + 8y + 16) = 11 + 9 + 16

(x - 3)² + (y + 4)² = 36

Answer: Center = (3, -4), Radius = 6

Example 4: Circle with Different Coefficients

Problem: Find the center and radius of the circle 2x² + 2y² - 8x + 12y - 24 = 0.

Solution:

Step 1: Divide everything by 2 to make coefficients of x² and y² equal to 1

x² + y² - 4x + 6y - 12 = 0

Step 2: Rearrange and group terms

(x² - 4x) + (y² + 6y) = 12

Step 3: Complete the square

For x² - 4x: (-4/2)² = (-2)² = 4

For y² + 6y: (6/2)² = (3)² = 9

(x² - 4x + 4) + (y² + 6y + 9) = 12 + 4 + 9

(x - 2)² + (y + 3)² = 25

Answer: Center = (2, -3), Radius = 5

Concept 3: Finding Center and Radius from Diameter Endpoints

Sometimes you're given the endpoints of a diameter and need to find the circle's equation. The center is the midpoint of the diameter, and the radius is half the diameter length.

Key Formulas

Given diameter endpoints (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

  • Center (midpoint): ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)
  • Diameter length: d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]
  • Radius: r = d/2

Example 5: Circle from Diameter Endpoints

Problem: A circle has a diameter with endpoints at (1, 2) and (7, 10). Write the equation of the circle.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the center (midpoint)

Center = ((1 + 7)/2, (2 + 10)/2) = (8/2, 12/2) = (4, 6)

Step 2: Find the diameter length

d = √[(7 - 1)² + (10 - 2)²] = √[6² + 8²] = √[36 + 64] = √100 = 10

Step 3: Find the radius

r = d/2 = 10/2 = 5

Step 4: Write the equation

(x - 4)² + (y - 6)² = 25

Answer: (x - 4)² + (y - 6)² = 25

Note: The diameter endpoints form a 6-8-10 right triangle with the center!

Concept 4: Ellipse Equations and Properties

An ellipse is an oval-shaped curve. It looks like a stretched or compressed circle. On the ACT, you need to recognize ellipse equations and understand their basic properties.

Standard Form of an Ellipse

Ellipse centered at (h, k):

(x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1

  • Center: (h, k)
  • Horizontal semi-axis: a (distance from center to edge horizontally)
  • Vertical semi-axis: b (distance from center to edge vertically)
  • If a > b: ellipse is wider (stretched horizontally)
  • If b > a: ellipse is taller (stretched vertically)
  • If a = b: it's a circle!

Special case: Ellipse centered at origin: x²/a² + y²/b² = 1

Example 6: Identifying Ellipse Properties

Problem: For the ellipse (x - 2)²/25 + (y + 1)²/9 = 1, find the center and determine if it's wider or taller.

Solution:

Compare to standard form: (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1

From (x - 2)²: h = 2

From (y + 1)² = (y - (-1))²: k = -1

Center = (2, -1)

a² = 25, so a = 5 (horizontal distance)

b² = 9, so b = 3 (vertical distance)

Since a > b (5 > 3), the ellipse is wider than it is tall

Answer: Center = (2, -1), ellipse is wider (stretched horizontally)

Example 7: Writing Ellipse Equations

Problem: Write the equation of an ellipse centered at (-3, 4) that extends 6 units horizontally and 2 units vertically from its center.

Solution:

Center (h, k) = (-3, 4)

Horizontal semi-axis: a = 6, so a² = 36

Vertical semi-axis: b = 2, so b² = 4

Using the standard form: (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1

(x - (-3))²/36 + (y - 4)²/4 = 1

Answer: (x + 3)²/36 + (y - 4)²/4 = 1

Concept 5: Hyperbola Basics

A hyperbola consists of two separate curves (branches) that open either horizontally or vertically. While less common on the ACT than circles and ellipses, you should be able to recognize the basic equation form.

Standard Form of a Hyperbola

Hyperbola centered at (h, k):

(x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1 (opens horizontally)

(y - k)²/a² - (x - h)²/b² = 1 (opens vertically)

  • Key difference from ellipse: Notice the minus sign (subtraction) instead of plus!
  • Center: (h, k)
  • The positive term determines the direction the hyperbola opens
  • If x term is positive, opens left-right (horizontally)
  • If y term is positive, opens up-down (vertically)

Example 8: Identifying Hyperbola Direction

Problem: For the hyperbola (x + 2)²/16 - (y - 3)²/9 = 1, identify the center and which direction it opens.

Solution:

From (x + 2)² = (x - (-2))²: h = -2

From (y - 3)²: k = 3

Center = (-2, 3)

The x term is positive (it comes first in the subtraction)

This means the hyperbola opens horizontally (left and right)

Answer: Center = (-2, 3), opens horizontally

Concept 6: Graphing Conic Sections

Conic sections (circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas) can all be graphed by identifying key features from their equations. The ACT may ask you to match equations with graphs or vice versa.

Quick Identification Guide

  • Circle: x² and y² have the same coefficient, addition between terms
  • Ellipse: x² and y² have different positive coefficients, addition between terms
  • Hyperbola: x² and y² have opposite signs (one positive, one negative)

All three share a center point (h, k) that can be found the same way!

Example 9: Distinguishing Conic Sections

Problem: Identify whether each equation represents a circle, ellipse, or hyperbola:
(a) x²/4 + y²/4 = 1
(b) x²/9 + y²/16 = 1
(c) x²/25 - y²/9 = 1

Solution:

(a) x²/4 + y²/4 = 1

Both denominators are the same (4 = 4), and there's addition

This is a circle with radius 2 centered at the origin

(b) x²/9 + y²/16 = 1

Different denominators (9 ≠ 16), with addition

This is an ellipse centered at the origin

It extends 3 units horizontally and 4 units vertically

(c) x²/25 - y²/9 = 1

Subtraction between terms (notice the minus sign!)

This is a hyperbola centered at the origin, opening horizontally

Answer: (a) Circle, (b) Ellipse, (c) Hyperbola

Example 10: Complex Application

Problem: A circle passes through the point (5, 7) and has its center at (2, 3). Write the equation of this circle in both standard form and general form.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the radius using the distance formula

The radius is the distance from the center (2, 3) to the point (5, 7)

r = √[(5 - 2)² + (7 - 3)²] = √[3² + 4²] = √[9 + 16] = √25 = 5

Step 2: Write the standard form

Center (h, k) = (2, 3), radius r = 5

(x - 2)² + (y - 3)² = 25

Step 3: Expand to get general form

(x - 2)² + (y - 3)² = 25

x² - 4x + 4 + y² - 6y + 9 = 25

x² + y² - 4x - 6y + 13 = 25

x² + y² - 4x - 6y - 12 = 0

Answer: Standard form: (x - 2)² + (y - 3)² = 25

General form: x² + y² - 4x - 6y - 12 = 0

Note: This is a 3-4-5 right triangle connecting the center to the point!

ACT Strategy Tips for Conic Sections

  • Watch the signs: (x - h) means the center's x-coordinate is positive h, while (x + h) means it's negative -h
  • Recognize the pattern: Addition = circle or ellipse; Subtraction = hyperbola
  • Coefficient check: If x² and y² have the same coefficient in standard form, it's a circle
  • Completing the square: Group x terms together, y terms together, then complete the square for each
  • Don't forget to divide: If x² and y² don't have coefficient 1, factor it out first!
  • Use midpoint formula: When given diameter endpoints, the center is the midpoint
  • Distance is key: Radius equals the distance from center to any point on the circle
  • Visual check: For ellipses, whichever denominator is larger tells you which direction is stretched more
  • Graph orientation: In hyperbolas, the positive term determines which way it opens
  • Test a point: If you're unsure about your equation, plug in a known point to verify

Quick Reference: Equation Forms Summary

Circle:

  • Standard: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²
  • General: x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
  • Center: (h, k), Radius: r

Ellipse:

  • Standard: (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1
  • Center: (h, k)
  • Semi-axes: a (horizontal), b (vertical)

Hyperbola:

  • Horizontal: (x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1
  • Vertical: (y - k)²/a² - (x - h)²/b² = 1
  • Center: (h, k)
  • The positive term determines opening direction
