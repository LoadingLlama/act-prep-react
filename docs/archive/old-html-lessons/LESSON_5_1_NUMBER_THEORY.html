Understanding number theory is crucial for ACT Math success—these concepts appear in 3-5 questions per test, testing your knowledge of prime numbers, divisibility rules, and GCD/LCM. While these problems may seem straightforward, they often contain traps involving special cases like "1 is neither prime nor composite" or mixing up GCD and LCM formulas. Mastering number theory fundamentals ensures you can quickly classify numbers, find factors, and solve integer problems without hesitation—skills that save precious time on test day.

1. Types of Numbers

The ACT tests your knowledge of different number classifications. Recognizing which category a number belongs to helps you quickly eliminate wrong answers and identify patterns.

Natural Numbers and Whole Numbers

Natural numbers (counting numbers) are {1, 2, 3, 4, ...}. Whole numbers include zero: {0, 1, 2, 3, ...}. Both sets contain only non-negative values with no fractions or decimals.

Integers, Rational, and Irrational Numbers

Integers include all whole numbers and their negatives: {..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ...}. Rational numbers can be expressed as a fraction p/q where p and q are integers (q ≠ 0): examples include 1/2, −3, 0.75, 2.333... Irrational numbers cannot be expressed as fractions: √2, π, e are irrational.

Prime and Composite Numbers

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13. Note that 2 is the only even prime. A composite number has more than two factors. Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9, 10. Special cases: 1 is neither prime nor composite; 0 is neither prime nor composite.

Example 1: Number Classification

Which of the following is NOT a rational number?

A) 0.5

B) −7

C) √16

D) √5

E) 2/3

Solution:

Step 1: Check each option. A) 0.5 = 1/2 (rational). B) −7 = −7/1 (rational). C) √16 = 4 = 4/1 (rational).

Step 2: E) 2/3 is already a fraction (rational).

Step 3: D) √5 ≈ 2.236... is a non-repeating, non-terminating decimal that cannot be expressed as a fraction.

Answer: D — √5 is irrational; all others are rational.

2. Divisibility and Factors

Understanding divisibility rules and factor relationships helps you solve problems quickly without lengthy calculations. These concepts are fundamental to finding GCD and LCM.

Divisibility Rules

Key divisibility rules: A number is divisible by 2 if its last digit is even. By 3 if the sum of digits is divisible by 3. By 4 if the last two digits form a number divisible by 4. By 5 if it ends in 0 or 5. By 6 if it's divisible by both 2 and 3. By 9 if the sum of digits is divisible by 9. By 10 if it ends in 0.

Factors and Factor Pairs

Factors of a number divide it evenly with no remainder. To find all factors of n, check integers from 1 to √n. Each factor i has a paired factor n/i. For example, factors of 24: 1×24, 2×12, 3×8, 4×6, giving {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24}.

Prime Factorization

Every integer greater than 1 can be expressed uniquely as a product of prime numbers. This is called prime factorization. For example, 60 = 2² × 3 × 5. To find it, divide by the smallest prime repeatedly, then move to the next prime. Prime factorization is essential for finding GCD and LCM.

Example 2: Prime Factorization

What is the prime factorization of 126?

A) 2 × 3² × 7

B) 2² × 3 × 7

C) 2 × 3 × 21

D) 3³ × 7

E) 2 × 63

Solution:

Step 1: Divide by 2: 126 ÷ 2 = 63.

Step 2: 63 is odd, try 3: 63 ÷ 3 = 21.

Step 3: 21 ÷ 3 = 7, which is prime.

Step 4: Combine: 126 = 2 × 3 × 3 × 7 = 2 × 3² × 7.

Answer: A — Prime factorization: 2 × 3² × 7.

3. GCD and LCM

The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) and Least Common Multiple (LCM) are essential concepts for solving problems involving shared factors and common multiples.

Finding GCD (Greatest Common Divisor)

The GCD is the largest number that divides both numbers evenly. Method 1: List all factors of each number and find the largest common one. Method 2: Use prime factorization and take the lowest power of each common prime factor. For example, GCD(12, 18): 12 = 2² × 3, 18 = 2 × 3². Common factors: 2¹ and 3¹, so GCD = 2 × 3 = 6.

Finding LCM (Least Common Multiple)

The LCM is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of both numbers. Method 1: List multiples of each number and find the smallest common one. Method 2: Use prime factorization and take the highest power of each prime that appears. For 12 and 18: highest powers are 2² and 3², so LCM = 4 × 9 = 36.

Relationship Between GCD and LCM

For any two positive integers a and b: GCD(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b. This relationship provides a quick way to find one value if you know the other three. For example, if GCD(a, b) = 6, LCM(a, b) = 36, and a = 12, then b = (6 × 36)/12 = 18.

Example 3: Finding LCM

What is the least common multiple of 15 and 20?

A) 5

B) 30

C) 60

D) 100

E) 300

Solution:

Step 1: Prime factorization: 15 = 3 × 5, 20 = 2² × 5.

Step 2: Take highest powers of all primes that appear: 2², 3¹, 5¹.

Step 3: LCM = 2² × 3 × 5 = 4 × 3 × 5 = 60.

Answer: C — LCM(15, 20) = 60.

4. Solution Types and Special Cases

Some ACT problems ask about the nature of solutions to equations or whether certain number types satisfy given conditions. Understanding these concepts helps you classify solutions correctly.

Integer Solutions

Many problems ask for integer solutions only. For example, "How many integer values of x satisfy 2 < x < 7?" Answer: x can be 3, 4, 5, or 6 (four values). Be careful with inequalities: strict inequalities (< or >) exclude endpoints, while ≤ or ≥ include them.

Even and Odd Number Properties

Key properties: Even + Even = Even, Odd + Odd = Even, Even + Odd = Odd. Even × Any = Even, Odd × Odd = Odd. These rules help determine whether expressions will be even or odd. For example, if n is odd, then n² is odd, and n² + 1 is even.

Consecutive Integers

Consecutive integers are integers that follow in order with no gaps: n, n+1, n+2, ... Consecutive even integers: n, n+2, n+4, ... (where n is even). Consecutive odd integers: n, n+2, n+4, ... (where n is odd). The sum of three consecutive integers is always divisible by 3.

Example 4: Consecutive Integers

The sum of three consecutive integers is 48. What is the largest of these integers?

A) 15

B) 16

C) 17

D) 18

E) 19

Solution:

Step 1: Let the three consecutive integers be n, n+1, n+2.

Step 2: Set up equation: n + (n+1) + (n+2) = 48, which simplifies to 3n + 3 = 48.

Step 3: Solve: 3n = 45, so n = 15.

Step 4: The three integers are 15, 16, 17. The largest is 17.

Answer: C — The largest integer is 17.
