Exponents and Roots

Understanding exponents and roots is essential for ACT Math success—these concepts appear in 8-12 questions per test and form the foundation for algebra, functions, and higher mathematics. While the rules may seem abstract at first, they follow logical patterns that, once mastered, make solving problems quick and efficient. In this lesson, we'll break down each rule with clear explanations and strategic insights that will help you tackle even the trickiest exponent questions on test day.

1. Basic Exponent Rules

An exponent indicates how many times to multiply a base by itself. For example, 3⁴ = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81. The base is 3, and the exponent is 4.

Product Rule (Multiplying with the Same Base):

x^a × x^b = x^(a+b)

When multiplying expressions with the same base, add the exponents. Think: if you have 2³ × 2⁴, you're multiplying (2×2×2) by (2×2×2×2), which gives you seven 2's total, or 2⁷.

Quotient Rule (Dividing with the Same Base):

x^a ÷ x^b = x^(a-b)

When dividing expressions with the same base, subtract the exponents. Division cancels out matching factors, so you're left with the difference.

Power Rule (Power to a Power):

(x^a)^b = x^(a×b)

When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents. For example, (2³)² means you're squaring the entire 2³, which is the same as 2³ × 2³ = 2⁶.

ACT Strategy: The most common mistake is confusing when to add, subtract, or multiply exponents. Remember: same base with multiplication → add; same base with division → subtract; power to a power → multiply. Write out the first step if you're unsure!

Simplify: (2³)² × 2⁴

A. 2⁹
B. 2¹⁰
C. 2¹²
D. 2²⁴
E. 4¹⁰

Solution:

Step 1: Apply the Power Rule

(2³)² = 2^(3×2) = 2⁶

Step 2: Apply the Product Rule

2⁶ × 2⁴ = 2^(6+4) = 2¹⁰

Answer: B

2. Zero and Negative Exponents

These special cases frequently appear on the ACT and often confuse students who try to memorize rules without understanding why they work.

Zero Exponent:

x⁰ = 1 (where x ≠ 0)

Any non-zero base raised to the zero power equals 1. Why? Think about the quotient rule: 5³ ÷ 5³ = 5^(3-3) = 5⁰. But 5³ ÷ 5³ obviously equals 1 (anything divided by itself is 1), so 5⁰ must equal 1.

Negative Exponent:

x^(-a) = 1/x^a

A negative exponent means "take the reciprocal and make the exponent positive." For example, 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8. This also works in reverse: 1/x⁻² = x².

Common pitfall: A negative exponent does NOT make the result negative. The number 2⁻³ equals 1/8 (positive), not -8.

ACT Strategy: When you see a negative exponent in the denominator, flip it to the numerator to make it positive. For example, 1/x⁻⁴ = x⁴. This can dramatically simplify complex expressions.

What is the value of 3⁻²?

A. -6
B. -9
C. 1/9
D. 1/6
E. 6

Solution:

Step 1: Apply negative exponent rule

3⁻² = 1/3²

Step 2: Calculate

1/3² = 1/9

Note: The result is positive (1/9), not negative!

Answer: C

3. Fractional Exponents and Radicals

Fractional exponents are another way to express roots. Understanding this connection is crucial for ACT success.

Basic Fractional Exponents:

x^(1/n) = ⁿ√x

  • x^(1/2) = √x (square root)
  • x^(1/3) = ³√x (cube root)
  • Example: 8^(1/3) = ³√8 = 2
  • Example: 16^(1/2) = √16 = 4

General Fractional Exponents:

x^(a/b) = (ᵇ√x)^a = ᵇ√(x^a)

The numerator becomes the power, and the denominator becomes the root. You can either: (1) take the root first, then raise to the power, or (2) raise to the power first, then take the root. Usually, taking the root first gives smaller numbers and is easier.

Example: 8^(2/3) = (³√8)² = 2² = 4, or equivalently 8^(2/3) = ³√(8²) = ³√64 = 4.

ACT Strategy: If you see a fractional exponent, convert it to root notation if that's more comfortable. The ACT often mixes notation to test whether you understand they're the same concept. Also, when evaluating expressions like 27^(2/3), always take the root first (27^(1/3) = 3), then apply the power (3² = 9).

What is the value of 27^(2/3)?

A. 3
B. 6
C. 9
D. 18
E. 729

Solution:

Method 1: Root first, then power (easier)

27^(2/3) = (³√27)²

³√27 = 3 (since 3³ = 27)

3² = 9

Method 2: Power first, then root

27^(2/3) = ³√(27²)

27² = 729

³√729 = 9

Taking the root first is much simpler!

Answer: C

4. Simplifying Radicals

A radical (√) indicates a root. The most common is the square root, but you may also see cube roots (³√) or higher.

Simplifying Square Roots:

Look for perfect square factors (4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, etc.) and factor them out:

  • √50 = √(25 × 2) = √25 × √2 = 5√2
  • √72 = √(36 × 2) = 6√2
  • √18 = √(9 × 2) = 3√2

Multiplying and Dividing Radicals:

  • Multiply: √a × √b = √(ab)
  • Example: √3 × √12 = √36 = 6
  • Divide: √a ÷ √b = √(a/b)
  • Example: √50 ÷ √2 = √25 = 5

ACT Strategy: Always check if your final answer can be simplified further. The ACT expects radicals in simplest form. Also, memorize perfect squares up to at least 15² = 225—this speeds up recognition of perfect square factors.

Simplify: √48

A. 2√3
B. 3√2
C. 4√3
D. 6√2
E. 12√2

Solution:

Step 1: Find the largest perfect square factor

48 = 16 × 3 (16 is a perfect square)

Step 2: Separate the radical

√48 = √(16 × 3) = √16 × √3

Step 3: Simplify

√16 = 4

So √48 = 4√3

Answer: C

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Product rule: x^a × x^b = x^(a+b); Quotient rule: x^a ÷ x^b = x^(a-b)
  • ✓ Power rule: (x^a)^b = x^(ab); Zero exponent: x⁰ = 1
  • ✓ Negative exponent: x^(-a) = 1/x^a (reciprocal—and the result is positive!)
  • ✓ Fractional exponents: x^(1/n) = ⁿ√x and x^(a/b) = (ᵇ√x)^a (take root first for easier calculation)
  • ✓ Simplify radicals by factoring out perfect squares—memorize perfect squares 1² through 15²
