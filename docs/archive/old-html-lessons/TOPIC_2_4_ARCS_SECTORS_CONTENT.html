Concept 1: Understanding Arcs and Central Angles

An arc is a portion of a circle's circumference. Arcs are measured by their central angle, which is the angle formed at the center of the circle by two radii extending to the endpoints of the arc. Understanding the relationship between central angles and arcs is fundamental to solving circle problems on the ACT.

Key Concepts: Arcs and Central Angles

  • Arc: A portion of the circumference of a circle
  • Central Angle: An angle whose vertex is at the center of the circle
  • Arc Measure: The measure of an arc in degrees equals the measure of its central angle
  • Minor Arc: An arc measuring less than 180°
  • Major Arc: An arc measuring more than 180°
  • Semicircle: An arc measuring exactly 180°

Important: The sum of all central angles in a circle is 360°

Example 1: Finding Arc Measure from Central Angle

Problem: In a circle, a central angle measures 75°. What is the measure of the arc it intercepts?

Solution:

The measure of an arc equals the measure of its central angle.

Central angle = 75°

Therefore, arc measure = 75°

Answer: 75°

Note: This is a fundamental property that simplifies many circle problems!

Concept 2: Arc Length Formula

Arc length is the actual distance along the curved line of an arc. While arc measure is given in degrees, arc length is a linear measurement given in units like inches, centimeters, or meters. The arc length depends on both the central angle and the radius of the circle.

Arc Length Formula

For a circle with radius r and a central angle of θ degrees:

Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr

Or equivalently:

Arc Length = (θ/360°) × C

where C = circumference = 2πr

Interpretation: Arc length is the fraction of the circle (θ/360°) times the total circumference.

Example 2: Finding Arc Length

Problem: A circle has a radius of 12 inches. What is the length of an arc with a central angle of 60°?

Solution:

Using the arc length formula: Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr

θ = 60°, r = 12 inches

Arc Length = (60°/360°) × 2π(12)

Arc Length = (1/6) × 24π

Arc Length = 4π inches

Answer: 4π inches (approximately 12.57 inches)

Example 3: Finding Radius from Arc Length

Problem: An arc of 90° has a length of 5π cm. What is the radius of the circle?

Solution:

Using the arc length formula: Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr

5π = (90°/360°) × 2πr

5π = (1/4) × 2πr

5π = (πr)/2

Multiply both sides by 2: 10π = πr

Divide both sides by π: r = 10

Answer: 10 cm

Tip: Notice that a 90° angle is 1/4 of 360°, so the arc is 1/4 of the circumference!

Concept 3: Sector Area

A sector is a "slice" of a circle, like a piece of pie. It consists of two radii and the arc between them. The area of a sector depends on the central angle and the radius of the circle.

Sector Area Formula

For a circle with radius r and a central angle of θ degrees:

Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr²

Interpretation: Sector area is the fraction of the circle (θ/360°) times the total area.

This formula is analogous to the arc length formula, but uses the area formula πr² instead of circumference 2πr.

Example 4: Finding Sector Area

Problem: A circle has a radius of 8 cm. What is the area of a sector with a central angle of 45°?

Solution:

Using the sector area formula: Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr²

θ = 45°, r = 8 cm

Sector Area = (45°/360°) × π(8)²

Sector Area = (1/8) × 64π

Sector Area = 8π cm²

Answer: 8π cm² (approximately 25.13 cm²)

Example 5: Finding Central Angle from Sector Area

Problem: A sector of a circle with radius 6 feet has an area of 9π square feet. What is the measure of its central angle?

Solution:

Using the sector area formula: Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr²

9π = (θ/360°) × π(6)²

9π = (θ/360°) × 36π

Divide both sides by π: 9 = (θ/360°) × 36

9 = 36θ/360°

9 = θ/10

θ = 90°

Answer: 90°

Check: 9π = (90°/360°) × 36π = (1/4) × 36π = 9π ✓

Concept 4: Radians vs. Degrees

While degrees are the most common way to measure angles, radians are another important unit, especially in advanced mathematics. The ACT may occasionally use radians, so it's important to understand the conversion.

Radians and Degrees

  • Definition: One radian is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc equal in length to the radius
  • Full Circle: 360° = 2π radians
  • Half Circle: 180° = π radians
  • Conversion: To convert degrees to radians, multiply by π/180°
  • Conversion: To convert radians to degrees, multiply by 180°/π

Arc Length in Radians: If θ is in radians, Arc Length = rθ (much simpler!)

Sector Area in Radians: If θ is in radians, Sector Area = (1/2)r²θ

Example 6: Converting Degrees to Radians

Problem: Convert 120° to radians.

Solution:

To convert degrees to radians, multiply by π/180°

120° × (π/180°) = 120π/180 = 2π/3 radians

Answer: 2π/3 radians

Common conversions to memorize: 90° = π/2, 180° = π, 270° = 3π/2, 360° = 2π

Example 7: Arc Length Using Radians

Problem: A circle has a radius of 10 cm. Find the arc length subtended by a central angle of π/3 radians.

Solution:

When using radians, the arc length formula is simply: Arc Length = rθ

r = 10 cm, θ = π/3 radians

Arc Length = 10 × (π/3) = 10π/3 cm

Answer: 10π/3 cm (approximately 10.47 cm)

Note: π/3 radians = 60°. Using degrees: (60°/360°) × 2π(10) = (1/6) × 20π = 10π/3 ✓

Concept 5: Arc and Sector Word Problems

The ACT often presents arc and sector problems in real-world contexts. These problems require you to identify what information is given and which formula to apply. Common scenarios include clock problems, pizza slices, pendulum swings, and circular tracks.

Example 8: Clock Problem

Problem: A clock has a minute hand that is 15 cm long. How far does the tip of the minute hand travel in 20 minutes?

Solution:

Step 1: Determine the central angle

The minute hand completes 360° in 60 minutes

In 20 minutes: θ = (20/60) × 360° = (1/3) × 360° = 120°

Step 2: Calculate the arc length

The tip traces an arc with radius r = 15 cm

Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr = (120°/360°) × 2π(15)

Arc Length = (1/3) × 30π = 10π cm

Answer: 10π cm (approximately 31.42 cm)

Example 9: Pizza Slice Problem

Problem: A circular pizza with a diameter of 16 inches is cut into 8 equal slices. What is the area of one slice?

Solution:

Step 1: Find the radius

Diameter = 16 inches, so radius = 8 inches

Step 2: Find the central angle of one slice

8 equal slices divide the circle into 8 equal parts

Central angle of one slice = 360°/8 = 45°

Step 3: Calculate the sector area

Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr² = (45°/360°) × π(8)²

Sector Area = (1/8) × 64π = 8π square inches

Answer: 8π square inches (approximately 25.13 square inches)

Alternative: Total area = π(8)² = 64π. One slice = 64π/8 = 8π ✓

Example 10: Combination Problem

Problem: A sector of a circle has a central angle of 135° and an arc length of 18π meters. Find both the radius of the circle and the area of the sector.

Solution:

Step 1: Find the radius using the arc length formula

Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr

18π = (135°/360°) × 2πr

18π = (3/8) × 2πr

18π = (3πr)/4

Multiply both sides by 4: 72π = 3πr

Divide by 3π: r = 24 meters

Step 2: Find the sector area

Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr²

Sector Area = (135°/360°) × π(24)²

Sector Area = (3/8) × 576π = 216π square meters

Answer: Radius = 24 meters, Sector Area = 216π m² (approximately 678.58 m²)

ACT Strategy Tips for Arcs and Sectors

  • Remember the relationship: Arc measure (in degrees) = Central angle measure
  • Think in fractions: θ/360° represents what fraction of the whole circle you're dealing with
  • Watch for common angles: 90° = 1/4 circle, 180° = 1/2 circle, 120° = 1/3 circle, 60° = 1/6 circle
  • Radius vs. Diameter: Always check whether you're given radius or diameter – don't forget to divide by 2!
  • Units matter: Arc length has linear units (cm, in, m), sector area has square units (cm², in², m²)
  • Leave π in the answer: Unless asked for a decimal approximation, leave answers in terms of π
  • Radian conversion: If radians appear, use the simpler formulas: Arc Length = rθ, Sector Area = (1/2)r²θ
  • Draw a diagram: Visualizing the problem helps identify the given information and what you need to find
  • Check reasonableness: Arc length should be less than circumference, sector area should be less than total area

Quick Reference: Formulas Summary

Using Degrees:

  • Arc Length = (θ/360°) × 2πr = (θ/360°) × C
  • Sector Area = (θ/360°) × πr²
  • Arc Measure (degrees) = Central Angle (degrees)

Using Radians:

  • Arc Length = rθ
  • Sector Area = (1/2)r²θ
  • Conversion: radians = degrees × (π/180°)
