The ACT English Test is a 35-minute, 50-question section that measures your ability to recognize and correct errors in grammar, punctuation, sentence structure, and rhetorical strategy. Understanding the test's format, question types, and strategic approach is essential for achieving a top score. This lesson provides a comprehensive overview of what to expect on test day, how to navigate each question type efficiently, and which common mistakes to avoid so you can maximize your performance.

1. What Is the ACT English Test?

The ACT English Test consists of six passages with underlined portions or boxed numbers that correspond to multiple-choice questions. You must determine whether the underlined portion contains an error and, if so, select the correct revision from five answer choices labeled A through E. The test is passage-based, meaning you read complete essays and edit them as you go, similar to proofreading a paper.

Test Format and Timing

The test includes four longer passages with 10 questions each and two shorter passages with 5 questions each. With 35 minutes to complete 50 questions, you have approximately 42 seconds per question or 7 minutes for every 10 questions. This pacing guideline helps you stay on track throughout the section.

  • Format:
    • Six prose passages covering various topics
    • 50 multiple-choice questions total
    • Five answer choices per question (A, B, C, D, E)
  • Timing:
    • 35 minutes for the entire section
    • Approximately 7 minutes per 10 questions
    • Check your pace after every 10 questions

Three Main Question Types

The ACT English Test features three distinct categories of questions: grammar questions, style questions, and paragraph modification questions. Each category tests different skills and requires a specific approach.

  • Grammar Questions:
    • Test knowledge of standard English conventions
    • Ask: "Which choice makes the sentence most grammatically acceptable?"
    • Cover sentence structure, punctuation, verbs, pronouns, modifiers, and parallel structure
    • Range from easy to very difficult
  • Style Questions:
    • Test effectiveness and clarity of writing
    • Focus on conciseness, redundancy, word choice, and transitions
    • Questions explicitly state the topic being tested
    • Generally easier once you understand the patterns
  • Paragraph Modification Questions:
    • Test comprehension and organization of ideas
    • Ask about adding, deleting, or reordering sentences
    • Require reading for context and understanding author's purpose
    • Often appear at the end of paragraphs in boxed format

Example 1: Identifying Question Type

The museum's collection includes paintings from the Renaissance, sculptures from ancient Greece, and artifacts that originated in Egypt.

Which choice makes the sentence most grammatically acceptable?

A. NO CHANGE
B. paintings from the Renaissance, sculptures from ancient Greece, and Egyptian artifacts.
C. paintings from the Renaissance, ancient Greek sculptures, and artifacts that originated in Egypt.
D. Renaissance paintings, ancient Greek sculptures, and Egyptian artifacts.
E. paintings from the Renaissance, sculptures that are from ancient Greece, and artifacts that originated in Egypt.

Solution:

This is a grammar question testing parallel structure. When listing items in a series, each element must follow the same grammatical pattern.

  • The original sentence (A) mixes different structures: "paintings from the Renaissance" (noun + prepositional phrase), "sculptures from ancient Greece" (noun + prepositional phrase), and "artifacts that originated in Egypt" (noun + relative clause)
  • Choice B fixes the third item but still creates inconsistency: "paintings from..." and "sculptures from..." match, but "Egyptian artifacts" uses an adjective instead of a prepositional phrase
  • Choice C maintains the mixed structure from the original
  • Choice D creates perfect parallel structure: "Renaissance paintings" (adjective + noun), "ancient Greek sculptures" (adjective + noun), "Egyptian artifacts" (adjective + noun)
  • Choice E makes all three items unnecessarily wordy and inconsistent in length

Answer: D

2. How to Approach the ACT English Test

Success on the ACT English Test requires more than just knowing grammar rules. You need a strategic approach that combines efficient reading, systematic error detection, and smart time management. Following these proven strategies will help you work through passages accurately and confidently.

Read the Entire Passage

The most critical strategy is to read the passage completely rather than jumping between underlined portions. Many questions require context from surrounding sentences or paragraphs, and paragraph modification questions are impossible to answer without understanding the passage's flow and purpose.

  • Read at a steady pace, not too fast or too slow
  • Process the content as you read to understand the author's argument
  • Stop at each underlined portion to evaluate and answer the question
  • Never skip ahead without reading the surrounding text

Identify the Question Type First

Before looking at answer choices, determine whether you're dealing with a grammar question, style question, or paragraph modification question. Each type requires a different approach and mindset.

  • Grammar questions ask: "Which choice makes the sentence most grammatically acceptable?"
    • Look for errors in punctuation, sentence structure, agreement, and usage
    • Trust your ear but verify with grammar rules
  • Style questions tell you exactly what to focus on
    • "Which choice is least redundant?" means eliminate repetitive wording
    • "Which transition is most logical?" means consider the relationship between ideas
  • Paragraph modification questions require reading more context
    • Read the entire paragraph or passage section mentioned
    • Consider the author's purpose and the logical flow of ideas

Always Check All Answer Choices

Even if the underlined portion seems correct, examine every answer choice before selecting "NO CHANGE." The answer choices often reveal the specific grammar rule or style principle being tested, helping you spot errors you might have missed initially.

  • Compare differences between choices to identify what's being tested
  • If all choices seem similar, look for subtle punctuation or word choice differences
  • When stuck between two choices, plug each back into the sentence
  • Choose the most concise option when grammar and clarity are equal

Manage Your Time Strategically

With 7 minutes per 10 questions, you have enough time to work carefully without rushing. Use strategic time management to ensure you answer every question and have time to review difficult ones.

  • Check the clock after every 10 questions to monitor your pace
  • If uncertain about a question, select your best guess, circle it, and move on
  • Don't spend more than 90 seconds on any single question
  • Return to circled questions if time permits at the end
  • A fresh look at difficult questions often reveals the correct answer

Example 2: Strategic Approach in Action

The research team collected data from three sources: interviews with participants, surveys distributed online, and they observed behavior in controlled settings.

Which choice makes the sentence most grammatically acceptable?

A. NO CHANGE
B. they made observations of behavior in controlled settings.
C. observations of behavior in controlled settings.
D. behavioral observations made in controlled settings.
E. they had observed behavior in settings that were controlled.

Solution:

Step 1: Identify the question type - This is a grammar question asking which choice is most grammatically acceptable.

Step 2: Read carefully and look for errors - The sentence lists three sources of data. The first two items use parallel noun phrases ("interviews with participants" and "surveys distributed online"), but the third switches to a clause with a subject and verb ("they observed").

Step 3: Check all answer choices - Compare how each option handles the third item:

  • Choice A keeps the faulty parallel structure with "they observed"
  • Choice B changes the wording but still uses "they made observations," maintaining the clause structure
  • Choice C creates perfect parallel structure: all three items are now noun phrases
  • Choice D also creates a noun phrase but is wordier than C
  • Choice E maintains the faulty structure with "they had observed"

Step 4: Apply the conciseness principle - Between C and D, both grammatically correct, choose C because it's more concise.

Answer: C

3. Common Test-Taking Mistakes

Even students who know grammar rules well can lose points by falling into predictable traps. Understanding these common mistakes will help you avoid them and approach each question with the right mindset. The ACT deliberately designs questions to test not just your knowledge but also your attention to detail and reading comprehension.

Mistake 1: Skipping Context

The biggest mistake students make is jumping directly to underlined portions without reading surrounding sentences. This approach fails because many questions test verb tense consistency, pronoun reference, transitions, and logical flow, all of which require understanding the broader context.

  • Always read at least one sentence before and after the underlined portion
  • For paragraph modification questions, read the entire paragraph or passage section
  • Check whether the passage is written in past, present, or future tense
  • Verify that pronouns have clear antecedents

Mistake 2: Relying Only on Your Ear

While your ear can help identify obvious errors, the ACT tests many rules that sound acceptable in casual speech but violate standard written English. Errors like comma splices, pronoun case mistakes, and subject-verb agreement with intervening phrases often "sound right" but are grammatically incorrect.

  • Use your ear as a starting point, not the final answer
  • Learn formal grammar rules to verify what sounds correct
  • Pay special attention to questions where all choices "sound okay"
  • These questions test subtle distinctions in formal written English

Mistake 3: Choosing Wordy Options

When multiple answer choices are grammatically correct, students often select longer, more "sophisticated-sounding" options. The ACT consistently prefers conciseness over wordiness. If two answers are equally clear and correct, choose the shorter one.

  • Eliminate choices with redundancy (saying the same thing twice)
  • Avoid unnecessarily complex or passive constructions
  • Choose direct, active language over indirect, passive voice
  • Remember: shorter is better when clarity and correctness are equal

Mistake 4: Not Checking "NO CHANGE"

Students often assume there must be an error and overlook "NO CHANGE" as a valid answer. Approximately 25% of questions have "NO CHANGE" as the correct answer. If the underlined portion is grammatically correct, clear, and concise, don't change it just because you feel you should.

  • Give "NO CHANGE" equal consideration as other choices
  • Verify that changes in other options actually improve the sentence
  • Don't change something just because other options exist
  • Trust your analysis when the original is correct

Mistake 5: Rushing Through Style and Paragraph Questions

Because grammar questions are most common, students sometimes rush through style questions and paragraph modification questions to save time. This is counterproductive because these questions are often straightforward once you read carefully and understand what's being asked.

  • Read the full question stem, especially for paragraph questions
  • Understand exactly what the question asks before evaluating choices
  • For "add or delete" questions, consider the passage's purpose
  • For ordering questions, identify logical transitions and chronological flow

Example 3: Avoiding Common Mistakes

The chef carefully prepared the meal. He made sure to season each dish with precision and attention to detail, ensuring that every component was perfectly balanced.

Which choice is least redundant in content?

A. NO CHANGE
B. season each dish with precision, ensuring
C. season each dish precisely and with attention to detail, ensuring
D. season each dish with great precision and meticulous attention to detail, ensuring
E. carefully season each dish with precision, ensuring

Solution:

This question tests your ability to avoid common mistakes. Let's analyze each trap:

  • Mistake avoided: Not reading context - The previous sentence already says "carefully prepared," so we must consider this information when evaluating redundancy
  • Mistake avoided: Relying only on your ear - All options "sound fine," but this is a style question specifically asking about redundancy, so we must apply that principle
  • Mistake avoided: Choosing wordy options - Choices C, D, and E add unnecessary words without adding meaning
  • Mistake to avoid: Not checking "NO CHANGE" - Choice A says "with precision and attention to detail," which is redundant because "precision" already means careful attention to detail

Analysis of each choice:

  • A: "precision and attention to detail" is redundant
  • B: Removes the redundant phrase, keeping only "precision" - most concise and least redundant
  • C: Adds "precisely" (adverb form) to "precision" (noun form), plus keeps "attention to detail" - highly redundant
  • D: Adds intensifiers "great" and "meticulous" without adding meaning - extremely wordy and redundant
  • E: Adds "carefully" when the previous sentence already established the chef worked carefully - redundant with context

Answer: B
