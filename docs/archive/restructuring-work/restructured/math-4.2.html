Many word problems on the ACT Math section are systems of equations questions in disguise. The key challenge is translating the words into equations, then solving the system using elimination or backsolving. Master these techniques to quickly solve multi-variable word problems.

1. What Are Systems of Equations Word Problems?

Systems of equations word problems present real-world scenarios with two or more unknowns. You translate the given information into equations, then solve the system to find the answer.

  • Key characteristics:
    • Multiple unknowns (typically 2 or more variables)
    • Information that creates separate equations
    • Real-world context (shopping, money, plants, tickets, etc.)
  • Common scenarios:
    • Purchasing different items with different prices
    • Counting different denominations of bills
    • Mixing quantities with constraints
    • Age problems with multiple people

2. The Translation and Solving Process

Follow these steps to solve systems of equations word problems efficiently:

Step 1: Identify the Unknowns

  • Determine what you're solving for
    • Assign variables (x, y, z) to each unknown quantity
    • Write down what each variable represents
    • Note which variable the question asks for

Step 2: Translate Words into Equations

  • Convert each piece of information into an equation
    • "Total" usually creates an addition equation
    • "Cost" or "price" equations multiply quantity by unit price
    • "More than" or "less than" creates equations with addition or subtraction
    • You need as many equations as you have unknowns

Step 3: Choose Your Solution Method

  • Decide between elimination and backsolving
    • Elimination: Use when 2 unknowns and the algebra is straightforward
    • Backsolving: Use when 3+ unknowns or answer choices are specific numbers

Step 4: Solve the System

  • Execute your chosen method
    • For elimination: multiply one equation to cancel a variable, then add/subtract
    • For backsolving: plug in an answer choice and verify all conditions
    • Solve for the variable the question asks for

3. Classic Examples

Example 1: Two Unknowns with Elimination

For her garden, Mary buys 42 plants for a total of $108. Mary is only going to plant tomatoes and peppers. If a tomato plant costs $3.00 and a pepper plant costs $2.00, how many pepper plants did Mary buy?

A. 18
B. 22
C. 24
D. 28

Solution:

  • Let x = tomato plants, y = pepper plants
  • Equation 1 (total plants): x + y = 42
  • Equation 2 (total cost): 3x + 2y = 108
  • Multiply Equation 1 by -3: -3x - 3y = -126
  • Add to Equation 2: 3x + 2y + (-3x - 3y) = 108 + (-126)
  • Simplify: -y = -18, so y = 18

Answer: A (18 pepper plants)

Example 2: Three Unknowns with Backsolving

Malik made $738 selling his artwork this month and is counting his cash. Malik only has $1, $5, and $20 bills. He counts and notices that he has 5 more $5 bills than $20 bills and 6 more $1 bills than $5 bills. How many $1 bills does Malik have?

A. 33
B. 38
C. 48
D. 53

Solution Method 1 - Algebra:

  • Let x = $1 bills, y = $5 bills, z = $20 bills
  • Equation 1 (total money): 1x + 5y + 20z = 738
  • Equation 2 (relationship): y = z + 5, or z = y - 5
  • Equation 3 (relationship): x = y + 6
  • Substitute into Equation 1: solving y in terms of x: y = x - 6
  • Then z = (x - 6) - 5 = x - 11
  • Substitute: 1x + 5(x - 6) + 20(x - 11) = 738
  • Simplify: x + 5x - 30 + 20x - 220 = 738
  • Combine: 26x - 250 = 738
  • Solve: 26x = 988, so x = 38

Solution Method 2 - Backsolve (MUCH EASIER!):

  • Try answer choice B: 38 $1 bills
  • If x = 38, then $5 bills = 38 - 6 = 32
  • If $5 bills = 32, then $20 bills = 32 - 5 = 27
  • Check total: 38($1) + 32($5) + 27($20) = 38 + 160 + 540 = 738 ✓

Answer: B (38 $1 bills)

4. When to Use Each Method

Choosing the right solution method saves valuable time on the ACT.

  • Use Elimination when:
    • You have exactly 2 unknowns
    • The algebra looks straightforward
    • You can easily multiply to cancel variables
  • Use Backsolving when:
    • You have 3 or more unknowns
    • Answer choices are specific numbers (not expressions)
    • The algebra looks complex or time-consuming
    • You can quickly test answer choices using the given relationships

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Systems of equations word problems require translating real-world scenarios into mathematical equations
  • ✓Always identify your unknowns first and assign variables before writing equations
  • ✓Use elimination for 2-variable systems with straightforward algebra
  • ✓Always backsolve when you have 3 or more unknowns - it's much faster than algebra
  • ✓Look for keywords like "total," "more than," and "less than" to build your equations
