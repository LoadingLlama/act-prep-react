Chapter 1: Sentence Structure

To conquer the ACT English Test, we first need to be able to identify the different parts of a sentence and know the rules for how they can and cannot be combined.

Clauses and Phrases

A clause is a group of words that contains both a subject and a verb. There are two types of clauses: independent clauses and dependent clauses.

An independent clause can stand as a sentence by itself. It always has a subject and a verb.

  • The dog chased its tail.
  • Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast.
  • He picked it up.

A dependent clause cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. Most often, clauses become dependent by adding a subordinating conjunction to the front of the clause.

Common subordinating conjunctions: after, although, as, because, before, even though, if, once, since, though, unless, until, when, whenever, where, whether, while

  • While the dog chased its tail...
  • Although Monique made some homemade peach scones for breakfast...
  • Since he picked it up...

A phrase lacks a subject or verb or both. A phrase does not express a complete thought and can never stand alone as a sentence.

  • Chasing its tail...
  • Making some homemade peach scones for breakfast...
  • Excited to open up his present...

5 Types of Compound Sentences

On the ACT, multiple clauses must always appear as one of these 5 types:

1. Two separate independent clauses with periods.

Mary loves dogs. Andrew loves cats.

2. Comma + FANBOYS joining 2 independent clauses.

Mary loves dogs, and Andrew loves cats.

FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So

3. Semicolon between 2 independent clauses.

Mary loves dogs; Andrew loves cats.

4. Dependent clause followed by independent clause (comma).

While Mary loves dogs, Andrew loves cats.

5. Independent clause straight into dependent clause (no comma).

Mary loves dogs while Andrew loves cats.

Comma Splice

A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined by only a comma. This is always incorrect on the ACT.

Incorrect: It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, they are actually colorblind.

Correct any of these ways:

  • It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red. They are actually colorblind.
  • It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red, but they are actually colorblind.
  • It is believed that bulls are enraged by the color red; they are actually colorblind.

Sentence Fragments

A complete sentence must contain a subject and a verb. Sentence fragments are missing a subject or a verb or both.

Incorrect: The student running to get to class on time. (missing a verb)

Correct: The student running to get to class on time dropped her water bottle.

Practice Problem 1

Most people drink coffee in the morning, although I prefer not to.

A. NO CHANGE
B. morning; while
C. morning; I
D. morning but I

Solution:

The answer is C. We have two independent clauses: "Most people drink coffee in the morning" and "I prefer not to." Option A creates a fragment because "although" makes the second clause dependent. Option B incorrectly uses "while" after a semicolon. Option D is missing the comma before "but."

Practice Problem 2

The famous scientist Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes made groundbreaking discoveries in radioactivity.

A. NO CHANGE
B. scientist, Marie Curie who won two Nobel Prizes,
C. scientist Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes,
D. scientist, Marie Curie, who won two Nobel Prizes

Solution:

The answer is C. "Marie Curie" is essential information (tells us which scientist), so no commas around it. "Who won two Nobel Prizes" is non-essential information (we already know it's Marie Curie), so it needs commas on both sides.
