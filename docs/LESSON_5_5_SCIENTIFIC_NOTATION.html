Scientific notation appears in 1-2 questions on the ACT, typically in science-related contexts involving astronomical distances, microscopic measurements, or population data. The ACT tests your ability to convert between standard form and scientific notation (a × 10n), perform multiplication and division with powers of 10, and recognize when answers should remain in scientific notation. Common traps include forgetting to adjust the coefficient after operations or miscounting decimal places—mastering these operations ensures you handle large and small numbers with confidence.

1. Understanding Scientific Notation

Standard Form

A number is in scientific notation when written as a × 10n, where 1 ≤ |a| < 10 and n is an integer. The coefficient a contains all significant digits, and the exponent n indicates how many places the decimal point has moved.

Converting to Scientific Notation

Large numbers (move decimal left): 4,500,000 = 4.5 × 106 (decimal moved 6 places left, so exponent is +6).
Small numbers (move decimal right): 0.000023 = 2.3 × 10-5 (decimal moved 5 places right, so exponent is -5).

Converting from Scientific Notation

Positive exponent: Move decimal right. 3.7 × 104 = 37,000.
Negative exponent: Move decimal left. 8.2 × 10-3 = 0.0082.

Example 1: Converting to Scientific Notation

The distance from Earth to the Sun is approximately 93,000,000 miles. Which of the following expresses this distance in scientific notation?

A. 9.3 × 106
B. 9.3 × 107
C. 93 × 106
D. 0.93 × 108
E. 9.3 × 108

Solution:

Start with 93,000,000. Move the decimal point 7 places to the left to get 9.3: 93,000,000 = 9.3 × 107. The answer is B.

2. Operations with Scientific Notation

Multiplication

Multiply the coefficients and add the exponents: (a × 10m)(b × 10n) = (a · b) × 10m+n.
Example: (3 × 104)(2 × 105) = 6 × 109.

Division

Divide the coefficients and subtract the exponents: (a × 10m) ÷ (b × 10n) = (a ÷ b) × 10m-n.
Example: (8 × 107) ÷ (4 × 103) = 2 × 104.

Addition and Subtraction

First, express both numbers with the same exponent, then add or subtract the coefficients.
Example: 3 × 105 + 2 × 104 = 3 × 105 + 0.2 × 105 = 3.2 × 105.

Example 2: Multiplication in Scientific Notation

What is the product of (4 × 106) and (3 × 10-2)?

F. 12 × 104
G. 1.2 × 105
H. 12 × 10-12
J. 1.2 × 10-12
K. 1.2 × 104

Solution:

Multiply coefficients: 4 × 3 = 12. Add exponents: 6 + (-2) = 4. Result: 12 × 104. Convert to proper form: 1.2 × 105. The answer is G.

3. Powers and Roots in Scientific Notation

Raising to a Power

Use the power rule: (a × 10m)n = an × 10mn.
Example: (2 × 103)2 = 22 × 106 = 4 × 106.

Taking a Root

Use fractional exponents: √(a × 10m) = a1/2 × 10m/2. If m is odd, rewrite so the exponent becomes even.
Example: √(9 × 106) = 3 × 103.

Example 3: Squaring a Number in Scientific Notation

If a = 5 × 10-3, what is a2?

A. 25 × 10-6
B. 2.5 × 10-5
C. 25 × 10-9
D. 2.5 × 10-6
E. 5 × 10-6

Solution:

(5 × 10-3)2 = 52 × 10-6 = 25 × 10-6. Convert to proper form: 2.5 × 10-5. The answer is B.

4. Real-World Applications

Astronomy and Physics

Scientific notation is essential for expressing distances in space (light-years, parsecs), masses of celestial bodies, and constants like the speed of light (3 × 108 m/s).

Chemistry and Biology

Use scientific notation to express Avogadro's number (6.022 × 1023), atomic masses, sizes of molecules, and concentrations in solutions.

Computing and Technology

Computer memory (bytes), processing speeds (operations per second), and data transfer rates are often expressed in scientific notation or with metric prefixes (kilo, mega, giga, tera).

Example 4: Word Problem with Scientific Notation

A light-year is the distance light travels in one year, approximately 9.46 × 1012 kilometers. If a star is 2.5 light-years away, approximately how many kilometers is that?

F. 2.365 × 1012
G. 2.365 × 1013
H. 23.65 × 1012
J. 2.365 × 1014
K. 11.96 × 1012

Solution:

Multiply: 2.5 × (9.46 × 1012) = (2.5 × 9.46) × 1012 = 23.65 × 1012. Convert to proper form: 2.365 × 1013. The answer is G.
