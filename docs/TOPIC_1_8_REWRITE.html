Mastering miscellaneous grammar rules is one of the most valuable skills for achieving a high score on the ACT English Test. While topics like subject-verb agreement and punctuation often get the most attention, a variety of specialized grammar rules can make the difference between a good score and a great one. This lesson covers essential miscellaneous topics including commonly confused words, comparisons, idioms, voice, and countable versus non-countable modifiers that appear regularly on the test.

1. What are the key miscellaneous rules?

The ACT English Test includes several miscellaneous grammar rules that don't fit neatly into other categories but are crucial for success. Understanding these rules helps you quickly identify errors and select the correct answer choice.

Commonly Confused Words

The most frequently tested commonly confused words on the ACT are pairs that sound similar or have related meanings. The key is knowing the grammatical function and context of each word.

  • Affect vs. Effect: "Affect" is a verb meaning to influence or produce a change. "Effect" is a noun meaning the result of a change.
    • Correct: "The rain affects my mood negatively."
    • Correct: "The rain has a negative effect on my mood."
  • Than vs. Then: "Than" is used only for comparisons. "Then" is used for time, sequence, or conditional statements.
    • Correct: "Chocolate is better than ice cream."
    • Correct: "I called my mother, and then we left."
  • Have vs. Of: After modal verbs like "could," "would," "should," and "might," always use "have" not "of."
    • Incorrect: "We could of won the game."
    • Correct: "We could have won the game."
  • Their vs. There vs. They're: "Their" is possessive. "There" indicates location. "They're" means "they are."
  • Accept vs. Except: "Accept" means to receive. "Except" means to exclude or leave out.

Active Voice vs. Passive Voice

In active voice, the subject performs the action. In passive voice, the subject receives the action. The ACT strongly prefers active voice because it is clearer and more direct.

  • Active Voice: "I picked up the pencil."
  • Passive Voice: "The pencil was picked up by me."
  • Active Voice: "The police chief announced the list of suspects."
  • Passive Voice: "The list of suspects was announced by the police chief."

Rule: Always choose the active voice option unless passive voice is absolutely necessary for meaning.

Countable vs. Non-Countable Modifiers

Certain adjectives can only modify countable nouns (things you can count) or non-countable nouns (things expressed in singular form). Using the wrong modifier is an error the ACT tests frequently.

  • Countable Modifiers: number, many, fewer, few
  • Non-Countable Modifiers: amount, much, less, little

Examples:

  • Correct: "The amount of time depends on the number of ingredients."
  • Correct: "I do not place much significance on how many trophies an athlete has won."
  • Correct: "Skim milk has fewer calories than whole milk, so I drink less whole milk."

Example 1: Commonly Confused Words

The new environmental regulations will have a significant affect on local manufacturing companies, forcing them to update their equipment and processes to meet stricter emissions standards.

A. NO CHANGE
B. effect
C. effects
D. affecting
E. affected

Solution:

  • The sentence needs a noun that means "result" or "consequence," not a verb.
  • "Affect" is a verb meaning to influence. This is incorrect in this context.
  • "Effect" is a noun meaning the result of something. The phrase "have an effect" is a common idiom.
  • The article "a" indicates we need a singular noun, so "effects" is incorrect.
  • "Affecting" and "affected" are verb forms that don't fit the grammatical structure.

Answer: B

2. Advanced miscellaneous techniques

Beyond the basic rules, the ACT tests more nuanced grammar concepts that require careful attention. These advanced topics appear less frequently but are often missed by test-takers who haven't studied them specifically.

Prepositional Idioms

A prepositional idiom is a phrase where a specific preposition must follow a particular word. These combinations are fixed by convention rather than logic, making them challenging for non-native speakers and even native speakers who haven't studied them.

The ACT tests prepositional idioms infrequently, but when they appear, you must recognize the correct pairing. Here are the most common patterns:

  • Different from (not "different than")
  • Afraid of (not "afraid by")
  • Capable of (not "capable to")
  • Known for (not "known with")
  • Responsible for (not "responsible of")
  • Depend on (not "depend of")
  • Consistent with (not "consistent to")

Strategy: When you see a question testing preposition choice, read the sentence aloud and trust what sounds natural. Your ear often recognizes correct idioms even if you haven't memorized the rule.

Comparison Words

When making comparisons, you must use parallel structure and the correct comparison word. Common comparison errors include:

  • Farther vs. Further: "Farther" refers to physical distance. "Further" means additional or moreover.
    • Correct: "Boston is farther from New York than Philadelphia is."
    • Correct: "We need to discuss this further before making a decision."
  • Fewer vs. Less: "Fewer" modifies countable nouns. "Less" modifies non-countable nouns.
    • Correct: "This recipe requires fewer ingredients."
    • Correct: "This recipe requires less time."

Double Negatives

A double negative occurs when two negative words appear in the same clause, creating confusion or accidentally reversing the intended meaning. The ACT considers double negatives grammatically incorrect in formal writing.

  • Incorrect: "I don't have nothing to say."
  • Correct: "I don't have anything to say." OR "I have nothing to say."
  • Incorrect: "She couldn't find no solution to the problem."
  • Correct: "She couldn't find any solution to the problem."

Word Choice by Context

Some commonly confused words require understanding context rather than just memorizing definitions:

  • Lie vs. Lay: "Lie" means to recline (no direct object). "Lay" means to place something (requires direct object).
    • Correct: "I need to lie down." (no object)
    • Correct: "Please lay the book on the table." (book is the object)
  • Complement vs. Compliment: "Complement" means to complete or enhance. "Compliment" means to praise.
  • Principal vs. Principle: "Principal" means chief or head of a school. "Principle" means a fundamental rule or belief.

Example 2: Active vs. Passive Voice

The groundbreaking research on climate change was conducted by scientists at the university's environmental laboratory, leading to new insights about ocean temperature patterns.

A. NO CHANGE
B. was being conducted by scientists
C. had been conducted by scientists
D. scientists conducted
E. scientists were conducting

Solution:

  • The original sentence uses passive voice: "was conducted by scientists." The subject (research) receives the action rather than performing it.
  • Options A, B, and C all maintain passive voice construction, making them wordy and indirect.
  • Option D changes to active voice: "scientists conducted." This is clearer and more direct.
  • Option E uses active voice but with progressive tense "were conducting," which suggests ongoing action rather than completed research.
  • The ACT prefers active voice, and the context suggests completed research, making "scientists conducted" the best choice.

Answer: D

3. Common miscellaneous mistakes

Even strong writers make predictable errors with miscellaneous grammar rules. Recognizing these common mistakes helps you spot errors quickly on test day and avoid them in your own writing.

Mistake 1: Confusing "Than" and "Then"

This is one of the most frequent errors on the ACT. Remember the simple rule: if the sentence makes a comparison, use "than." For everything else (time, sequence, conditions), use "then."

  • Incorrect: "Her performance was better then mine."
  • Correct: "Her performance was better than mine." (comparison)
  • Incorrect: "First we ate dinner, than we watched a movie."
  • Correct: "First we ate dinner, then we watched a movie." (sequence)

Mistake 2: Using "Of" Instead of "Have"

Because "could've" sounds like "could of" when spoken, many writers mistakenly write "of" instead of "have" after modal verbs. This error is always incorrect in formal writing.

  • Incorrect: "She should of studied more for the test."
  • Correct: "She should have studied more for the test."
  • Incorrect: "They might of arrived earlier if traffic had been lighter."
  • Correct: "They might have arrived earlier if traffic had been lighter."

Mistake 3: Mismatching Countable Modifiers

Using countable modifiers with non-countable nouns (or vice versa) creates an obvious error that the ACT frequently tests.

  • Incorrect: "The amount of students increased this year." (students are countable)
  • Correct: "The number of students increased this year."
  • Incorrect: "We have less options available now." (options are countable)
  • Correct: "We have fewer options available now."

Mistake 4: Using Passive Voice Unnecessarily

Writers often default to passive voice because it sounds more formal or academic. However, the ACT strongly prefers active voice for its clarity and directness.

  • Weak: "The experiment was designed by the research team."
  • Strong: "The research team designed the experiment."
  • Weak: "Many important discoveries were made by Marie Curie."
  • Strong: "Marie Curie made many important discoveries."

Mistake 5: Incorrect Prepositional Idioms

Because prepositional idioms follow convention rather than logic, writers often pair words with the wrong preposition.

  • Incorrect: "His approach is different than mine."
  • Correct: "His approach is different from mine."
  • Incorrect: "The team is capable to win the championship."
  • Correct: "The team is capable of winning the championship."

Example 3: Countable vs. Non-Countable Modifiers

Although the new policy may reduce the amount of accidents on campus, some students argue that the restrictions place too many limitations on their freedom.

A. NO CHANGE
B. number
C. total
D. quantity
E. sum

Solution:

  • The noun "accidents" is countable (you can have one accident, two accidents, etc.).
  • "Amount" is a non-countable modifier and cannot be used with countable nouns like "accidents."
  • "Number" is the countable modifier that pairs with countable nouns.
  • While "total," "quantity," and "sum" might sound acceptable, "number" is the standard, precise term for countable items.
  • The phrase "the number of accidents" is grammatically correct and widely accepted.

Answer: B

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Master commonly confused word pairs like affect/effect, than/then, and have/of by understanding their grammatical functions
  • ✓Always prefer active voice over passive voice unless passive construction is absolutely necessary for meaning
  • ✓Use countable modifiers (number, many, fewer) with countable nouns and non-countable modifiers (amount, much, less) with non-countable nouns
  • ✓Remember that "than" is used only for comparisons while "then" is used for time, sequence, and conditional statements
  • ✓For prepositional idioms, trust your ear and read the sentence aloud to identify which preposition sounds correct
  • ✓Avoid double negatives in formal writing by using only one negative word per clause
