Beyond basic mean, median, and mode, the ACT tests advanced statistics in 2-3 questions per test, focusing on range, quartiles (Q1, Q2, Q3), interquartile range (IQR), standard deviation, and box plots. These measures describe spread and variability—how spread out data is from the center. Understanding outliers, the Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7), and how to interpret five-number summaries helps you tackle the more challenging statistics problems that separate high scorers from average performers.

1. Range and Interquartile Range

Range

The range is the difference between the maximum and minimum values in a data set: Range = Maximum - Minimum.
Example: For {3, 7, 9, 15, 20}, range = 20 - 3 = 17. The range is sensitive to outliers.

Quartiles and Interquartile Range (IQR)

Quartiles divide a data set into four equal parts:
- Q1 (first quartile): Median of the lower half of the data (25th percentile).
- Q2 (second quartile): The median of the entire data set (50th percentile).
- Q3 (third quartile): Median of the upper half of the data (75th percentile).
Interquartile Range (IQR) = Q3 - Q1, which measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data and is not affected by outliers.

Example 1: Finding Quartiles and IQR

The data set is: 4, 7, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21. What is the interquartile range (IQR)?

A. 9
B. 10
C. 11
D. 12
E. 17

Solution:

Median (Q2) = 12. Lower half: {4, 7, 9}; Q1 = 7. Upper half: {15, 18, 21}; Q3 = 18. IQR = Q3 - Q1 = 18 - 7 = 11. The answer is C.

2. Standard Deviation and Variance

Understanding Standard Deviation

Standard deviation (σ or s) measures how spread out the data is from the mean. A small standard deviation means the data is clustered close to the mean; a large standard deviation means the data is more spread out. The variance is the square of the standard deviation: Variance = σ².

Calculating Standard Deviation

1. Find the mean of the data set.
2. For each value, compute the deviation from the mean (value - mean).
3. Square each deviation.
4. Find the average of the squared deviations (this is the variance).
5. Take the square root of the variance to get the standard deviation.
On the ACT, you usually interpret standard deviation rather than calculate it from scratch.

Example 2: Interpreting Standard Deviation

Two classes have the same mean test score of 75. Class A has a standard deviation of 5, and Class B has a standard deviation of 15. Which statement is true?

F. Class A scores are more spread out than Class B.
G. Class B scores are more spread out than Class A.
H. Both classes have the same spread of scores.
J. Class A has more students than Class B.
K. Standard deviation does not measure spread.

Solution:

A larger standard deviation indicates greater spread. Class B (σ = 15) has scores more spread out than Class A (σ = 5). The answer is G.

3. Box Plots and Five-Number Summary

Five-Number Summary

The five-number summary consists of: Minimum, Q1, Median (Q2), Q3, Maximum. These five values fully describe the distribution of the data and are used to create box plots (box-and-whisker plots).

Reading a Box Plot

A box plot displays the five-number summary visually:
- The box extends from Q1 to Q3 (containing the middle 50% of data).
- A line inside the box marks the median.
- "Whiskers" extend from the box to the minimum and maximum values (or to 1.5×IQR from the quartiles if there are outliers).
- Outliers are plotted as individual points beyond the whiskers.

Example 3: Interpreting a Box Plot

A box plot shows the following five-number summary for test scores: Minimum = 60, Q1 = 70, Median = 80, Q3 = 90, Maximum = 100. What percent of students scored between 70 and 90?

A. 25%
B. 40%
C. 50%
D. 75%
E. Cannot be determined

Solution:

Q1 = 70 (25th percentile) and Q3 = 90 (75th percentile). The range from Q1 to Q3 contains the middle 50% of the data. The answer is C.

4. Outliers and Data Distributions

Identifying Outliers

A common rule: A value is an outlier if it is more than 1.5 × IQR below Q1 or above Q3.
- Lower bound: Q1 - 1.5 × IQR
- Upper bound: Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
Any values outside these bounds are considered outliers.

Normal Distribution and Empirical Rule

In a normal (bell-shaped) distribution:
- About 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean.
- About 95% falls within 2 standard deviations.
- About 99.7% falls within 3 standard deviations.
This is known as the Empirical Rule or 68-95-99.7 Rule.

Example 4: Using the Empirical Rule

Test scores are normally distributed with a mean of 500 and a standard deviation of 50. Approximately what percent of scores fall between 450 and 550?

F. 34%
G. 50%
H. 68%
J. 95%
K. 99.7%

Solution:

450 = 500 - 50 (1 standard deviation below mean). 550 = 500 + 50 (1 standard deviation above mean). By the Empirical Rule, about 68% of scores fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean. The answer is H.
