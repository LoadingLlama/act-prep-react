Advanced punctuation mastery is one of the most powerful skills for the ACT English Test. Understanding how to use semicolons, colons, dashes, apostrophes, and quotation marks correctly can help you identify errors quickly and confidently. These punctuation rules appear on nearly every ACT English Test, and mastering them will significantly improve your score. This lesson covers the five essential punctuation marks and the specific rules the ACT tests most frequently.

1. What are the 5 essential punctuation rules?

The ACT English Test focuses on five punctuation marks that follow specific, testable rules. Understanding these rules allows you to eliminate wrong answers instantly and identify correct punctuation with confidence.

Semicolons

A semicolon connects two independent clauses. The key rule is that a semicolon functions exactly like a period. If you can replace a semicolon with a period and create two complete sentences, then the semicolon is used correctly.

  • Key characteristics of correct semicolon usage:
    • Everything before the semicolon must be an independent clause
    • Everything after the semicolon must be an independent clause
    • Both clauses must express complete thoughts that could stand alone as sentences

Colons

A colon introduces a list, example, explanation, clarification, or definition. The part before a colon must always be an independent clause, while the part after provides additional information.

  • Three essential rules for colons:
    • The clause before the colon must be an independent clause
    • The part after the colon must only include the list, example, or explanation
    • Never use a colon with "including," "such as," or "for example"

Dashes

A dash can function like a colon, parentheses, or a pair of commas. Most commonly, two dashes set apart unnecessary information in the middle of a sentence, similar to how a pair of commas creates a nonrestrictive clause.

  • When using dashes:
    • Pairs of dashes separate unnecessary information in a sentence
    • Never mix punctuation (cannot use a comma with a dash)
    • A single dash can introduce lists or explanations like a colon

Apostrophes

An apostrophe shows possession or creates contractions. The ACT frequently tests whether you can distinguish between possessive forms and plural forms, as well as possessive pronouns and contractions.

  • Key apostrophe rules:
    • For singular nouns, add apostrophe + s (dog's bone)
    • For plural nouns ending in s, add apostrophe only (dogs' collars)
    • Possessive pronouns never use apostrophes (its, theirs, yours)

Quotation Marks

Quotation marks enclose direct quotes and indicate when a word is used as a technical term or in an unusual way. The ACT tests whether you place commas correctly with spoken quotes versus non-spoken quotes.

  • When using quotation marks:
    • Direct quotes that are spoken use commas before or after the quote
    • Direct quotes that are not spoken have no comma
    • Use quotation marks for technical terms, slang, or ironic usage

Example 1: Semicolons and Independent Clauses

Sarah wanted to travel to Japan for the summer; the trip would give her a chance to practice speaking Japanese and experience the culture firsthand.

A. NO CHANGE
B. summer, the trip
C. summer: the trip
D. summer. The trip
E. summer, and the trip

Solution:

  • Test the semicolon rule: Can we replace it with a period?
  • "Sarah wanted to travel to Japan for the summer" is a complete independent clause
  • "The trip would give her a chance to practice speaking Japanese and experience the culture firsthand" is also a complete independent clause
  • Since both halves are independent clauses, the semicolon is correct
  • Choice B creates a comma splice (incorrect connection of two independent clauses)
  • Choice C (colon) and Choice D (period) are also correct, but if A is an option, it is the answer
  • Choice E incorrectly uses "and" without establishing proper connection

Answer: A

2. Advanced punctuation techniques

Beyond the basic rules, the ACT tests advanced applications of these punctuation marks. Understanding these nuances will help you tackle the most difficult punctuation questions on the test.

Advanced Colon Rules

A colon can connect two independent clauses when the second clause acts as a definition, example, explanation, or clarification of the first clause. This advanced rule rarely appears, but it is important to recognize.

When a colon connects two independent clauses, the second clause must directly explain or define the first clause. If this relationship exists, the colon is correct even though both sides could stand as complete sentences.

  • Examples of colons connecting independent clauses:
    • My father gave me one rule to live by: honesty is always the best policy.
    • The scientist made a startling discovery: the bacteria could survive in extreme heat.
    • The results were clear: voters strongly opposed the new legislation.

The "Including" and "Such As" Rule

This is one of the most important colon rules on the ACT. Never use a colon before or after "including," "such as," or "for example." If you see a colon near these words, it is always incorrect.

When "including" or "for example" introduces a list, use a comma instead. For "such as," you can use a comma or no punctuation depending on whether the information is essential to the sentence.

  • Correct usage with list introducers:
    • I bought several items at the store, including milk, bread, and eggs.
    • Many vegetables grow well in winter, for example garlic and potatoes.
    • The study used techniques such as direct observation and surveying.

Dash Variations

A single dash can function exactly like a colon when introducing lists, examples, or explanations. When a dash acts as a colon, it must follow all three colon rules.

Less commonly, a single dash can separate information at the end of a sentence, functioning like a single parenthesis. This usage is rare but occasionally appears on difficult ACT questions.

  • Examples of dash variations:
    • You will need three ingredients - flour, eggs, and milk.
    • After months of waiting, the patient was frustrated with his doctor's plan - or lack of a plan.
    • The tournament attracted players from across the country - California, Texas, and New York.

Possessive Pronouns vs. Contractions

The ACT frequently tests the difference between possessive pronouns and contractions. Possessive pronouns like "its," "their," "your," and "whose" never use apostrophes. Contractions like "it's," "they're," "you're," and "who's" always use apostrophes because they combine two words.

To determine which form is correct, expand the contraction in the sentence. If "it is" makes sense, use "it's." If "it is" does not make sense, use "its."

  • Common possessive pronoun vs. contraction pairs:
    • its (possessive) vs. it's (it is)
    • their (possessive) vs. they're (they are)
    • your (possessive) vs. you're (you are)
    • whose (possessive) vs. who's (who is)

Quotation Marks for Spoken vs. Non-Spoken Quotes

The placement of commas with quotation marks depends on whether the quote is spoken. When someone speaks, yells, whispers, or exclaims, use a comma to offset the quote. When a quote is simply part of the sentence and not being spoken aloud, do not use a comma.

  • Spoken quotes (use comma):
    • Maria said, "The concert starts at seven o'clock."
    • "I will finish the project by Friday," John promised.
  • Non-spoken quotes (no comma):
    • The report described the results as "highly significant."
    • My teacher considers this assignment "optional but recommended."

Example 2: Colons and Lists

The research team examined several factors that influence plant growth, including: sunlight, water quality, soil composition, and temperature.

A. NO CHANGE
B. growth: including sunlight,
C. growth, including sunlight,
D. growth: sunlight,
E. growth; including sunlight,

Solution:

  • Apply the "including" rule: Never use a colon before or after "including"
  • Choice A places a colon after "including" (incorrect)
  • Choice B places a colon before "including" (also incorrect)
  • Choice C correctly uses a comma with "including"
  • Choice D removes "including" and uses only a colon, which would be correct, but changes the meaning
  • Choice E incorrectly uses a semicolon (the second part is not an independent clause)
  • When "including" introduces a list, always use a comma

Answer: C

3. Common punctuation mistakes

The ACT tests specific punctuation errors repeatedly. Learning to recognize these common mistakes will help you identify incorrect answer choices quickly and avoid falling for common traps.

Comma Splices with Semicolons

A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are connected with only a comma. Many students incorrectly use semicolons when one side is not an independent clause. Remember that both sides of a semicolon must be complete sentences.

  • Common semicolon mistakes:
    • Using a semicolon when the first part is a dependent clause
    • Using a semicolon when the second part is a phrase
    • Using a comma when a semicolon is needed (creates comma splice)

Incomplete Clauses Before Colons

The most common colon error is placing a colon after an incomplete clause. If the part before the colon cannot stand alone as a complete sentence, the colon is incorrect.

  • Common colon mistakes:
    • Placing a colon after a verb that requires an object
    • Using a colon with "including," "such as," or "for example"
    • Allowing the sentence to continue beyond the list or explanation

Mixed Punctuation with Dashes

When using pairs of dashes to set apart unnecessary information, never mix dashes with commas or parentheses. The punctuation must match on both sides.

  • Common dash mistakes:
    • Starting with a dash but ending with a comma
    • Starting with a comma but ending with a dash
    • Using only one dash when two are needed for unnecessary information

Apostrophe Errors

Students commonly confuse possessive forms with plural forms, and possessive pronouns with contractions. The ACT designs answer choices to test whether you can hear the difference between these similar-sounding words.

  • Common apostrophe mistakes:
    • Adding an apostrophe to plural nouns that show no possession
    • Using "it's" when "its" is correct (or vice versa)
    • Adding apostrophes to possessive pronouns like "theirs" or "yours"
    • Placing the apostrophe on the wrong side of the "s" for plural possessives

Quotation Mark Comma Errors

The most common quotation mark error is adding a comma before a non-spoken quote. If the quote is simply part of the sentence and is not being spoken aloud, no comma is needed.

  • Common quotation mark mistakes:
    • Adding a comma before a non-spoken quote
    • Omitting a comma before a spoken quote
    • Placing quotation marks around words that do not need them

Example 3: Apostrophes and Possession

The three musicians' instruments were carefully packed in their cases before the orchestra loaded its equipment onto the tour bus for the long journey to the concert hall.

A. NO CHANGE
B. musician's instruments were carefully packed in their cases before the orchestra loaded it's
C. musicians instruments were carefully packed in they're cases before the orchestra loaded its
D. musicians' instruments were carefully packed in there cases before the orchestra loaded its
E. musician's instruments were carefully packed in their cases before the orchestra loaded its

Solution:

  • Identify each apostrophe usage: "three musicians'" indicates plural possession (correct)
  • "Their" is a possessive pronoun showing the cases belong to them (correct)
  • "Its" is a possessive pronoun showing the equipment belongs to the orchestra (correct)
  • Choice B uses "musician's" (singular possessive) but the sentence says "three musicians" (incorrect)
  • Choice B also uses "it's" (it is), which does not make sense when expanded (incorrect)
  • Choice C uses "they're" (they are), which does not make sense: "packed in they are cases" (incorrect)
  • Choice D uses "there" (location word) instead of "their" (possessive) (incorrect)
  • Choice E uses "musician's" (singular) when plural is needed (incorrect)
  • Choice A correctly uses all three apostrophe forms

Answer: A
