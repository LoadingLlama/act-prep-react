Algebra Skills

Mastering fundamental algebra skills is absolutely essential for ACT Math success. These foundational concepts appear in over 20 questions per test across all difficulty levels. Whether you're simplifying expressions, solving equations, or working with polynomials, these skills form the bedrock of mathematical reasoning. In this lesson, we'll build your confidence with clear explanations, strategic tips, and plenty of practice that mirrors what you'll see on test day.

1. Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

The order of operations determines which calculations to perform first when evaluating expressions. Without a consistent order, the expression 6 + 3 × 2 could equal either 12 or 18 depending on which operation you do first.

PEMDAS helps you remember the correct order:

  • Parentheses (and other grouping symbols like brackets)
  • Exponents (including square roots and powers)
  • Multiplication and Division (left to right—same priority level!)
  • Addition and Subtraction (left to right—same priority level!)

Critical reminder: Multiplication and division have equal priority—work left to right. The same applies to addition and subtraction. A common mistake is thinking multiplication always comes before division or that addition always comes before subtraction.

ACT Strategy: When you see a complex expression, circle or underline each operation in the order you'll perform it. This prevents skipping steps and helps you avoid careless errors, especially on questions designed to trap students who rush.

What is the value of 18 - 6 ÷ 2 + 3²?

A. 15
B. 18
C. 21
D. 24
E. 30

Solution:

Follow PEMDAS step by step:

Step 1: Exponents

3² = 9

Expression becomes: 18 - 6 ÷ 2 + 9

Step 2: Division

6 ÷ 2 = 3

Expression becomes: 18 - 3 + 9

Step 3: Subtraction and Addition (left to right)

18 - 3 = 15

15 + 9 = 24

Answer: D

2. Working with Negative Numbers

Understanding how negative numbers interact in operations is crucial for avoiding careless errors. The ACT deliberately includes negative numbers to test whether you truly understand the rules or just memorize formulas.

Addition and Subtraction Rules:

  • Subtracting a negative = adding a positive: 5 - (-3) = 5 + 3 = 8
  • Adding a negative = subtracting: 5 + (-3) = 5 - 3 = 2
  • Two negatives make a positive when side by side: --3 = +3

Multiplication and Division Rules:

  • Same signs = Positive result: (-3) × (-4) = 12 or (-12) ÷ (-3) = 4
  • Different signs = Negative result: (-3) × 4 = -12 or 12 ÷ (-3) = -4

ACT Strategy: When you see multiple negative signs, slow down and process each operation separately. Write out intermediate steps if needed. The ACT knows students rush through negative number problems and counts on these mistakes.

What is the value of -8 - (-3) × (-2)?

A. -14
B. -10
C. -2
D. 2
E. 10

Solution:

Follow order of operations (multiplication before subtraction):

Step 1: Multiplication

(-3) × (-2) = 6 (same signs = positive)

Expression becomes: -8 - 6

Step 2: Rewrite subtraction

-8 - 6 = -8 + (-6)

Step 3: Add

-8 + (-6) = -14

Answer: A

3. The Distributive Property and Combining Like Terms

The distributive property allows you to multiply a term across terms inside parentheses. This is one of the most frequently used algebraic techniques on the ACT.

Formula: a(b + c) = ab + ac

Examples:

  • 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12
  • -2(x - 5) = -2x + 10 (remember to distribute the negative!)
  • 5(2x + 3y - 1) = 10x + 15y - 5

After distributing, you'll often need to combine like terms—terms with the same variable raised to the same power. Only like terms can be combined through addition or subtraction.

Like term examples:

  • 3x + 5x = 8x (both are x terms)
  • 4x² - 2x² = 2x² (both are x² terms)
  • 3x + 5 CANNOT be simplified (different types)
  • 2x + 3x² CANNOT be simplified (different powers)

Common error alert: When distributing a negative, ALL signs inside change. For example: -(x - 3) = -x + 3, NOT -x - 3.

ACT Strategy: On simplification problems, work methodically: distribute first, then identify and combine like terms. Circle like terms in different colors if it helps you organize your work.

Simplify: 3(x + 4) - 2(x - 1)

A. x + 2
B. x + 10
C. x + 14
D. 5x + 10
E. 5x + 14

Solution:

Step 1: Distribute the first term

3(x + 4) = 3x + 12

Step 2: Distribute the second term (watch the negative!)

-2(x - 1) = -2x + 2

Step 3: Write out all terms

3x + 12 - 2x + 2

Step 4: Combine like terms

x terms: 3x - 2x = x

Constant terms: 12 + 2 = 14

Final answer: x + 14

Answer: C

4. Solving Linear Equations

To solve for a variable, your goal is to isolate it on one side of the equation using inverse operations. Think of an equation like a balanced scale—whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other to maintain balance.

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Distribute if necessary to eliminate parentheses
  2. Combine like terms on each side separately
  3. Move all variable terms to one side (usually left)
  4. Move all constant terms to the other side (usually right)
  5. Divide (or multiply) both sides to isolate the variable

Inverse Operations:

  • Addition ↔ Subtraction
  • Multiplication ↔ Division
  • Squaring ↔ Square root

ACT Strategy: Always check your answer by substituting it back into the original equation. This catches arithmetic errors and ensures you didn't lose track of a negative sign. On the ACT, wrong answer choices are often the result of common mistakes—checking your work helps you avoid falling for these traps.

Solve for x: 3(x - 2) + 4 = 2x + 5

A. 1
B. 3
C. 5
D. 7
E. 9

Solution:

Step 1: Distribute on the left side

3(x - 2) = 3x - 6

Equation becomes: 3x - 6 + 4 = 2x + 5

Step 2: Combine like terms on left side

-6 + 4 = -2

Equation becomes: 3x - 2 = 2x + 5

Step 3: Subtract 2x from both sides

3x - 2x - 2 = 2x - 2x + 5

x - 2 = 5

Step 4: Add 2 to both sides

x - 2 + 2 = 5 + 2

x = 7

Check: 3(7 - 2) + 4 = 3(5) + 4 = 15 + 4 = 19, and 2(7) + 5 = 14 + 5 = 19 ✓

Answer: D

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ PEMDAS: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division (left to right), Addition/Subtraction (left to right)
  • ✓ Subtracting a negative equals adding a positive: a - (-b) = a + b
  • ✓ Same signs multiply to positive; different signs multiply to negative
  • ✓ When distributing a negative, all signs inside parentheses change
  • ✓ Only combine like terms (same variable, same power)—check carefully before simplifying
