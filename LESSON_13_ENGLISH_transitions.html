Transition questions test your ability to connect ideas logically across sentences and paragraphs on the ACT English Test. These questions require you to understand logical relationships between ideas—whether you're showing contrast, cause and effect, addition, or emphasis. Appearing 6-8 times per test, transition questions reward students who read actively for meaning and understand how sentences build on each other to develop ideas.

1. Types of Logical Relationships

Transitions signal specific relationships between ideas. Understanding these categories helps you select the appropriate transition word or phrase.

Addition and Continuation

Use these transitions when adding similar or supporting information.

Addition transitions: additionally, also, furthermore, moreover, in addition, likewise, similarly, as well

Contrast and Opposition

Use these transitions when presenting contrasting or opposing ideas.

Contrast transitions: however, nevertheless, nonetheless, on the other hand, in contrast, conversely, although, yet, but, despite, still

Cause and Effect

Use these transitions when showing that one idea results from or causes another.

Cause/effect transitions: therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, for this reason, because, since, so, accordingly, hence

Examples and Emphasis

Use these transitions when providing examples or emphasizing a point.

Example/emphasis transitions: for example, for instance, specifically, in particular, indeed, in fact, notably, especially

2. Choosing the Correct Transition

To select the right transition, you must understand the relationship between the ideas it connects. Always read the sentences before and after the transition.

Step 1: Identify the Logical Relationship

Read the sentence before the transition and the sentence with/after the transition. Ask: What's the relationship?

Example:

"The experiment was carefully planned. _____, the results were inconclusive."

Analysis: First sentence is positive (carefully planned). Second sentence is negative (inconclusive). This is a CONTRAST relationship.

✓ Correct answer: However / Nevertheless / Despite this

Step 2: Eliminate Wrong Relationships

Once you've identified the relationship, eliminate transitions that signal different relationships.

In the example above, eliminate:

• "Therefore" → suggests cause/effect, not contrast

• "For example" → suggests illustration, not contrast

• "Additionally" → suggests addition, not contrast

Common ACT Transition Traps

The ACT loves to offer transitions that sound sophisticated but create illogical connections.

Watch out for:

• Using "however" when ideas don't actually contrast

• Using "therefore" when there's no cause-effect relationship

• Using "for example" when the second sentence isn't an example

3. Transition Placement and Punctuation

Transitions can appear at different positions in a sentence, and their punctuation varies by placement. Understanding these patterns helps you choose grammatically correct options.

Beginning of Sentence

Most transitions appear at the start of sentences, followed by a comma.

✓ Correct: However, the results were surprising.

✓ Correct: Therefore, we revised our approach.

Middle of Sentence

Some transitions can interrupt a sentence. Set them off with commas on both sides.

✓ Correct: The results, however, were inconclusive.

✓ Correct: This approach, in fact, proved successful.

No Transition Needed

Sometimes the best answer is no transition at all—when the logical connection is already clear or when adding a transition makes the writing awkward.

Important: If "DELETE the underlined portion" is an option and the sentences flow logically without the transition, consider choosing DELETE.

4. Advanced Transition Strategies

Beyond knowing transition words, successful students understand how to analyze passage flow and recognize subtle logical relationships that the ACT tests.

Reading for Relationship, Not Just Words

Don't just match transition words to categories. Understand what the sentences actually mean.

Strategy:

1. Read sentence before transition (understand its point)

2. Read sentence with/after transition (understand its point)

3. Ask: Are these ideas similar, opposite, or cause-effect?

4. Choose the transition that matches that relationship

Multiple Valid Transitions

Often several transitions could work logically. Choose the one that's most precise and fits the passage tone.

For contrast, all these could work:

• However (neutral, common)

• Nevertheless (more formal, emphatic)

• On the other hand (shows two sides)

• In contrast (emphasizes difference)

→ Choose based on passage tone and emphasis

Paragraph-Level Transitions

Sometimes transitions connect entire paragraphs, not just sentences. These require understanding the main idea of each paragraph.

For paragraph transitions, ask:

• What was the main point of the previous paragraph?

• What's the main point of this paragraph?

• How do these main ideas relate?

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Four main relationship types: Addition (furthermore, moreover); Contrast (however, nevertheless); Cause/effect (therefore, consequently); Example/emphasis (for instance, indeed)
  • ✓Selection strategy: Read sentences before and after the transition; identify the logical relationship (similar, opposite, or cause-effect); eliminate transitions signaling different relationships; choose the most precise option
  • ✓Placement and punctuation: Transitions at sentence beginnings need comma after; transitions interrupting sentences need commas on both sides; sometimes DELETE is correct when logical connection is already clear
  • ✓Advanced strategies: Understand meaning, not just memorize words; when multiple transitions could work, choose the most precise one for passage tone; for paragraph transitions, consider main ideas of both paragraphs
  • ✓Common traps: Using "however" when ideas don't contrast; using "therefore" without cause-effect; using "for example" when second sentence isn't an example; always verify the logical relationship matches the transition
