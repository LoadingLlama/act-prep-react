Misplaced modifier questions make up about 5-7% of the ACT English Test—roughly 4-5 questions per test! These questions test whether descriptive words, phrases, or clauses are placed next to the nouns they're supposed to modify.

1. What is a Misplaced Modifier?

  • A misplaced modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that is improperly separated from the noun it modifies
    • This leads to a sentence with an incorrect or unintended meaning
    • Misplaced modifiers are hard to spot because we usually know what the sentence is really supposed to be saying

Example of a Misplaced Modifier

  • ❌ Incorrect: "Consistently erupting, Jack loves taking pictures of the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park."
    • What's wrong? The modifier "consistently erupting" is supposed to modify the Old Faithful geyser, but instead it's modifying Jack!
    • This sentence suggests Jack is consistently erupting (which doesn't make sense)
  • When a sentence begins with a modifying phrase, the phrase must modify the very first noun that follows the comma

How to Fix Misplaced Modifiers

  • Option 1: Put the modifier right next to what it's describing
    • ✓ Correct: "Consistently erupting, the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park is one of Jack's favorite things to take pictures of."
    • Now "consistently erupting" is right next to "the Old Faithful geyser"
  • Option 2: Change the modifier from a phrase to a dependent clause
    • ✓ Correct: "Since it is consistently erupting, Jack loves taking pictures of the Old Faithful geyser at Yellowstone National Park."
    • Adding "it is" changes the modifier to a dependent clause, making it clear the geyser is erupting

The "Next-Door Neighbor" Rule

  • For a modifier to be properly placed, it must be right "next-door" to the noun it's describing
    • Modifiers can be at the beginning, middle, or end of a sentence
  • If the modifier is at the beginning of the sentence, the first noun after it must be the word being modified
    • Example: "Running from the police, Eric hopped the fence and hid underneath a car."
    • "Running from the police" modifies "Eric" (the first noun after the comma)
  • If the modifier is in the middle or end of the sentence, it must be modifying the noun directly before it
    • Example: "Dr. Anderson, a world-renowned surgeon, fixed my broken leg."
    • "A world-renowned surgeon" modifies "Dr. Anderson" (the noun directly before it)

2. Front of the Sentence Modifiers with a Comma

  • On the ACT, most misplaced modifier questions have a modifier at the front of the sentence separated by a comma
    • For this type of question, consider who or what the modifier is describing
    • Then make sure that person, place, or thing is the first noun in the rest of the sentence

Example: Front Modifier Error

  • ❌ Incorrect: "Not popular since the early 1980s, the documentary about mullets explored the origins of the wildly popular hairstyle."
    • The modifier is "not popular since the early 1980s"
    • What is "not popular since the early 1980s"? Mullets, not documentaries!
    • But the first noun after the comma is "documentary," which is wrong
  • ✓ Correct: "Not popular since the early 1980s, mullets were the topic of a recently released documentary."
    • Now "mullets" (the thing that's not popular since the 1980s) is the first noun after the comma
    • The modifier is right "next-door" to what it describes

Strategy for Front Modifiers

  • Step 1: Identify the modifying phrase at the beginning (before the comma)
    • Look for descriptive phrases before the first comma
  • Step 2: Ask yourself: Who or what is being described by this modifier?
    • Think about what noun makes logical sense with the description
  • Step 3: Find the first noun after the comma
    • This is what the modifier is currently describing
  • Step 4: Make sure the first noun matches who or what should be described
    • If it doesn't match, the modifier is misplaced
    • Choose the answer that puts the correct noun first

3. Middle or End of the Sentence Modifiers with Commas

  • A modifier in the middle or at the end of the sentence must be directly after whoever or whatever it is modifying
    • These modifiers are often set off by commas or use "which" or "that"

Example: Middle Modifier Error

  • ❌ Incorrect: "The research team, which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year, tracked the movements of the blue storks."
    • The modifier is "which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year"
    • This is placed right after "research team," so it's describing the research team
    • But the blue storks (not the research team) arrived at their nesting grounds early!

How to Fix Middle/End Modifiers

  • Option 1: Move the modifier to be right after what it describes
    • ✓ Correct: "The research team tracked the movements of the blue storks, which arrived at their nesting grounds early this year."
    • Now the modifier is right after "blue storks"
  • Option 2: Change the modifier to describe what's actually before it
    • ✓ Correct: "The research team, which tracked the movement of the blue storks, noted that the birds arrived at their nesting grounds early."
    • Now the modifier correctly describes the research team

Strategy for Middle/End Modifiers

  • Step 1: Identify the modifying phrase (often with "which," "that," or set off by commas)
    • Look for descriptive phrases in the middle or end of the sentence
  • Step 2: Ask: What noun is the modifier describing?
    • Think logically about what makes sense
  • Step 3: Check what noun comes right before the modifier
    • This is what the modifier is currently describing
  • Step 4: Make sure the noun before the modifier is what should be described
    • If not, choose the answer that moves the modifier or changes the sentence structure

4. Modifiers without Commas

  • In some questions, the modifier will not be separated by any commas
    • These are harder to spot, but the "next-door neighbor" rule still applies
    • Keep an eye out for phrases starting with "that" or words ending in "-ing" and "-ed"

Common Patterns without Commas

  • Phrases starting with "that"
    • Example: "The book that I borrowed from the library is overdue."
    • "That I borrowed from the library" modifies "book" (right before it)
  • Words ending in "-ing" (present participles)
    • Example: "The dog chasing the cat ran into the street."
    • "Chasing the cat" modifies "dog" (right before it)
    • ❌ Incorrect: "Chasing the cat, the street was where the dog ran."
    • This suggests the street is chasing the cat!
  • Words ending in "-ed" (past participles)
    • Example: "The cake baked by my grandmother was delicious."
    • "Baked by my grandmother" modifies "cake" (right before it)

Strategy for Modifiers without Commas

  • Look for descriptive phrases that aren't set off by commas
    • Watch for "that," "-ing," and "-ed" words
  • Apply the same "next-door neighbor" rule
    • The modifier should be right next to what it's describing
    • If it's at the beginning, check the first noun after it
    • If it's in the middle or end, check the noun right before it
  • Ask yourself: Does the modifier logically describe the noun next to it?
    • If not, it's misplaced

Key Takeaways

  • ✓A misplaced modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that is improperly separated from the noun it modifies, leading to a sentence with an incorrect or unintended meaning. Use the "next-door neighbor" rule: modifiers must be right next to the nouns they describe.
  • ✓For front-of-sentence modifiers separated by a comma, the first noun after the comma must be what the modifier describes. Ask yourself: "Who or what is being described by this modifier?" and make sure that noun comes first after the comma.
  • ✓For middle or end-of-sentence modifiers with commas (often using "which" or "that"), the modifier must come directly after the noun it describes. Check what noun comes right before the modifier—that's what it's currently modifying—and make sure it's the correct noun.
  • ✓Modifiers without commas follow the same "next-door neighbor" rule. Watch for phrases starting with "that" or words ending in "-ing" and "-ed." These descriptive phrases must be right next to the nouns they modify, whether at the beginning, middle, or end of the sentence.
