Working backwards is one of the most powerful problem-solving strategies on the ACT Math test, allowing you to solve complex problems by reverse-engineering the solution from the answer choices. Instead of setting up algebraic equations and solving forward, you test each answer choice by substituting it into the problem to see which one works. This strategy is particularly effective for word problems, multi-step equations, and situations where the algebraic setup is unclear or time-consuming.

1. When to Work Backwards

Identifying Appropriate Problems

Working backwards is most effective when:

The problem asks "which value" or "what number": Questions that ask you to find a specific numerical value are perfect candidates for testing answer choices.

Example: "When 5 is added to twice a number, the result is 23. What is the number?"

Instead of solving 2x + 5 = 23 algebraically, you can test each answer choice by substituting it for "the number."

Answer choices are simple numbers: When answer choices are concrete values (not variables or expressions), you can easily test each one.

The algebraic setup is complex or unclear: Some word problems involve multiple steps or relationships that are easier to check than to set up formally.

Testing is faster than solving: For some problems, plugging in numbers and checking is quicker than working through the algebra, especially under time pressure.

When NOT to Work Backwards

This strategy is less effective when:

  • Answer choices are variables or expressions: You can't substitute expressions easily, so algebraic solving is necessary
  • The problem is simple and direct: Basic one-step or two-step problems are faster to solve conventionally
  • Testing would be very time-consuming: If checking each answer involves extensive calculation, direct solving may be more efficient
  • The question asks "all of the following EXCEPT": You'd need to test all five choices rather than stopping when you find one that works

Strategic Answer Choice Selection

When working backwards, you don't always need to test all five answer choices. Use this strategic approach:

Start with choice C (the middle value): Answer choices are typically arranged in numerical order. If you test C and it's too large or too small, you can eliminate C and either A & B or D & E based on whether you need a bigger or smaller value.

Example: Answer choices are A. 3 B. 5 C. 7 D. 9 E. 11

Test C (7) first. If 7 is too small, eliminate A, B, and C—the answer must be D or E. Now you only need to test one more choice to find the answer.

Test easy-to-calculate values first: If one answer choice is particularly simple (like 0, 1, or 10), it might be worth testing first for quick elimination.

Stop when you find one that works: Once an answer choice satisfies all conditions in the problem, that's your answer—no need to test the remaining choices.

2. Step-by-Step Process

Reading the Problem Carefully

Before testing answer choices, ensure you understand what the problem requires:

  • Identify what you're solving for: What does the question ask? A number, a measurement, a price?
  • Note all conditions: List every constraint or relationship the correct answer must satisfy
  • Understand the operations: What happens to the value in the problem? Is it doubled, increased, multiplied?
  • Check units: Are answers in dollars, hours, feet? Ensure you're testing correctly

Testing an Answer Choice

Follow this systematic process when testing each choice:

Step 1: Assume the answer choice is correct. Substitute the value everywhere the unknown appears in the problem.

Step 2: Work through the problem step-by-step. Follow the sequence of operations described, calculating what would happen if this value were the answer.

Step 3: Check the final result. Does your calculation match what the problem says should happen? Does it satisfy all conditions?

Step 4: Accept or eliminate. If all conditions are satisfied, you've found the answer. If not, eliminate this choice and test another.

Complete example:

Problem: Sarah has some money. If she spends $15 and then triples what remains, she'll have $60. How much money did Sarah start with?

A. $20
B. $25
C. $30
D. $35
E. $40

Testing choice C ($30):
Step 1: Assume Sarah starts with $30
Step 2: She spends $15: $30 - $15 = $15 remains
Step 3: She triples what remains: $15 × 3 = $45
Step 4: Check: Does she have $60? No, she has $45. ❌ Eliminate C.

$45 is less than the required $60, so we need Sarah to start with more money. Eliminate A, B, and C.

Testing choice D ($35):
Step 1: Assume Sarah starts with $35
Step 2: She spends $15: $35 - $15 = $20 remains
Step 3: She triples what remains: $20 × 3 = $60
Step 4: Check: Does she have $60? Yes! ✓

Answer: D

Organizing Your Work

When working backwards, stay organized to avoid errors:

  • Write down what you're testing: Note "Testing C: 7" so you don't lose track
  • Show each calculation step: Write out the arithmetic for each operation in sequence
  • Check all conditions: Some problems have multiple requirements—verify each one
  • Mark eliminated choices: Cross out choices you've ruled out to avoid re-testing them

This systematic approach prevents calculation errors and helps you work efficiently.

3. Common Problem Types

Age Problems

Age problems describe relationships between people's ages and are ideal for working backwards:

Problem: Marcus is 3 years older than twice his sister's age. If Marcus is 19, how old is his sister?

F. 7
G. 8
H. 9
J. 10
K. 11

Testing H (9):
- Sister's age: 9
- Twice sister's age: 9 × 2 = 18
- 3 years older than that: 18 + 3 = 21
- Check: Is Marcus 19? No, we got 21. ❌

21 > 19, so the sister must be younger than 9. Eliminate H, J, K.

Testing G (8):
- Sister's age: 8
- Twice sister's age: 8 × 2 = 16
- 3 years older than that: 16 + 3 = 19
- Check: Is Marcus 19? Yes! ✓

Answer: G

Consecutive Integer Problems

Problems about consecutive integers (like n, n+1, n+2) or consecutive even/odd integers work well with this strategy:

Problem: The sum of three consecutive even integers is 84. What is the smallest of these integers?

A. 26
B. 27
C. 28
D. 29
E. 30

Testing C (28):
- First integer: 28
- Consecutive even integers: 28, 30, 32
- Sum: 28 + 30 + 32 = 90
- Check: Is the sum 84? No, we got 90. ❌

90 > 84, so we need smaller integers. Eliminate C, D, E.

Testing A (26):
- First integer: 26
- Consecutive even integers: 26, 28, 30
- Sum: 26 + 28 + 30 = 84
- Check: Is the sum 84? Yes! ✓

Answer: A

Number Property Problems

Problems describing relationships between a number and its operations (doubling, squaring, adding) are perfect for testing:

Problem: When a number is decreased by 8 and the result is multiplied by 4, the final answer is 28. What is the original number?

F. 9
G. 11
H. 13
J. 15
K. 17

Testing H (13):
- Original number: 13
- Decreased by 8: 13 - 8 = 5
- Multiplied by 4: 5 × 4 = 20
- Check: Is the result 28? No, we got 20. ❌

20 < 28, so we need a larger original number. Eliminate F, G, H.

Testing J (15):
- Original number: 15
- Decreased by 8: 15 - 8 = 7
- Multiplied by 4: 7 × 4 = 28
- Check: Is the result 28? Yes! ✓

Answer: J

Percent and Money Problems

Problems involving discounts, tips, taxes, or percent changes often work well with this approach:

Problem: After a 20% discount, a jacket costs $64. What was the original price?

A. $76.80
B. $80.00
C. $84.00
D. $88.00
E. $92.00

Testing B ($80):
- Original price: $80
- 20% discount: $80 × 0.20 = $16
- Sale price: $80 - $16 = $64
- Check: Is the sale price $64? Yes! ✓

Answer: B

4. Advanced Strategies and Tips

Combining Working Backwards with Estimation

You can often narrow down answer choices before testing by estimating:

Problem: A number is multiplied by 7, then 5 is added. The result is 103. What is the number?

Estimation: The result (103) is close to 105. If we subtract 5, we get 98. What times 7 equals 98? Since 7 × 14 = 98, the answer should be around 14.

Look for answer choices near 14 and test those first, rather than testing from the middle.

When You Have Multiple Conditions

Some problems require the answer to satisfy two or more separate conditions:

Problem: A number is divisible by both 4 and 6, and it's between 20 and 40. What could the number be?

Strategy: Test each answer choice against BOTH conditions:
1. Is it divisible by 4? (Check: does it divide evenly by 4?)
2. Is it divisible by 6? (Check: does it divide evenly by 6?)
3. Is it between 20 and 40?

The correct answer must satisfy all three conditions.

Check the easiest condition first to eliminate choices quickly, then verify remaining conditions.

Avoiding Common Mistakes

When working backwards, watch out for these errors:

Mistake 1: Following the wrong order of operations. Carefully track the sequence described in the problem. "5 less than twice a number" means 2n - 5, not 5 - 2n.

Mistake 2: Stopping too soon. Ensure you've completed all steps before checking the final result. If the problem describes three operations, perform all three.

Mistake 3: Misreading what you're solving for. Double-check that the question asks for the value you're testing. If it asks for "Sarah's age in 5 years," don't test her current age.

Mistake 4: Arithmetic errors. Work carefully through calculations. A small arithmetic mistake can lead you to eliminate the correct answer.

Time Management

Working backwards should save time, not cost it:

  • Set a time limit: If testing the first two choices takes longer than expected, consider switching to algebraic solving
  • Use strategic choice selection: Starting with the middle value and eliminating half the choices makes this strategy more efficient
  • Don't overthink: If a choice clearly doesn't work, move on quickly rather than double-checking
  • Practice mental math: The faster you can calculate in your head, the more efficient this strategy becomes

When to Switch Strategies

Be flexible. If working backwards isn't efficient for a particular problem, switch to algebraic solving:

  • If testing two choices takes more than 30 seconds total, the algebra might be faster
  • If the calculations when testing are complex or error-prone, set up an equation instead
  • If you realize you need to test all five choices (rare), algebraic solving will be faster

The goal is to solve problems correctly and efficiently—use whichever method best accomplishes that for each specific question.

Final Example (Multi-step problem):

Problem: A store increases a shirt's price by 25%, then offers a 20% discount on the new price. If the final price is $30, what was the original price?

A. $25
B. $30
C. $32
D. $35
E. $40

Testing C ($32):
- Original price: $32
- Increase by 25%: $32 × 1.25 = $40
- 20% discount on $40: $40 × 0.80 = $32
- Check: Is the final price $30? No, we got $32. ❌

$32 > $30, so we need a smaller original price. Eliminate C, D, E.

Testing A ($25):
- Original price: $25
- Increase by 25%: $25 × 1.25 = $31.25
- 20% discount on $31.25: $31.25 × 0.80 = $25
- Check: Is the final price $30? No, we got $25. ❌

Testing B ($30):
- Original price: $30
- Increase by 25%: $30 × 1.25 = $37.50
- 20% discount on $37.50: $37.50 × 0.80 = $30
- Check: Is the final price $30? Yes! ✓

Answer: B
