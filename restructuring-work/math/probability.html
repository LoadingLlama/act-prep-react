Understanding probability is essential for success on the ACT Math section. This lesson covers basic probability, compound events, conditional probability, independent events, and expected value. Probability questions appear in 3-5 problems per test, making them a significant component of your math score. You'll learn to calculate probabilities for simple and complex scenarios, understand how events relate to each other, and predict long-term outcomes using expected value calculations.

1. Basic Probability Concepts

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring, expressed as a number between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%). The sample space includes all possible outcomes, while an event is a specific outcome or set of outcomes. Understanding the fundamental probability formula and how to count favorable and total outcomes is the foundation for all probability problems.

The Basic Probability Formula

Formula: P(Event) = (Number of favorable outcomes) / (Total number of possible outcomes)

Properties:
• 0 ≤ P(Event) ≤ 1
• P(impossible event) = 0
• P(certain event) = 1
• P(not A) = 1 - P(A)

Example: What is the probability of rolling a 4 on a standard die?

Favorable outcomes: 1 (only the face showing 4)
Total outcomes: 6 (faces showing 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6)
Probability: P(4) = 1/6 ≈ 0.167 or about 16.7%

Probability with Cards, Coins, and Dice

Standard deck of cards:
• 52 total cards
• 4 suits (hearts, diamonds, clubs, spades)
• 13 ranks per suit (A, 2-10, J, Q, K)
• 26 red cards, 26 black cards

Example: Probability of drawing a heart?
P(heart) = 13/52 = 1/4 = 0.25 or 25%

Coin flips:
P(heads) = 1/2
P(tails) = 1/2

Standard die:
P(any specific number) = 1/6
P(even number) = 3/6 = 1/2
P(number > 4) = 2/6 = 1/3

Complementary Events

The complement of an event A (written as A' or "not A") includes all outcomes that are NOT in A.

Formula: P(A') = 1 - P(A)

Example: If P(rain tomorrow) = 0.3, what is P(no rain tomorrow)?

P(no rain) = 1 - P(rain) = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7 or 70%

Why this is useful: Sometimes it's easier to calculate the probability something doesn't happen and subtract from 1.

Probability with Replacement vs. Without Replacement

With replacement: Item is returned before next selection (outcomes independent)
Without replacement: Item is not returned (outcomes dependent)

Example: Bag contains 3 red and 2 blue marbles

With replacement:
P(red on first draw) = 3/5
P(red on second draw) = 3/5 (same, because marble was replaced)

Without replacement:
P(red on first draw) = 3/5
P(red on second draw given red on first) = 2/4 = 1/2 (one red marble is gone)

ACT Example 1: Basic Probability

A bag contains 5 red marbles, 3 blue marbles, and 7 green marbles. If one marble is selected at random, what is the probability it is NOT green?

  • A) 3/15
  • B) 5/15
  • C) 7/15
  • D) 8/15
  • E) 12/15
  • Solution:
  • Method 1: Count non-green marbles directly
  • Non-green = red + blue = 5 + 3 = 8
  • Total = 5 + 3 + 7 = 15
  • P(not green) = 8/15
  • Method 2: Use complement
  • P(green) = 7/15, so P(not green) = 1 - 7/15 = 8/15
  • Answer: D — Both methods work equally well!

2. Compound Events and the Multiplication Rule

Compound events involve two or more simple events occurring together. For independent events (where one outcome doesn't affect another), we multiply probabilities. For dependent events (where one outcome affects another), we adjust probabilities as we go. Understanding when to multiply versus when to add probabilities is crucial for solving complex probability problems.

Multiplication Rule for Independent Events

If events A and B are independent: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

Example: What is the probability of flipping heads twice in a row?

First flip: P(heads) = 1/2
Second flip: P(heads) = 1/2
Both heads: P(H and H) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4 = 0.25 or 25%

The flips are independent because the first flip doesn't affect the second.

Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events

For dependent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B given A happened)

Example: A box has 4 red and 6 blue balls. Two balls are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are red?

First draw: P(red) = 4/10 = 2/5
Second draw: P(red | first was red) = 3/9 = 1/3
(Only 3 red balls remain out of 9 total)

Both red: P(R and R) = 2/5 × 1/3 = 2/15 ≈ 0.133 or about 13.3%

Addition Rule for Mutually Exclusive Events

Mutually exclusive events cannot happen at the same time. For these events:
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)

Example: What is the probability of rolling a 2 or a 5 on a die?

P(2) = 1/6
P(5) = 1/6
P(2 or 5) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3

These are mutually exclusive because you can't roll both 2 and 5 on a single die roll.

Addition Rule for Non-Mutually Exclusive Events

When events can overlap: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
We subtract P(A and B) to avoid counting the overlap twice.

Example: In a class of 30 students, 18 play soccer, 12 play basketball, and 6 play both. What is the probability a randomly selected student plays soccer or basketball?

P(soccer) = 18/30
P(basketball) = 12/30
P(both) = 6/30

P(soccer or basketball) = 18/30 + 12/30 - 6/30 = 24/30 = 4/5 = 0.8 or 80%

ACT Example 2: Compound Probability

A coin is flipped three times. What is the probability of getting exactly two heads?

  • F) 1/8
  • G) 1/4
  • H) 3/8
  • J) 1/2
  • K) 5/8
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: List all possible outcomes with exactly 2 heads
  • HHT, HTH, THH (3 outcomes)
  • Step 2: Each sequence has probability (1/2)³ = 1/8
  • Step 3: Total probability = 3 × (1/8) = 3/8
  • Answer: H — Count arrangements, multiply by each probability!

3. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability measures the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. Written as P(A|B) (read as "probability of A given B"), it represents how our knowledge of one event changes the probability of another. This concept is crucial for understanding dependent events and making predictions based on partial information. Two-way tables are often used to organize conditional probability problems.

Conditional Probability Formula

Formula: P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)

This tells us: "What fraction of times that B occurs does A also occur?"

Example: Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability the second card is a heart given that the first card was a heart?

After first heart is drawn:
• 12 hearts remain (out of 13 original)
• 51 total cards remain (out of 52 original)

P(second is heart | first is heart) = 12/51 = 4/17

Using Two-Way Tables

Example: Survey of 100 students

                Own Car   No Car   Total
Junior       25          15        40
Senior       45          15        60
Total        70          30       100

Question: What is P(owns car | senior)?

Solution: Look only at the senior row
45 seniors own cars out of 60 total seniors
P(owns car | senior) = 45/60 = 3/4 = 0.75 or 75%

Testing for Independence

Events A and B are independent if: P(A|B) = P(A)
In other words, knowing B occurred doesn't change the probability of A.

Example: Are flipping a coin and rolling a die independent?

P(heads) = 1/2
P(heads | rolled a 6) = 1/2

Since these are equal, the events are independent—rolling a die doesn't affect the coin flip.

Counterexample: Drawing cards without replacement
P(second card is ace) = 4/52
P(second is ace | first was ace) = 3/51

Since these differ, the events are dependent.

Conditional Probability in Context

Example: Medical testing

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% accurate for those with the disease and 90% accurate for those without it. If someone tests positive, what's the probability they actually have the disease?

This is asking: P(disease | positive test)

Setup: Out of 10,000 people:
• 100 have disease (1%)
• Of 100 with disease: 95 test positive (95% accuracy)
• 9,900 don't have disease (99%)
• Of 9,900 without disease: 990 test positive (10% false positive rate)

Total positive tests: 95 + 990 = 1,085
True positives: 95

P(disease | positive) = 95/1,085 ≈ 8.8%

This surprisingly low probability shows the importance of base rates!

ACT Example 3: Conditional Probability

In a class, 60% of students are girls. Of the girls, 40% play sports. Of the boys, 70% play sports. If a randomly selected student plays sports, what is the probability the student is a girl?

  • A) 24%
  • B) 40%
  • C) 46%
  • D) 52%
  • E) 60%
  • Solution:
  • Assume 100 students: 60 girls, 40 boys
  • Girls who play sports: 60 × 0.40 = 24
  • Boys who play sports: 40 × 0.70 = 28
  • Total athletes: 24 + 28 = 52
  • P(girl | plays sports) = 24/52 ≈ 46%
  • Answer: C — Use concrete numbers to organize the information!

4. Expected Value and Long-Run Probabilities

Expected value represents the average outcome you'd expect over many trials. It's calculated by multiplying each possible outcome by its probability and summing these products. Expected value is crucial for making decisions involving risk and reward, analyzing games of chance, and predicting long-term results. Understanding expected value helps you determine whether a probabilistic situation is favorable or unfavorable over time.

Calculating Expected Value

Formula: E(X) = Σ [x × P(x)]
Sum of (each outcome × its probability)

Example: You roll a die. You win $10 if you roll a 6, otherwise you win $0. What is the expected value?

Outcomes:
• Win $10 with probability 1/6
• Win $0 with probability 5/6

E(winnings) = 10 × (1/6) + 0 × (5/6) = 10/6 ≈ $1.67

Interpretation: On average, you'd expect to win about $1.67 per roll over many games.

Expected Value in Games and Gambling

Example: A raffle sells 100 tickets at $5 each. First prize is $300, second prize is $100. What is the expected value of buying one ticket?

Outcomes:
• Win $300 with probability 1/100 (net gain: $300 - $5 = $295)
• Win $100 with probability 1/100 (net gain: $100 - $5 = $95)
• Win $0 with probability 98/100 (net loss: -$5)

E(net gain) = 295 × (1/100) + 95 × (1/100) + (-5) × (98/100)
E(net gain) = 2.95 + 0.95 - 4.90 = -$1.00

Interpretation: On average, you lose $1 per ticket. This is not a favorable game!

Fair Games vs. Unfair Games

Fair game: Expected value = 0 (break even on average)
Favorable game: Expected value > 0 (gain on average)
Unfavorable game: Expected value < 0 (lose on average)

Example of fair game:
Flip a coin. Heads you win $10, tails you lose $10.
E(winnings) = 10 × (1/2) + (-10) × (1/2) = 5 - 5 = $0

To make this favorable: You'd need to win more than $10 on heads, or have better than 50% chance of winning.

Expected Value with Multiple Events

Example: You flip a coin 100 times. What is the expected number of heads?

For each flip: E(heads) = 1 × (1/2) + 0 × (1/2) = 0.5
For 100 flips: E(total heads) = 100 × 0.5 = 50 heads

Property: E(aX + b) = aE(X) + b
This allows us to scale and shift expected values easily.

Decision Making with Expected Value

Example: You can choose between two options:
Option A: Guaranteed $50
Option B: 40% chance of $100, 60% chance of $20

Expected value of A: $50 (certain)
Expected value of B: 100 × 0.4 + 20 × 0.6 = 40 + 12 = $52

Conclusion: Option B has slightly higher expected value, but Option A has no risk. The choice depends on your risk tolerance!

ACT Example 4: Expected Value

A spinner has 3 equal sections: one showing 5, one showing 10, and one showing 20. What is the expected value of one spin?

  • F) 10
  • G) 11
  • H) 11⅔
  • J) 12
  • K) 15
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Each section has probability 1/3
  • Step 2: E(spin) = 5×(1/3) + 10×(1/3) + 20×(1/3)
  • Step 3: E(spin) = (5 + 10 + 20)/3 = 35/3 = 11⅔
  • Answer: H — Multiply each outcome by its probability!

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ ✓Basic probability = (favorable outcomes) / (total outcomes), always between 0 and 1
  • ✓ ✓Complement rule: P(not A) = 1 - P(A), useful when it's easier to calculate what doesn't happen
  • ✓ ✓Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B); outcome of one doesn't affect the other
  • ✓ ✓Dependent events: adjust probabilities after each event (drawing without replacement)
  • ✓ ✓Mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B); can't happen simultaneously
  • ✓ ✓Overlapping events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B) to avoid double-counting
  • ✓ ✓Conditional probability P(A|B) asks "what's the probability of A in the world where B happened?"
  • ✓ ✓Two-way tables help organize conditional probability; focus on the relevant row or column
  • ✓ ✓Expected value = Σ(outcome × probability); represents long-run average
  • ✓ ✓Fair game has E(V) = 0; favorable has E(V) > 0; unfavorable has E(V) < 0
