Circle Equations on the Coordinate Plane

A circle is all points that are the same distance (radius) from a center point. On the coordinate plane, we can write this as an equation!

Standard Form of a Circle

(x − h)² + (y − k)² = r²

  • (h, k) = center of the circle
  • r = radius of the circle
  • r² = radius squared (what appears on the right side)
  • 💡 Key Pattern:
  • The signs in the equation tell you the center! If you see (x − 3)², the center x-coordinate is +3. If you see (x + 2)², the center x-coordinate is −2. The signs flip!

Example 1: Finding Center and Radius

Problem: What is the center and radius of the circle (x − 5)² + (y + 2)² = 16?

Solution:
Compare to (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r²

(x − 5)² means h = 5 ✓
(y + 2)² means (y − (−2))², so k = −2 ✓
r² = 16, so r = 4 ✓

Center: (5, −2)
Radius: 4

Example 2: Writing Circle Equation

Problem: Write the equation of a circle with center (−3, 1) and radius 7.

Solution:
Use (x − h)² + (y − k)² = r²
h = −3, k = 1, r = 7

(x − (−3))² + (y − 1)² = 7²
(x + 3)² + (y − 1)² = 49

Completing the Square

Sometimes circle equations aren't in standard form. You'll need to complete the square to find the center and radius.

📝 Steps to Complete the Square:

  1. Group x terms and y terms separately
  2. Take half of the x coefficient, square it, add to both sides
  3. Take half of the y coefficient, square it, add to both sides
  4. Factor each perfect square

Example 3: Completing the Square

Problem: Convert x² + y² − 4x + 6y + 9 = 0 to standard form.

Solution:
Step 1: Group x and y terms
(x² − 4x) + (y² + 6y) = −9

Step 2: Complete the square for x
Half of −4 is −2, square it: (−2)² = 4
(x² − 4x + 4) + (y² + 6y) = −9 + 4

Step 3: Complete the square for y
Half of 6 is 3, square it: 3² = 9
(x² − 4x + 4) + (y² + 6y + 9) = −9 + 4 + 9

Step 4: Factor
(x − 2)² + (y + 3)² = 4

Center: (2, −3), Radius: 2

Ellipse Basics

An ellipse is a stretched circle. It has two axes: a longer one (major axis) and a shorter one (minor axis).

Standard Form of an Ellipse

(x − h)²/a² + (y − k)²/b² = 1

  • (h, k) = center of the ellipse
  • a = semi-major axis (longer radius)
  • b = semi-minor axis (shorter radius)

Example 4: Ellipse Center and Axes

Problem: What is the center of the ellipse (x − 1)²/25 + (y + 2)²/9 = 1?

Solution:
Compare to (x − h)²/a² + (y − k)²/b² = 1

(x − 1)² means h = 1
(y + 2)² means k = −2

Center: (1, −2)
Note: a² = 25 so a = 5, b² = 9 so b = 3

Hyperbola Basics (Brief Overview)

A hyperbola has two separate curves that open outward. The equation looks similar to an ellipse, but with a minus sign.

Standard Form of a Hyperbola

(x − h)²/a² − (y − k)²/b² = 1 (opens left-right)
or
(y − k)²/a² − (x − h)²/b² = 1 (opens up-down)

  • 🔑 Quick Recognition:
  • Circle: x² + y² (both positive, same coefficients)
  • Ellipse: x²/a² + y²/b² = 1 (both positive, different denominators)
  • Hyperbola: x²/a² − y²/b² = 1 (one is negative!)

Ellipse (x−h)²/a² + (y−k)²/b² = 1 (h, k)
