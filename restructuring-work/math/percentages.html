Percentages represent parts of a whole as fractions of 100. On the ACT, you'll encounter percent increase and decrease, percent word problems, and multi-step percent calculations, requiring you to convert between forms, calculate changes, and solve real-world percentage problems.

1. Understanding Percentages

Converting Between Forms

Percent means "per 100": 45% = 45/100 = 0.45

To convert percent to decimal: Divide by 100 (move decimal left 2 places)

To convert decimal to percent: Multiply by 100 (move decimal right 2 places)

Examples:

75% = 75/100 = 0.75 = 3/4
0.08 = 8/100 = 8%
1.5 = 150/100 = 150%
0.005 = 0.5/100 = 0.5%
3/8 = 0.375 = 37.5%

The Basic Percent Equation

Part = Percent × Whole or is = % × of

Practice Problem: What is 30% of 80? Part = 0.30 × 80 = 24 Example 2: 18 is what percent of 72? 18 = x × 72 x = 18/72 = 0.25 = 25% Example 3: 15 is 25% of what number? 15 = 0.25 × x x = 15/0.25 = 60

2. Percent Increase and Decrease

Calculating Percent Change

Percent Change = (New - Original)/Original × 100%

  • If New > Original, it's an increase (positive result)
  • If New < Original, it's a decrease (negative result)
  • Always divide by the original value, not the new value

Practice Problem: A price increases from $40 to $50. What is the percent increase? Percent change = (50 - 40)/40 × 100% = 10/40 × 100% = 0.25 × 100% = 25% increase Example 2: A population decreases from 8,000 to 6,400. What is the percent decrease? Percent change = (6400 - 8000)/8000 × 100% = -1600/8000 × 100% = -0.20 × 100% = 20% decrease

Applying Percent Increase/Decrease

To increase by r%: Multiply by (1 + r)

To decrease by r%: Multiply by (1 - r)

Practice Problem: Increase $200 by 15% New amount = 200 × (1 + 0.15) = 200 × 1.15 = $230 Or: 15% of 200 = 30, so 200 + 30 = $230 Example 2: Decrease 80 by 35% New amount = 80 × (1 - 0.35) = 80 × 0.65 = 52 Or: 35% of 80 = 28, so 80 - 28 = 52

Working Backwards from New Value

Example: After a 20% increase, a price is $72. What was the original price?

Let: x = original price
After 20% increase: x × 1.20 = 72
x = 72/1.20
x = 60

Original price: $60
Check: 60 × 1.20 = 72 ✓

3. Multi-Step Percent Problems

Successive Percent Changes

Important: Successive percent changes don't add! You must apply each change separately.

Example: A price increases by 20%, then increases by another 10%. What is the overall percent change?

Start with $100:
After 20% increase: 100 × 1.20 = 120
After 10% increase: 120 × 1.10 = 132

Overall change: (132 - 100)/100 = 32%

Note: NOT 20% + 10% = 30%!

General formula: 100 × 1.20 × 1.10 = 100 × 1.32 = 132
Overall increase: 32%

Increase Then Decrease

Example: A price increases by 25%, then decreases by 20%. If the final price is $60, what was the original?

Let: x = original price
After 25% increase: x × 1.25
After 20% decrease: x × 1.25 × 0.80 = 60

Simplify:
x × 1.00 = 60
x = 60

Interesting: The two changes exactly cancel out!
Check: 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00

Discount and Tax Problems

Example: A $150 jacket is on sale for 30% off. With 8% sales tax, what is the final price?

After discount:
150 × (1 - 0.30) = 150 × 0.70 = $105

After tax:
105 × (1 + 0.08) = 105 × 1.08 = $113.40

Or in one step:
150 × 0.70 × 1.08 = $113.40

4. Percent Word Problems

Finding the Whole

Example: In a class, 12 students (30% of the class) are left-handed. How many students are in the class?

Let: x = total students
0.30 × x = 12
x = 12/0.30
x = 40 students

Finding the Percent

Example: Sarah answered 42 out of 50 questions correctly. What percent did she get right?

Percent = 42/50 = 0.84 = 84%

Percent Greater/Less Than

Example: John's salary is $48,000. Maria's salary is $60,000. Maria's salary is what percent greater than John's?

Difference = 60,000 - 48,000 = 12,000
Percent greater = 12,000/48,000 = 0.25 = 25%

Important: Divide by John's salary (the base), not Maria's!

5. Special Percent Concepts

Percents Greater Than 100%

Example: A stock price tripled from $20 to $60. What is the percent increase?

Percent change = (60 - 20)/20 × 100%
= 40/20 × 100%
= 200%

Makes sense: Tripling is an increase of 200% (original + 200% of original = 300% of original)

Percent of a Percent

Example: 60% of students passed the exam. Of those who passed, 75% got an A. What percent of all students got an A?

Percent with A = 0.60 × 0.75 = 0.45 = 45%

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Using wrong base. "20% more than 50" means 50 × 1.20 = 60, not 20% of some other number.

Mistake 2: Adding successive percents. 20% increase then 30% increase ≠ 50% increase!

Mistake 3: Forgetting to convert. Remember: use 0.15, not 15, when multiplying.

Mistake 4: Dividing by new instead of original. Percent change always divides by the original value.

ACT-Specific Tips

Tip 1: Use 100 as a starting value to test percent changes—it makes calculations easier.

Tip 2: Remember common percent-decimal-fraction conversions: 50% = 0.5 = 1/2, 25% = 0.25 = 1/4.

Tip 3: For "what percent of A is B," always calculate B/A × 100%.

Tip 4: If answers are far apart, estimate using nice percentages like 10%, 25%, or 50%.
