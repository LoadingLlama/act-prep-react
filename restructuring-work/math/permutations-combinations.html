Mastering counting techniques is crucial for solving complex ACT probability and arrangement problems. This lesson covers the fundamental counting principle, permutations (ordered arrangements), combinations (unordered selections), and factorial notation. Understanding when order matters versus when it doesn't is the key to choosing the right counting method. These concepts typically appear in 2-3 questions per ACT test and are essential for students targeting scores above 30.

1. Fundamental Counting Principle and Factorials

The fundamental counting principle states that if one event can occur in m ways and another independent event can occur in n ways, then both events together can occur in m × n ways. This principle extends to any number of events and forms the foundation for more complex counting. Factorial notation (n!) represents the product of all positive integers from 1 to n and is essential for calculating permutations and combinations.

The Fundamental Counting Principle

If Event 1 can occur in m ways, Event 2 in n ways, Event 3 in p ways, then all events together can occur in m × n × p ways.

Example: A restaurant offers 4 appetizers, 6 main courses, and 3 desserts. How many different three-course meals are possible?

Solution:
Choices for appetizer: 4
Choices for main course: 6
Choices for dessert: 3

Total meals = 4 × 6 × 3 = 72 different meals

Understanding Factorials

Factorial notation: n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 3 × 2 × 1

Special cases:
0! = 1 (by definition)
1! = 1

Examples of factorials:

3! = 3 × 2 × 1 = 6
4! = 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24
5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120
6! = 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 720

Useful property: n! = n × (n-1)!
Example: 7! = 7 × 6! = 7 × 720 = 5,040

Simplifying Factorial Expressions

Example: Simplify 8! / 6!

Method 1: Cancel common factors
8! / 6! = (8 × 7 × 6!) / 6! = 8 × 7 = 56

Method 2: Expand and cancel
8! = 8 × 7 × 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1
6! = 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1
8! / 6! = (8 × 7 × 6!)/ 6! = 8 × 7 = 56

Key insight: When dividing factorials, cancel the smaller factorial from the larger one.

Counting with Restrictions

Example: How many 3-digit numbers can be formed using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 if no digit can be repeated?

First digit: 5 choices (any of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5)
Second digit: 4 choices (any except the first digit used)
Third digit: 3 choices (any except the two digits already used)

Total = 5 × 4 × 3 = 60 different 3-digit numbers

ACT Example 1: Fundamental Counting Principle

A password must contain 2 letters followed by 3 digits. Letters can be any of the 26 letters (A-Z) and can repeat. Digits can be 0-9 and can repeat. How many different passwords are possible?

  • A) 260
  • B) 2,600
  • C) 67,600
  • D) 676,000
  • E) 6,760,000
  • Solution:
  • First letter: 26 choices
  • Second letter: 26 choices (can repeat)
  • First digit: 10 choices (0-9)
  • Second digit: 10 choices
  • Third digit: 10 choices
  • Total = 26 × 26 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 676,000
  • Answer: D — Multiply choices for each position!

2. Permutations (Order Matters)

A permutation is an arrangement of objects where order matters. The key question is: "In how many ways can we arrange these objects?" Different orders count as different permutations. For example, ABC and BAC are different permutations of the same three letters. The formula nPr calculates the number of ways to arrange r objects selected from n total objects.

Permutations of All Objects

Formula: The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects is n!

Example: In how many ways can 5 people stand in a line?

First position: 5 choices
Second position: 4 choices
Third position: 3 choices
Fourth position: 2 choices
Fifth position: 1 choice

Total arrangements = 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120 ways

Permutations of Some Objects (nPr)

Formula: nPr = n! / (n-r)!
This gives the number of ways to arrange r objects chosen from n total objects.

Example: How many ways can a president, vice president, and secretary be chosen from 8 candidates?

We're choosing 3 people from 8, and order matters (president ≠ vice president).

Using formula:
₈P₃ = 8! / (8-3)! = 8! / 5!
= (8 × 7 × 6 × 5!) / 5!
= 8 × 7 × 6
= 336 ways

Using counting principle:
President: 8 choices
Vice president: 7 choices (one person already chosen)
Secretary: 6 choices (two people already chosen)
Total = 8 × 7 × 6 = 336 ways

Circular Permutations

For arranging n objects in a circle: (n-1)!

We use (n-1)! instead of n! because rotations of the same arrangement are considered identical in a circle.

Example: In how many ways can 6 people sit around a circular table?

Solution: (6-1)! = 5! = 120 ways

Why not 6!? Because rotating everyone one seat clockwise produces the same circular arrangement.

Permutations with Repetition

When objects repeat: n! / (n₁! × n₂! × ... × nₖ!)
where n₁, n₂, ..., nₖ are the frequencies of each repeated object

Example: How many distinct arrangements of the letters in MISSISSIPPI?

Count letters:
M: 1, I: 4, S: 4, P: 2
Total letters: 11

Formula:
11! / (1! × 4! × 4! × 2!)
= 39,916,800 / (1 × 24 × 24 × 2)
= 39,916,800 / 1,152
= 34,650 distinct arrangements

ACT Example 2: Permutations

A bookshelf has space for 4 books. If you have 7 different books to choose from, in how many ways can you arrange 4 books on the shelf?

  • F) 28
  • G) 35
  • H) 210
  • J) 840
  • K) 5,040
  • Solution:
  • Order matters (different arrangements on shelf), so use permutations
  • ₇P₄ = 7! / (7-4)! = 7! / 3!
  • = (7 × 6 × 5 × 4 × 3!) / 3!
  • = 7 × 6 × 5 × 4 = 840
  • Answer: J — Order matters, so permutation not combination!

3. Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter)

A combination is a selection of objects where order does NOT matter. The key question is: "In how many ways can we select these objects?" Different selections count as different combinations, but different orders of the same selection do not. For example, selecting {A, B, C} is the same combination as {C, A, B}. The formula nCr (also written as "n choose r") calculates the number of ways to select r objects from n total objects.

Combinations Formula

Formula: nCr = n! / [r! × (n-r)!]

This gives the number of ways to choose r objects from n total objects when order doesn't matter.

Example: How many ways can you choose 3 students from a class of 10 to form a committee?

Order doesn't matter (choosing Alice, Bob, Carlos is the same as Carlos, Alice, Bob).

₁₀C₃ = 10! / [3! × (10-3)!]
= 10! / (3! × 7!)
= (10 × 9 × 8 × 7!) / (3! × 7!)
= (10 × 9 × 8) / (3 × 2 × 1)
= 720 / 6
= 120 ways

Permutations vs. Combinations

Key difference: nPr counts ordered arrangements; nCr counts unordered selections

Relationship: nPr = nCr × r!
(Each combination can be arranged in r! ways)

Example with 3 items from {A, B, C, D, E}:

Permutations ₅P₃: ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA, ABD, ADB, ... = 60 total
(All different orderings count)

Combinations ₅C₃: {ABC}, {ABD}, {ABE}, {ACD}, ... = 10 total
(ABC and ACB count as same selection)

Check: ₅P₃ = 60 = ₅C₃ × 3! = 10 × 6 ✓

Complementary Counting

Sometimes it's easier to count what you DON'T want, then subtract from the total.

Example: How many ways can you choose at least 2 people from a group of 5?

Direct method: ₅C₂ + ₅C₃ + ₅C₄ + ₅C₅ = 10 + 10 + 5 + 1 = 26

Complementary method:
Total ways to choose any number: 2⁵ = 32 (each person in or out)
Subtract choosing 0 people: ₅C₀ = 1
Subtract choosing 1 person: ₅C₁ = 5
Result: 32 - 1 - 5 = 26 ✓

Combinations with Multiple Groups

Example: A committee of 5 people must include 3 women and 2 men. There are 8 women and 6 men available. How many different committees can be formed?

Step 1: Choose 3 women from 8
₈C₃ = 8! / (3! × 5!) = (8 × 7 × 6) / (3 × 2 × 1) = 336 / 6 = 56

Step 2: Choose 2 men from 6
₆C₂ = 6! / (2! × 4!) = (6 × 5) / (2 × 1) = 30 / 2 = 15

Step 3: Multiply (each women selection can pair with each men selection)
Total committees = 56 × 15 = 840

ACT Example 3: Combinations

A pizza shop offers 8 different toppings. If you want to order a pizza with exactly 3 toppings, how many different pizzas can you order?

  • A) 24
  • B) 56
  • C) 120
  • D) 336
  • E) 512
  • Solution:
  • Order doesn't matter (pepperoni + mushroom + olive = olive + pepperoni + mushroom)
  • Use combinations: ₈C₃ = 8! / (3! × 5!)
  • = (8 × 7 × 6) / (3 × 2 × 1)
  • = 336 / 6 = 56
  • Answer: B — Order doesn't matter, so combination not permutation!

4. Advanced Counting Problems

Advanced counting problems often combine multiple techniques and require careful analysis to determine which method to use. You'll encounter problems with restrictions (certain objects must be together or apart), multiple stages (combining permutations and combinations), and casework (breaking into separate cases). The key is identifying whether order matters, what restrictions apply, and whether to count directly or use complementary counting.

Problems with "Together" Restrictions

Strategy: Treat grouped objects as a single unit, then arrange within the group.

Example: In how many ways can 5 people sit in a row if two specific people must sit next to each other?

Step 1: Treat the two people as one unit
Now we have 4 "units" to arrange: (AB), C, D, E
These can be arranged in 4! = 24 ways

Step 2: The two people can be arranged within their unit
AB or BA: 2! = 2 ways

Total: 24 × 2 = 48 ways

Problems with "Apart" Restrictions

Strategy: Total arrangements - arrangements where they're together

Example: Using the same 5 people, in how many ways can they sit if two specific people must NOT sit next to each other?

Total arrangements: 5! = 120
Arrangements where they ARE together: 48 (from previous example)
Arrangements where they're NOT together: 120 - 48 = 72

Distributing Objects into Groups

Example: How many ways can 9 students be divided into three groups of 3?

Step 1: Choose 3 from 9 for first group: ₉C₃ = 84
Step 2: Choose 3 from remaining 6 for second group: ₆C₃ = 20
Step 3: Remaining 3 automatically form third group: ₃C₃ = 1

Product: 84 × 20 × 1 = 1,680

But wait! The groups are indistinguishable (no "first," "second," "third")
We've overcounted by a factor of 3! = 6 (number of ways to label 3 groups)

Final answer: 1,680 / 6 = 280 ways

Choosing When to Use Each Method

Use Fundamental Counting Principle when:
• Making a series of independent choices
• Building something step by step
• Creating passwords, codes, or sequences

Use Permutations when:
• Order matters
• Arranging objects in positions
• Assigning distinct roles (president, VP, etc.)

Use Combinations when:
• Order doesn't matter
• Selecting committee members
• Choosing items from a menu
• Forming groups without assigned positions

Key question to ask: "If I swap two items, does it create a different outcome?"
If YES → permutation
If NO → combination

ACT Example 4: Complex Counting

How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 without repetition, where the number must be even?

  • F) 60
  • G) 120
  • H) 180
  • J) 240
  • K) 360
  • Solution:
  • Start with the restriction: last digit must be even (2, 4, or 6)
  • Last digit: 3 choices (2, 4, or 6)
  • First digit: 5 choices (any of remaining 5 digits)
  • Second digit: 4 choices (any of remaining 4 digits)
  • Third digit: 3 choices (any of remaining 3 digits)
  • Total = 3 × 5 × 4 × 3 = 180
  • Answer: H — Handle restrictions first, then count remaining choices!
