Understanding repeating patterns and cyclic sequences is crucial for ACT Math success. These problems test your ability to identify pattern rules, predict the nth term, and work with periodic functions and modular arithmetic. Pattern questions appear in 2-4 questions per ACT—that's about 3-7% of all math questions! This lesson covers visual patterns, number sequences, remainder patterns, and calendar/time problems that rely on understanding cycles and repetition.

1. Visual and Geometric Patterns

Visual patterns involve shapes, symbols, or arrangements that repeat according to a rule. On the ACT, you'll need to identify the pattern length (also called the period or cycle length) and use division to find which element appears at a given position. Understanding remainders is key to solving these problems efficiently.

Identifying Pattern Length

The first step in any repeating pattern problem is to identify how many elements compose one complete cycle.

Example: A pattern repeats: ★, ●, ■, ★, ●, ■, ★, ●, ■, ...

Pattern length: 3 (the pattern is ★, ●, ■ and then repeats)

Pattern elements:
Position 1: ★
Position 2: ●
Position 3: ■
Position 4: ★ (pattern repeats)

Using Division to Find the Nth Term

Key strategy: Divide the position number by the pattern length. The remainder tells you which element in the pattern appears at that position.

Example: Using the pattern ★, ●, ■ (length 3), what is the 47th element?

Step 1: Divide 47 by 3
47 ÷ 3 = 15 remainder 2

Step 2: The remainder is 2, so look at position 2 in the pattern

Answer: The 47th element is ● (the second element)

Special case: If the remainder is 0, use the last element in the pattern
Example: 48 ÷ 3 = 16 remainder 0, so position 48 is ■ (the 3rd element)

Letter and Symbol Patterns

Example: A sequence follows the pattern: A, B, C, D, A, B, C, D, ...

What is the 100th letter?

Pattern length: 4

Calculation: 100 ÷ 4 = 25 remainder 0

Since remainder is 0, use the last element: D

Geometric Shape Patterns

Example: A border uses tiles in this repeating pattern: red, red, blue, white, red, red, blue, white, ...

Pattern length: 4 (red, red, blue, white)

If 200 tiles are used, how many are blue?

Step 1: Find complete cycles: 200 ÷ 4 = 50 complete cycles

Step 2: In each cycle, there is 1 blue tile

Answer: 50 blue tiles

ACT Example 1: Visual Pattern

A sequence of shapes follows the pattern: circle, square, triangle, triangle, circle, square, triangle, triangle, ... This pattern continues. What is the 85th shape in the sequence?

  • A) Circle
  • B) Square
  • C) Triangle
  • D) Cannot be determined
  • E) Pentagon
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Identify the pattern: circle, square, triangle, triangle (length = 4)
  • Step 2: Divide 85 by 4: 85 ÷ 4 = 21 remainder 1
  • Step 3: Remainder 1 means position 1 in the pattern
  • Step 4: Position 1 is circle
  • Answer: A — The remainder tells you which position in the repeating pattern!

2. Number Patterns and Sequences

Number patterns follow mathematical rules that repeat or cycle. You'll encounter patterns involving digit patterns, last-digit cycles, sign alternation, and remainder patterns. These problems often involve powers, factorials, or operations that produce predictable cycles.

Powers and Last-Digit Patterns

The last digit of powers follows a repeating pattern that varies by base number.

Example: Powers of 2
21 = 2 (last digit: 2)
22 = 4 (last digit: 4)
23 = 8 (last digit: 8)
24 = 16 (last digit: 6)
25 = 32 (last digit: 2) ← Pattern repeats!

Pattern: 2, 4, 8, 6 (length 4)

What is the last digit of 247?
47 ÷ 4 = 11 remainder 3
Remainder 3 → position 3 in pattern → 8

Common last-digit patterns:

Powers of 3: 3, 9, 7, 1 (length 4)
Powers of 4: 4, 6 (length 2)
Powers of 5: 5 (length 1, always 5)
Powers of 6: 6 (length 1, always 6)
Powers of 7: 7, 9, 3, 1 (length 4)
Powers of 8: 8, 4, 2, 6 (length 4)
Powers of 9: 9, 1 (length 2)

Alternating Sign Patterns

Example: A sequence alternates: +1, -2, +3, -4, +5, -6, ...

Pattern rule: Positive for odd positions, negative for even positions

What is the 73rd term?
73 is odd, so the term is positive: +73

What is the sum of the first 100 terms?
Pairs: (+1, -2) = -1; (+3, -4) = -1; ...
50 pairs, each summing to -1
Total: 50 × (-1) = -50

Remainder Patterns in Division

Example: What is the pattern of remainders when dividing consecutive integers by 5?

1 ÷ 5 = 0 R1
2 ÷ 5 = 0 R2
3 ÷ 5 = 0 R3
4 ÷ 5 = 0 R4
5 ÷ 5 = 1 R0
6 ÷ 5 = 1 R1 ← Pattern repeats

Pattern: 1, 2, 3, 4, 0 (length 5)

What is the remainder when 87 is divided by 5?
87 ÷ 5 = 17 R2, so remainder is 2

Digit Sum Patterns

Example: Sum of digits pattern for multiples of 9:

9: 9 (digit sum = 9)
18: 1+8 = 9
27: 2+7 = 9
36: 3+6 = 9

Pattern: Multiples of 9 always have digit sums that are multiples of 9

This is useful for checking divisibility by 9!

Cyclic Decimal Patterns

Example: 1/7 = 0.142857142857...

Pattern: 142857 (length 6)

What is the 50th digit after the decimal?
50 ÷ 6 = 8 remainder 2
Position 2 in pattern "142857" is 4

Answer: The 50th digit is 4

ACT Example 2: Power Pattern

What is the units digit (ones place) of 782?

  • F) 1
  • G) 3
  • H) 7
  • J) 9
  • K) Cannot be determined
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Find the pattern of last digits for powers of 7
  • 71 = 7, 72 = 49, 73 = 343, 74 = 2401, 75 = 16807
  • Step 2: Pattern of last digits: 7, 9, 3, 1 (length 4, then repeats)
  • Step 3: Divide 82 by 4: 82 ÷ 4 = 20 remainder 2
  • Step 4: Remainder 2 means position 2 in the pattern, which is 9
  • Answer: J — You don't need to calculate 782, just find the pattern!

3. Calendar and Time Patterns

Calendar problems involve understanding days of the week cycles, date patterns, and recurring events. Since there are 7 days in a week, most calendar problems use division by 7 to find the day of the week. You'll also work with modulo 7 arithmetic and understand how days shift from year to year.

Day of the Week Patterns

The days of the week follow a 7-day cycle. To find the day of the week after n days, divide n by 7 and use the remainder.

Example: Today is Monday. What day of the week will it be in 100 days?

Step 1: Divide 100 by 7
100 ÷ 7 = 14 remainder 2

Step 2: Count forward 2 days from Monday
Monday → Tuesday (1 day) → Wednesday (2 days)

Answer: Wednesday

Pattern reference:
Remainder 0: Same day
Remainder 1: Next day
Remainder 2: Two days later
Remainder 3: Three days later
... and so on

Counting Backwards

Example: Today is Friday. What day was it 50 days ago?

Step 1: Divide 50 by 7
50 ÷ 7 = 7 remainder 1

Step 2: Count backward 1 day from Friday
Friday → Thursday

Answer: Thursday

Date Patterns Within a Month

Example: If the 3rd of a month is a Tuesday, what day is the 24th?

Step 1: Find days between: 24 - 3 = 21 days later

Step 2: Divide by 7: 21 ÷ 7 = 3 remainder 0

Step 3: Remainder 0 means same day of the week

Answer: Tuesday

Recurring Events

Example: A class meets every Monday and Thursday. If January 1st is a Monday, how many class meetings are there in January (31 days)?

Step 1: List days: Jan 1 (Mon), 4 (Thu), 8 (Mon), 11 (Thu), 15 (Mon), 18 (Thu), 22 (Mon), 25 (Thu), 29 (Mon)

Alternative method: There are 4 complete weeks (28 days), which gives 8 meetings (4 Mon + 4 Thu)
Plus 3 remaining days (29, 30, 31): Jan 29 is Monday
Total: 8 + 1 = 9 meetings

Year-to-Year Day Shifts

Pattern: A regular (non-leap) year has 365 days
365 ÷ 7 = 52 remainder 1

So the same date shifts forward by 1 day each year

Example: If January 1, 2020 is a Wednesday, what day is January 1, 2021?
2020 is a leap year (366 days)
366 ÷ 7 = 52 remainder 2
Wednesday + 2 days = Friday

ACT Example 3: Calendar Problem

Today is Wednesday, March 15th. On what day of the week will it be 45 days from today?

  • A) Monday
  • B) Tuesday
  • C) Wednesday
  • D) Friday
  • E) Saturday
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Divide 45 by 7: 45 ÷ 7 = 6 remainder 3
  • Step 2: The remainder is 3, so count 3 days forward from Wednesday
  • Step 3: Wednesday → Thursday (1) → Friday (2) → Saturday (3)
  • Answer: E — Use division by 7 for all day-of-the-week problems!

4. Advanced Pattern Recognition and Modular Arithmetic

Advanced pattern problems combine multiple concepts and require modular arithmetic thinking. You'll work with composite patterns, overlapping cycles, and patterns that involve multiple operations. Understanding least common multiples helps solve problems where multiple patterns interact.

Modular Arithmetic Basics

Modular arithmetic is the mathematics of remainders. We write "a ≡ b (mod n)" to mean "a and b have the same remainder when divided by n."

Examples:
17 ≡ 2 (mod 5) because 17 ÷ 5 = 3 R2 and 2 ÷ 5 = 0 R2
23 ≡ 3 (mod 10) because both have remainder 3 when divided by 10
100 ≡ 0 (mod 4) because 100 ÷ 4 = 25 R0

Overlapping Cycle Problems

Example: Two lights blink in patterns. Light A blinks every 6 seconds, Light B blinks every 8 seconds. If they both blink together at time 0, when is the next time they blink together?

Solution: Find LCM(6, 8)
6 = 2 × 3
8 = 23
LCM = 23 × 3 = 24

Answer: They blink together again at 24 seconds

Composite Pattern Problems

Example: A sequence follows two rules:
- Every 3rd term is doubled
- Every 5th term is tripled

Starting sequence: 1, 1, 2, 1, 3, 2, 1, 1, 4, 3, ...

Position 3: doubled → 1 × 2 = 2
Position 5: tripled → 1 × 3 = 3
Position 6: doubled (mult. of 3) → 1 × 2 = 2
Position 9: doubled (mult. of 3) → 2 × 2 = 4
Position 10: tripled (mult. of 5) → 1 × 3 = 3
Position 15: both (mult. of 3 and 5) → 1 × 2 × 3 = 6

Sum Patterns in Repeating Sequences

Example: A pattern repeats: 2, 5, 1, 2, 5, 1, ... What is the sum of the first 100 terms?

Step 1: Pattern length is 3
Sum of one complete cycle: 2 + 5 + 1 = 8

Step 2: Find complete cycles in 100 terms
100 ÷ 3 = 33 remainder 1

Step 3: Calculate sum
33 complete cycles: 33 × 8 = 264
Plus 1 extra term (position 1 = 2): 264 + 2 = 266

Answer: 266

Nested Pattern Problems

Example: A grid is colored with pattern A horizontally (red, blue, red, blue, ...) and pattern B vertically (yellow, green, yellow, green, ...). What color is the cell at position (7, 13)?

Horizontal (position 7):
Pattern: red (odd), blue (even)
7 is odd → red

Vertical (position 13):
Pattern: yellow (odd), green (even)
13 is odd → yellow

Combined: Red + Yellow = Orange (or state "red horizontally, yellow vertically")

Chinese Remainder Theorem Applications

Example: A number gives remainder 2 when divided by 5 and remainder 3 when divided by 7. What is the smallest such positive number?

Method: Test multiples of 7 plus 3:
3: 3 ÷ 5 = 0 R3 ✗
10: 10 ÷ 5 = 2 R0 ✗
17: 17 ÷ 5 = 3 R2 ✓

Answer: 17

Pattern: The next such numbers are 17 + 35k (where 35 = LCM(5,7))
So: 17, 52, 87, 122, ...

Problem-Solving Strategies

Strategy 1: Always find the pattern length first

Count elements in one complete cycle before attempting to find the nth term. This prevents errors and simplifies calculation.

Strategy 2: Use division with remainder

Divide the position number by the pattern length. The remainder tells you which element in the pattern to use. If remainder is 0, use the last element.

Strategy 3: Look for multiple patterns simultaneously

Some problems have overlapping patterns (like multiples of 3 and multiples of 5). Use LCM to find when both patterns align.

Strategy 4: Draw it out for small examples

If confused, write out the first 10-15 terms to see the pattern clearly. Once identified, use arithmetic to find the nth term without continuing the entire sequence.

ACT Example 4: Complex Pattern

A sequence of numbers follows this pattern: 3, 3, 6, 3, 3, 6, 3, 3, 6, ... What is the sum of the first 50 terms?

  • F) 150
  • G) 200
  • H) 198
  • J) 204
  • K) 300
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Identify pattern: 3, 3, 6 repeats (length 3)
  • Step 2: Sum of one cycle: 3 + 3 + 6 = 12
  • Step 3: Find complete cycles: 50 ÷ 3 = 16 remainder 2
  • Step 4: 16 complete cycles: 16 × 12 = 192
  • Step 5: Plus 2 remaining terms (both 3): 192 + 3 + 3 = 198
  • Answer: H — Break into complete cycles plus remaining terms for efficient calculation!
