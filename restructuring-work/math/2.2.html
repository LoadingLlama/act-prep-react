Understanding area, volume, surface area, and triangle properties is essential for ACT Math success. These concepts appear in 8-12 questions per test—that's nearly 20% of all math questions! This lesson will teach you the essential formulas, how to apply them quickly, and tricks to avoid common mistakes that cost students points.

1. Area Formulas for Common Shapes

The ACT tests your ability to calculate area for basic shapes. You must memorize these formulas—they are NOT provided on the test. The most common shapes tested are rectangles, triangles, circles, and trapezoids.

Rectangle and Square

For rectangles, use A = length × width. For squares, since all sides are equal, use A = s² where s is the side length. Remember: if you're given the perimeter, divide by 4 for a square or by 2 and solve for individual sides for a rectangle.

Triangle

The fundamental triangle formula is A = ½ × base × height. The height must be perpendicular to the base! Many students make the mistake of using a slant height instead. For right triangles, the two legs can serve as base and height since they're already perpendicular.

Circle

Circle area is A = πr² where r is the radius. If given the diameter, remember to divide by 2 to get the radius first. The ACT often leaves answers in terms of π, so don't approximate unless specifically asked to do so.

Trapezoid

A trapezoid has two parallel bases. The formula is A = ½(b₁ + b₂) × h where b₁ and b₂ are the parallel sides and h is the perpendicular height between them. Think of it as the average of the two bases, multiplied by the height.

2. Triangle Properties and Special Types

Beyond calculating area, the ACT frequently tests special triangle properties. Understanding right triangles, isosceles triangles, equilateral triangles, and the Pythagorean theorem will help you solve problems quickly.

Right Triangles and Pythagorean Theorem

For any right triangle with legs a and b and hypotenuse c, a² + b² = c². The ACT loves testing common Pythagorean triples: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, and their multiples (6-8-10, 9-12-15, etc.). Recognizing these can save time instead of calculating from scratch.

Special Right Triangles: 45-45-90

In a 45-45-90 triangle, the sides are in the ratio x : x : x√2. If each leg has length x, the hypotenuse is x√2. This appears frequently when dealing with squares or when triangles are formed by diagonals.

Special Right Triangles: 30-60-90

In a 30-60-90 triangle, the sides are in the ratio x : x√3 : 2x. The shortest side (opposite the 30° angle) is x, the middle side (opposite 60°) is x√3, and the hypotenuse is 2x. These often appear in problems involving equilateral triangles.

Isosceles and Equilateral Triangles

An isosceles triangle has two equal sides and two equal angles. An equilateral triangle has all three sides equal and all angles equal to 60°. For equilateral triangles, if the side length is s, the area is A = (s²√3)/4.

3. Volume Formulas for 3D Shapes

Volume measures the space inside a three-dimensional object. The ACT provides some volume formulas on the reference sheet, but you should memorize them anyway to save time during the test.

Rectangular Prism and Cube

For a rectangular prism (box), use V = length × width × height. For a cube with side length s, use V = s³. Remember that all edges of a cube are equal.

Cylinder

A cylinder is like stacking circles. The formula is V = πr²h where r is the radius of the circular base and h is the height. Think of it as the area of the base (πr²) multiplied by how tall it is (h).

Sphere

The volume of a sphere is V = (4/3)πr³. Make sure you cube the radius (r³), not square it. This formula IS on the ACT reference sheet, but memorizing it speeds up your work.

Cone and Pyramid

Both cones and pyramids use the formula V = (1/3) × base area × height. For a cone, the base is a circle, so V = (1/3)πr²h. For a pyramid, multiply by the area of the base shape (often a square or triangle).

4. Surface Area and Advanced Applications

Surface area is the total area of all faces of a 3D shape. The ACT sometimes tests this concept, especially for rectangular prisms and cylinders. Understanding how to break down shapes into their faces makes these problems manageable.

Surface Area of Rectangular Prisms

A rectangular prism has 6 faces. The formula is SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh where l, w, and h are length, width, and height. For a cube with side s, this simplifies to SA = 6s² since all faces are identical squares.

Surface Area of Cylinders

A cylinder has two circular bases and one rectangular side (when "unrolled"). The formula is SA = 2πr² + 2πrh. The first term (2πr²) accounts for the top and bottom circles, and the second term (2πrh) is the lateral surface area.

Composite Shapes

The ACT loves composite shapes—shapes made by combining or subtracting basic shapes. The strategy is to break them into recognizable pieces, calculate each piece separately, then add or subtract as needed. For example, finding the area of a rectangular garden with a circular pond means calculating the rectangle's area and subtracting the circle's area.

Scale Factor and Area/Volume Relationships

If you scale a shape by a factor of k, area scales by k² and volume scales by k³. This is a powerful shortcut: if you double all dimensions of a box (k=2), the surface area increases by 4× and the volume increases by 8×.
