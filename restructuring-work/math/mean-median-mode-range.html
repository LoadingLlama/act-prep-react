Understanding measures of central tendency and measures of spread is fundamental for ACT Math statistics problems. These problems test your ability to calculate mean (average), median (middle value), mode (most frequent), and range (spread), as well as interpret what these values reveal about data sets. Statistics questions appear in 4-6 questions per ACT—that's about 7-10% of all math questions! This lesson covers basic calculations, effects of outliers, weighted averages, and how to find missing values when given summary statistics.

1. Understanding Mean (Average)

The mean, or arithmetic average, is the sum of all values divided by the number of values. It's the most common measure of central tendency and is sensitive to outliers (extremely high or low values). The ACT frequently tests your ability to work backwards from a given mean to find missing values or calculate required scores.

Calculating the Mean

Formula: Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)

Example: Find the mean of 12, 18, 15, 20, 10

Step 1: Sum = 12 + 18 + 15 + 20 + 10 = 75

Step 2: Count = 5 values

Step 3: Mean = 75 ÷ 5 = 15

Finding Missing Values

Example: The mean of 5 test scores is 84. Four scores are 78, 82, 88, and 90. What is the fifth score?

Method 1: Use the formula
Mean = Total / Number of scores
84 = Total / 5
Total = 84 × 5 = 420

Sum of known scores: 78 + 82 + 88 + 90 = 338
Fifth score = 420 - 338 = 82

Method 2: Think about deviations from mean
78 is 6 below mean (-6)
82 is 2 below mean (-2)
88 is 4 above mean (+4)
90 is 6 above mean (+6)
Total deviation so far: -6 - 2 + 4 + 6 = +2
Fifth score must be 2 below mean: 84 - 2 = 82

Effect of Adding or Removing Values

Example: Five numbers have a mean of 20. If 30 is added to the set, what is the new mean?

Original sum: 5 × 20 = 100
New sum: 100 + 30 = 130
New mean: 130 ÷ 6 ≈ 21.67

Key insight: Adding a value above the mean increases the mean; adding below decreases it

Weighted Averages

Example: A student scores 85 on a test worth 40% of the grade and 90 on a test worth 60%. What is the weighted average?

Calculation: (85 × 0.40) + (90 × 0.60) = 34 + 54 = 88

Formula: Weighted average = Σ(value × weight) where weights sum to 1

ACT Example 1: Mean Calculation

Sarah's test scores are 88, 92, 85, and 79. What score must she earn on her fifth test to have an average of exactly 88?

  • A) 88
  • B) 92
  • C) 95
  • D) 96
  • E) 100
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Required total for average of 88 over 5 tests: 88 × 5 = 440
  • Step 2: Current sum: 88 + 92 + 85 + 79 = 344
  • Step 3: Fifth score needed: 440 - 344 = 96
  • Answer: D — Work backwards from the desired mean!

2. Understanding Median

The median is the middle value when data is arranged in order. Unlike the mean, the median is resistant to outliers, making it a better measure of central tendency for skewed data. For an odd number of values, the median is the middle value. For an even number, it's the average of the two middle values.

Finding the Median (Odd Number of Values)

Example: Find the median of 15, 22, 18, 30, 12

Step 1: Arrange in order: 12, 15, 18, 22, 30

Step 2: Count: 5 values (odd)

Step 3: Middle position = (5 + 1) / 2 = 3rd value

Median: 18

Finding the Median (Even Number of Values)

Example: Find the median of 8, 12, 15, 20, 25, 30

Step 1: Already in order

Step 2: Count: 6 values (even)

Step 3: Two middle positions: 3rd and 4th values (15 and 20)

Median: (15 + 20) / 2 = 17.5

Median vs Mean with Outliers

Example: Data set: 5, 6, 7, 8, 100

Mean: (5 + 6 + 7 + 8 + 100) / 5 = 126 / 5 = 25.2
Median: 7 (middle value)

The outlier (100) dramatically affects the mean but not the median

This is why median income is often reported instead of mean income—a few billionaires would skew the mean upward!

Finding Missing Values Given the Median

Example: Five numbers in order are x, 12, 18, 24, 30. If the median is 18, what could x be?

Analysis: The median (3rd value) is 18 ✓

Constraint: For the ordering to be valid, x must be ≤ 12

Answer: x can be any value ≤ 12 (like 5, 10, 12, etc.)

ACT Example 2: Median Calculation

The test scores for 7 students are: 72, 85, 91, 88, 76, 95, 82. What is the median score?

  • F) 82
  • G) 85
  • H) 86.5
  • J) 88
  • K) 91
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Arrange in order: 72, 76, 82, 85, 88, 91, 95
  • Step 2: Count: 7 values (odd number)
  • Step 3: Middle position = (7 + 1) / 2 = 4th value
  • Step 4: The 4th value is 85
  • Answer: G — Always arrange data in order first!

3. Mode and Range

The mode is the value that appears most frequently in a data set. A set can have no mode (all values appear equally), one mode (unimodal), or multiple modes (bimodal, trimodal, or multimodal). The range measures the spread of data and equals the difference between the maximum and minimum values.

Finding the Mode

Practice Problem: One mode Data: 3, 7, 7, 9, 12, 7, 15 Mode: 7 (appears 3 times, more than any other value) Example 2: Two modes (bimodal) Data: 5, 8, 8, 10, 12, 12, 15 Modes: 8 and 12 (both appear twice) Example 3: No mode Data: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 Mode: None (all values appear exactly once)

Calculating the Range

Formula: Range = Maximum value - Minimum value

Example: Find the range of 12, 8, 15, 22, 9, 18

Maximum: 22
Minimum: 8
Range: 22 - 8 = 14

Effect of Adding/Removing Values on Range

Original data: 5, 10, 15, 20 (range = 20 - 5 = 15)

Add 25: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 (range = 25 - 5 = 20) ← increased

Add 12: 5, 10, 12, 15, 20 (range = 20 - 5 = 15) ← unchanged

Key insight: Range only changes if you add a new maximum or minimum

When to Use Which Measure

Use Mean when: Data is roughly symmetric with no extreme outliers

Use Median when: Data has outliers or is skewed (like income or home prices)

Use Mode when: You want the most common value (like shoe size or survey responses)

Use Range when: You want a simple measure of spread (though it's sensitive to outliers)

ACT Example 3: Mode and Range

A data set contains the values: 14, 18, 14, 22, 26, 18, 30, 14. What are the mode and range?

  • A) Mode = 14, Range = 14
  • B) Mode = 14, Range = 16
  • C) Mode = 18, Range = 16
  • D) Mode = 14, Range = 30
  • E) Mode = 19, Range = 16
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Count frequencies: 14 appears 3 times, 18 appears 2 times, others appear once
  • Step 2: Mode = 14 (most frequent)
  • Step 3: Range = Max - Min = 30 - 14 = 16
  • Answer: B — Count carefully to find the mode!

4. Advanced Applications and Comparisons

Advanced statistics problems require comparing multiple measures, understanding their relationships, and interpreting what they reveal about data distribution and skewness. You'll also need to work with grouped data, calculate measures from frequency tables, and understand how transforming data affects summary statistics.

Relationships Between Mean, Median, and Mode

Symmetric distribution: Mean ≈ Median ≈ Mode
Example: 2, 4, 5, 6, 6, 7, 8, 10

Right-skewed (positively skewed): Mode < Median < Mean
Example: 2, 3, 3, 4, 4, 5, 12 (the 12 pulls the mean up)

Left-skewed (negatively skewed): Mean < Median < Mode
Example: 1, 8, 9, 9, 10, 10, 11 (the 1 pulls the mean down)

Working with Frequency Tables

Example: Calculate the mean from this frequency table:

Value: 2, Frequency: 3
Value: 4, Frequency: 5
Value: 6, Frequency: 2

Step 1: Calculate weighted sum: (2×3) + (4×5) + (6×2) = 6 + 20 + 12 = 38

Step 2: Total frequency: 3 + 5 + 2 = 10

Step 3: Mean = 38 / 10 = 3.8

Mode: 4 (highest frequency of 5)

Effect of Transformations on Statistics

Original data: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10
Mean = 6, Median = 6, Range = 8

Add 5 to each value: 7, 9, 11, 13, 15
Mean = 11 (increased by 5)
Median = 11 (increased by 5)
Range = 8 (unchanged!)

Multiply each value by 2: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20
Mean = 12 (multiplied by 2)
Median = 12 (multiplied by 2)
Range = 16 (multiplied by 2)

Combining Data Sets

Example: Class A has 20 students with mean score 80. Class B has 30 students with mean score 90. What is the combined mean?

Wrong approach: (80 + 90) / 2 = 85 ✗

Right approach:
Total from Class A: 20 × 80 = 1,600
Total from Class B: 30 × 90 = 2,700
Combined total: 1,600 + 2,700 = 4,300
Combined count: 20 + 30 = 50
Combined mean: 4,300 / 50 = 86

ACT Example 4: Complex Application

The mean of six numbers is 15. If one of the numbers is removed, the mean of the remaining five numbers is 12. What was the removed number?

  • F) 3
  • G) 12
  • H) 15
  • J) 27
  • K) 30
  • Solution:
  • Step 1: Original sum of 6 numbers: 15 × 6 = 90
  • Step 2: New sum of 5 numbers: 12 × 5 = 60
  • Step 3: Removed number: 90 - 60 = 30
  • Answer: K — Calculate total sums, then subtract!

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ ✓Mean = sum ÷ count; to find missing values, work backwards using total sum
  • ✓ ✓Median requires arranging data in order first; it's the middle value (or average of two middle values)
  • ✓ ✓Mode is the most frequent value; a data set can have no mode, one mode, or multiple modes
  • ✓ ✓Range = maximum - minimum; it only changes if you add a new extreme value
  • ✓ ✓Mean is sensitive to outliers; median is resistant to outliers
  • ✓ ✓In symmetric data: mean ≈ median; in skewed data: they differ
  • ✓ ✓For frequency tables: multiply each value by its frequency, sum, then divide by total frequency
  • ✓ ✓Adding constant to all values: mean and median increase by that constant, range unchanged
  • ✓ ✓Multiplying all values by constant: mean, median, and range all multiply by that constant
  • ✓ ✓When combining data sets, use weighted averages based on group sizes
