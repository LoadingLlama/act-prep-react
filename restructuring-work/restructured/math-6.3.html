Hyperbolas rarely appear on the ACT, but recent tests have included more advanced conic section questions. You need to recognize hyperbola equations in standard form, identify whether they're vertical or horizontal, and locate their vertices. If hyperbolas appear, they're typically in questions 45-60 and require understanding the relationship between the equation and the graph.

1. What Is a Hyperbola?

A hyperbola is a conic section consisting of two separate curves (called branches) that mirror each other. Unlike circles and ellipses, hyperbolas have a negative sign in their standard form equation and equal 1.

  • Key characteristics:
    • Two separate curved branches
    • Always has a subtraction sign between terms
    • Standard form equation equals 1
    • Has a center point (h, k)
    • Has two vertices (the closest points on each branch)
  • When you'll see hyperbolas:
    • Questions 45-60 (advanced topics)
    • Usually asks you to identify the equation from a graph
    • Or find vertices given an equation

2. Standard Equations for Hyperbolas

The standard form of a hyperbola equation depends on whether it opens horizontally or vertically.

Horizontal Hyperbola (Opens Left and Right)

Center at origin (0, 0):
x²/a² − y²/b² = 1

  • The positive term is x²
  • Opens horizontally (left and right)
  • Vertices are at (−a, 0) and (a, 0)
  • The a-value tells you how far left and right from the center

Center at (h, k):
(x − h)²/a² − (y − k)²/b² = 1

  • Vertices are at (h − a, k) and (h + a, k)
  • Move a units left and right from center

Vertical Hyperbola (Opens Up and Down)

Center at origin (0, 0):
y²/a² − x²/b² = 1

  • The positive term is y²
  • Opens vertically (up and down)
  • Vertices are at (0, −a) and (0, a)
  • The a-value tells you how far up and down from the center

Center at (h, k):
(y − k)²/a² − (x − h)²/b² = 1

  • Vertices are at (h, k − a) and (h, k + a)
  • Move a units up and down from center

3. Identifying Hyperbola Direction

The key to identifying whether a hyperbola opens horizontally or vertically is looking at which term is positive.

Step 1: Look at the Signs

  • Hyperbolas always have a subtraction sign between the two terms
  • One term is positive (+), one term is negative (−)
  • The equation equals 1

Step 2: Identify the Positive Term

  • If x² term is positive: Horizontal hyperbola (opens left/right)
  • If y² term is positive: Vertical hyperbola (opens up/down)

Step 3: Find the Center

  • Look at the form (x − h)² and (y − k)²
  • Center is at (h, k)
  • Watch out: (x − 3) means h = 3, but (x + 3) means h = −3

Step 4: Find the Vertices

  • Find a² under the positive term, then take the square root to get a
  • Horizontal hyperbola: Move a units left and right from center
    • Vertices: (h − a, k) and (h + a, k)
  • Vertical hyperbola: Move a units up and down from center
    • Vertices: (h, k − a) and (h, k + a)

Example 1: Horizontal Hyperbola with Center at Origin

Consider the equation: x²/4 − y²/9 = 1

Solution:

  • Step 1: The x² term is positive → horizontal hyperbola
  • Step 2: Center at origin (0, 0)
  • Step 3: a² = 4, so a = 2
  • Step 4: Vertices are 2 units left and right of center: (−2, 0) and (2, 0)

Example 2: Vertical Hyperbola with Center at Origin

Consider the equation: y²/4 − x²/9 = 1

Solution:

  • Step 1: The y² term is positive → vertical hyperbola
  • Step 2: Center at origin (0, 0)
  • Step 3: a² = 4, so a = 2
  • Step 4: Vertices are 2 units up and down from center: (0, −2) and (0, 2)

Example 3: Finding the Equation from Vertices

A hyperbola has vertices (2, 4) and (2, 10) in the standard (x, y) coordinate plane. Which of the following equations represents this hyperbola?

Solution:

  • Step 1: Both vertices have the same x-coordinate (2) → vertical hyperbola
  • Step 2: Find the center (midpoint of vertices):
    • x-coordinate: 2
    • y-coordinate: (4 + 10) / 2 = 7
    • Center: (2, 7)
  • Step 3: Find a (distance from center to vertex):
    • Distance from (2, 7) to (2, 10): |10 − 7| = 3
    • Therefore a = 3, so a² = 9
  • Step 4: Write the equation for vertical hyperbola with center (2, 7):
    • (y − 7)²/9 − (x − 2)²/b² = 1

Key insight: For ACT questions, you typically don't need to know the b-value. Focus on identifying the center, determining the direction, and finding a.

4. When NOT to Spend Time on Hyperbolas

Hyperbola questions are rare and appear late in the test. Consider skipping them if:

  • You're aiming for a score below 30 (focus on earlier, easier questions)
  • You're running low on time (these are typically in the last 15 questions)
  • You haven't mastered more common topics like circles, parabolas, and ellipses
  • The question involves concepts beyond basic identification (like asymptotes or foci)

When TO study hyperbolas:

  • You're aiming for a 33+ (targeting questions 45-60)
  • You've mastered all other conic sections
  • You want to maximize your score on advanced topics
