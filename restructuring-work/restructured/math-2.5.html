Understanding circles, ellipses, and hyperbolas is essential for ACT Math success. These conic sections appear in 4-6 questions per test—about 10% of all math questions. This lesson teaches you how to identify each shape, work with their equations, and solve problems involving distance, tangent lines, and geometric properties.

1. Circle Fundamentals and Equations

A circle is the set of all points equidistant from a center point. The ACT tests your ability to work with circle equations, calculate circumference, area, and understand geometric properties.

Standard Form of Circle Equation

  • Standard equation: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²
    • Center: (h, k)
    • Radius: r (take the square root of the right side)
  • Example: (x - 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25
    • Center: (3, -2)
    • Radius: √25 = 5
  • Common mistake: Forgetting to take the square root of r²

Circumference and Area

  • Circumference: C = 2πr or C = πd
  • Area: A = πr²
  • Warning: Don't confuse radius and diameter
    • If given diameter, divide by 2 to get radius
    • Using diameter in A = πr² gives an answer 4 times too large

Chords, Tangents, and Secants

  • Chord: Line segment connecting two points on a circle
  • Diameter: Longest chord, passes through center
  • Tangent line: Touches circle at exactly one point
    • Perpendicular to radius at point of tangency
  • Secant: Line intersecting circle at two points

Converting from General Form

  • General form: x² + y² + Dx + Ey + F = 0
  • Must complete the square for both x and y terms
  • Example: x² + y² - 6x + 4y - 12 = 0
    • Becomes: (x - 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25
    • Center: (3, -2), Radius: 5

2. Ellipses: Stretched Circles

An ellipse is an oval shape—essentially a stretched circle. It has two axes: the longer major axis and the shorter minor axis.

Standard Form of Ellipse Equation

  • Standard equation: (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1
    • Center: (h, k)
    • a and b are semi-major and semi-minor axes
  • If a > b: ellipse stretched horizontally
  • If b > a: ellipse stretched vertically
  • Example: (x - 2)²/25 + (y + 1)²/9 = 1
    • Center: (2, -1)
    • Horizontal stretch: 5, Vertical stretch: 3

Foci and Major/Minor Axes

  • Foci: Two special points inside the ellipse
    • Sum of distances from any point on ellipse to both foci is constant
  • Major axis: Longest diameter, length 2a
  • Minor axis: Shortest diameter, length 2b
  • Distance from center to focus: c² = a² - b² (for a > b)

Distinguishing Ellipses from Circles

  • Circle is a special ellipse where a = b
  • Check the denominators:
    • Equal denominators → circle
    • Different denominators → ellipse
  • Area of ellipse: A = πab

Example: Identifying an Ellipse

Problem: What is the center and shape of (x - 1)²/16 + (y + 2)²/9 = 1?

Solution:

  • Center: (1, -2)
  • Denominators: 16 ≠ 9, so it's an ellipse (not a circle)
  • a = 4, b = 3 (ellipse stretched horizontally)

3. Hyperbolas: Open Curves

A hyperbola consists of two separate curves (called branches) that open away from each other. Unlike circles and ellipses, hyperbolas extend infinitely.

Standard Form of Hyperbola Equation

  • Horizontal opening: (x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1
  • Vertical opening: (y - k)²/a² - (x - h)²/b² = 1
  • Key difference from ellipse: minus sign between terms
  • The positive term determines opening direction

Asymptotes

  • Asymptotes: Imaginary lines the branches approach but never touch
  • For horizontal opening: y - k = ±(b/a)(x - h)
  • Form an "X" shape through the center
  • Branches get closer to these lines as you move away from center

Vertices and Foci

  • Vertices: Points where hyperbola is closest to center
    • Located at distance a from center
  • Foci: Located at distance c from center
  • For hyperbolas: c² = a² + b² (note: plus, not minus)
  • Absolute difference of distances to foci is constant for any point on hyperbola

Recognizing Hyperbolas

  • Look for minus sign between squared terms
  • Alternative form: xy = k (rectangular hyperbola)
    • Asymptotes along x and y axes

4. Comparing and Identifying Conic Sections

The ACT often tests your ability to quickly distinguish between conic sections. Use these identification strategies.

Quick Identification Guide

  • Circle: x² + y² terms with same coefficient, plus sign
    • Example: x² + y² = 16
  • Ellipse: x² + y² terms with different coefficients, plus sign
    • Example: x² + 4y² = 16
  • Hyperbola: x² and y² terms with opposite signs
    • Example: x² - 4y² = 16
  • Parabola: Only one variable is squared
    • Example: y = x² + 3

Converting Between Forms

  • Step 1: Complete the square for both variables
  • Step 2: Group terms by variable
  • Step 3: Factor out coefficients if needed
  • Step 4: Divide to get standard form (right side = 1)

Example: Converting to Standard Form

Problem: Convert 4x² + 9y² - 16x + 18y - 11 = 0 to standard form.

Solution:

  • Group: 4(x² - 4x) + 9(y² + 2y) = 11
  • Complete square: 4(x - 2)² + 9(y + 1)² = 36
  • Divide by 36: (x - 2)²/9 + (y + 1)²/4 = 1
  • This is an ellipse centered at (2, -1)

Distance and Point Problems

  • To check if point (a, b) lies on a curve:
    • Substitute coordinates into equation
    • Check if it equals the right side
  • Distance properties:
    • Circle: every point is distance r from center
    • Ellipse: sum of distances to foci is constant
    • Hyperbola: difference of distances to foci is constant

5. When to Use Each Formula

  • Always identify the conic section first
    • Look at signs and coefficients
    • Count how many variables are squared
  • Sketch if helpful
    • Quick sketch can eliminate wrong answers
    • Start with center point, then mark key features
  • Remember the focal relationships
    • Ellipse: c² = a² - b²
    • Hyperbola: c² = a² + b²

.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓Circle equation: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r². Remember to square root to find radius
  • ✓Ellipse has plus sign between terms: (x - h)²/a² + (y - k)²/b² = 1
  • ✓Hyperbola has minus sign between terms: (x - h)²/a² - (y - k)²/b² = 1
  • ✓For ellipses: c² = a² - b². For hyperbolas: c² = a² + b²
  • ✓Tangent lines to circles are perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency
  • ✓Complete the square to convert general form to standard form and reveal center
