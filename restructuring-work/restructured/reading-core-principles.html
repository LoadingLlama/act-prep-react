The ACT Reading section requires mastering seven core principles that separate high-scoring students from those who struggle. These fundamental concepts—including evidence-based answering, process of elimination, active engagement, and strategic time allocation—form the foundation of every successful reading strategy. By internalizing these principles and applying them consistently, you can dramatically improve both speed and accuracy.

1. Principle 1: Every Answer Must Be Supported by the Passage

Every correct answer must have direct textual support. ACT Reading questions are designed to have exactly one objectively correct answer based solely on what the passage states or implies.

  • Why it matters:
    • Answers are passage-based, not knowledge-based
    • Outside knowledge is irrelevant—even if you disagree with the author
    • Levels the playing field: no prior knowledge needed about Shakespeare, physics, or art
    • Everything you need is in front of you
  • How to apply:
    • Ask: "Can I point to specific words or sentences that support this answer?"
    • If you can't identify clear evidence, the answer is likely wrong
    • For inference questions, answers must still be strongly supported by combining passage information
    • Wild speculation or far-fetched connections are never correct
  • Avoiding outside knowledge traps:
    • ACT creates wrong answers that seem correct if you rely on outside knowledge
    • Work entirely within the four corners of the passage
    • Never import information from outside the passage

The Gold Standard: Line Reference Verification

  • When uncertain between two choices, return to the passage and identify precisely where the relevant information appears
  • Read a 3-5 line window around the line reference carefully
  • Compare passage's exact wording to each answer choice
  • Correct answers align closely with passage's language and meaning
  • Wrong answers introduce subtle distortions, exaggerations, or contradictions
  • Process: Locate → Verify → Select
  • Takes a few extra seconds but dramatically improves accuracy

Example: Evidence-Based Selection

Question: "The author's primary purpose in discussing the artist's early work is to..."

Wrong approach: "I remember learning that this artist's early work was revolutionary, so I'll choose the answer about innovation."

Right approach: "Let me find where the passage discusses the early work. Lines 23-28 say 'Though initially dismissed by critics, these early pieces laid the groundwork for his later masterworks.' This suggests the purpose is showing how early work influenced later development, not discussing innovation."

Takeaway: Always verify your answer against the passage's actual words.

2. Principles 2-3: Active Reading and Strategic Annotation

Success requires active engagement with the text, not passive reading. These two principles work together to maximize comprehension while minimizing wasted time.

Principle 2: Read Actively, Not Passively

  • Active reading = constantly engaging your mind with questions and predictions
  • Ask yourself:
    • "What's the main point of this paragraph?"
    • "Why did the author include this example?"
    • "How does this connect to what I just read?"
    • "What might they ask about this section?"
  • Passive reading warning signs:
    • Eyes moving across words but mind wandering
    • Reaching end of paragraph with no memory of content
    • Having to reread constantly
  • If you catch yourself reading passively, stop immediately, go back, and restart with active engagement
  • Better to read slowly with full attention than race through and remember nothing

Principle 3: Annotate Strategically

  • Strategic annotation = creating a roadmap without cluttering the text
  • What to mark:
    • Underline or circle key names, dates, and terms
    • Put brackets around important examples
    • Mark transitions: "however," "moreover," "in contrast"
    • Write brief margin notes (2-4 words max per paragraph)
    • Mark tone shifts or strong author opinions
  • What NOT to mark:
    • Don't underline full sentences
    • Don't highlight every fact (creates visual clutter)
    • Don't write lengthy margin notes

Focus Areas for Annotation

  • Structural elements: Topic sentences (first or second sentence of paragraphs)
  • Proper nouns: Circle names of people, places, organizations for quick relocation
  • Tone indicators: "unfortunately," "remarkably," "surprisingly," "problematic"
  • Brief labels: "background," "first example," "counterargument," "author's view"

Time Investment Pays Off

  • Active reading + annotation saves time overall
  • Students who race passively spend time rereading entire paragraphs multiple times
  • Extra 30-60 seconds during initial reading saves seconds per question
  • With practice, active reading becomes automatic

Example: Strategic Annotation in Action

Original paragraph:

"Marie Curie's groundbreaking research on radioactivity transformed modern physics. Born in Warsaw in 1867, she overcame significant obstacles as a woman in science. Her discovery of polonium and radium earned her two Nobel Prizes, making her the first person to win the award in two different sciences."

Effective annotation:

  • Circle "Marie Curie," "polonium," "radium"
  • Underline "groundbreaking research" and "two Nobel Prizes"
  • Margin note: "MC's achievements"

Result: Minimal marking helps you quickly locate information without cluttering the text.

3. Principles 4-5: Process of Elimination and Wrong Answer Recognition

Focus on eliminating wrong answers rather than immediately identifying the right answer. This approach helps you navigate difficult questions where the correct answer isn't obvious.

Principle 4: Use Aggressive Process of Elimination

  • Process of elimination (POE) is often more effective than searching for the correct answer directly
  • For each answer choice, ask:
    • "What's wrong with this answer?"
    • "How does it contradict, distort, or go beyond the passage?"
  • Physically cross out wrong answers as you eliminate them
  • Even eliminating 1-2 choices significantly improves guessing odds
  • Wrong answers often contain obvious flaws once you look for them
  • Be skeptical of every answer choice—actively search for reasons to eliminate

Principle 5: Recognize Common Wrong Answer Patterns

The ACT uses six predictable wrong answer types:

  • Too Extreme: Uses absolute language like "always," "never," "impossible," "only" when passage is more nuanced
  • Distortion: Takes passage information but twists or exaggerates it
  • Out of Scope: Discusses topics not mentioned or brings in outside information
  • Opposite: States the reverse of what the passage says
  • Partially Correct: Contains some accurate information but includes a critical error
  • Wrong Section: Accurately describes passage information but from a different paragraph than what the question asks about

Spotting "Too Extreme" Answers

  • Easiest wrong answer pattern to identify
  • ACT passages rarely make absolute claims
  • Red flag words: "always," "never," "all," "none," "impossible," "guaranteed," "only," "exclusively," "completely"
  • These sound strong and definitive, but usually wrong
  • Correct answers use measured language: "may affect," "is likely to," "suggests," "many believe"
  • Flag extreme language and check if passage truly supports absolute claims

Catching Distortions and Partial Truths

  • Distortion answers are tricky: they use passage vocabulary but subtly change meaning
  • How they distort:
    • Word substitutions
    • Reversed cause-effect relationships
    • Exaggerated claims
  • Partially correct answers describe 80-90% accurately but include one critical error:
    • Switched dates
    • Misidentified actors
    • Incorrectly stated outcomes
  • Defense: Careful, word-by-word comparison between answer choices and relevant passage section
  • Don't let familiar language fool you—verify every detail

Example: Wrong Answer Patterns

Passage states: "Some historians argue that the Industrial Revolution improved living standards for workers, while others contend it created harsh working conditions."

Too Extreme: "All historians agree that the Industrial Revolution always improved workers' lives."
→ WRONG—passage says "some argue," not "all agree"

Distortion: "Historians believe the Industrial Revolution only harmed workers."
→ WRONG—passage mentions both positive and negative views

Correct: "Historians disagree about whether the Industrial Revolution benefited workers."
→ RIGHT—accurately captures the debate described

4. Principles 6-7: Strategic Time Management and Flexibility

Manage the Reading section's intense time pressure and maintain strategic flexibility to maximize your score.

Principle 6: Allocate Time Strategically, Not Equally

  • Standard advice: 8:45 per passage
  • Better approach: Strategic time allocation = spend less time on easy passages, more on challenging ones
  • Example strategy:
    • Strong passages (e.g., Prose Fiction, Humanities): 7-7.5 minutes each
    • Moderate passages (e.g., Social Science): 8-9 minutes
    • Challenging passages (e.g., Natural Science): 10-11 minutes
  • Consider doing hardest passage type third (not last) while mentally fresh
  • Banks time early, allocates extra time where needed most

Avoiding the Time Trap of Difficult Questions

  • Biggest timing mistake: Spending 2-3 minutes on a single difficult question
  • Remember: Every question is worth one point regardless of difficulty
  • Rule: If you've spent more than 45-60 seconds without progress:
    • Make your best guess
    • Circle it in your test booklet
    • Move on immediately
    • Return if time permits
  • Think like fruit picking: Gather low-hanging fruit first before climbing for hard-to-reach pieces
  • Often, circled questions become easier when you return with full passage context

Principle 7: Stay Flexible and Adapt Your Strategy

  • Strategic flexibility = adapting your approach based on what's working during the test
  • No single strategy works perfectly for every passage
  • Adaptation examples:
    • Straightforward passage with clear structure → Lighten annotation
    • Confusing literary passage with shifting perspectives → Read more carefully first time
    • Running behind schedule → Skim next passage more quickly or skip to easier questions first

Knowing When to Cut Your Losses

  • If 5 minutes into a passage and still confused, you have two choices:
    • Power through carefully (but accept less time for other passages)
    • Switch to different passage and return if time permits
  • Neither choice is wrong—depends on your strengths and remaining time
  • Some students attempt every question; others strategically guess on hardest 2-3 per passage
  • Make decisions consciously rather than letting time pressure force your hand
  • Practice different approaches during preparation to discover what works best

Example: Strategic Time Allocation

Standard approach: 8:45 per passage (Prose, Social, Humanities, Natural Science in order)

Strength-based approach:

  • Passage 1 (Strong area—Prose Fiction): 7:30
  • Passage 3 (Strong area—Humanities): 7:30
  • Passage 2 (Moderate—Social Science): 9:00
  • Passage 4 (Challenging—Natural Science): 11:00

Result: By reordering and reallocating time, you maximize points in areas of strength while giving adequate time to challenging passages. With practice, this becomes natural.

5. When to Use These Principles

  • Every single ACT Reading question—these are foundational principles
  • During practice sessions to build automatic habits
  • On test day when time pressure is high
  • When you're stuck between two answer choices
  • When facing unfamiliar passage topics
  • When reviewing practice tests to identify error patterns

6. When NOT to Use These Principles

  • Never—these principles apply universally to ACT Reading
  • However, the intensity of application may vary:
    • Easy passages may need less annotation
    • Straightforward questions may not require extensive POE
    • Familiar topics still require passage-based answering (no outside knowledge)

  • ✓Use aggressive process of elimination by actively searching for reasons to eliminate each wrong answer
