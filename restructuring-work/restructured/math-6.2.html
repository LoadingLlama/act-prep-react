Advanced statistics concepts including sample proportions, box and whisker plots, and normal distribution appear in 2-3 questions on the ACT Math test. You'll need to interpret quartiles, understand the empirical rule, and analyze data using standard deviation. These advanced topics typically appear in questions 35-50 and separate top scorers from average performers.

1. What Are Sample Proportions?

A sample proportion represents the fraction or percentage of items in a sample that have a specific characteristic. It's calculated by dividing the number of items with the characteristic by the total sample size.

Calculating Sample Proportions

  • Formula: Sample proportion = (Count with characteristic) / (Total sample size)
    • Express as a decimal, fraction, or percentage
    • The total sample size is the denominator
    • Ignore any information about the larger population unless specifically asked

Example: Sample Proportion Calculation

A furniture company randomly samples 120 tables built in 2022. Of these, 9 have poor stability. The company built 15,000 tables total in 2022. What proportion of the sample has poor stability?

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the sample: 120 tables sampled
  • Step 2: Count with characteristic: 9 tables with poor stability
  • Step 3: Calculate proportion: 9 / 120 = 0.075 or 7.5%
  • Key insight: The total of 15,000 tables is irrelevant—we only use the sample data

Answer: 0.075 or 7.5%

2. Box and Whisker Plots

A box and whisker plot visually displays the five-number summary of a data set: minimum, first quartile (Q1), median (Q2), third quartile (Q3), and maximum. This visualization makes it easy to see the spread and center of data at a glance.

Understanding the Five-Number Summary

  • Minimum: The smallest value in the data set (left whisker endpoint)
  • First Quartile (Q1): The 25th percentile—25% of data falls below this value (left edge of box)
  • Median (Q2): The 50th percentile—the middle value (line inside the box)
  • Third Quartile (Q3): The 75th percentile—75% of data falls below this value (right edge of box)
  • Maximum: The largest value in the data set (right whisker endpoint)

Reading Box and Whisker Plots

  • The Box: Contains the middle 50% of the data (from Q1 to Q3)
    • Width represents the interquartile range (IQR)
    • IQR = Q3 - Q1
  • The Whiskers: Extend from the box to the minimum and maximum values
    • Left whisker shows the range of the lowest 25% of data
    • Right whisker shows the range of the highest 25% of data
  • The Median Line: Vertical line inside the box
    • If closer to Q1: data is right-skewed
    • If closer to Q3: data is left-skewed
    • If centered: data is roughly symmetric

Example: Comparing Box Plots

Ms. Pearson's first and second period classes each sold brownies for charity. There are 24 students in first period and 29 in second period. The box and whisker plots below show the number of brownies sold by each student. If a represents the median for first period and b represents the median for second period, what is the value of b − a?

[Imagine a box plot showing: Class 1 with median at 80, Class 2 with median at 60]

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the median line for Class 1: a = 80
  • Step 2: Identify the median line for Class 2: b = 60
  • Step 3: Calculate the difference: b − a = 60 − 80 = −20

Answer: −20

3. Normal Distribution and The Empirical Rule

A normal distribution is a bell-shaped, symmetric curve where most data clusters around the mean. The empirical rule (also called the 68-95-99.7 rule) describes how data spreads in a normal distribution using standard deviations. This concept rarely appears on the ACT, but when it does, it's typically in questions 40-50 and is surprisingly easy if you've memorized the rule.

The Empirical Rule

For data that follows a normal distribution:

  • 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean
    • From (mean − 1σ) to (mean + 1σ)
  • 95% of data falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean
    • From (mean − 2σ) to (mean + 2σ)
  • 99.7% of data falls within 3 standard deviations of the mean
    • From (mean − 3σ) to (mean + 3σ)

Step 1: Identify the Mean and Standard Deviation

  • The problem will tell you these values explicitly
  • Mean (μ) is the center of the distribution
  • Standard deviation (σ) measures the spread

Step 2: Count How Many Standard Deviations Away

  • Calculate the difference between the given value and the mean
  • Divide by the standard deviation to find how many σ away
  • Example: If mean = 100, σ = 15, and value = 130
    • Difference: 130 − 100 = 30
    • Standard deviations: 30 / 15 = 2σ above the mean

Step 3: Apply the Empirical Rule

  • Use the appropriate percentage based on the number of standard deviations
  • Within ±1σ: 68%
  • Within ±2σ: 95%
  • Within ±3σ: 99.7%

Example: Using the Empirical Rule

The scores on a fitness test are normally distributed. The mean score was 110 and the standard deviation was 10. What percentage of the scores on the fitness test are between 90 and 130?

Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify mean = 110, σ = 10
  • Step 2: Calculate deviations:
    • 90 is 20 below 110 → (110 − 20) = 110 − 2σ
    • 130 is 20 above 110 → (110 + 20) = 110 + 2σ
  • Step 3: Apply empirical rule: Within ±2σ contains 95% of data

Answer: 95%

4. When These Concepts Appear on the ACT

Understanding when and how these advanced statistics concepts are tested helps you prioritize your study time:

  • Sample proportions: Appear in questions 20-40
    • Usually straightforward calculations
    • Watch for distractor information (like total population)
    • Express your answer in the requested format (decimal, fraction, or percent)
  • Box and whisker plots: Appear in questions 25-45
    • Know how to read the five-number summary
    • Be able to calculate IQR (Q3 − Q1)
    • Understand what each component represents
  • Normal distribution/Empirical rule: Rarely tested, but appears in questions 40-50
    • Memorize the 68-95-99.7 rule
    • Practice identifying how many standard deviations away a value is
    • These questions are easier than their position suggests if you know the rule
