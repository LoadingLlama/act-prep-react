Graphing inequalities extends beyond simple lines to include parabolas, circles, and ellipses on the ACT Math section. Understanding when to use solid versus dashed lines and where to shade the solution set is crucial for answering these higher-difficulty questions correctly. Master these visual concepts to confidently tackle any inequality graphing problem.

1. What Are Inequality Graphs?

Inequality graphs show all coordinate points that satisfy an inequality or system of inequalities. The shaded region represents the solution set.

  • Two key components:
    • The boundary line, curve, or shape
    • The shaded region showing valid solutions
  • Core concepts:
    • Solid lines/curves mean ≤ or ≥ (boundary included)
    • Dashed lines/curves mean < or > (boundary excluded)
    • Shading indicates which side satisfies the inequality

2. Graphing Linear Inequalities

Linear inequalities follow consistent rules for lines:

Step 1: Draw the Boundary Line

  • Graph the line as if the inequality were an equals sign
    • Use solid line for ≤ or ≥
    • Use dashed line for < or >
    • Find slope and y-intercept to draw the line

Step 2: Determine Shading Direction

  • For greater than (> or ≥):
    • Shade above the line
    • Example: y > 2x + 1 shades above
  • For less than (< or ≤):
    • Shade below the line
    • Example: y < -x + 3 shades below

Step 3: Test a Point (Optional Verification)

  • Pick a test point not on the line (usually origin if possible)
    • Plug coordinates into inequality
    • If true: shade the side containing that point
    • If false: shade the opposite side

3. Systems of Linear Inequalities

When multiple inequalities create a system, the solution set is where shaded regions overlap:

  • Graph each inequality separately
    • Draw all boundary lines with correct solid/dashed styling
    • Identify shading region for each inequality
  • The solution is the intersection
    • Only the area where ALL shadings overlap
    • Must satisfy every inequality simultaneously
  • Lines not bordering the shaded region are distractors
    • ACT includes extra lines to confuse you
    • Ignore any line not directly forming a boundary

4. Advanced Graphing: Parabolas

Parabolas follow the same shading logic as lines:

  • For greater than (> or ≥):
    • Shade above the parabola
    • Example: y > x2 - 2x - 1 shades the region above the curve
  • For less than (< or ≤):
    • Shade below the parabola
    • Example: y < -x2 + 4 shades the region below the curve
  • Same solid/dashed rules apply:
    • Solid curve for ≤ or ≥
    • Dashed curve for < or >

5. Advanced Graphing: Circles and Ellipses

For circles and ellipses, shading rules reverse compared to lines:

Circles

  • Standard circle equation: x2 + y2 = r2
    • For greater than (> or ≥): shade outside the circle
    • For less than (< or ≤): shade inside the circle
    • Example: x2 + y2 < 9 shades the interior (dashed circle)

Ellipses

  • Follow the same inside/outside shading as circles
    • For greater than (> or ≥): shade outside the ellipse
    • For less than (< or ≤): shade inside the ellipse
    • Must know how to graph circles and ellipses from equations (see Chapter 21)

6. Worked Examples

Example 1: System with Line and Line

A shaded region is bounded by two lines. One has slope 2 and y-intercept at -3 (solid line, shading above). The other has slope -1/2 and y-intercept at 2 (dashed line, shading below). What system of inequalities represents this?

Solution:

  • First line: y ≥ 2x - 3 (solid, shading above means ≥)
  • Second line: y < (-1/2)x + 2 (dashed, shading below means <)
  • System: y ≥ 2x - 3 and y < (-1/2)x + 2

Example 2: Parabola and Line System

A shaded region is bounded by a parabola and a line. The parabola equation is given as y = x2 - 2x - 1 (solid curve). The line is y = -2x + 2 (solid line). The shaded region is above the parabola and below the line. What system represents this?

Solution:

  • Parabola: solid curve with shading above means y ≥ x2 - 2x - 1
  • Line: solid line with y-intercept at 2 and slope -2, shading below means y ≤ -2x + 2
  • System: y ≥ x2 - 2x - 1 and y ≤ -2x + 2
  • Key: On ACT, parabola equation is usually given in all answer choices - just identify correct inequality sign

Example 3: Circle and Line System

A shaded region shows the interior of a circle (dashed, radius 3 centered at origin) and below a solid line y = (-1/3)x - 1. Another line y = 3x - 4 is shown but doesn't border the shaded region. What system represents this?

Solution:

  • Circle: x2 + y2 < 9 (dashed, shading inside means <)
  • Line: y ≤ (-1/3)x - 1 (solid, shading below means ≤)
  • Ignore the line y = 3x - 4 since it doesn't border the shaded region
  • System: x2 + y2 < 9 and y ≤ (-1/3)x - 1

7. When to Use Each Shading Rule

Quick reference guide for shading direction:

  • Lines and Parabolas:
    • Greater than (> or ≥) → shade above
    • Less than (< or ≤) → shade below
  • Circles and Ellipses:
    • Greater than (> or ≥) → shade outside
    • Less than (< or ≤) → shade inside
  • Boundary line style:
    • ≥ or ≤ → solid line (boundary included)
    • > or < → dashed line (boundary excluded)

8. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Don't include lines that don't border the shaded region
    • ACT adds distractor lines to confuse you
    • Only include inequalities for boundaries of the shaded area
  • Don't reverse circle/ellipse shading rules
    • Remember: inside = less than, outside = greater than
    • Opposite of line shading logic
  • Don't confuse solid and dashed lines
    • Solid = "or equal to" (≤ or ≥)
    • Dashed = strictly less/greater (< or >)
