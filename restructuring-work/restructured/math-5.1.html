This comprehensive reference guide contains essential definitions, formulas, and concepts for ACT Math success. From number properties and algebraic rules to geometry formulas and trigonometric identities, this guide organizes key information for quick review and test preparation.

1. Number Types and Properties

Basic Number Classifications

  • Integers: Whole numbers including negative numbers
    • Examples: -4, 0, 3, 102
    • No fractions or decimals
  • Prime Numbers: Integers only divisible by 1 and itself
    • Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23...
    • Note: 1 is NOT a prime number
    • Note: 2 is the only even prime number
  • Rational Numbers: Any number expressible as a fraction
    • Examples: 1/2, 3/4, -2/5
    • All integers are also rational (can be written as n/1)
  • Irrational Numbers: Numbers that CANNOT be expressed as fractions
    • Examples: π, √2, √5, e
    • Decimal expansions never terminate or repeat
  • Undefined Numbers: Expressions with zero in the denominator
    • Examples: 5/0, x/0
    • Division by zero is undefined
  • Imaginary Numbers: Real numbers multiplied by imaginary unit i (where i² = -1)
    • Examples: 3i, -2i, 5i
    • Complex numbers: a + bi (real part + imaginary part)

Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

  • The largest positive integer that divides evenly into all numbers in a set
    • Example: GCF of 12, 18, and 30 is 6
    • Method: List factors of each number, find largest common factor

Least Common Multiple (LCM)

  • The smallest positive integer divisible by all numbers in a set
    • Example: LCM of 2 and 3 is 6
    • Example: LCM of 4 and 6 is 12

2. Essential Algebra Formulas

Ratios and Proportions (Chapter 10)

  • Direct Proportion: y = kx (where k is a constant)
  • Cross-multiplication: If a/b = c/d, then ad = bc

Statistics (Chapter 12)

  • Mean (Average): Sum of all values ÷ number of values
  • Median: Middle value when data is ordered
  • Mode: Most frequently occurring value
  • Range: Maximum value - minimum value
  • Weighted Average: [(average₁)(weight₁) + (average₂)(weight₂)] ÷ (weight₁ + weight₂)

Exponents and Roots (Chapter 13)

  • x^a · x^b = x^(a+b)
  • x^a ÷ x^b = x^(a-b)
  • (x^a)^b = x^(ab)
  • x^0 = 1 (when x ≠ 0)
  • x^(-a) = 1/x^a
  • x^(1/n) = ⁿ√x (nth root of x)
  • √(ab) = √a · √b

Quadratic Formula

  • For ax² + bx + c = 0: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a
  • Discriminant (b² - 4ac):
    • If b² - 4ac > 0: Two real solutions
    • If b² - 4ac = 0: One real solution
    • If b² - 4ac < 0: Zero real solutions, two complex solutions

3. Geometry Formulas

Angles (Chapter 3)

  • Sum of angles in a triangle: 180°
  • Sum of interior angles of n-sided polygon: (n - 2) × 180°
  • Supplementary angles: Add to 180°
  • Complementary angles: Add to 90°
  • Vertical angles: Equal when two lines intersect
  • Parallel lines with transversal:
    • Corresponding angles are equal
    • Alternate interior angles are equal
    • Alternate exterior angles are equal

Shapes - Area and Perimeter (Chapter 4)

  • Rectangle:
    • Area = length × width
    • Perimeter = 2(length + width)
  • Square:
    • Area = side²
    • Perimeter = 4 × side
  • Triangle:
    • Area = (1/2) × base × height
    • Perimeter = sum of all three sides
  • Trapezoid:
    • Area = (1/2) × (base₁ + base₂) × height
  • Parallelogram:
    • Area = base × height

Circles, Ellipses, and Hyperbolas (Chapter 21)

  • Circle:
    • Area = πr²
    • Circumference = 2πr or πd
    • Standard equation: (x - h)² + (y - k)² = r² (center at (h, k))
  • Arc length: (θ/360°) × 2πr (where θ is the central angle in degrees)
  • Sector area: (θ/360°) × πr²

4. Trigonometry Essentials

Basic Trigonometric Ratios (SOH-CAH-TOA)

  • sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse
  • cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse
  • tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent = sin(θ) / cos(θ)

Pythagorean Identity

  • sin²(θ) + cos²(θ) = 1

Periods of Trigonometric Functions

  • Period for sine, cosine, secant, and cosecant = 2π
  • Period for tangent and cotangent = π

Law of Sines and Law of Cosines

  • Law of Sines: a/sin(A) = b/sin(B) = c/sin(C)
  • Law of Cosines: a² = b² + c² - 2bc·cos(A)

5. Probability and Counting

Probability Rules (Chapter 22)

  • Rule #1: Sum of all probabilities = 1
  • Rule #2 (OR): P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) for mutually exclusive events
  • Rule #3 (AND): P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) for independent events
  • Expected Value: E = P(A)·A + P(B)·B + P(C)·C + ...

Permutations and Combinations (Chapter 23)

  • Factorial: n! = n × (n-1) × (n-2) × ... × 2 × 1
  • Permutation (order matters): ₙPᵣ = n! / (n - r)!
  • Combination (order doesn't matter): ₙCᵣ = n! / [r!(n - r)!]

6. Sequences and Series

Arithmetic Sequences

  • Add or subtract a common difference between terms
  • nth term formula: tₙ = t₁ + d(n - 1)
    • t₁ = first term
    • d = common difference
    • n = term number

Geometric Sequences

  • Multiply or divide by a common ratio between terms
  • nth term formula: tₙ = t₁·r^(n-1)
    • t₁ = first term
    • r = common ratio
    • n = term number

7. Advanced Topics

Complex Numbers (Chapter 25)

  • Complex conjugates: a + bi and a - bi
  • Graphing: Complex number a + bi is plotted at point (a, b) on complex plane
  • Distance between complex numbers: For a + bi and c + di, distance = √[(a - c)² + (b - d)²]

Inequalities (Chapter 27)

  • When multiplying or dividing by negative number, flip the inequality sign
  • Graphing linear inequalities:
    • For > and <: use dashed line
    • For ≥ and ≤: use solid line
    • For > and ≥: shade above the line
    • For < and ≤: shade below the line
  • Circles and ellipses:
    • For < and ≤: shade inside
    • For > and ≥: shade outside

Exponential Growth and Decay (Chapter 28)

  • General formula: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
    • A = final amount
    • P = initial (principal) amount
    • r = rate of growth or decay (as decimal)
    • n = number of times compounded per time interval
    • t = time
  • Continuous growth: A = Pe^(rt)

9. Memorization Tips

  • Focus on formulas NOT provided on the test
  • Use mnemonics (like SOH-CAH-TOA for trig ratios)
  • Practice writing formulas from memory daily
  • Group similar formulas together (all circle formulas, all triangle formulas, etc.)
  • Understand WHY formulas work, not just WHAT they are
  • Apply formulas to real problems - application aids retention
