Understanding mean, median, mode, and range is essential for ACT statistics questions. These measures help describe data sets, but knowing when to use each one and how to calculate weighted averages separates efficient problem-solvers from those who struggle. Master these concepts to confidently tackle any statistics question on test day.

1. What Are Mean, Median, Mode, and Range?

These four measures describe different aspects of a data set:

  • Mean (Average):
    • Sum of all values divided by the number of values
    • Formula: Mean = (Sum of all values) / (Number of values)
    • Most commonly tested measure on the ACT
  • Median:
    • The middle value when data is arranged in order
    • For odd number of values: the middle number
    • For even number of values: average of the two middle numbers
    • Less affected by outliers than mean
  • Mode:
    • The value that appears most frequently
    • A data set can have multiple modes or no mode
  • Range:
    • The difference between the largest and smallest values
    • Formula: Range = Maximum - Minimum

2. The Weighted Average Process

When combining averages from different groups, use the weighted average formula to account for different group sizes:

Step 1: Identify the Averages and Weights

  • Find the average for each group
    • Identify what makes each group's average "weighted" (size, time, percentage, etc.)
    • Note the weight for each group (number of items, minutes, students, etc.)

Step 2: Apply the Weighted Average Formula

  • Use this formula for two groups:
    • Weighted Average = [(Average #1)(Weight #1) + (Average #2)(Weight #2)] / (Total Weight)
    • Multiply each average by its corresponding weight
    • Add the products together
    • Divide by the total of all weights

Step 3: Calculate and Simplify

  • Perform the arithmetic carefully
    • Use your calculator to avoid errors
    • Check that your answer makes sense (should be between the two averages)

3. Finding the Mean: Key Technique

The most important formula for ACT mean questions:

The Sum Formula

  • Average × Number of Items = Sum
    • Use this to find the total when given an average
    • Essential for combining multiple groups
    • Example: If 20 students average 85, total points = 20 × 85 = 1,700

4. Finding the Median: Process and Shortcuts

Standard Method

  • Arrange all values in ascending order
    • Cross out numbers from both ends simultaneously
    • Continue until one or two numbers remain
    • Odd count: the remaining number is the median
    • Even count: average the two remaining numbers

Quick Formula for Finding Median Position

  • For data sets with n items:
    • Median position = (n + 1) / 2
    • If result is whole number: that's the median position
    • If result ends in 0.5: average the two positions around it (e.g., 10.5 means average of 10th and 11th values)

Finding Median in Frequency Tables

  • For tables showing frequency of values:
    • Find the median position using (n + 1) / 2
    • Start at the top of the table
    • Add frequencies until you reach the median position
    • The value at that position is the median

5. Worked Examples

Example 1: Combining Averages from Two Classes

Class A has 20 students with an average of 85. Class B has 30 students with an average of 90. What is the combined average for all students?

Solution:

  • Find total points for each class: Class A = 20 × 85 = 1,700; Class B = 30 × 90 = 2,700
  • Total points = 1,700 + 2,700 = 4,400
  • Total students = 20 + 30 = 50
  • Combined average = 4,400 / 50 = 88

Example 2: Weighted Average with Variables

Erica's golden retriever had two litters. The first had 7 puppies with average weight p pounds. The second had 5 puppies with average weight m pounds. What is the average weight w of all puppies?

Solution Method 1 - Algebra:

  • Total weight of first litter = 7p
  • Total weight of second litter = 5m
  • Total puppies = 12
  • Average weight w = (7p + 5m) / 12

Solution Method 2 - Pick Numbers:

  • Let p = 1 and m = 3 (pick easy numbers)
  • First litter total = 7 × 1 = 7 pounds
  • Second litter total = 5 × 3 = 15 pounds
  • Average = 22 / 12 = 11/6
  • Test answer choices with p = 1 and m = 3 to find which equals 11/6

Example 3: Weighted Average with Time

Vaivani decorated 23 cookies per minute for 10 minutes, then 14 cookies per minute for 20 minutes. What was her average rate?

Solution:

  • Use weighted average formula: [(23)(10) + (14)(20)] / 30
  • = (230 + 280) / 30
  • = 510 / 30 = 17 cookies per minute

Example 4: Finding Required Score for Target Average

Patricia's average on her first 4 tests is 88. What must she score on her 5th test to raise her average to 90?

Solution:

  • Current total = 88 × 4 = 352 points
  • Target total for 5 tests = 90 × 5 = 450 points
  • Required 5th test score = 450 - 352 = 98
  • This is 10 points above current average (98 - 88 = 10)

Example 5: Effect of Outliers

A neighborhood with 80 residents has mean household income of $90,250. A new family moves in with income of $1,859,000. Which measure changes the least?

Solution:

  • The new income is an outlier (far from other values)
  • Mean: will increase significantly due to the high outlier
  • Maximum: will definitely change to $1,859,000
  • Range: will increase dramatically
  • Median: changes minimally because it's just the middle value
  • Answer: Median changes the least

Example 6: Finding Median from Two Data Sets

Tree frog jumps (inches): 41, 18, 30, 8, 14, 33. Bullfrog jumps (inches): 38, 19, 66, 48, 45, 33, 10. If t is the median for tree frog and b is median for bullfrog, what is b - t?

Solution:

  • Arrange tree frog data: 8, 14, 18, 30, 33, 41 (6 values, even count)
  • Tree frog median t = (18 + 30) / 2 = 24
  • Arrange bullfrog data: 10, 19, 33, 38, 45, 48, 66 (7 values, odd count)
  • Bullfrog median b = 38 (middle value)
  • b - t = 38 - 24 = 14

Example 7: Median in a Frequency Table

51 students were surveyed about monthly cheeseburger consumption. The results: 0 burgers (7 students), 1 burger (19 students), 2-3 burgers (16 students), 4-5 burgers (9 students). What is the median?

Solution:

  • Find median position: (51 + 1) / 2 = 26th student
  • Add frequencies from top: 7 students (0 burgers) + 19 students (1 burger) = 26 students
  • The 26th student ate 1 burger
  • Median = 1

6. When to Use Each Measure

  • Use mean when:
    • Data has no extreme outliers
    • You need to represent the "typical" value mathematically
    • Combining multiple groups with different sizes
  • Use median when:
    • Data contains outliers that would skew the mean
    • You need the "middle" value regardless of extremes
    • Working with ranked or ordered data
  • Use mode when:
    • You need to know the most frequent value
    • Working with categorical data
  • Use range when:
    • You need to understand the spread of data
    • Identifying how dispersed values are

  • ✓Median is less affected by outliers than mean - use it when data has extreme values
