Understanding area, volume, and surface area is essential for ACT Math success—these concepts appear in 8-12 questions per test, nearly 20% of all math questions. This lesson teaches you the essential formulas for 2D shapes and 3D solids, how to apply them quickly, and tricks to avoid common mistakes that cost students points.

1. Area Formulas for Common Shapes

The ACT tests your ability to calculate area for basic shapes. You must memorize these formulas—they are NOT provided on the test.

  • Rectangle and Square
    • Rectangle: A = length × width
    • Square: A = s² (all sides equal)
    • If given perimeter, divide by 4 for squares or solve for individual sides for rectangles
  • Triangle
    • A = ½ × base × height
    • Height must be perpendicular to the base (not slant height)
    • For right triangles, use the two legs as base and height
  • Circle
    • A = πr² where r is radius
    • If given diameter, divide by 2 to get radius first
    • ACT often leaves answers in terms of π
  • Trapezoid
    • A = ½(b₁ + b₂) × h
    • Average of the two parallel bases, multiplied by height

2. Triangle Properties and Special Types

Understanding special right triangles, isosceles triangles, and Pythagorean theorem helps you solve problems quickly.

Right Triangles and Pythagorean Theorem

  • For any right triangle: a² + b² = c² where c is the hypotenuse
  • Common Pythagorean triples save time:
    • 3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17
    • Multiples: 6-8-10, 9-12-15, etc.

Special Right Triangle: 45-45-90

  • Side ratio: x : x : x√2
  • If each leg has length x, hypotenuse is x√2
  • Appears frequently with squares or diagonals

Special Right Triangle: 30-60-90

  • Side ratio: x : x√3 : 2x
  • Shortest side (opposite 30°): x
  • Middle side (opposite 60°): x√3
  • Hypotenuse: 2x
  • Often appears with equilateral triangles

Isosceles and Equilateral Triangles

  • Isosceles: Two equal sides, two equal angles
  • Equilateral: All three sides equal, all angles 60°
  • Equilateral area formula: A = (s²√3)/4

3. Volume Formulas for 3D Shapes

Volume measures the space inside a three-dimensional object. Memorize these formulas to save time.

  • Rectangular Prism
    • V = length × width × height
  • Cube
    • V = s³ where s is side length
    • All edges of a cube are equal
  • Cylinder
    • V = πr²h
    • Think: base area (πr²) × height (h)
  • Sphere
    • V = (4/3)πr³
    • Cube the radius (r³), not square it
  • Cone and Pyramid
    • V = (1/3) × base area × height
    • Cone base is circle: V = (1/3)πr²h
    • Pyramid base varies (square, triangle, etc.)

4. Surface Area and Advanced Applications

Surface area is the total area of all faces of a 3D shape. Breaking down shapes into their faces makes these problems manageable.

Surface Area Formulas

  • Rectangular Prism
    • SA = 2lw + 2lh + 2wh (6 faces)
    • Cube: SA = 6s² (all faces identical)
  • Cylinder
    • SA = 2πr² + 2πrh
    • 2πr² = top and bottom circles
    • 2πrh = lateral surface (when unrolled)

Composite Shapes Strategy

  • Step 1: Break into recognizable pieces
  • Step 2: Calculate each piece separately
  • Step 3: Add or subtract as needed
  • Example: Rectangle with circular pond = rectangle area - circle area

Scale Factor Relationships

  • If dimensions scale by factor k:
    • Area scales by k²
    • Volume scales by k³
  • Example: Double all dimensions (k=2)
    • Surface area increases 4×
    • Volume increases 8×

Example: Rectangular Garden Problem

Problem: A rectangular garden measures 20 ft by 15 ft with a circular pond of radius 3 ft. What is the planting area?

Solution:

  • Rectangle area: 20 × 15 = 300 ft²
  • Circle area: π(3)² = 9π ≈ 28.3 ft²
  • Planting area: 300 - 28.3 ≈ 271.7 ft²

5. When to Use These Formulas

  • Use area formulas when:
    • Problem asks about covering, painting, or tiling a surface
    • Finding regions in 2D coordinate planes
    • Calculating shaded regions
  • Use volume formulas when:
    • Problem asks about capacity, filling, or storage
    • Finding space inside containers
    • Calculating how much material fits inside a shape
  • Use surface area when:
    • Problem asks about wrapping, painting exterior surfaces
    • Finding total material needed to construct all sides
    • Calculating heat loss or material costs

  • ✓For triangles, always use perpendicular height, not slant height
  • ✓Recognize Pythagorean triples (3-4-5, 5-12-13) to save calculation time
  • ✓Know special right triangles: 45-45-90 (x:x:x√2) and 30-60-90 (x:x√3:2x)
  • ✓For composite shapes, break into simple pieces, calculate separately, then combine
