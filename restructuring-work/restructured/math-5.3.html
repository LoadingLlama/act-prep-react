Ratios and proportions are fundamental tools for making comparisons and solving relationship problems on the ACT. A ratio compares two quantities using a colon (like 2:3), while a proportion shows that two ratios are equal. Understanding direct proportions (where values move together) and indirect proportions (where values move opposite) is essential for ACT success.

1. What Are Ratios?

We use ratios to make comparisons between two things. Ratios do not give the exact number of items but instead allow us to concisely compare the relationship between two things at the same time.

  • Ratios show relationships, not exact amounts
    • If oranges to apples is 2:3, it means for every 2 oranges there are 3 apples
    • It does NOT mean there are exactly 2 oranges and 3 apples
  • The "x trick" for ratios:
    • Think of 2:3 as 2x:3x
    • You must multiply both parts by the same value to keep the ratio
    • This helps when given a ratio but not actual quantities

2. Four Types of Ratio Problems

On the ACT, you will need to solve ratio questions in four different ways:

Type 1: Given Ratio and Total, Find One Quantity

  • Use the "x trick" (2x + 3x = total)
  • Solve for x, then multiply to find the quantity you need
  • Alternatively, set up a proportion: part/total = ratio_part/ratio_total

Type 2: Unit Rate Conversion

  • Set up a proportion: amount1/amount2 = amount1'/amount2'
  • Cross multiply and solve
  • Useful for recipe problems, rate problems, etc.

Type 3: Connecting Two Ratios Through a Common Variable

  • Make the common variable equal in both ratios
  • Multiply one or both ratios to match the common variable
  • Then compare across to find the relationship you need

Type 4: Using Ratios with Perimeter or Other Formulas

  • Label sides using the ratio terms (8x and 5x)
  • Apply the geometric formula (e.g., perimeter = 2(8x) + 2(5x))
  • Solve for x, then find the required measurement

3. Ratio Examples

Example 1: Finding One Quantity from Ratio and Total

The ratio of red marbles to green marbles in a bag is 4:6. If there are a total of 80 marbles in the bag and all of the marbles in the bag are red or green, how many of the marbles are red?

A. 16
B. 20
C. 32
D. 48

Solution Method 1 - Use the "x trick":

  • Red marbles = 4x, Green marbles = 6x
  • Red + Green = Total: 4x + 6x = 80
  • 10x = 80, so x = 8
  • Red marbles = 4x = 4(8) = 32

Solution Method 2 - Set up a proportion:

  • Red marbles/Total marbles = 4/10 (since 4+6=10 total parts)
  • Red marbles/80 = 4/10
  • Cross multiply: 10 × Red = 4 × 80 = 320
  • Red = 32

Answer: C

Example 2: Unit Rate Conversion

Beth is baking her famous chocolate cupcakes for her mother's birthday party. For every 3 cupcakes, Beth uses 10 pieces of chocolate. If Beth needs to make 42 cupcakes for the party, how many pieces of chocolate will she need to buy?

A. 126
B. 70
C. 140
D. 140

Solution:

  • Set up proportion: pieces of chocolate/cupcakes = pieces of chocolate/cupcakes
  • 10/3 = x/42
  • Cross multiply: 3x = 10 × 42 = 420
  • x = 140

Answer: D

Example 3: Connecting Two Ratios

The ratio of x to y is 3:4. The ratio of y to z is 2:10. What is the ratio of x to z?

Solution:

  • x:y = 3:4, so y = 4
  • y:z = 2:10, but we need y = 4
  • Multiply the second ratio by 2: y:z = 4:20
  • Now with y = 4 in both ratios:
  • x:y:z = 3:4:20
  • Therefore x:z = 3:20

Answer: B (3:20)

Example 4: Ratios with Perimeter

In rectangle ABCD, the ratio of the lengths of side AB to side BC is 8:5. If the total perimeter of the rectangle is 156 feet, what is the length of the longer side of the rectangle?

A. 24
B. 30
C. 30
D. 48

Solution:

  • Label sides: AB = 8x (longer), BC = 5x (shorter)
  • Perimeter = 2(8x) + 2(5x) = 156
  • 16x + 10x = 156
  • 26x = 156, so x = 6
  • Longer side = 8x = 8(6) = 48

Answer: D

4. What Are Proportions?

On the ACT, you need to know the equations for two types of proportions:

Direct Proportions

  • In a direct proportion, y varies directly with x
  • x and y move together: as x increases, y increases
  • Equation: y = kx (where k is a constant)
  • Example: distance and time at constant speed

Indirect Proportions (Inverse Variation)

  • In an indirect proportion, y varies inversely with x
  • x and y move opposite: as x increases, y decreases
  • Equation: y = k/x (where k is a constant)
  • Can also vary with powers: y = k/x²
  • Example: speed and time for a fixed distance

5. Proportion Examples

Example 5: Direct Proportion

If y varies directly with x and y = 27 when x = 3, what is the value of x when y = 90?

A. 10
B. 15
C. 20
D. 30

Solution:

  • Equation: y = kx
  • Find k using given values: 27 = k(3), so k = 9
  • The equation is y = 9x
  • Plug in y = 90: 90 = 9x
  • x = 10

Answer: A

Example 6: Indirect Proportion with Powers

If y varies inversely with the square of x and y = 45 when x = 2, what is the value of y when x = 6?

A. 5
B. 15
C. 78
D. 135

Solution:

  • Equation: y = k/x² (note the square!)
  • Find k using given values: 45 = k/(2²) = k/4
  • k = 180
  • The equation is y = 180/x²
  • Plug in x = 6: y = 180/(6²) = 180/36 = 5

Answer: A

6. When to Use Each Approach

  • Use the "x trick" when:
    • You have a ratio and need to find actual quantities
    • The problem involves totals or sums
  • Use proportions when:
    • You're converting between rates or units
    • You see "varies directly" or "varies inversely"
  • Watch for:
    • Powers in variation problems (x², x³, etc.)
    • The need to connect two ratios through a common variable
    • Geometric formulas combined with ratios
