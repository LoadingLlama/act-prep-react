Working with fractions in equations can be challenging, but the ACT Math section offers multiple efficient strategies to simplify these problems. Whether you need to find a common denominator, eliminate fractions entirely, or convert to decimals, understanding these techniques will save you valuable time. Master these methods to turn intimidating fraction equations into straightforward arithmetic.

1. What Are Fraction Equations?

Fraction equations are algebraic equations that contain fractions with variables or numbers in the numerator or denominator. The key to solving them efficiently is choosing the right simplification strategy.

  • Key advantages of mastering fraction strategies:
    • Reduces calculation errors
    • Speeds up problem-solving time
    • Allows you to use your calculator effectively
    • Makes complex equations manageable
  • When to tackle fraction equations:
    • Equations with fractional coefficients
    • "Equivalent expression" questions
    • Word problems involving ratios and proportions

2. Three Methods for Solving Fraction Equations

Choose from three powerful strategies based on the problem type:

Method 1: Getting Rid of Fractions (Multiply by LCD)

  • For equations with only numbers in the denominator, eliminate fractions entirely
    • Find the least common multiple (LCD) of all denominators
    • Multiply every term by the LCD
    • The fractions cancel out, leaving whole numbers
    • Solve the resulting equation normally
  • Example: For denominators 4, 2, and 8, the LCD is 8
    • Multiply all terms by 8
    • Fractions disappear completely

Method 2: Convert Fractions to Decimals

  • Use your calculator to convert fractions to decimals before solving
    • Great if you struggle with fraction arithmetic
    • Convert each fraction to decimal using calculator
    • Solve equation using decimal values
    • Convert final answer back to fraction if needed
  • Most calculators can convert between fractions and decimals automatically

Method 3: Substitution for "Equivalent" Questions

  • For questions asking which expression is equivalent:
    • Pick a simple value for the variable (like x = 2)
    • Plug that value into the original expression
    • Calculate the result
    • Test answer choices to find which gives the same result

3. Step-by-Step: Eliminating Fractions

Step 1: Identify All Denominators

  • Look at every fraction in the equation
    • List all the denominators
    • Find the least common multiple (LCD)

Step 2: Multiply Every Term by the LCD

  • Distribute the LCD to all terms on both sides
    • Don't forget any terms!
    • Each fraction should cancel out

Step 3: Simplify and Solve

  • With fractions eliminated, solve the equation using standard algebra
    • Combine like terms
    • Isolate the variable
    • Check your answer

4. Worked Examples

Example 1: Splitting the Numerator

For simple fractions with multiple terms in the numerator, split them and simplify separately.

Solution:

  • Split the numerator into separate fractions
  • Simplify each fraction individually
  • Alternative: Use substitution with a test value for x

Example 2: Eliminating Fractions

What is the value of x in the equation: (x/4) - (1/2) = (x/8)?

Solution:

  • The least common multiple of 4, 2, and 8 is 8
  • Multiply all terms by 8: 8(x/4) - 8(1/2) = 8(x/8)
  • Simplifies to: 2x - 4 = x
  • Solve: x = 4
  • Shortcut: Backsolve by plugging answer choices into the original equation

Example 3: Converting to Decimals

For the equation: (3/4)x - 0.4 = (1/2)x, what is the value of x?

Solution:

  • Convert fractions to decimals: 3/4 = 0.75 and 1/2 = 0.5
  • Equation becomes: 0.75x - 0.4 = 0.5x
  • Solve: 0.25x = 0.4
  • x = 1.6
  • Use calculator to convert answer to fraction if needed

6. When to Use Each Method

Choose the most efficient strategy based on the problem type:

  • Eliminate fractions when:
    • Denominators are simple numbers
    • Finding LCD is easy
    • You're comfortable with fraction operations
  • Convert to decimals when:
    • You struggle with fraction arithmetic
    • Your calculator has fraction-to-decimal conversion
    • Answer choices can be converted to decimals
  • Use substitution when:
    • Question asks for an "equivalent expression"
    • Variables make direct solving difficult
    • Picking numbers is faster than algebra
  • Backsolve when:
    • Answer choices are concrete numbers
    • Plugging in is faster than solving
