Arcs and sectors represent portions of circles and appear frequently on the ACT Math section. Understanding the relationship between central angles, arc length, and sector area helps you solve these problems efficiently using proportional reasoning.

1. What Are Arcs and Sectors?

An arc is a portion of a circle's circumference, while a sector is a pie-shaped slice of a circle bounded by two radii and an arc. Both are determined by the central angle that cuts them from the circle.

  • Key circle fundamentals:
    • Radius (r): Distance from center to any point on the circle
    • Diameter (d): d = 2r, distance across through center
    • Circumference (C): C = 2πr or C = πd
    • Area (A): A = πr²
  • Central angle properties:
    • Vertex at the center of the circle
    • Measured in degrees (360° in full circle) or radians (2π in full circle)
    • Central angle measure equals the arc measure it intercepts
  • Important angle relationships:
    • Inscribed angle = (1/2) × Central angle (when both intercept same arc)
    • Common conversions: 90° = π/2, 180° = π, 270° = 3π/2, 360° = 2π radians

2. Calculating Arc Length

Arc length depends on two factors: the circle's circumference and the fraction of the circle the arc represents. The formulas differ based on whether angles are in degrees or radians.

Step 1: Identify the Angle Measure

  • Check whether the central angle is given in degrees or radians
    • Degrees: Look for the ° symbol (e.g., 72°, 120°)
    • Radians: Often shown with π (e.g., π/3, 2π/5) or as decimals

Step 2: Apply the Correct Formula

  • For degrees: Arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr
    • θ is the central angle in degrees
    • θ/360° represents the fraction of the full circle
  • For radians: Arc length = rθ (much simpler!)
    • θ is the central angle in radians
    • This elegant formula makes radian calculations faster

Step 3: Simplify and Calculate

  • Simplify fractions before multiplying
    • Recognize common fractions: 72° = 1/5 of 360°, 120° = 1/3 of 360°
    • Leave answers in terms of π unless asked to approximate

Example 1: Arc Length with Degrees

A circle has radius 10 inches. What is the arc length for a central angle of 72°?

Solution:

  • Arc length = (72°/360°) × 2π(10)
  • Arc length = (1/5) × 20π
  • Arc length = 4π inches ≈ 12.6 inches
  • Interpretation: The arc is 1/5 of the total circumference (because 72° is 1/5 of 360°)

Example 2: Arc Length with Radians

A circle has radius 8 cm. What is the arc length for a central angle of π/3 radians?

Solution:

  • Arc length = rθ = 8 × (π/3) = 8π/3 cm ≈ 8.4 cm
  • Notice how much faster the radian formula is!

Example 3: Working Backward from Arc Length

An arc length is 15π cm on a circle with radius 18 cm. What is the central angle in degrees?

Solution:

  • Using arc length = (θ/360°) × 2πr
  • 15π = (θ/360°) × 2π(18)
  • 15π = (θ/360°) × 36π
  • 15 = (θ/360°) × 36
  • θ/360° = 15/36 = 5/12
  • θ = 360° × (5/12) = 150°

3. Calculating Sector Area

Sector area follows the same proportional logic as arc length, but uses the circle's area instead of circumference.

Step 1: Choose the Right Formula

  • For degrees: Sector area = (θ/360°) × πr²
    • This is the fraction of the circle multiplied by the total circle area
  • For radians: Sector area = (1/2)r²θ
    • More compact and elegant formula

Step 2: Calculate and Verify

  • Use the same proportional reasoning as arc length
    • A 45° angle creates a sector that is 1/8 of the circle
    • Double-check: Does your answer make sense relative to the total circle area?

Example 4: Sector Area with Degrees

A circle has radius 12 feet. What is the area of a sector with central angle 45°?

Solution:

  • Sector area = (45°/360°) × π(12)²
  • Sector area = (1/8) × 144π
  • Sector area = 18π ft² ≈ 56.5 ft²
  • Check: 45° is 1/8 of 360°, so the sector is 1/8 of the total circle area ✓

Example 5: Sector Perimeter

A sector has radius 9 cm and central angle 60°. What is its perimeter?

Solution:

  • Sector perimeter = arc length + 2r
  • Arc length = (60°/360°) × 2π(9) = (1/6) × 18π = 3π cm
  • Sector perimeter = 3π + 2(9) = 3π + 18 cm ≈ 27.4 cm

4. Special Applications

Proportional Reasoning

The key insight for all arc and sector problems:

  • Angle/360° = Arc/Circumference = Sector area/Circle area
    • If you know one relationship, you can find the others
    • This often eliminates the need to memorize formulas

Segment Area

  • A segment is the region between a chord and the arc it cuts off
    • Segment area = Sector area - Triangle area
    • The triangle is formed by the two radii and the chord

Major and Minor Arcs

  • Minor arc: Central angle < 180°
  • Major arc: Central angle > 180°
  • Minor + Major arc angles always equal 360°

Example 6: Real-World Application

A 16-inch diameter pizza is cut into 8 equal slices. What is the area of one slice?

Solution:

  • Radius = 16/2 = 8 inches
  • Central angle per slice = 360°/8 = 45°
  • Sector area = (45°/360°) × π(8)² = (1/8) × 64π = 8π ≈ 25.1 square inches

5. When to Use These Techniques

Arc and sector formulas work best when:

  • You're given a central angle and need to find arc length or sector area
  • The problem involves portions of circles (slices, wedges, partial circles)
  • You need to work backward from arc length or sector area to find angles
  • The problem involves rotating objects or circular motion

Common mistakes to avoid:

  • Using diameter instead of radius in formulas
  • Mixing degrees and radians (check which formula to use!)
  • Forgetting to multiply by the fraction (arc length is not just 2πr)
  • Confusing arc length with sector area
