Taking square roots in algebraic equations is a crucial skill for the ACT Math section. When solving equations with squared variables, you must add a plus-minus sign (±) to capture both solutions. Understanding this rule prevents common mistakes and ensures you get full credit for quadratic solutions.

1. The Plus-Minus Rule for Squared Variables

When solving equations where a variable is squared, you need to understand the critical difference between taking the square root of a number versus taking the square root of a variable term.

The Core Rule

  • When taking the square root of a squared variable (like x²), add a ± sign
  • Example: If x² = 9, then x = ±3
    • This gives two solutions: x = 3 or x = -3
    • Both work because (-3)² = 9 and 3² = 9
  • Important distinction: √9 = 3 only (no ± sign for pure numbers)

Why This Matters

  • Squaring a positive or negative number gives the same result
    • (-5)² = 25
    • (5)² = 25
  • When you undo the square by taking the square root, both original values must be considered
  • Missing the negative solution is one of the most common ACT Math mistakes

2. Step-by-Step Process for Taking Square Roots

Follow these steps to correctly solve equations with squared variables.

Step 1: Isolate the Squared Term

  • Get the x² (or other squared variable) by itself on one side
  • Use addition, subtraction, multiplication, or division as needed
  • Example: x² + 10 = 46 becomes x² = 36

Step 2: Take the Square Root of Both Sides

  • Apply √ to both sides of the equation
  • Remember: √x² = |x|, which means x or -x

Step 3: Add the ± Sign

  • Place a ± in front of the number on the right side
  • Example: x = ±6
  • This represents two solutions: x = 6 or x = -6

Step 4: Write Both Solutions

  • Separate the ± into two distinct answers
  • x = 6 and x = -6
  • On the ACT, the correct answer will typically include both values

3. Examples of Solving with Square Roots

Example 1: Basic Squared Variable

Problem: What value(s) of x are solutions to the equation x² + 10 = 46?

A. -6
B. 6
C. 0, 6
D. -6, 6

Solution:

  • Step 1: Isolate x²
    • x² + 10 = 46
    • x² = 36
  • Step 2: Take square root of both sides
    • √(x²) = √36
  • Step 3: Add ± sign
    • x = ±6
  • Step 4: Write both solutions
    • x = 6 or x = -6
  • Answer: D

Example 2: Equation Requiring Simplification

Problem: Solve for x: 3x² - 12 = 60

Solution:

  • Add 12 to both sides: 3x² = 72
  • Divide by 3: x² = 24
  • Take square root: x = ±√24
  • Simplify: x = ±2√6
  • Two solutions: x = 2√6 or x = -2√6

Example 3: Common Mistake to Avoid

Incorrect approach: x² = 9, therefore x = 3

Why it's wrong: You're missing the negative solution

Correct approach:

  • x² = 9
  • x = ±3
  • So x = 3 or x = -3

4. When to Use the ± Sign vs When NOT to Use It

Use ± Sign When:

  • Taking the square root of a squared variable (x²)
    • Example: x² = 25 → x = ±5
  • Solving quadratic equations by taking square roots
    • Example: (x - 3)² = 16 → x - 3 = ±4
  • Any algebraic equation where you're undoing a square

Do NOT Use ± Sign When:

  • Taking the square root of only a number (no variable)
    • Example: √9 = 3 (not ±3)
    • Square roots of numbers are always positive by definition
  • Simplifying radical expressions
    • Example: √25 = 5
  • Using the Pythagorean theorem
    • Example: If a² + b² = c², and you know a and b, then c = √(a² + b²) is positive
    • Lengths are always positive

5. Practice Strategy for Test Day

  • Look for both solutions in answer choices
    • ACT often includes answers with just the positive solution
    • These are trap answers for students who forget the ± sign
    • The correct answer usually shows both values
  • Check your work
    • Plug both solutions back into the original equation
    • Both should work if you solved correctly
  • Use your calculator wisely
    • Calculators only give positive square roots
    • You must remember to consider the negative solution manually
