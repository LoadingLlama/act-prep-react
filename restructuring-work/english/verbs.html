Verb questions are among the most frequently tested topics on the ACT English Test, accounting for approximately 10-15% of all grammar questions. The ACT tests three critical verb concepts: subject-verb agreement, verb tense consistency, and correct verb forms. The test deliberately makes these questions challenging by separating subjects from verbs with long phrases, using compound and collective subjects, and testing irregular verb forms. Once you master identifying the true subject and recognizing context clues for tense, verb questions become quick, predictable points.

1. Subject-Verb Agreement

Subject-verb agreement means the verb must match its subject in number (singular or plural). This sounds simple, but the ACT makes it challenging by placing distracting words and phrases between the subject and verb. The key is learning to identify the true subject.

Basic Agreement Rules

Singular subjects take singular verbs (add -s or -es), while plural subjects take plural verbs (no -s ending).

✓ Singular: The student walks to class. (one student, verb adds -s)

✓ Plural: The students walk to class. (multiple students, verb has no -s)

Memory Trick: In present tense, -s on the noun means no -s on the verb, and vice versa. You can't have -s on both or neither.

Ignoring Prepositional Phrases

The most common ACT trick is placing a prepositional phrase between the subject and verb. The object of a preposition is NEVER the subject. Cross out prepositional phrases to find the true subject.

✓ Correct: The box of tools is in the garage.

→ Subject is "box" (singular), not "tools"; ignore "of tools"

✗ Wrong: The collection of paintings are valuable.

→ Subject is "collection" (singular), not "paintings"; should be "is valuable"

Common prepositions to watch for: of, in, on, at, by, with, from, about, between, among, during, without, including

Compound Subjects

Compound subjects joined by "and" are plural and take plural verbs. Subjects joined by "or" or "nor" take verbs that agree with the nearest subject.

✓ "And" (plural): The teacher and the student are meeting after class.

✓ "Or/Nor" (nearest): Either the teacher or the students are responsible.

✓ "Or/Nor" (nearest): Either the students or the teacher is responsible.

Indefinite Pronouns

Many indefinite pronouns are singular and take singular verbs, even though they may seem plural.

Always singular: each, every, either, neither, one, everyone, everybody, anyone, anybody, someone, somebody, no one, nobody, nothing

✓ Correct: Everyone is ready for the test.

✓ Correct: Each of the students has a textbook.

Always plural: both, few, many, several

✓ Correct: Both of the teachers are attending.

Collective Nouns and Inverted Sentences

Collective nouns (team, group, family, committee) are usually singular. In inverted sentences ("There is/are..."), the subject comes after the verb.

✓ Collective: The team is winning. (team as one unit)

✓ Inverted: There are many reasons for this decision.

→ Subject is "reasons" (plural), not "there"

2. Verb Tense Consistency

Verb tense shows when an action occurs. The ACT tests whether you can maintain consistent tense within a passage and choose the correct tense based on context clues and time markers.

The Six Main Tenses

Understanding the six main tenses and their uses is essential for ACT verb questions.

Present: She walks every day. (habitual action, general truth)

Past: She walked yesterday. (completed action)

Future: She will walk tomorrow. (action yet to occur)

Present Perfect: She has walked for miles. (past action with present relevance)

Past Perfect: She had walked before lunch. (action completed before another past action)

Future Perfect: She will have walked 10 miles by noon. (action to be completed by a future time)

Context Clues and Time Markers

Look for time markers in the passage to determine the correct tense. The ACT provides context through dates, time words, and surrounding verbs.

Present markers: today, now, every day, usually, always, currently

Past markers: yesterday, last week, in 1995, ago, when I was young

Future markers: tomorrow, next week, soon, in the future, eventually

Perfect markers: already, just, since, for, before, by the time, yet

Maintaining Consistency Within a Passage

Unless there's a clear reason to shift tenses (like a flashback or future projection), maintain the same tense throughout a passage. Check surrounding sentences for the dominant tense.

✓ Consistent: Maria walked to the library. She checked out three books and returned home before dinner.

→ All past tense; consistent timeline

✗ Inconsistent: Maria walked to the library. She checks out three books and returned home before dinner.

→ Mixing past and present inappropriately

Using Perfect Tenses on the ACT

Present perfect (has/have + past participle) connects past to present. Past perfect (had + past participle) shows one past action occurred before another past action.

✓ Present Perfect: I have lived here for five years. (started in past, continues now)

✓ Past Perfect: By the time she arrived, I had finished cooking.

→ Cooking (earlier) happened before arriving (later), both in past

3. Correct Verb Forms

The ACT tests whether you know the correct forms of verbs, particularly irregular verbs and the difference between simple past and past participles. Many common errors involve confusing these forms.

Regular vs. Irregular Verbs

Regular verbs form past tense and past participle by adding -ed. Irregular verbs have unique forms that must be memorized.

Regular: walk → walked → (have) walked

Irregular: go → went → (have) gone

Irregular: see → saw → (have) seen

Common Irregular Verbs Tested on the ACT

These irregular verbs appear frequently on the ACT. Know all three forms (present → past → past participle):

begin → began → (have) begun

break → broke → (have) broken

bring → brought → (have) brought

come → came → (have) come

do → did → (have) done

drink → drank → (have) drunk

eat → ate → (have) eaten

give → gave → (have) given

lie (recline) → lay → (have) lain

lay (place) → laid → (have) laid

ring → rang → (have) rung

rise → rose → (have) risen

run → ran → (have) run

see → saw → (have) seen

sing → sang → (have) sung

sink → sank → (have) sunk

speak → spoke → (have) spoken

swim → swam → (have) swum

take → took → (have) taken

write → wrote → (have) written

Past vs. Past Participle

Use simple past for completed actions. Use past participle (with has/have/had) for perfect tenses. Never use past participle alone without a helping verb.

✓ Simple past: I saw the movie yesterday.

✓ Past participle: I have seen the movie three times.

✗ Wrong: I seen the movie yesterday.

→ Past participle needs a helping verb; use "saw" for simple past

Lie vs. Lay, Rise vs. Raise, Sit vs. Set

These commonly confused verb pairs differ in transitivity. One takes an object (transitive), the other doesn't (intransitive).

lie (intransitive): to recline → I lie down every afternoon.

lay (transitive): to place → I lay the book on the table.

rise (intransitive): to go up → The sun rises in the east.

raise (transitive): to lift → Please raise your hand.

sit (intransitive): to be seated → I sit in this chair.

set (transitive): to place → I set the cup on the counter.

4. Advanced Verb Concepts

Beyond basic agreement and tense, the ACT tests more subtle verb issues including mood, voice, and proper use of helping verbs. Understanding these advanced concepts helps you catch errors that less-prepared students miss.

Subjunctive Mood

The subjunctive mood expresses wishes, suggestions, or hypothetical situations. Use the base form of the verb (without -s) in certain constructions.

✓ Correct: I suggest that she study more. (not "studies")

✓ Correct: It is important that he arrive on time. (not "arrives")

✓ Correct: If I were rich, I would travel the world. (not "was")

Common subjunctive triggers: suggest, recommend, request, demand, insist, require, propose, essential, important, necessary, if/wish statements

Active vs. Passive Voice

Active voice (subject performs action) is generally preferred for clarity. Passive voice (subject receives action) is acceptable when the actor is unknown or unimportant.

✓ Active (preferred): The scientist discovered a new element.

✓ Passive (acceptable): A new element was discovered by the scientist.

ACT Strategy: If an answer choice uses passive voice without good reason, it's often wrong. Choose active voice when both are grammatically correct.

Modal Verbs and Helping Verbs

Modal verbs (can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would) combine with base verbs to express possibility, necessity, or permission.

✓ Correct: You should study for the test.

✓ Correct: She might have forgotten about the meeting.

✗ Wrong: You should to study. → ✓ Correct: You should study.

→ Modal verbs take base form, not infinitive

Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences show cause-and-effect relationships. Match the verb forms correctly between the if-clause and the main clause.

Present real: If it rains, we will cancel the picnic.

Past unreal: If I had studied, I would have passed.

Present unreal: If I were president, I would change that law.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ ✓Subject-verb agreement: Ignore prepositional phrases between subject and verb; singular subjects take singular verbs (-s ending), plural subjects take plural verbs (no -s); indefinite pronouns like "each," "everyone," and "neither" are singular
  • ✓ ✓Verb tense: Use context clues and time markers to determine correct tense; maintain consistency within passages unless context requires a shift; perfect tenses (has/have/had + past participle) connect past to present or show one past action before another
  • ✓ ✓Irregular verbs: Know three forms (present → past → past participle) for commonly tested verbs; use simple past for completed actions, past participle only with helping verbs (has/have/had); never use past participle alone
  • ✓ ✓Confusing pairs: lie (recline, intransitive) vs. lay (place, transitive); rise (go up, intransitive) vs. raise (lift, transitive); sit (be seated, intransitive) vs. set (place, transitive)
  • ✓ ✓Advanced concepts: Subjunctive mood uses base verb form after suggest/require/essential and "were" for hypothetical; prefer active voice over passive when both work; modal verbs take base form, not infinitive
