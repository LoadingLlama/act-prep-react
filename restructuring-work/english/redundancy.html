Redundancy and wordiness questions test whether you can identify and eliminate unnecessary repetition and verbose expressions on the ACT English Test. The ACT values clarity and conciseness—saying what needs to be said in the fewest necessary words. These questions appear 6-10 times per test and require you to recognize when information is repeated, when phrases can be shortened without losing meaning, and when "DELETE the underlined portion" is the best answer. Mastering redundancy means understanding that less is often more.

1. Identifying Redundancy

Redundancy occurs when a word, phrase, or idea is repeated unnecessarily. The repeated information adds no new meaning and should be eliminated.

Common Redundant Pairs

Certain word combinations are inherently redundant because both words convey the same meaning.

✗ Redundant: past history → ✓ history

  • ✗ Redundant: advance warning → ✓ warning
  • ✗ Redundant: end result → ✓ result
  • ✗ Redundant: repeat again → ✓ repeat
  • ✗ Redundant: return back → ✓ return
  • ✗ Redundant: combine together → ✓ combine

Information Already Stated

Watch for phrases that repeat information already present in the sentence or surrounding context.

✗ Redundant: The museum, which was founded in 1950, was established in 1950.

✓ Concise: The museum was founded in 1950.

Implied Meanings

Some words or phrases contain implied meanings that make additional qualifiers unnecessary.

✗ 12 noon → ✓ noon (noon always means 12)

✗ a true fact → ✓ a fact (facts are always true)

✗ completely eliminate → ✓ eliminate (eliminate means remove completely)

Visual or Descriptive Redundancy

Descriptions that are obvious from context should be omitted.

✗ Redundant: The round circle had a diameter of 10 feet.

→ Circles are always round

✓ Concise: The circle had a diameter of 10 feet.

2. Wordy Phrases and Expressions

Wordiness occurs when a phrase uses more words than necessary to convey an idea. The ACT prefers direct, concise language over verbose expressions.

Common Wordy Phrases and Concise Alternatives

✗ due to the fact that → ✓ because

✗ in spite of the fact that → ✓ although/despite

✗ at this point in time → ✓ now

✗ for the purpose of → ✓ to/for

✗ in the event that → ✓ if

✗ it is important to note that → ✓ [delete and state directly]

✗ what I mean to say is → ✓ [delete and state directly]

Unnecessary That/Who/Which Clauses

Sometimes relative clauses can be reduced to simpler structures.

✗ Wordy: The scientist who was responsible for the discovery won an award.

✓ Concise: The scientist responsible for the discovery won an award.

Passive Voice Wordiness

Active voice is often more concise than passive voice.

✗ Wordy (passive): The experiment was conducted by the students.

✓ Concise (active): The students conducted the experiment.

Strategy: Count the words. If one answer has 8 words and another has 4 words with the same meaning, choose the 4-word version.

3. When DELETE Is the Answer

One of the four answer choices for many ACT questions is "DELETE the underlined portion." This is correct more often than students expect—approximately 10-15% of the time it appears as an option.

When to Choose DELETE

Choose DELETE when the underlined portion adds no new or necessary information.

Choose DELETE when:

• The information repeats something already stated

• The phrase is obvious or self-evident

• The sentence is clearer and more direct without it

• All other options are wordy or redundant

Example: DELETE in Action

Original: The ancient ruins, which are very old, attract many tourists.

Analysis: "Ancient" already means "very old." The underlined phrase is redundant.

✓ Best answer: DELETE the underlined portion.

Result: The ancient ruins attract many tourists.

When NOT to DELETE

Don't choose DELETE if removing the phrase makes the sentence unclear, changes the meaning, or creates a grammatical error.

4. ACT-Specific Redundancy Patterns

The ACT tests specific redundancy patterns repeatedly. Learning these common traps helps you spot errors quickly.

Numbers and Time Redundancy

✗ in the year of 2020 → ✓ in 2020

✗ the month of January → ✓ January

✗ a period of three years → ✓ three years

Location and Direction Redundancy

✗ close proximity → ✓ proximity / close

✗ surround on all sides → ✓ surround

Action and Process Redundancy

✗ first begin → ✓ begin

✗ continue on → ✓ continue

✗ plan ahead → ✓ plan

Comparison Redundancy

✗ same identical → ✓ same / identical

✗ completely unique → ✓ unique

✗ each individual → ✓ each

ACT Strategy: If you can remove a word or phrase without changing the meaning or creating an error, remove it. The ACT almost always prefers the most concise grammatically correct option.

Hidden Separator

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ ✓Redundancy: Eliminate unnecessary repetition like "past history," "repeat again," "return back," and "combine together"; watch for information that's already stated elsewhere in the sentence or implied by other words
  • ✓ ✓Wordy phrases: Replace verbose expressions with concise alternatives—"due to the fact that" → "because"; "at this point in time" → "now"; "in the event that" → "if"
  • ✓ ✓DELETE option: Choose "DELETE the underlined portion" when the phrase repeats information, is self-evident, or makes the sentence clearer without it; DELETE is correct 10-15% of the time it appears
  • ✓ ✓Common ACT patterns: Watch for time/number redundancy ("in the year of 2020"), location redundancy ("close proximity"), action redundancy ("first begin," "continue on"), and comparison redundancy ("completely unique")
  • ✓ ✓General principle: When choosing between grammatically correct options, the ACT prefers the most concise version; if you can remove words without changing meaning or creating errors, do it
