Modifier questions test one of the most logical concepts on the ACT English Test: modifying phrases and clauses must be placed next to the words they describe. When modifiers are misplaced or dangling, sentences become illogical or unintentionally humorous. The ACT tests three main modifier issues: misplaced modifiers (placed too far from what they modify), dangling modifiers (missing the word they should modify), and squinting modifiers (unclear which word they modify). Once you learn to identify what each modifier describes and place it correctly, these questions become straightforward.

1. Understanding Modifiers

A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that describes, limits, or adds detail to another word. Modifiers include adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases, participial phrases, and various clause types. The fundamental rule: place modifiers as close as possible to the words they modify.

Types of Modifiers

Understanding the different types of modifiers helps you spot modifier errors on the ACT.

Adjective: The red car is fast. (modifies "car")

Adverb: She ran quickly. (modifies "ran")

Prepositional phrase: The book on the table is mine. (modifies "book")

Participial phrase: Running down the street, John tripped. (modifies "John")

Relative clause: The student who studied hard passed. (modifies "student")

The Proximity Principle

Place modifiers next to what they modify. The farther away a modifier is from its target, the more likely confusion will occur.

✓ Clear: I nearly read the entire book yesterday.

→ "Nearly" modifies "read" (almost read it)

✗ Confusing: I read nearly the entire book yesterday.

→ "Nearly" incorrectly seems to modify "entire" (almost entire?)

Introductory Modifying Phrases

When a phrase begins a sentence and modifies the subject, the subject must immediately follow the comma. This is the most common modifier pattern tested on the ACT.

✓ Correct: Exhausted from the long hike, Maria went straight to bed.

→ "Exhausted from the long hike" correctly modifies "Maria"

Golden Rule: Whatever comes immediately after the comma must be what the opening phrase describes.

2. Misplaced Modifiers

A misplaced modifier is positioned too far from the word it modifies, creating confusion or unintentional meaning. The modifier exists and has a logical target in the sentence, but poor placement makes the sentence unclear or illogical.

Misplaced Adjective Phrases

Adjective phrases should be placed immediately after the nouns they modify.

✗ Misplaced: The dog caught the frisbee running across the yard.

→ This says the frisbee was running across the yard

✓ Correct: Running across the yard, the dog caught the frisbee.

✓ Correct: The dog, running across the yard, caught the frisbee.

Misplaced Adverb Modifiers

Words like "only," "almost," "nearly," "just," "even," and "merely" should be placed immediately before the word they modify. Moving these words changes the sentence meaning.

I only told Maria the secret. (I didn't tell anyone else)

I told only Maria the secret. (Maria was the only person)

I told Maria only the secret. (the secret and nothing else)

✗ Misplaced: She nearly spent $500 on textbooks.

→ Did she nearly spend it, or spend nearly $500?

✓ Correct: She spent nearly $500 on textbooks.

Limiting Modifiers and Placement

Limiting modifiers (only, just, merely, simply, even, almost, nearly) are precise. Their position dramatically affects meaning.

Only John passed the test. (no one else passed)

John only passed the test. (he passed but didn't excel)

John passed only the test. (he passed the test but nothing else)

Squinting Modifiers

A squinting modifier is positioned between two words and could logically modify either one, creating ambiguity.

✗ Squinting: Students who practice regularly often improve their scores.

→ Does "often" modify "practice" or "improve"?

✓ Clear: Students who practice often regularly improve their scores.

✓ Clear: Students who regularly practice often improve their scores.

3. Dangling Modifiers

A dangling modifier occurs when the word it's supposed to modify is missing from the sentence entirely. This is the most serious modifier error and appears frequently on the ACT. Dangling modifiers often create illogical or humorous meanings.

Identifying Dangling Modifiers

When an introductory phrase modifies a subject, that subject must appear immediately after the comma. If a different noun appears, the modifier dangles.

✗ Dangling: Walking to school, the rain started pouring.

→ This says the rain was walking to school; who was walking?

✓ Correct: Walking to school, I got caught in the pouring rain.

✓ Correct: While I was walking to school, the rain started pouring.

Dangling Participial Phrases

Participial phrases (-ing or -ed verb forms) at the beginning of sentences must modify the subject that follows.

✗ Dangling: Excited about the concert, my ticket was in my hand.

→ The ticket was excited? Who was excited?

✓ Correct: Excited about the concert, I held my ticket in my hand.

Dangling Infinitive Phrases

Infinitive phrases (to + verb) that begin sentences must have a clear actor in the main clause.

✗ Dangling: To improve her grade, extra credit was completed by Maria.

→ Who wanted to improve the grade? The passive voice hides the actor

✓ Correct: To improve her grade, Maria completed extra credit.

Fixing Dangling Modifiers: Two Strategies

You can fix dangling modifiers in two ways: add the missing subject after the comma, or convert the modifier into a full clause with its own subject.

Original (dangling): After studying for hours, the test seemed easy.

✓ Fix #1 (add subject): After studying for hours, I found the test seemed easy.

✓ Fix #2 (add clause): After I studied for hours, the test seemed easy.

ACT Strategy: When you see an introductory phrase, immediately check what comes after the comma. If it's not the word being described, you've found a dangling modifier error.

4. Advanced Modifier Issues

Beyond basic misplaced and dangling modifiers, the ACT tests more subtle issues including relative clause placement, modifier overload, and logical modification. Mastering these advanced concepts helps you catch errors that many students miss.

Misplaced Relative Clauses

Relative clauses (starting with who, which, that) should immediately follow the noun they modify.

✗ Misplaced: The car was found by police that had been stolen.

→ This suggests the police had been stolen

✓ Correct: The car that had been stolen was found by police.

Split Infinitives

While split infinitives (placing a word between "to" and the verb) are increasingly accepted in modern English, the ACT may test your awareness of them. Generally, avoid splitting infinitives when an alternative sounds natural.

Split infinitive: She decided to quickly finish her homework.

✓ Preferred: She decided to finish her homework quickly.

Note: Sometimes split infinitives are the clearest option ("to boldly go"). On the ACT, if moving the modifier creates awkwardness, the split infinitive may be acceptable.

Modifier Overload and Clarity

Too many modifiers, especially when stacked together, create confusion. Sometimes the best fix is restructuring the sentence.

✗ Confusing: The extremely highly successfully performing students received awards.

→ Too many modifiers create awkwardness

✓ Clear: The students who performed extremely well received awards.

Logical Modification

Beyond grammar rules, modifiers must make logical sense. Ask: Can this modifier actually describe this noun?

✗ Illogical: As a young child, my mother told me stories.

→ Grammatically this says "mother" was the young child

✓ Logical: When I was a young child, my mother told me stories.

✓ Logical: As a young parent, my mother told me stories.

ACT Modifier Question Pattern

On the ACT, modifier questions typically offer four answer choices that reposition the modifier or restructure the sentence. The correct answer will place the modifier logically next to what it describes.

Strategy: For each answer choice, ask: "What does the opening phrase describe?" Then check if that word comes immediately after the comma.
