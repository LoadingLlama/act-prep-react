Parallel structure (also called parallelism) is one of the most elegant and testable grammar concepts on the ACT English Test. When listing items, comparing ideas, or connecting phrases with coordinating conjunctions, all elements must be in the same grammatical form. The ACT tests parallelism in lists, parallelism with conjunctions, parallelism in comparisons, and correlative conjunction pairs. Once you recognize the pattern—matching verb forms, noun types, and phrase structures—parallel structure questions become some of the quickest points on the test.

1. Parallelism in Lists and Series

When listing three or more items, all items must use the same grammatical structure. If one item is a noun, all should be nouns. If one is a verb phrase, all should be verb phrases. This applies to lists with commas, semicolons, or conjunctions.

Parallel Nouns and Noun Phrases

When listing nouns, keep all items as nouns or noun phrases—don't mix with gerunds, verbs, or clauses.

✓ Parallel: I enjoy reading, writing, and hiking.

→ All gerunds (-ing nouns)

✗ Not parallel: I enjoy reading, to write, and hiking.

→ Mixing gerund, infinitive, and gerund

✓ Alternative (all infinitives): I enjoy to read, to write, and to hike.

Parallel Verb Phrases

When listing actions, keep all verbs in the same form and tense.

✓ Parallel: She studied for the test, reviewed her notes, and practiced with flashcards.

→ All simple past tense verbs

✗ Not parallel: She studied for the test, reviewing her notes, and had practiced with flashcards.

→ Mixing simple past, present participle, and past perfect

Parallel Adjectives and Adverbs

When listing descriptive words, keep all items as single adjectives or all as prepositional phrases—don't mix.

✓ Parallel: The movie was exciting, suspenseful, and unpredictable.

→ All adjectives

✗ Not parallel: The movie was exciting, suspenseful, and had an unpredictable ending.

→ Mixing adjectives with a clause

Parallel Clauses

When listing complete clauses, maintain the same structure for each clause.

✓ Parallel: The teacher explained why the answer was correct, how the solution worked, and when to use this strategy.

→ All dependent clauses starting with question words

Quick Test: Read list items one at a time with the introductory phrase. Each should sound grammatically complete on its own: "I enjoy...reading," "I enjoy...writing," "I enjoy...hiking."

2. Parallelism with Coordinating Conjunctions

When joining elements with coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, yet, so—FANBOYS), the elements on both sides must be parallel. This applies to two-item pairs, not just lists of three or more.

Parallel Elements Joined by "And"

Whatever comes before "and" must match the grammatical form of what comes after "and."

✓ Parallel: The essay was well-written and persuasive.

→ Both adjectives

✗ Not parallel: The essay was well-written and persuaded the reader.

→ Mixing adjective with verb phrase

Parallel Elements Joined by "But" or "Or"

The same parallelism rule applies to all coordinating conjunctions, not just "and."

✓ Parallel: You can study now or review later.

→ Both verb phrases

✓ Parallel: She is intelligent but modest.

→ Both adjectives

Verbs with Shared Subjects

When one subject performs multiple actions joined by conjunctions, keep all verbs parallel.

✓ Parallel: Maria woke up, ate breakfast, and left for school.

→ All simple past tense verbs

✗ Not parallel: Maria woke up, ate breakfast, and was leaving for school.

→ Mixing simple past with past continuous

Prepositional Phrase Parallelism

When using multiple prepositional phrases, maintain parallel structure. Watch for unnecessary repetition or inconsistent prepositions.

✓ Parallel: She searched in the closet, under the bed, and behind the dresser.

→ All prepositional phrases (preposition + article + noun)

✗ Not parallel: She searched in the closet, under the bed, and behind.

→ Last phrase incomplete

3. Correlative Conjunctions and Parallelism

Correlative conjunctions are pairs of connecting words that require especially strict parallelism. The structure that follows the first word must match the structure after the second word. Common pairs: either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, both...and, whether...or.

Either...Or and Neither...Nor

Whatever grammatical structure follows "either" must match what follows "or."

✓ Parallel: You can either call me or text me.

→ Both: verb + object

✗ Not parallel: You can either call me or sending a text.

→ "call" (verb) doesn't match "sending" (gerund)

✓ Parallel: She is neither interested in math nor skilled at science.

→ Both: adjective + prepositional phrase

Not Only...But Also

This pair requires precise placement. What follows "not only" must parallel what follows "but also."

✓ Parallel: She not only studied hard but also practiced regularly.

→ Both: verb + adverb

✗ Not parallel: She not only studied hard but also was practicing regularly.

→ Mixing simple past with past continuous

Both...And

"Both...and" connects two parallel elements with equal emphasis.

✓ Parallel: The project is both challenging and rewarding.

→ Both adjectives

✗ Not parallel: The project is both challenging and it rewards students.

→ Mixing adjective with clause

Placement Matters

Place correlative conjunctions immediately before the parallel elements. Moving the conjunction changes what must be parallel.

✓ Version 1: She will either study biology or study chemistry.

→ Both: verb + object

✓ Version 2: She will study either biology or chemistry.

→ Both: nouns (more concise!)

Strategy: Position the correlative conjunction as close to the varying elements as possible. This makes the parallel structure clearer and more concise.

4. Parallelism in Comparisons and Special Structures

Beyond lists and conjunctions, parallelism applies to comparisons, repeated structures, and certain idiomatic expressions. The ACT tests whether you can maintain consistent form in these more complex situations.

Comparisons with "Than" and "As"

When comparing two things using "than" or "as...as," the elements being compared must be parallel.

✓ Parallel: Reading a book is more relaxing than watching television.

→ Both gerunds

✗ Not parallel: Reading a book is more relaxing than to watch television.

→ Mixing gerund with infinitive

✓ Parallel: She is as talented as she is dedicated.

→ Both adjectives with "is"

Comparing Nouns to Nouns

Make sure you're comparing equivalent things. Don't compare a thing to a possessive or a characteristic.

✗ Not parallel: The population of Texas is larger than California.

→ Comparing "population" to "California" (should compare populations)

✓ Parallel: The population of Texas is larger than that of California.

✓ Parallel: Texas's population is larger than California's.

Repeated Structures

When a sentence uses parallel structure for rhetorical effect, maintain that structure throughout.

✓ Parallel: To succeed, you must work hard, stay focused, and never give up.

→ All imperative verb phrases

With Prepositions and Articles

When listing items with repeated prepositions or articles, either repeat them for each item or include them only once before the entire list.

✓ Parallel (repeat): She is interested in biology, in chemistry, and in physics.

✓ Parallel (once): She is interested in biology, chemistry, and physics.

✗ Not parallel: She is interested in biology, chemistry, and in physics.

→ Inconsistent preposition use

ACT Test-Taking Strategy

When you see lists, conjunctions, or comparisons underlined on the ACT, immediately check for parallel structure.

Three-step check:

1. Identify the list items or elements being connected

2. Name the grammatical form of each element

3. Verify all elements use the same form
