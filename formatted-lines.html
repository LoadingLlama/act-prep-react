ACT questions commonly ask you how to use and solve linear functions, which is just a fancy term for lines. Lines appear in the form y = mx + b. In this chapter, we will cover all the equations and common types of questions that appear on the ACT involving lines in the xy-plane.

1. Slope and Its Meaning

The slope measures how steep a line is. Given any two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

Slope = (y₁ - y₂)/(x₁ - x₂) = rise/run

Understanding Slope

  • Positive slope: line goes up and to the right
    • Higher slope = steeper line
    • Example: slope of 2 means up 2 units for every 1 unit right
  • Negative slope: line goes down and to the right
  • Zero slope: horizontal line
  • Undefined slope: vertical line

Example 1: Finding Slope from Two Points

What is the slope of the line that passes through (-7, 6) and (2, 3)?

A. -9/5
B. -1/3
C. 1/3
D. 3

Solution:

  • Slope = (6 - 3)/(-7 - 2) = 3/(-9) = -1/3

Answer: B

2. Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

Understanding relationships between lines is crucial for ACT success.

Key Relationships

  • Parallel lines have the same slope
    • If line A has slope 3, any parallel line also has slope 3
  • Perpendicular lines have negative reciprocal slopes
    • If line A has slope 2/3, perpendicular line has slope -3/2
    • To find negative reciprocal: flip fraction and change sign
    • Product of perpendicular slopes always equals -1

Example 2: Perpendicular Lines

Points (4, 10) and (1, 31) are on line g. If line p is perpendicular to line g, which equation could be line p?

A. y = 7x + 28
B. y = (1/7)x - 2
C. y = -7x + 10
D. y = (-1/7)x + 2

Solution:

  • Find slope of line g: (10 - 31)/(4 - 1) = -21/3 = -7
  • Perpendicular slope is negative reciprocal: -(-1/7) = 1/7

Answer: B

3. Forms of Linear Equations

Slope-Intercept Form (Most Useful)

The form y = mx + b is the simplest and most useful form for lines.

  • m = slope (steepness of line)
  • b = y-intercept (where line crosses y-axis)
  • You can immediately see both slope and y-intercept

Point-Slope Form

Use when you know the slope and one point: y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

  • (x₁, y₁) is a point on the line
  • m is the slope
  • Can convert to slope-intercept by solving for y

Standard Form

Format Ax + By = C is not particularly useful - convert to slope-intercept form by solving for y.

Example 3: Converting from Standard Form

What is the slope of the line -4x + 3y = 15?

A. -4
B. 4/3
C. 3
D. 5

Solution:

  • Solve for y: 3y = 4x + 15
  • y = (4/3)x + 5
  • Slope is 4/3

Answer: B

4. Important Formulas

Midpoint Formula

Given two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

Midpoint = ((x₁ + x₂)/2, (y₁ + y₂)/2)

Distance Formula

Given two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

Distance = √[(x₁ - x₂)² + (y₁ - y₂)²]
  • Alternative: Use Pythagorean theorem by sketching a right triangle

Finding Intercepts

  • X-intercept: where line crosses x-axis
    • Set y = 0 and solve for x
  • Y-intercept: where line crosses y-axis
    • Set x = 0 and solve for y
    • In y = mx + b form, the y-intercept is simply b

5. ACT Strategy Tips

Tip 1: Always Convert to Slope-Intercept Form

  • When given standard form or point-slope form:
    • Solve for y to get y = mx + b
    • This immediately shows slope and y-intercept
    • Makes comparing lines much easier

Tip 2: Remember the Perpendicular Slope Trick

  • To find perpendicular slope:
    • Flip the fraction (reciprocal)
    • Change the sign (positive ↔ negative)
    • Example: 3/4 becomes -4/3

Tip 3: Use Graphs to Check Your Answer

  • When working with equations:
    • Quickly sketch the line to visualize
    • Check if slope sign matches graph direction
    • Verify y-intercept location
