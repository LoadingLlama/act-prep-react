<p style="font-size: 16px; line-height: 1.7; margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0;">
Pronoun questions make up about 9% of the ACT English Test—roughly 7 questions per test! You'll need to know how to select the proper pronoun based on case ("he" vs. "him" or "who" vs. "whom"), make sure that a pronoun agrees with the word it's replacing, and spot ambiguous pronouns.
</p>

<h3 style="margin-top: 5rem; margin-bottom: 0.75rem; font-weight: 700;">
1. Pronoun Case: Subject vs. Object
</h3>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;"><strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">Pronouns</strong> are words that stand in for a person, place, or thing
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Pronouns have two cases: subject and object</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The pronoun's role in the sentence (subject or object) dictates which type is correct</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Subject Pronouns vs. Object Pronouns
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;"><strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">Subject pronouns</strong> (I, we, you, he, she, it, they, who) are used when the pronoun is doing the action
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "They went to the street festival on Saturday afternoon."</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;"><strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">Object pronouns</strong> (me, us, you, him, her, it, them, whom) are used when the pronoun is receiving the action
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "The man handed us a stack of flyers."</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">For simple pronoun case questions, trust your ear</li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Pronouns in a List
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Pronouns in lists are tricky because we often say them incorrectly in conversation
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Ignore the other items in the list and just read the pronoun by itself to tell which is correct</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "The policeman asked to speak to my friends and me."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Test: "The policeman asked to speak to me." ✓ (not "speak to I")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "His girlfriend and he went to the hockey game."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Test: "He went to the hockey game." ✓ (not "him went")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Pronouns in Prepositional Phrases
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Pronouns in prepositional phrases are ALWAYS in the object case
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">This rule can be difficult to hear by ear alone, so remember it</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "The bet between Joe and me was for just five dollars." (not "between Joe and I")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "To whom was the award given?" (not "to who")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "Jerry claims that a great rivalry exists among Tim, Scott, and him." (not "among...and he")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Who vs. Whom
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">The difference between "who" and "whom" is tested on almost every ACT
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Technically, "who" is the subject and "whom" is the object</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">But here's the trick you need to memorize:</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">The he/him replacement trick
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">If you can replace the word with "he," "she," or "they" → use "who"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">If you can replace the word with "him," "her," or "them" → use "whom"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">You may need to rearrange the sentence, but this trick works for any who vs. whom question</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Examples
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Who stole the apple off the teacher's desk?" → Test: "He stole the apple" ✓ → Use "who"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"To whom should I address this letter?" → Test: "I should address this letter to him" ✓ → Use "whom"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Who do you want to win the Bachelor?" → Test: "You want him to win" ✓ → Actually "whom" is correct here!</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Who vs. Whom in Descriptive Phrases
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">On difficult questions, who/whom can introduce a phrase describing a person
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The normal "he/him" trick doesn't work if you look at the entire sentence</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Instead, look only at the phrase itself</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Example: "The child whom I invited to the party was very noisy."
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Look only at the phrase: "whom I invited to the party"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Test: "I invited him to the party" ✓ → Use "whom"</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Example: "The child who was running down the street was very noisy."
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Look only at the phrase: "who was running down the street"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Test: "He was running down the street" ✓ → Use "who"</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Quick trick: Look at the word right after "who" or "whom"
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">If the word right after is a verb → use "who"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">If the word right after is a noun → use "whom"</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Reflexive Pronouns (myself, yourself, himself...)
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;"><strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">Reflexive pronouns</strong> use the "-self" ending and can only be used when the pronoun refers back to a person or thing elsewhere in the sentence
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "I grabbed three donuts and put them in a box for myself." (refers back to "I")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">❌ Incorrect: "I asked my coworker to put three donuts in a box for myself." ("myself" doesn't work because the subject is "my coworker," not "I")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "I asked my coworker to put three donuts in a box for me."</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h3 style="margin-top: 5rem; margin-bottom: 0.75rem; font-weight: 700;">
2. Possessive Pronouns
</h3>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;"><strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">Possessive pronouns</strong> show ownership
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">These pronouns must match with the word(s) they're replacing</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">They come in two forms: possessive nouns and possessive adjectives</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Possessive Nouns vs. Possessive Adjectives
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Possessive nouns stand alone: mine, ours, yours, his, hers, its, theirs
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "That book is mine." (stands alone, not followed by a noun)</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Possessive adjectives come before a noun: my, our, your, his, her, its, their
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "That is my book." (comes before the noun "book")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Matching Possessive Pronouns to the Antecedent
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Possessive pronouns must match the word they're replacing in number (singular vs. plural)
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">❌ Incorrect: "The entire team went on stage to pick up their trophy."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The pronoun refers back to "team," which is singular (remember: group nouns are singular!)</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "The entire team went on stage to pick up its trophy."</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">More examples
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Emmanuel and James came back home after forgetting their phones." (plural subjects → "their")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Thousands of tourists travel to Egypt each year to see its ancient pyramids." (Egypt is singular → "its")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h3 style="margin-top: 5rem; margin-bottom: 0.75rem; font-weight: 700;">
3. Pronoun Agreement
</h3>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Pronouns must agree with the <strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">antecedent</strong>, which is the noun the pronoun is replacing
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Whenever you see a pronoun on the ACT, find exactly who or what it's referring to</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The antecedent can be in the same sentence or in a preceding sentence</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The antecedent can appear anywhere (unnecessary information, prepositional phrases, etc.)</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Matching in Plurality
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">A singular antecedent must have a singular pronoun; a plural antecedent must have a plural pronoun
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">❌ Incorrect: "Each attendee at the party raised their drink for a toast."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Each attendee" is singular → pronoun must be singular</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "Each attendee at the party raised his or her drink for a toast."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Also correct: "The guests at the party raised their drinks for a toast." (plural → plural)</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Matching in Gender
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">A male antecedent must have a male pronoun; a female antecedent must have a female pronoun
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: "Jane and Sarah, after a long conversation with another group of hikers on the trail, said they were too tired to go any farther today."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Jane and Sarah" (female, plural) → "they" (plural, works for any gender)</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Common Tricky Cases
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Watch out for singular words that seem plural
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"Each member of the women's track team improved her time." ("each member" is singular → "her")</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"A person who is committed to his or her dreams can achieve them." ("a person" is singular → "his or her")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Consistency with pronoun style
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">"If you plan to give a presentation on the importance of accurate data, you should check your facts first."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Keep the same pronoun style throughout: "you...you...your" (not "you...one...one's")</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h3 style="margin-top: 5rem; margin-bottom: 0.75rem; font-weight: 700;">
4. Ambiguous Pronouns
</h3>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">For a pronoun to be correct, we must know exactly who or what it's referring to
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">If we don't know exactly who or what a pronoun is referring to, the pronoun is <strong style="color: #2563eb; font-weight: 600; text-decoration: underline;">ambiguous</strong> and is always incorrect</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Examples of Ambiguous Pronouns
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Ambiguous "he"
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">❌ Incorrect: "Stephen smiled at his father as he threw the ball."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">We don't know if "he" refers to Stephen or his father, so "he" is ambiguous</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "Stephen smiled at his father as his father threw the ball."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Even though it seems redundant, replacing "he" with "his father" fixes the error</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Ambiguous "some"
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">❌ Incorrect: "Even though the order had not yet shown up, the librarian insisted that she had ordered some."</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">We have no idea what "some" refers to</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">✓ Correct: "Even though the order had not yet shown up, the librarian insisted that she had ordered four copies of the new book."</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h4 style="margin-top: 2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 600;">
Spotting Ambiguous Pronouns Questions
</h4>

<ul style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem; line-height: 1.7;">
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Anytime you see a pronoun in the answer choices, check if it's ambiguous
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Pronouns to watch for: he, him, his, she, her, hers, they, them, their, it, its, this, that, these, those, some, none</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
  <li style="margin: 0.3rem 0;">Look for answer choices that replace the pronoun with a specific noun
    <ul style="margin: 0.3rem 0; padding-left: 1.5rem;">
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">The correct answer often specifies exactly what or who is being referred to</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: Instead of "he is the most famous," the answer might be "Biggie is the most famous"</li>
      <li style="margin: 0.2rem 0;">Example: Instead of "asked for one," the answer might be "asked for a slider"</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>
